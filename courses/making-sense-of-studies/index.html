Statistics & Data

Making Sense of Studies

How to read the statistics behind a headline: what a p-value and a confidence interval actually claim, why "significant" doesn't mean "big," and the careful wording that keeps you from being fooled.

Statistics & Data Beginner 3 modules ~0.8h Path · Statistical Literacy
Before you start
  • Comfort with percentages and the idea that samples vary
  • The Statistics Essentials course is the ideal warm-up, but this course stands on its own

“New study links X to Y.” “The effect was statistically significant.” “The poll has a margin of error of three points.” These phrases are everywhere, and almost all of us misread at least one of them — often the most important one. This short course is about reading them correctly.

It’s the second mini-course in the Statistics, From the Ground Up series, and it stands on its own: where it leans on a shared idea — that results wobble from one sample to the next, that the job is to tell a real signal from random noise — it reminds you in a line. (If you want the full grounding, the Statistics Essentials course is the warm-up.) Across three short modules we take apart the machinery behind a study’s claims: the null hypothesis and the p-value (what “could this just be chance?” really asks), why statistical significance is not the same as a big or important effect, and how to read a confidence interval without falling for the tempting misreading almost everyone makes.

There’s no arithmetic to grind here. The whole point is the careful meaning of a few famous numbers — because the difference between what a p-value is and what people think it is, is exactly the difference between being informed and being fooled.

Modules

  1. 01 Could This Just Be Chance? The question every study secretly asks — if nothing were really going on, would data like ours be surprising? — and the careful meaning of the number that answers it, the p-value. 16 min
  2. 02 Significant Isn't the Same as Big Why a "statistically significant" result can be trivially small, why hunting through many tests manufactures fake findings, and why "no effect found" never settles that there's nothing there. 14 min
  3. 03 A Range, Not a Point Why a careful study reports a range instead of a single number, and the much-misquoted meaning of a 95% confidence interval. 14 min
