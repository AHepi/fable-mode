Module 3 of 10

The Case Against Moral Facts

What you'll be able to do
  • State Hume's is/ought gap precisely: that no valid inference runs from purely descriptive premises to a normative conclusion.
  • Distinguish Moore's naturalistic fallacy and open-question argument, which is about definition, from Hume's point, which is about inference.
  • Explain Mackie's error theory and argument from queerness, and how it differs from Ayer's emotivism.

The hook

The case from the last module was the easy one to answer. Relativism leans on a fact about cultures, and Ayer’s emotivism on a fact about how moral talk feels, and a defender of objective morality can at least imagine what would tell against each of them. Show that one culture’s moral code corrected another’s, and relativism is in trouble. Show that “slavery is wrong” can be reasoned about and got right, and “boo to slavery” starts to look like a thin description of what is going on.

The arguments in this module are harder, because they do not lean on a fact that might come out the other way. They are about the shape of moral thought itself, and three different philosophers found three different things wrong with that shape. One found a broken inference. One found a failed definition. One found a fact that could not exist. Each, on its own, would make a defender of objective morality sit up. Run together, as they usually are, they harden into a single vague conviction that “you just can’t get morality from facts,” and that conviction is the wall this whole course was built to get over.

So the three have to be taken apart before they can be answered, because an answer to one is not an answer to the others. Keep them as three different keys, not one. They open three different locks.

Context & background

The three thinkers do not even share a century. David Hume, the eighteenth-century Scottish philosopher whose work runs under almost everything in modern philosophy, published the argument in 1739. G. E. Moore, an early-twentieth-century Cambridge philosopher who helped found the analytic tradition, published his in 1903. J. L. Mackie, an Australian philosopher writing in Oxford, published his in 1977. More than two centuries separate the first from the last, and the differences between them are not period detail. They are differences in what part of moral thought is supposed to be broken.

A word to fix first, since all three arguments turn on it. An inference is the move from premises to a conclusion, and a valid inference is one where, if the premises are true, the conclusion cannot be false. From “all whales are mammals” and “this is a whale,” the conclusion “this is a mammal” follows validly: granting the premises, you cannot deny the conclusion without contradicting yourself. That is the standard the first argument holds moral reasoning to, and claims it fails.

Hold one more distinction ready, because the whole module depends on not letting it collapse. There is a difference between an inference (moving from one claim to another) and a definition (saying what a word means). “All bachelors are unmarried” is true by definition; nothing is inferred. “Socrates is mortal” is inferred from premises; no definition is involved. The first argument below is about a broken inference. The second is about a failed definition. They are not the same complaint in different words, and the single most common mistake in this area is to hear them as if they were.

Narrative / exposition

Hume: the inference that does not go through

Start with the descriptive and the normative, the is and the ought. A descriptive claim says how things are: the prisoner is in pain, the climate is warming, most people disapprove of theft. A normative claim says how things ought to be, or what is good, or what one should do: you ought not torture, theft is wrong, this is the better choice. Hume’s observation is about the relation between the two.

He noticed that moral arguments tend to run for a while in the descriptive register, piling up claims about what is, about God and human nature and the consequences of actions, and then, without warning, the verbs change. Is and is not give way to ought and ought not. A conclusion about what should be done appears, resting on premises that were only ever about what is the case. And Hume’s point is that this transition is never explained, because it cannot be: an ought is a new thing, expressing a new relation, and you cannot validly deduce it from premises in which it never appears.

This is a claim about inference, and it is worth being exact about its scope. Hume is not saying moral claims are meaningless, or that they are only feelings, or that there are no moral facts. He is making a narrower and harder point. Take any set of premises that are purely descriptive, containing no ought anywhere, and no conclusion containing an ought follows from them by valid inference. The conclusion always smuggles in something the premises did not contain. “This child is starving” is a fact. “You ought to feed it” is a different kind of claim, and the move from the first to the second, however obvious it feels, is not a valid deduction. Something has to be added, some further premise that already carries an ought, and then the question only moves back a step: where did that ought come from?

That is the is/ought gap, and it leaves a great deal open. It does not say there are no moral truths. It says that if there are, they do not follow from the non-moral facts by logic alone. The objection cuts at the bridge between facts and values, not at the values themselves.

Moore: the definition that always leaves a question open

Moore comes at morality from a different angle, and a tempting reply to Hume is what brings him in. Suppose someone grants Hume’s point about bare inference but tries to close the gap with a definition. Let “good,” they say, just mean some natural property, some feature of the world you could in principle observe or measure. Maybe “good” means “what produces pleasure,” or “what we desire to desire,” or “what promotes survival.” If “good” simply means one of these, then the gap closes, because now a fact about pleasure or survival just is a fact about goodness, by definition, the way a fact about bachelors is already a fact about unmarried men.

Moore’s argument is that no such definition can work, and his test for showing it is the open-question argument. Take any natural property you like, call it N, and propose that “good” means N. Now ask: “This action is N, but is it good?” If “good” really meant N, that question would be closed, as senseless as asking “This man is unmarried, but is he a bachelor?” The predicate would already be contained in the subject; there would be nothing left to ask. But for every candidate N anyone has ever proposed, the question stays open. “This produces the most pleasure, but is it good?” is a real question, one a thoughtful person can ask without confusion and answer either way. The same holds for survival, for desire, for social approval, for any natural property at all. Since the question stays open, “good” cannot mean N. To define goodness as any natural property, Moore says, is to commit what he calls the naturalistic fallacy.

Here is the seam, and it must not be blurred. Moore’s argument is about definition, not inference. Hume said you cannot deduce an ought from an is. Moore says something logically distinct: you cannot define good as any natural property in the first place. You could accept Hume completely and still hope to rescue morality by defining goodness in natural terms, and Moore is the argument that shuts that hope. And you could accept Moore completely, agreeing that “good” names some non-natural property all its own, and still face Hume’s question of how any ought gets validly inferred. The two objections attack two different joints. One says the bridge from facts to values is not a valid inference. The other says goodness cannot be turned into a natural fact by definition so as to make the bridge unnecessary. Fuse them into “you can’t get morality from facts” and you have lost exactly the distinction that makes each one bite.

Mackie: the values are real targets, and they all miss

Mackie grants more than either of the others and ends in a darker place. He agrees that ordinary moral language is not just disguised feeling. When a person says “cruelty is wrong,” Mackie thinks they really are asserting something, really are claiming that wrongness belongs to the cruelty itself, out there, independent of anyone’s attitude, the way a shape belongs to an object. Ordinary moral thought aims at objective facts. On this point Mackie and the plain moral realist agree completely.

And then Mackie says: every one of those claims is false. Not meaningless, not merely local, but false, in the way “there are unicorns” is false. The claims aim at objective moral facts, and there are no objective moral facts for them to hit, so they all miss. This is his error theory: ordinary morality is a systematic error, a whole region of human thought built around a presupposition that nothing answers to. We are not expressing feelings and we are not stating local truths. We are making genuine claims about objective values, and the claims are wrong.

Why think there are no such values? Here is his argument from queerness, and it has two edges. The metaphysical edge: objective values, if they existed, would be entities or qualities or relations utterly unlike anything else in the universe. An objective wrongness would have to be a feature of an action that somehow, just by being perceived, told you not to do the thing, that carried its own “to-be-pursued” or “not-to-be-done” built into it. Nothing else in the natural world is like that. Facts about atoms and forces and pains do not come with built-in directions to the will. A value that did would be, in Mackie’s word, queer, strange past anything we have reason to believe in. The epistemological edge: to know such values, we would need some special faculty of moral perception, wholly different from sense perception and from ordinary reasoning, a faculty no one has ever been able to describe. We have eyes for color and instruments for charge. By what organ do we detect wrongness? The absence of any credible answer is the second edge of the queerness argument.

Now hold Mackie against Ayer, the emotivist from the last module, because here the two are most easily confused and most genuinely opposed. Ayer says moral sentences state nothing. “Cruelty is wrong” has no more factual content than a wince; it expresses an attitude and is neither true nor false. Mackie says the exact opposite about content: moral sentences do state something, they make a real claim about objective wrongness, and that is precisely why they can be false. For Ayer there is no claim, so there is nothing to be false. For Mackie there is a claim, and it fails. The two views could not be reconciled, even though both end up denying that anything moral is objectively true. They disagree about what is going on in a person’s mouth when they condemn cruelty: a feeling vented, or a fact-claim that misfires.

The worry the course inherits

Stand back from the three. Hume says no valid inference carries you from the facts to an ought. Moore says goodness cannot be defined into the facts. Mackie says the facts morality reaches for are not there to be reached. Put together, they sketch a single forbidding picture. There seem to be no moral facts, nothing out in the world for a moral claim to be answerable to, and no test, no procedure that could tell a true moral claim from a false one the way an experiment tells a true physics claim from a false one. The cupboard the first module worried might be bare now has three independent arguments saying it is bare.

That is the strongest form of the case against moral objectivity, and this module does no more than build it. The reply begins in the next module, with a problem older than ethics, and it does not come from inside ethics at all.

Primary source

The is/ought passage is one paragraph, near the end of the first section of Book III. Hume has been arguing about whether moral distinctions come from reason, and he pauses to add what he calls a small observation that may be of some importance.

In every system of morality, which I have hitherto met with, I have always remarked, that the author proceeds for some time in the ordinary way of reasoning, and establishes the being of a God, or makes observations concerning human affairs; when of a sudden I am surprised to find, that instead of the usual copulations of propositions, is, and is not, I meet with no proposition that is not connected with an ought, or an ought not. This change is imperceptible; but is, however, of the last consequence.

David Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–40), Book III, Part I, Section I

Read what the complaint actually is. It is not that the moralists reach false conclusions. It is that they make a move, from is to ought, that is “imperceptible,” slipped in without notice, and yet “of the last consequence,” meaning of the greatest importance. The verbs change and no one explains how the new kind of proposition was earned. Hume goes on to say that this small attention, if the reader pays it, “would subvert all the vulgar systems of morality,” not by refuting their particular claims but by exposing the unguarded step every one of them takes. The threat is to the method, the silent crossing from fact to value, and that is exactly why a defender of objective morality cannot wave it off as old or quaint. The crossing is one we all make daily, and Hume’s question is what licenses it.

Analysis & interpretation

It would be easy to read all three as the same person making the same complaint in three accents, and that reading is the one to resist, because it is wrong about the philosophy and it makes the arguments weaker than they are. Take them as one and you get a mood. Take them apart and you get three theses, each of which has to be met on its own ground.

The cleanest way to keep them separate is to ask, of each, what exactly fails. For Hume, an inference fails: granting any pile of descriptive premises, the ought does not follow. For Moore, a definition fails: “good” cannot be made to mean any natural property, because the question always stays open. For Mackie, the world fails the claims: moral assertions are perfectly meaningful and aim at real targets, but the objective values they aim at are too queer to exist, so the assertions come out false. Inference, definition, ontology. Three different parts of the picture, three different verdicts, three different keys.

There is room for honest disagreement about how far each argument reaches, and the seams are exactly where the later modules press. Hume’s gap is widely accepted as stated, but its force depends on whether valid deductive inference is the only kind of support a conclusion can have, and a later module will ask whether scientific conclusions have that kind of support either. Moore’s open-question argument has been pressed hard: a critic can grant that “good” and “N” differ in meaning while denying that they pick out different properties, the way “water” and “H2O” mean different things yet name one substance. And Mackie’s queerness can be turned around: one might insist that the moral is simply a real and distinctive part of the world, queer or not, and that strangeness is no proof of absence. None of these replies is offered here, and none of them is cheap. The point of laying out where each argument might be questioned is only to show that three distinct arguments invite three distinct replies, which is the surest sign that they were three distinct arguments all along.

What the module establishes is not that morality is subjective. It is that the case against moral facts is real, specific, and threefold, and that anyone who means to defend moral objectivity owes an answer to each of the three, not a single gesture at all of them. The course will answer them. It cannot do so until it has stopped treating them as one.

Check yourself

From memory. A friend says: “Hume and Moore both proved you can’t get morality from facts, so they’re really making the same point.” Name the difference the friend has missed, and state precisely what fails in each argument.

Show answer

The friend has fused an argument about inference with an argument about definition. For Hume, an inference fails: from premises that are purely descriptive (only is, no ought), no normative conclusion (ought) follows by valid deduction. The gap is in the move from facts to values. For Moore, a definition fails: “good” cannot be defined as any natural property N, because for every N the question “this is N, but is it good?” stays open, which it would not if “good” just meant N (the naturalistic fallacy, shown by the open-question argument). The two are logically independent. You could accept Hume’s point about inference and still try to define goodness in natural terms; Moore is what blocks that. You could accept Moore’s point about definition and still owe Hume an account of how any ought gets validly inferred. Different joints, different failures, different replies.

Check yourself

From memory. Ayer (last module) and Mackie both deny that any moral claim is objectively true. So why are they not the same view? State what each thinks is going on when a person says “cruelty is wrong.”

Show answer

They disagree about whether the sentence states anything at all. For Ayer (emotivism), “cruelty is wrong” states no fact; it expresses an attitude, closer to “boo to cruelty,” and so is neither true nor false. There is no claim, so there is nothing to be false. For Mackie (error theory), the sentence does state something: it genuinely asserts that wrongness belongs to the cruelty objectively, independent of anyone’s attitude. Because it is a real claim about an objective value, it can be false, and Mackie says it is false, because no such objective values exist (they would be metaphysically and epistemologically “queer,” knowable only by some special faculty no one can describe). So both deny moral objectivity, but for opposite reasons: Ayer because there is no fact-claim to assess, Mackie because there is a fact-claim and it misfires.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down. Each objection in this module must be handled at full strength, in its own terms, before any criticism of it.

  1. State one, sharpen one. Pick whichever of the three arguments you find strongest and reconstruct it in your own words as a short, valid-looking piece of reasoning, premises then conclusion. Then state, as precisely as you can, what a defender of objective morality would have to deny to escape it.

  2. The inference and the definition. Construct a moral argument you find compelling (any: that a promise creates an obligation, that gratuitous cruelty is wrong) and locate in it the exact step where is becomes ought. Then say whether Moore’s worry about defining “good” touches your argument at all, or whether it leaves Moore’s point entirely untouched, and why.

  3. Take Mackie’s side against Ayer. Argue, in Mackie’s voice, that emotivism gets ordinary moral experience wrong: that when people condemn cruelty they take themselves to be stating a fact, not venting a feeling. Then say what this buys Mackie and what it costs him, given that he must now call all those fact-stating claims false.

  4. Where would you push back? Choose one of the three arguments and write the beginning of a reply to it, the strongest objection you can raise, while conceding everything in the argument that genuinely holds. Do not resolve it. The aim is to find the one joint where the argument might be pried, not to win.

What a strong response does
  • Keeps the three arguments distinct and can say, for any one of them, what exactly fails: an inference (Hume), a definition (Moore), or the existence of the targeted facts (Mackie). Treating them as one complaint is the error these prompts test against.
  • States each objection in its strongest, most sympathetic form before raising any difficulty with it, and can tell steelmanning apart from setting up a weak version to knock down.
  • Gets the Mackie/Ayer contrast right: Ayer holds moral claims state no fact (no truth-value); Mackie holds they state a fact that is false. A response that blurs these has missed the module’s central distinction.
  • Grounds claims in the actual structure of each argument (the open-question test, the is-to-ought step, the two edges of queerness), not in a vague sense that “morality isn’t objective.”
  • Stays inside what the module established. It does not import a Popperian or Deutschian reply; those answers belong to later modules, and a strong response here builds the case rather than pre-empting its refutation.

The story

In 1739 a Scotsman not yet thirty published, in two anonymous volumes, the book that contained the is/ought paragraph. He had drafted much of it in France, at La Flèche, in his twenties, in a burst of confidence that he had found a new science of human nature as consequential as anything in physics. A third volume, the one on morals where the is and the ought finally part, followed in 1740. He expected the work to make him.

It did nothing of the kind. Hume looked back on the reception years later, in the short autobiography he wrote near the end of his life, and the verdict he reached for has outlived almost everything else he said about himself. “Never literary attempt was more unfortunate than my Treatise of Human Nature,” he wrote. “It fell dead-born from the press, without reaching such distinction as even to excite a murmur among the zealots.” Not attacked, not refuted, not even noticed by the people whose business was to be scandalized. Ignored. He blamed the writing more than the matter, decided he had published too young, and spent much of the rest of his career recasting the same ideas in essays and enquiries that found the audience the Treatise had missed.

The paragraph about is and ought went down with the rest. There is no record of a single contemporary fastening on it, no early storm over the gap between fact and value, nothing. The most discussed argument in the modern philosophy of morality spent its first century or more almost unread, a few sentences in a commercially failed book by a young man who thought he had failed.

And then it would not stay buried. Long after Hume’s death the Treatise came to be seen as his deepest work, and the dead-born paragraph became, in the twentieth century, one of the most cited passages in all of ethics, the thing now taught under his name to every student who meets the subject, the wall that any theory of objective morality must still find a way over. A claim about an inference, made by a man who thought no one was listening, in a book that fell dead from the press, turned out to be exactly the kind of thing the first module said an objective truth could be. It was answerable to more than its author’s reputation. It could be ignored for a century and be no less forceful for it, because its force never depended on anyone noticing. The neglect of the Treatise did not touch the argument. The argument was waiting, and it is waiting still, for the answer the next module begins to build.
