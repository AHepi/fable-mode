Module 4 of 10

Knowledge Without Foundations

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain fallibilism (all knowledge is conjectural and corrigible) and why it rejects knowledge as justified true belief.
  • State the problem of induction (observations never prove a general theory) and Popper's dissolution of it: we conjecture and test by attempted refutation.
  • Explain how knowledge grows by conjecture and criticism rather than by derivation from a base.

The hook

Count swans. You have spent your whole life by the river, and every swan you have ever seen has been white. Your parents saw only white swans, and so did theirs. The poultry books say white, the paintings show white, the word “swan” all but means “white bird with a long neck.” Ten thousand sightings, not one exception. So you say it: all swans are white. How could you not.

Now ask what those ten thousand sightings actually entitle you to. They are all about swans you have seen. The claim is about swans, full stop, including the ones swimming on rivers no European had yet reached. Between the evidence and the claim there is a gap, and nothing in the evidence closes it. One more sighting could blow the whole thing up, and one day, on a river far from any of yours, one did. The general rule “all swans are white” was never proved by the white swans. It was only ever a guess that the white swans had not yet killed.

This is not a quirk of swans. It is the shape of every general claim about the world, and the three philosophers of the last module thought it was a special wound in morality. The reader who has come this far is owed the news that it is nothing of the kind. The wound runs through everything, physics included, and yet physics is the surest knowledge we have. How that can be, and how knowledge grows when nothing underneath it is secure, is the work of the philosopher who saw it most clearly.

Context & background

The last module left morality looking foundationless. Hume’s gap between is and ought says you cannot validly reason from how the world is to how it ought to be. Moore’s open question says “good” cannot be pinned to any natural fact. Mackie says the moral facts we reach for would be too strange to exist, so all our moral claims fail without our noticing. Stacked together, they deliver a verdict that feels devastating: there is nothing solid under morality, no bedrock from which “slavery is wrong” could be derived and made to compel every rational mind. No foundation, so no objectivity. Just opinion after all.

That verdict has a hidden premise, and the hidden premise is false. The premise is that real knowledge rests on a foundation, that somewhere under physics and history and mathematics there is a base of certain truths from which the rest is built up, and that morality’s disgrace is to lack one. Take away the premise and the verdict collapses, because the lack of a foundation stops being morality’s private shame and becomes the universal condition of knowing anything at all.

The man who took away the premise was Karl Popper, the twentieth-century philosopher of science whose account of how knowledge grows the first module already leaned on, when he called the old ideal of certain, demonstrable knowledge an idol and argued that genuine objectivity keeps every claim forever open to correction. That was the opening note. Here is the full argument behind it. Popper spent his life on a question that has nothing on its face to do with ethics: how does science actually work, given that no amount of evidence can ever prove a scientific law? His answer dissolves the demand for a foundation everywhere it appears, and morality is one of the places it appears.

Three ideas do the work, and they are really one idea seen from three sides.

The first is fallibilism, sometimes called critical rationalism: the view that all our knowledge is conjectural and could be wrong, that certainty is not on offer in any subject, and that knowledge grows not by reaching a secure base but by finding and correcting errors. Fallibilism is not despair. A fallibilist is not saying we know nothing. A fallibilist is saying that everything we know, we hold open to revision, and that this openness is the strength of knowledge and not its weakness.

The second is anti-justificationism. For most of the history of philosophy, knowledge was defined as justified true belief: to know something was to believe it, for it to be true, and crucially to have a justification, a reason that secured the belief and put it beyond reasonable doubt. The whole tradition then went hunting for the justification, the base of certainty that would do the securing. Popper’s answer is that the hunt was a mistake from the start. There is no such justification, anywhere, for anything. Knowledge is never justified. It is only ever criticized, exposed to every attempt we can mount to show it false, and provisionally kept when those attempts fail. The demand for a secure foundation is not unmet by accident. It is a demand that cannot be met, and the surprise is how little we lose by giving it up.

The third is the engine that makes the first two more than a counsel of humility: the problem of induction and Popper’s dissolution of it. That is where the swans come in, and it is the tool the next module turns on morality.

Narrative / exposition

The problem of induction is old, and Hume, the same Hume who drew the is/ought gap, is the one who sharpened it. Here is the problem. We believe countless general truths about the world: that bread nourishes, that the sun will rise, that unsupported rocks fall, that all swans are white. Each of these is a claim about every case, including the cases we have not met and never will. But all our evidence is a finite pile of cases we have met. How does the finite pile justify the universal claim? It does not, and cannot. No number of confirming instances adds up to a proof of the rule, because the very next instance is free to break it. Ten thousand white swans do not prove all swans are white; they are merely ten thousand chances the claim had to be refuted and was not. The rule reaches infinitely beyond the evidence, and the evidence can never catch up.

For three centuries this looked like a scandal at the heart of science. If observations cannot prove a theory, and science is built on observation, then science rests on a logical mistake, a leap from “some” to “all” that no rule of reasoning permits. Philosophers tried to patch it. Maybe many observations make a theory probable even if they cannot make it certain. Popper showed that this does not work either, because a universal claim covers infinitely many cases and any finite pile of evidence is an infinitesimal fraction of them, so the probability it confers stays stuck at zero. The patch fails. There is no valid inference, certain or probable, from the evidence to the law.

Popper’s move was to stop trying to patch it and look again at what science actually does, and what he saw was that science never makes the forbidden inference in the first place. Scientists do not start with observations and grind them into laws. They start with a problem, throw out a bold guess at the answer, a conjecture that leaps far beyond any evidence, and then they turn on their own guess and try with everything they have to break it. The guess is not derived from anything. It is invented, freely, the way a hypothesis or a hunch or a work of imagination is invented. What makes it science is not where it came from but what happens next: it is exposed to tests that could kill it. A good test is one the conjecture might well fail. If it fails, the conjecture is wrong and we have learned something real, a genuine error found and discarded. If it survives, we have learned only that it has not been refuted yet, and we keep it, provisionally, as our current best guess, and we keep trying to break it.

This is the dissolution, and it deserves the word. Popper did not solve the problem of induction by finding the missing justification. He showed there was never any induction to justify. Knowledge does not flow up from observations to laws. It is conjectured at the top and tested from below, and what survives criticism is retained, not because it has been proved but because it has not been refuted. The asymmetry is everything. No number of white swans can prove “all swans are white,” but a single black swan refutes it outright. Confirmation is powerless; refutation is decisive. So the rational thing to do with any general claim is not to pile up confirmations, which prove nothing, but to hunt for the refutation, the one case that would show it false, and to trust the claim exactly as far as it has survived that hunt.

Give the engine its name. A conjecture is a guess offered to solve a problem. Criticism is the attempt to find what is wrong with the guess, by argument, by test, by any means that could expose an error. Knowledge grows by the interplay of the two: conjecture and criticism, bold guessing and ruthless error-hunting, with no foundation underneath and none needed. This is the engine of the whole course, and from here on the words carry it.

See what has happened to the demand for a foundation. It was supposed to be the thing that made knowledge respectable, and morality’s failure to provide one was supposed to be a disgrace. But science has no foundation either. There is no base of certain truths under physics from which the laws are derived, because the laws are not derived from anything; they are guessed and tested. Physics is the clearest case of objective knowledge in the world, and it grows with no foundation at all, by exactly the conjecture-and-criticism that needs no base. So “no foundation” cannot be what disqualifies a body of claims from objectivity. If it were, it would disqualify physics, which is absurd. The foundationlessness that the last module made morality wear as a mark of shame turns out to be the ordinary dress of all knowledge.

What this does to the case against moral facts is not yet spelled out. That is the next module’s work, and it is the hinge the whole course turns on. The piece that had to be in place first is the one now in place: there are no foundations anywhere, and knowledge grows without them.

Check yourself

Before reading on, answer from memory. Someone says: “We have observed a million sunrises, so we have proved the sun will rise tomorrow.” State the problem of induction this ignores, and describe how Popper says we actually come to trust such a claim.

Show answer

The problem of induction is that no number of observed cases can prove a general claim, because the claim covers cases not yet observed, and the next one is always free to break the rule. A million observed sunrises are all about past mornings; “the sun will rise tomorrow” is about a morning not yet observed, and no valid inference, certain or even probable, carries us from the one to the other. Confirmations cannot prove a universal claim.

Popper’s dissolution: we never make that inference. We conjecture that the sun will rise, a bold guess that goes far beyond the evidence, and we expose it to attempted refutation, every test and argument that could show it false. It has survived all of them so far, so we keep it as our best current conjecture. We trust it exactly as far as it has withstood criticism, not because the past sunrises proved it. Confirmation is powerless; a single refutation would be decisive.

Primary source

Popper stated the whole picture, fallibilism and anti-justification and the conjecture-and-criticism engine together, in the Preface to his 1963 collection Conjectures and Refutations. He is describing knowledge in general, science above all, with no thought of ethics. Read it slowly; every clause is load-bearing.

The way in which knowledge progresses, and especially our scientific knowledge, is by unjustified (and unjustifiable) anticipations, by guesses, by tentative solutions to our problems, by conjectures. These conjectures are controlled by criticism; that is, by attempted refutations, which include severely critical tests. They may survive these tests; but they can never be positively justified: they can neither be established as certainly true nor even as ‘probable’ (in the sense of the probability calculus).

Karl Popper, Conjectures and Refutations (1963), Preface

Take the clauses in turn. Our knowledge progresses “by unjustified (and unjustifiable) anticipations”: the guesses come first, before any evidence could license them, and not only are they not justified, they cannot be, because there is no justification to be had. That is anti-justificationism stated flatly. “These conjectures are controlled by criticism; that is, by attempted refutations”: the guesses are not left to stand or fall on their pedigree but are attacked, tested against everything that could break them. That is the engine. “They may survive these tests; but they can never be positively justified”: survival is all we ever get, never proof. A conjecture that has withstood the fiercest criticism we can mount is not thereby shown true; it is shown to be the best guess still standing. And the last clause shuts the back door that philosophers kept trying to prop open, the hope that evidence at least makes a theory “probable.” It does not, Popper says, not even that. The hope was the last refuge of justificationism, and it fails for the same reason the rest does. There is no inference up from the evidence at all, only conjecture from above and refutation from below.

Analysis & interpretation

The strongest objection to all this is that it sounds like surrender. If knowledge is never justified, never proved, never even made probable, then on what basis does anyone prefer Einstein to astrology, or a careful history to a wild rumour? Popper’s critics, including serious philosophers of science after him, pressed exactly this. If confirmations count for nothing, the worry goes, then a theory that has survived a thousand brutal tests is in no better position than one nobody has bothered to test, since neither is justified. That cannot be right, and if Popper’s view implies it, the view is in trouble.

Popper’s answer is that the asymmetry between confirmation and refutation does the work that justification was supposed to do, and does it without the impossible promise. The two theories are not in the same position at all. The well-tested theory has been exposed, again and again, to circumstances that could have refuted it and did not. The untested theory has faced none of that. Neither is proved, true enough, but one has survived a gauntlet and the other has not even entered it, and surviving a gauntlet is a real, objective difference, the only kind on offer. To prefer the theory that has withstood severe criticism is not to claim it has been justified. It is to back the conjecture that has earned its standing by risk, against rivals that risked nothing. That is rational preference without proof, and Popper’s wager is that it is the only kind there ever was.

A reader could still resist. Perhaps, the resistance runs, this picture fits physics, where you can run a clean experiment that a theory might fail, but morality has no such test, nothing that plays the role the experiment plays, and so the conjecture-and-criticism story cannot simply be carried over to ethics. That is a fair and serious worry, and this module does not answer it; the question of whether morality has its own form of criticism is precisely what the later modules must earn. What is established here is narrower and indispensable. The demand that knowledge rest on a foundation is a demand that nothing meets, science least of all, and a body of claims is not disqualified from objectivity by failing to meet it. That much is Popper’s, it is about knowledge in general, and it clears the ground the next module builds on.

Check yourself

From memory: the traditional definition of knowledge is justified true belief. Which word does Popper reject, and what does he put in its place? Answer in two or three sentences.

Show answer

Popper rejects justified. On the traditional view, to know something is to have a belief that is true and that you can justify, a reason securing it against doubt by tracing it back to some foundation. Popper denies there is any such justification for anything; the search for a secure base cannot succeed. In its place he puts criticism: knowledge is belief that has been exposed to attempted refutation and has so far survived. It is never justified, only criticized and provisionally retained. This is fallibilism: all knowledge is conjectural and corrigible, held open to correction rather than proved.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Run the engine on a belief you hold. Take a general belief you are confident of, in any field. State it as a conjecture: what is the guess, what problem was it offered to solve, and what observation or argument would refute it if it were false? If you cannot name anything that would refute it, say what that tells you about the belief, on Popper’s view.

  2. Press the surrender objection. Reconstruct, as strongly as you can, the worry that a view in which knowledge is never justified or even made probable cannot explain why we trust well-tested science over guesswork. Then either defend Popper’s reply (the asymmetry of refutation does the work justification was meant to do) or argue that the reply does not fully meet the objection, and say precisely where it falls short.

  3. The missing test. Popper’s picture was built for science, where a theory can meet an experiment that might break it. Argue either that morality has something that plays the role the experiment plays, or that it has nothing of the kind and the analogy therefore fails. State what would count as moral criticism, or why nothing could.

  4. Fallibilism versus “anything goes.” A common slide runs: “all our knowledge could be wrong, therefore no view is any better than any other.” Explain why fallibilism does not license that conclusion, using the difference between a claim that has survived criticism and one that has not.

What a strong response does
  • Uses the module’s terms precisely: a conjecture is a guess offered to solve a problem; criticism is the attempt to refute it; fallibilism is the view that all knowledge is conjectural and corrigible, not the view that nothing is known; anti-justificationism rejects the demand for a securing foundation.
  • Keeps the asymmetry straight: confirmations cannot prove a universal claim, but a single refutation can decide against it, and rational preference rests on surviving criticism, not on proof.
  • States any opposing view in its strongest form before answering it, and can tell steelmanning a position from knocking down a weak version.
  • Attributes correctly: fallibilism, anti-justificationism, and the dissolution of induction are Popper’s, and the response does not smuggle in claims later modules have not yet built, in particular any verdict about whether morality has its own form of test.
  • Does not confuse “no foundation” with “no objectivity,” and can say why the lack of a foundation is universal rather than special to any one subject.

The story

In the spring of 1847, the First Obstetrical Clinic of the Vienna General Hospital was a place where women went to give birth and a fearful number of them died. The killer was childbed fever, puerperal fever, and it fell on the clinic unevenly in a way that tormented the young Hungarian physician on its staff, Ignaz Semmelweis. The hospital had two maternity clinics. The First, where the doctors and the medical students worked, buried mothers at a dreadful rate. The Second, staffed by midwives, lost far fewer. The mothers knew the difference and begged to be admitted to the Second; some gave birth in the street rather than enter the First, and survived the better for it. Whatever was killing them was concentrated where the doctors were.

Semmelweis chased the difference like a man hunting a refutation. He ruled out one explanation after another by finding the case that broke it. Overcrowding could not be it, for the dreaded First Clinic was the less crowded. Climate could not be it, for both clinics breathed the same Viennese air. The cause had to be something the doctors did and the midwives did not, and in 1847 a colleague’s death handed him the conjecture. A professor, cut by a student’s scalpel during an autopsy, sickened and died of something that looked, at the post-mortem, exactly like the disease killing the mothers. The doctors and students of the First Clinic spent their mornings dissecting the dead and then walked, with unwashed hands, to deliver the living. The midwives never went near the dissection room. Semmelweis’s guess was that the doctors were carrying some deadly matter from the corpses on their hands, into the bodies of the women they were trying to help.

It was a conjecture, and he put it to a test that could have killed it. In May 1847 he ordered the staff of the First Clinic to scrub their hands in a solution of chlorinated lime before examining the mothers. If the guess was wrong, the scrubbing would change nothing. The mortality on the ward fell hard and fast, from a rate that had run to roughly one in six or more down toward the low figures of the midwives’ clinic. The conjecture had survived the test that could have broken it. This was decades before the germ theory of disease would explain why the chlorinated lime worked, so Semmelweis could not say what the deadly matter was. He had no foundation, no proof, no underlying theory. He had a bold guess and a result that did not refute it, which on Popper’s account is exactly what knowledge looks like before the explanation arrives.

What happened next is the part that earns the story its place here. His colleagues did not embrace the result. The conjecture carried an unbearable implication: that the doctors themselves, the educated men of a great hospital, had been carrying death on their hands into the bodies of the women in their care. Offended, the profession largely turned away. Some doubted the figures, some resented the affront, and the institution did not change. Semmelweis grew strident as the years passed and the rejection hardened, publishing his full account late and angrily, attacking his opponents as murderers. He was eventually committed to an asylum, where he died in 1865, at forty-seven, his finding still unaccepted by the men it could have taught.

The tragedy is precise, and it is the failure the conjecture-and-criticism picture is built to diagnose. Semmelweis was right. His guess had survived the one test that mattered, and the mothers on the scrubbed ward stopped dying at the old rate. What defeated him was not a refutation, because there was none; it was a community that would not subject itself to criticism, that preferred the comfort of an unexamined practice to the discomfort of admitting an error. The error was correctable. The correction was available, cheap, and already demonstrated. And it was refused, for years, by exactly the refusal of error-correction that this whole way of thinking names as the deep failure. Knowledge grows by conjecture and criticism, but only where a community will let its conjectures be criticized and its errors be found. Vienna would not, and the cost was counted in graves.
