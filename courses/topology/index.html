Topology, From the Coffee Mug

An intuition-first tour of topology, built from high-school math with no calculus: open sets, continuity, and the invariants that prove why a mug equals a doughnut but not a sphere.

Mathematics Foundational 11 modules ~3h Path · Pure Mathematics, From the Ground Up
Before you start
  • Comfortable with high-school algebra and functions
  • Familiar with coordinates and basic set notation
  • No calculus or proof experience needed

A topologist, the joke goes, can’t tell a coffee mug from a doughnut. Each is a single lump of stuff with exactly one hole — the mug’s hides in its handle — and you can squash one into the other without ever tearing or gluing. That joke is the whole subject in one sentence: topology studies what survives when you allow any amount of stretching and bending but forbid cutting and pasting. Size, angle, and straightness all dissolve; what remains are the stubborn features of a shape, like how many pieces it has and how many holes run through it.

This is a first course in topology that assumes only high-school math — algebra, functions, coordinates, and a little set notation. No calculus, and no prior experience with proofs. We start from the rubber-sheet intuition, then build the precise language that backs it up: open sets, what makes a collection of them a topology, and a definition of continuity that needs no limits. From there we forge the tools that let you prove two shapes are genuinely different — connectedness, the cut-and-count argument, counting holes, the one-sided Möbius band, and the Euler characteristic, the little number you get by counting corners, edges, and faces that is 2 for any sphere and 0 for any doughnut.

By the end you will be able to say exactly what it means for two shapes to be “the same,” compute an invariant with your own hands, and use it to settle the question we opened with — why the mug really does equal the doughnut, and why neither will ever equal a sphere.

Modules

  1. 01 Rubber-Sheet Geometry Why a topologist sees a coffee mug and a doughnut as the same shape — the idea of continuous deformation. 14 min
  2. 02 Room Around Every Point The single most important idea in topology: an open set is one where every point has a little room around it that stays inside. 18 min
  3. 03 The Rules of Openness A topology is a chosen collection of open sets obeying three rules — and those rules are what make all of topology work. 16 min
  4. 04 Continuity Without Limits Continuity rebuilt without calculus: a map is continuous when the preimage of every open set is open. 18 min
  5. 05 Same Shape, Made Precise The precise version of "the same shape": a continuous bijection whose inverse is also continuous. 18 min
  6. 06 What a Deformation Can't Change A topological invariant is a property deformation can't change — so a difference in one proves two shapes are genuinely different; connectedness is the first. 18 min
  7. 07 Cut It and Count A reusable proof move: remove a point and count the pieces — enough to prove a line is not a circle. 16 min
  8. 08 Counting the Holes Surfaces and their holes: the sphere has none, the doughnut has one, and that count is what tells them apart. 16 min
  9. 09 The One-Sided Strip Build a Möbius band and meet orientability — a surface that genuinely has only one side and one edge. 16 min
  10. 10 Euler's Number for Shapes Count vertices minus edges plus faces and you get a number — 2 for anything sphere-like, 0 for the torus — that no deformation can change. 20 min
  11. 11 Telling Shapes Apart Bringing the invariants together to settle the opening question — and an honest look at what invariants can and can't prove. 18 min
