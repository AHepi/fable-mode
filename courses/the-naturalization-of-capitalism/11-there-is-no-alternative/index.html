Module 11 of 11

There Is No Alternative: and How to Doubt It

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain neoliberalism as a constructed, organized project rather than a spontaneous return to natural markets.
  • Explain capitalist realism, and why "there is no alternative" is an achievement of naturalization rather than evidence of necessity.
  • Apply the denaturalizing toolkit (historicize, compare, make-strange, ask who benefits) to a "natural" claim of your own choosing.

The hook

A society can be governed from inside a room so well built that the people in it never notice it has walls. The light is even, the temperature comfortable, the floor steady. Nothing presses on you. And because nothing presses, it does not occur to you that you are anywhere in particular at all. This is a built place, with an architect and a date of construction and a door, rather than the world itself, the only place there is. The most complete form of enclosure is the kind you cannot see, because to see a wall you have to imagine the space on its other side, and that imagining is precisely what the walls are built to prevent.

That room has a name now, and a slogan painted over the invisible door: there is no alternative. It was built recently and deliberately, by people whose names and meeting dates we know, and the walls can be found. The lens was pointing here all along.

Context & background

Back at the start we named a move: naturalization, making a recent, contingent arrangement look like an unchangeable fact of nature (contingent meaning it happened this way but could have gone otherwise, module 01). Every module since has been a worked example: the worker made by enclosure (04); the “free market” built and enforced by the state, not left alone: Polanyi’s “laissez-faire was planned” (06); economics recasting a recent order as natural law (07); the whole thing coming to feel natural as labour vanishes behind a price and the self learns to police itself (10).

This module takes the strongest version of the claim, the one that goes past normal to necessary: not “this is how we live” but “there is no other way to live, and there never could be.” That sense has a name, given by Mark Fisher, a British writer and critic, in a short, sharp book called Capitalist Realism (2009): capitalist realism: the felt sense that no alternative to capitalism is even imaginable. Not that the alternatives are bad. That there are none to picture. Fisher popularized a line that captures it (he credited it to the critics Fredric Jameson and Slavoj Žižek): it has become easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism. Asteroids, plagues, the collapse of the climate: the imagination supplies these freely. A different way of organizing work and ownership it cannot supply at all.

You might take that as the final proof that the thing really is natural: if nobody can even picture an outside, surely there isn’t one. That is the deepest version of the intuition this whole course has been answering. Grant it its full strength, then turn to where the walls came from.

Narrative / exposition

Start with the honest steelman, because a lens that only works on opponents is a toy. The case for capitalist realism is not stupid. Every alternative actually tried at scale in the twentieth century (the centrally planned economies above all) produced shortages, repression, or both, and many produced corpses. The reader who says “I’ve seen the alternatives and they were worse” is not being naive; they are citing real history. Grant it fully. The course does not pretend that record away.

But notice what the steelman establishes and what it does not. It establishes that particular twentieth-century alternatives failed. It does not establish that the present arrangement is the natural condition of the species. And here the history bites: the part of our common sense that feels most like untouched nature (the sheer obviousness of markets, the unthinkability of anything else) turns out to have the clearest construction record. We have the minutes of the meetings.

In April 1947, a group of economists and intellectuals gathered at a hotel on Mont Pèlerin, above Lake Geneva, at the invitation of the economist Friedrich Hayek. They founded a society, the Mont Pèlerin Society, with a long-range purpose: to rebuild the intellectual case for the market at a moment when it looked finished. This was the high tide of the planned economy and the welfare state; the market order they cared about seemed to most observers like yesterday’s idea. They did not regard their view as the natural common sense of mankind. They regarded it, correctly, as a minority view that had lost, one that would have to be rebuilt, through scholarship, funding, think tanks, and decades of patient work, until it became again the water people swam in. This is the project later historians named neoliberalism: the organized effort to remake institutions and common sense around markets.

Sit with the strangeness of that. The conviction that markets are just natural (the conviction that feels like no one taught it to me, I just know it) was, decades ago, a thing a small group set out to manufacture because it did not yet exist. You cannot organize a decades-long campaign to make people believe what they already believe. The campaign is the confession: the “natural” had to be built.

Philip Mirowski and Quinn Slobodian, present-day historians of neoliberalism, both insist the project was not a return to leaving-things-alone, and their framing is worth keeping visibly as framing, a reading rather than a fact. Mirowski documents the organized “thought collective” that moved ideas from seminars into ministries. Slobodian, in Globalists (2018), supplies the precise image: neoliberalism was never about freeing markets from rules but about encasing them, wrapping the market in law and in supranational institutions placed deliberately beyond the reach of democratic majorities, so that whatever a population might vote for, the core of the market order could not be touched. Not the market left alone. The market walled off from the people, by design.

That is the structure. The walls are real (treaty and statute and institution), built so that the space outside them, where a population decides differently, drops out of the picture. And this is the exact same move we met two centuries earlier and a world away. In module 06 the lesson was that the “free market” did not grow wild; it was constructed and enforced by deliberate state action: “laissez-faire was planned.” Neoliberalism is that same sentence spoken again in the twentieth century, with better lawyers. The most “natural” thing was the most carefully made.

Then the slogan, where naturalization arrives at its destination. In Britain in the late 1970s and 80s, Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher (her again, the phrase she’s best remembered for) used a line so often it acquired an acronym: there is no alternative, TINA. It does not argue. It says there is no alternative: that the question of how to live is already closed, that to debate it is to misunderstand that it is not a debate. A claim about how a society ought to be organized arrives dressed, one last time, as a claim about what unalterably is: the costume of naturalization we learned to recognize in module 01, now worn by a head of government.

So see it clearly. “There is no alternative” is not the discovery that no alternative exists. It is the achievement of a state in which no alternative can be thought, and an achievement is, by definition, something accomplished, against resistance, that could have failed. The unthinkability is not the bedrock beneath the walls. It is the walls.

Primary source

The cleanest specimen is the slogan itself, in the form Thatcher made famous, defending her economic programme against the idea that some other course was available:

“There is no alternative.”

— Margaret Thatcher, a phrase used repeatedly through her premiership (UK, 1979–1990), so insistently that it became known by the acronym TINA.

Four words. Read them as our anthropologist would, not as a position to cheer or boo, but as a specimen of a move. The sentence takes the most contestable question a society can ask (how shall we arrange work, ownership, and care for one another?) and answers it not with an argument but with a closure: a genuinely open question presented as a settled fact, the way you might correct someone about the boiling point of water.

And mark the seam between evidence and interpretation. That Thatcher used the phrase, and that it stuck, is evidence: checkable, uncontested. That the phrase naturalizes (converts a political choice into a fact of nature and so forecloses the very debate it pretends to have already won) is interpretation. A strong reading, yours to weigh: someone could hear “there is no alternative” as sober realism rather than ideology. The honest test between the two is the one the last ten modules have laid out: was this order built, recently, by identifiable hands, such that it might have been built differently? If it was (and the record from enclosure to Mont Pèlerin says it was) then “no alternative” is a wall, not a horizon.

Check yourself

Before reading on, in your own words: what is the difference between saying no alternative exists and saying no alternative can be imagined, and which one is “there is no alternative” actually reporting?

Show answer

No alternative exists is a claim about the world: that the present order is the only one that could function. No alternative can be imagined is a claim about us, about the limits of what people can picture. “There is no alternative,” and the wider condition Fisher called capitalist realism, reports the second: a state in which the imagination comes up empty. And an empty imagination is an achievement of naturalization, produced recently and partly on purpose, not proof that the world contains only one possibility. The walls being unseeable is not evidence there’s nothing outside them.

Analysis & interpretation

Here the reader has every right to push back. Fine. You’ve shown it was built. So tell me the alternative, what to put in its place once it comes down.

The honest answer is that this course does not have one, and that this is a position, not an evasion. Denaturalizing is not the same as prescribing. The lens gives you eyes, not a blueprint. Everything in these eleven modules works to recover the sentence it didn’t have to be this way, to show that a thing which feels like the floor under your feet was laid by human hands and could have been laid differently. That is a claim about contingency, not a claim that some other arrangement is ready to install, or would be better. A worked-out replacement economy is a different and enormous project, and you should be suspicious of anyone who hands you one in a closing chapter.

But notice (this is the sharp part) that the demand for a ready-made alternative is itself part of how foreclosure works. “You have nothing to put in its place, so be quiet” is the room defending itself. It sets a test no honest critic of a deep arrangement could pass on the spot: nobody who first doubted the divine right of kings, or the naturalness of a world where women could not own property, had a finished replacement constitution in their pocket. They had a prior, indispensable thing: the recovered ability to see the arrangement as an arrangement, a fit subject for human decision rather than a fact to be endured. Requiring the blueprint before you will permit the doubt is requiring the cure before you will allow the diagnosis: capitalist realism’s deepest trick is to make the absence of a blueprint feel like proof the walls are load-bearing.

And hold the line one last time against the trap this course has refused from the first page: just as capitalism was not fated to arrive, nothing here says it is fated to end. Contingency runs in both directions. That the walls were built does not tell you they will fall, or stand. It tells you only that they are a building, and that buildings are the kind of thing people make decisions about.

Which leaves the one thing the course can hand you, and has been handing you for ten modules without naming it: the method. Gather the moves you have watched work, again and again, and they make a small, portable toolkit: four questions to put to anything that presents itself as obviously, naturally, beyond argument:

  • Historicize. When did this begin, and what came before it? Almost nothing that claims to be eternal survives the question honestly asked. The factory clock had a first day (module 08); “the economy” as a single measurable thing is barely a century old (module 07); the unthinkability of alternatives was being manufactured in a Swiss hotel in 1947.
  • Compare. Do all human societies do this, or only ours? Find one that arranged things otherwise (exchange that built bonds rather than clearing a price (module 02), debt that long preceded coined money (module 03)) and what looked like human nature is revealed as one local form of it.
  • Make-strange. What would this look like to someone who had never seen it? The anthropologist’s move from module 01: watch a job interview, a mortgage, a price tag as an alien would, and the habit that sanded off the strangeness lifts.
  • Ask who benefits. The habit we started in module 01 and can now name: cui bono, “to whose good?” Not an accusation; a probe. An arrangement that could have taken several shapes usually took the one that suited someone. Ask, against any “natural” order, who profits from its seeming inevitable, and the answer often shows you where the walls are.

None of these is new. That is the point. The method is just the lens of module 01, wielded, and you have been wielding it the whole way down.

Check yourself

Apply the toolkit before reading on. Take a claim you’ve heard treated as plain common sense about how the economy must work, “wages just reflect what you’re worth,” say, or “you have to compete or you’ll be left behind.” Run two of the four questions on it. What does each reveal that the claim’s air of obviousness was hiding?

Show answer

There is no single right answer; a strong one runs the questions on the specific claim rather than restating the toolkit. For “wages reflect what you’re worth,” historicize asks when pay came to be seen as a measure of personal worth at all (rather than of custom, status, or what one could be compelled to accept, module 08), and finds the link recent and arranged. Who benefits asks whom it serves to have low pay read as a verdict on the low-paid rather than on the arrangement that sets it, and the answer points away from the worker. Compare asks whether every society has tied sustenance to the sale of one’s hours (module 02 says no); make-strange describes the claim as an alien would (a person’s value to others is announced by a number set by the person buying their time) and the obviousness curdles into something contestable. A strong answer shows the claim’s air of obviousness thinning once the questions are actually asked.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Run the full method (the course in one exercise). Pick one “natural” claim about the economy that you have believed, or still believe. Run all four tools on it: historicize, compare, make-strange, ask who benefits. Then write the honest verdict: did denaturalizing it show the claim to be contingent, or did it survive the test? (A claim that survives is a fair result; the method is not rigged to debunk.)
  2. Steelman capitalist realism, then locate the leap. State the strongest honest version of “there is no alternative: we’ve tried the others and they were worse.” Then identify the exact point where it slides from these particular alternatives failed to the present order is the natural and only possible one, and say what evidence would defend that second step.
  3. The blueprint demand. This module claims that demanding a ready-made replacement before permitting any doubt is itself part of how foreclosure works. Argue for or against. Is there a version of “what’s your alternative?” that is a fair challenge rather than a wall, and how would you tell the two apart?
  4. Argue against your own tool. Where might the denaturalizing method be overused? Where might “but it was constructed!” become a lazy way to dismiss an arrangement that was built, yes, and is also well-founded and worth keeping? Make the case that showing a thing is contingent is the beginning of judging it, not the end.
What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of this module, and not a flat verdict that capitalism is good or bad.
  • Works from a specific claim or example, reading its actual details rather than gesturing at “the system.”
  • Keeps evidence separate from interpretation: what demonstrably happened (the order was built, recently, by these hands) versus the reading placed on it (that its necessity is therefore manufactured).
  • Steelmans the other side honestly before answering it, and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.
  • Resists the twin traps: treating “constructed” as if it meant “false” or “worthless,” and predicting capitalism’s collapse (or its permanence): contingency is a claim about the past’s openness, not a forecast.

Recap

We have reached the place the lens was always pointing. The most “natural”-seeming common sense of all (that no alternative is even thinkable, the condition Mark Fisher named capitalist realism and Margaret Thatcher compressed into there is no alternative) turns out to have the clearest construction record of all: built in living memory by an organized project the historians call neoliberalism, whose architects (Mont Pèlerin, 1947) set out to manufacture the obviousness that now feels like untouched nature. The same move Polanyi caught two centuries earlier (“laissez-faire was planned”) spoken again with treaties for bricks: the market encased from democracy on purpose (Slobodian), an enclosure built so its walls can’t be seen. So the unthinkability of alternatives is an achievement, not a fact of nature, and the demand for a finished replacement before one may even doubt is the walls defending themselves, not a fair test. This course has handed you no blueprint; it has handed you eyes. The method is the lens of module 01, now wielded: historicize, compare, make-strange, ask who benefits. Turn it on whatever next tells you, in the voice of the weather, that it is just how things are. Whether the room stands or falls is not for any course to predict. That you can now see it is a room: that was the whole journey.
