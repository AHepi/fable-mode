Module 8 of 11

Clocks, Bells, and the Made Self

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain how clock-time and work-discipline replaced task-orientation, and how punctuality and idleness became moral categories.
  • Explain "possessive individualism": the picture of a person as essentially an owner, including a self-owner.
  • Evaluate the "it's just human nature" / acquisitiveness claim, steelmanning it before showing that the form it takes is historically produced.

The hook

A bell rings at five in the morning, and a town gets out of bed because a factory told it to.

That sentence would have made no sense to most people who ever lived. For the overwhelming majority of human history, work answered to the task and the season, not the clock. You milked the cow when the cow needed milking, fished when the tide was right, hauled in the harvest while the weather held and rested when it broke. The day had a shape, but the shape came from the work and the light, not from a number on a face. A weaver might labour furiously for two days and idle on the third; the rhythm was uneven because life is uneven. To call someone “late” for the morning would have been almost a category error. Late for what?

Then, over a couple of centuries, a strange new thing took hold: time you could be late for. Time you could waste, save, spend, lose: time owned by someone, and owed to them. The factory bell that woke the town was the audible edge of one of the deepest remakings in this whole course. Earlier modules showed capitalism remaking the world outside the worker: the fenced field, the dispossession of module 04, where the “free” worker was made by stripping away every other way to live. The remaking now moves inside: how the person came to experience time as a possession, the self as a thing it owns, and acquisitiveness as simply human.

Context & background

Two histories run together here, and they meet in a single sensation: the feeling that of course you are this kind of person, living this kind of day.

The first is the history of time. The clock is old; the tyranny of the clock is not. What was new in the industrial period was time as the measure of labour itself: the moment when an employer was no longer buying a job done but buying hours. The course term for this is work-discipline: training people to work by the clock, making time something you spend and owe. The classic account is by E. P. Thompson, an English social historian whose 1967 essay on time and work-discipline maps the shift.

The second is the history of the self: the picture of a person that arrived alongside the timed workday. Call it possessive individualism: the picture of a person as essentially an owner, of things and of themselves. The phrase is the Canadian political theorist C. B. Macpherson’s, from a 1962 study. We will unpack it, and flag what is disputed about it, below.

These two threads braid into the module’s hardest knot: the conviction that the acquisitive, time-conscious, self-maximizing individual is not a product of any of this but what humans are. That is the deepest naturalizing claim in the course, and it gets the longest hearing here.

Narrative / exposition

Start with the bell, because it is concrete and Thompson made it so.

Picture the older world it replaced, the world of task-orientation: work organized by the job to be done rather than the time it takes. Its hallmark is that work and life are not yet split. The shepherd watching through a cold night, the housewife (the labour was overwhelmingly gendered, a point module 09 takes up) moving between cooking and childcare and the field. These people are not “at work” from nine to five and “off” thereafter. The day is stitched together by needs, not by a schedule. To an outsider, Thompson notes, such labour looks wasteful and undisciplined, all bursts and lulls. But the unevenness is not a failure of discipline. It is what working to the task looks like.

Now drop a clock into the middle of it, and attach money to the clock’s reading. If you are paying by the hour, the worker’s idleness is now your loss, measured and visible; the lull that was once just the shape of the work becomes “time wasted.” So you install the bell, the gate, the timekeeper. You fine the latecomer. You forbid the song, the drink, the wandering off. And (this is the part that does the naturalizing) you teach people to do this to themselves. The early factory had to coerce time-discipline with bells and fines and sermons; within a few generations, the worker carried a watch and felt the guilt of “wasting” a Sunday afternoon with no overseer in sight. The discipline had moved inside. “Time is money” stopped being a slogan and became a reflex: a thing you feel, the way you feel hunger.

Notice what naturalization has done. A particular arrangement (selling your hours to an employer who has every interest in filling them) has become a property of you: your conscientiousness, your work ethic, your bad conscience about a lazy morning. The arrangement has dressed itself up as character.

Check yourself

Before reading on, in your own words: what is the difference between task-orientation and work-discipline (clock-time), and what new moral categories appear when the second replaces the first?

Show answer

Task-orientation organizes work by the job to be done: you work until the task is finished, in the rhythm the task and the season demand, with no clean line between “work” and “life.” Work-discipline organizes work by the clock: an employer buys hours, so time becomes measured, regular, and accountable, with a fixed shift you can be early or late for. When clock-time takes over, punctuality becomes a virtue and idleness a sin: time you could have spent and didn’t. A neutral fact about how labour is organized turns into a moral judgment about the kind of person you are.

Then the second thread, the self that owns itself, and the question of where the idea of the person-as-owner came from.

Your abilities are yours the way your boots are yours. You do not merely have capacities, you own them, and you are free precisely insofar as you can sell or contract them out. That is possessive individualism, and Macpherson’s claim is that this model got built into early modern political thought (roughly, in the seventeenth-century English world of Hobbes and Locke) and then hardened into “common sense.” On this model, society is at bottom a marketplace of such proprietors making deals. Macpherson’s sharp move was to point out how much the picture assumes. It does not describe humanity as such. It describes a human being already shaped like a market participant, and then presents that shape as nature.

Here a discipline this course insists on. Macpherson’s reading is influential and contestable, and you should hold the two parts apart. That a powerful picture of the self-as-owner exists, and has done real work in how moderns understand freedom, is hard to deny; you can hear it in any sentence that treats “I own my own body” or “my time is mine to sell” as a flat fact rather than a particular idea. But Macpherson’s specific claim that he found this doctrine in Locke’s text (that Locke was covertly theorizing capitalist appropriation) has been disputed by intellectual historians ever since, who read Locke’s own words in his own context differently. So take the concept as a genuine description of a modern self-image; treat the textual case against Locke as one contested reading among others. This is exactly the kind of seam (strong concept, disputed evidence) the lens taught you to keep visible.

Steelmanning the deepest claim

Time-discipline and possessive individualism both feed one master intuition, the one the reader is most likely to hold and least likely to have examined: it’s just human nature. People are acquisitive, competitive, out for the best deal; an economy built on those drives is the only honest one, because it is the only one that matches what we actually are. Module 01 raised this objection and left it standing for later. Later is now, and it earns the steelman it deserves, because a lens that only cuts weak arguments is a toy.

So state it at full strength. Humans really do want, trade, compete, and accumulate. Self-interest is not a Western invention or a recent corruption; you can find it in every society anthropologists have ever described, and any economic arrangement that pretended people were pure altruists would shatter on the first selfish person it met. Children hoard. Strangers haggle. The drive to get and to get ahead is about as close to a human universal as we have. This is true. The course does not flinch from it and does not pretend it away.

The classic statement of the claim is worth meeting in its own words, which is why it sits in the Primary source below: Smith, more careful than his caricature, grounding the whole edifice of commerce in “a certain propensity in human nature… to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another.” Read that as the strongest version of the intuition: trading is not something a particular society teaches; it is a propensity in human nature, as native as language.

Grant all of it. And then watch for the quiet leap, the same one the lens caught in module 01. There is a difference between two claims that the sentence runs together:

  1. Humans have acquisitive, self-interested, exchanging drives. (True.)
  2. This particular way of organizing those drives (impersonal, maximizing, market-mediated, with self-interest as the master motive) is the natural one. (Does not follow.)

The second does not come free with the first. The drives are general; the form is specific and historically produced. You have already seen the proof, which is why this counter is a callback, not a fresh argument. Recall module 02’s embedded economies, where exchange built bonds rather than clearing a price: the gift, in which giving creates obligation, repayment sustains relationship, and a transaction is a thread in the social weave rather than a deal closed and forgotten. Those societies were not populated by selfless saints. The same human drives (to give, to get, to compete for standing, to come out ahead) ran straight through them. They were channeled elsewhere: through honour, kinship, reciprocity, status, obligation. The acquisitive market-individual, maximizing impersonally against strangers, is one local channel for universal drives, and a recent one. To read that single channel back onto all of humanity is to do precisely what Smith’s sentence does: take a true thing about human beings and use it to smuggle in a contingent thing about one human society.

One caution, because the lens cuts both ways and this course does not romanticize. Saying the embedded past channeled the drives otherwise is not saying it channeled them more kindly. Gift economies had their own hierarchies, their own cruelties, their own ways of trapping people in debt and deference. “It could have been arranged otherwise” is a claim about contingency, full stop. It is not a claim that otherwise was better. And one more, against anachronism: the point is not that medieval peasants were secret maximizers held back by custom. That would just be the market-self read backwards into people who did not have it. The honest claim is narrower and stronger: the maximizing, time-owning, self-possessing individual is not the timeless floor of human nature but a historical achievement, built by bells and fines and a particular picture of the self, and then experienced as nature.

Primary source

The cleanest specimen of the claim under examination comes from the book that did more than any other to make political economy a science (module 07): Adam Smith’s The Wealth of Nations. In its second chapter, Smith traces the entire division of labour back to a single trait:

“This division of labour, from which so many advantages are derived, is not originally the effect of any human wisdom… It is the necessary, though very slow and gradual, consequence of a certain propensity in human nature… the propensity to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another.”

— Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations (1776), Book I, Chapter 2

Read it as the anthropologist of module 01 would: as a specimen, not a verdict. Notice the work the phrase “in human nature” is doing. Smith is not saying people in commercial Britain like to trade; he is saying the propensity is native to the species, the engine under everything, present before any society shaped it. That is a strong, falsifiable claim about all humans. It is exactly the move the lens watches for: a feature of one historical arrangement relocated into “nature,” where it can no longer be questioned, only obeyed.

Be fair to Smith, though, which the caricature is not. He was a moral philosopher before he was an economist, and his Theory of Moral Sentiments roots human conduct in sympathy and fellow-feeling, not in greed alone. The flattened “selfish Smith” of later economics is a distortion. The sentence above is still doing naturalizing work, but Smith himself was a subtler thinker than the doctrine that wears his name.

Check yourself

Smith calls trading a “propensity in human nature.” Using module 02, what is the single distinction that lets you accept the drives are real while denying his conclusion?

Show answer

The distinction between the drives and their form. It is true that humans everywhere want, trade, compete, and accumulate (the drives are real and probably universal). But the form Smith treats as natural (impersonal, maximizing exchange against strangers, with self-interest as the master motive) is only one way those drives can be channeled. Module 02’s embedded economies ran the very same drives through the gift, reciprocity, status, and obligation, where exchange built and repaid social bonds instead of clearing a price. So “people are acquisitive” does not get you to “this market arrangement is the natural one.” That second step is the naturalizing leap, and the embedded past is the evidence against it.

Analysis & interpretation

Keep the seam between evidence and interpretation visible, because this module mixes the two more than most.

The evidence is robust and largely uncontested. Task-oriented work, organized by the job and the season rather than the clock, is well documented across pre-industrial societies. Factory bells, clocking-in, fines for lateness, and a flood of moralizing tracts against “idleness” are matters of record. Smith wrote the sentence quoted above in 1776. These are checkable facts.

The interpretation is where the argument lives, and it is an argument, not a decree. That work-discipline remade the self (not merely regulating behaviour but reshaping what people feel to be their own character) is a reading, Thompson’s and this course’s, and a strong one, but a reading. So is the claim that possessive individualism naturalizes a market self rather than describing a real one. A careful skeptic could grant every fact and still hold that the timed, acquisitive individual was always latent in us, and that industry only released what custom had suppressed. That is the steelman in its most stubborn form, and the honest answer is not a sneer but the comparative evidence: module 02’s embedded economies channeled the same drives so differently that “released the latent human” looks less like an explanation than like the naturalizing move itself, asserting as timeless exactly the thing in question.

Now module 01’s probe, turned on this material: whom does the acquisitive self, seeming natural, serve? Not as an accusation, as a probe. An arrangement that needs workers to internalize the clock, and citizens to experience themselves as self-owning contractors, is well served by the belief that people just are that way, because a built thing can be questioned and a natural fact cannot. That the belief serves the arrangement does not make it false. It does make it worth examining rather than swallowing.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. Write for thinking; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

  1. Make your own time strange. Describe one ordinary way you experience time as a possession: “wasting” an afternoon, feeling you “owe” someone hours, guilt over an unproductive day. Apply the anthropologist’s eye from module 01: what would this feeling look like to someone from a task-oriented world, and what does the estrangement reveal that habit had hidden?
  2. Steelman, then locate the leap. State the strongest honest version of “the acquisitive, competitive individual is just human nature.” Then identify the exact sentence where it slides from a claim about human drives to a claim about one particular arrangement of them, and say what the module 02 evidence does to that second step.
  3. Test the self-owner. “I own my own body; my time is mine to sell.” Is that a plain fact, a useful legal fiction, or a contestable picture of the person (possessive individualism)? Argue a position, and say what would count as evidence for or against treating it as natural.
  4. Argue against the tool. Where might “work-discipline made the self” be overstated? Could some attention to time be a genuine human good rather than an imposed character, and how would you tell the difference between a discipline that was made and one worth keeping?
What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of the module, and not a verdict that capitalism is good or bad.
  • Works from a specific scene, feeling, or sentence and reads its actual details, rather than gesturing at “society.”
  • Keeps evidence separate from interpretation: distinguishes the documented record (task-orientation, factory bells, Smith’s 1776 sentence) from the reading placed on it.
  • Steelmans the “human nature” claim honestly before answering it, and cashes the counter through module 02’s gift/reciprocity rather than asserting it.
  • Avoids the twin traps: romanticizing the embedded past as kinder, and anachronism (reading the maximizing self back into people who did not have it). Uses task-orientation, work-discipline, and possessive individualism precisely.

Recap

Two remakings met here, and both ended up passing for nature. Work-discipline turned time from the shape of a task into a possession you spend and owe, until the bell that once had to be rung from outside was rung inside, and “idleness” became a private sin. Possessive individualism offered a picture of the person as an owner (of things, of labour, of the self) and presented that picture as a fact about humanity rather than one image of it (a picture whose strongest source-claim, Macpherson on Locke, stays contested). Both feed the course’s deepest naturalizing intuition, it’s just human nature, which we granted at full strength, with Smith’s “propensity to truck, barter, and exchange,” and then answered not by denying the drives but by recalling module 02: the same drives, run otherwise, through the gift. The acquisitive, clock-bound, self-owning individual is real. What this module unsettles is the word that does all the quiet work in front of it: natural. Next we follow the made worker home, to the unpaid labour that kept the whole arrangement standing and was coded, like so much else here, as nothing but the way of nature.
