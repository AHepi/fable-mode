Module 7 of 11

The Science That Made It Law

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain how political economy recast historically specific arrangements as natural laws, using Smith's "invisible hand" and Ricardo's "iron law of wages."
  • Explain how the marginalist turn shifted economics toward timeless individual preference rather than class and history, and mark that reading as interpretation.
  • Trace how "the economy" became a single measurable national object only in the twentieth century.

The hook

Open a newspaper to the business page and you will find the word doing the strangest work in modern English. “The economy slowed last quarter.” “The economy needs confidence.” “What’s good for the economy.” There it sits, a single thing with a temperature and a mood, something experts measure and politicians tend like a sick relative. Of course there is the economy. What else would you call it?

Hand that sentence to a literate person from 1700, though, and they would not know what you meant. They knew the word economy; they used it daily. It meant running a household well: managing your estate and stores without waste. What they could not have had was the economy: a single national object, separate from politics and family and morality, that a country can be said to have. That thing is not much older than the photograph. And once you notice that the most ordinary word on the business page is a recent invention, a question opens under the whole page: what else that now reads as a permanent fact was, not long ago, something somebody had to build?

Context & background

Here naturalization puts on a lab coat. We have watched the order get built: the commons fenced, the worker made, land and labour and money turned into things-for-sale; laissez-faire was planned, the market was made and maintained, not left alone (module 06). Now we watch a young discipline take that recent, made order and re-describe it as a system of natural laws, the same naturalization the course has tracked from the start (module 01), but performed in the most respectable register there is: the language of science.

Political economy is the older name for economics, when it still openly studied power and class. From the late eighteenth century into the nineteenth, the people studying markets, wages, and rent called their field political economy. The word was honest. It announced that they studied wealth and power together: how a society’s product gets divided among landlords, workers, and owners; which class gained from a tariff. It studied a society of conflicting groups, out loud.

Keep one line from module 02 in your back pocket: markets are ancient; a market society is not. What was new, and what the new science took as its eternal subject, was a whole society organized around the market, where most people must sell their labour to live.

Narrative / exposition

Start with Smith, the qualified moral philosopher rather than his caricature, because the gap between the two is the lesson.

The cartoon Smith is the prophet of selfishness: leave everyone to chase profit, an invisible hand turns private greed into public good, so government should get out of the way. That picture is carved over the door of a certain kind of economics. It is also a thin reading. Smith wrote an entire earlier book, The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759), about sympathy as the ground of moral life; he worried that merchants conspired against the public; he thought the division of labour could stupefy the worker stuck at one tiny task; he defended public education. The phrase he is hanged with, the invisible hand, appears exactly once in The Wealth of Nations, inside a limited argument, not as a cosmic law. To make him the patron saint of “greed is good,” you must crop the man down to a slogan. It is the same crop module 05 caught: keep the flattering fragment, lose the rest.

And the cropping is the naturalizing act. Strip a moral philosopher’s qualified argument of its qualifications and its particular commercial society, and what remains is a clean general law: markets, left alone, produce good outcomes by nature. That work was not done by Smith. It was done to him, by readers who needed a founder and a law.

Think of a map. A good map is a marvel. It lets you see a city you could never see all at once, find a route, plan. But a map is always a choice: it leaves things out, flattens hills, picks what counts as a road. The danger comes when a map is so good people forget it is a map and treat it as the territory, when they stop saying “the map shows no path here” and start saying “there is no path.” A theory of the economy is a map. The naturalizing move, every time, is the map that forgets it is a map: the model mistaken for nature, the drawing passed off as the land.

A second figure shows it sharper. David Ricardo, an early-nineteenth-century stockbroker turned political economist (Principles of Political Economy and Taxation, 1817), built tighter, more abstract models than Smith, and from them came what later got the chilling name the iron law of wages, the idea that wages are pushed down toward bare subsistence, just enough to keep workers alive and replaced, no more. Now notice the word iron, and notice law. A pattern that held under particular nineteenth-century conditions, a growing propertyless population, no organized labour, no welfare floor, is restated as fixed as the freezing point of water. The political effect is exact: if low wages are a law of nature, raising them is like legislating against gravity. The science removes a human choice from the table by reclassifying it as weather. That is the first way the discipline naturalized the order, by promoting patterns specific to a built arrangement into natural laws.

There is a second way, later and subtler, and here the course owes you a marker.

Around the 1870s, three thinkers working separately, William Stanley Jevons in England, Carl Menger in Austria, Léon Walras in Switzerland, remade the field’s foundations. This is the marginalist turn: the late-1800s shift that made economics about individual preference and equilibrium. The older political economy had asked where value comes from: from labour, from land, divided among classes. The marginalists reframed value as something arising in the mind of an individual chooser at the margin: how much is one more cup of coffee worth to you, given what you already have? Value became subjective preference, the economy a system of such choosers settling into balance, an equilibrium. They reached, on purpose, for the prestige and mathematics of physics.

Here the course owes a marker. There is a widely held critical reading, the one this course works within, that the marginalist turn depoliticized economics, that by making the field about timeless individual preference it dissolved the older vocabulary of class, power, and history, so capitalism’s specific institutions vanished into “the economy as such,” a neutral machine of rational agents studied like a natural system. That reading is powerful. But mark it as interpretation, not settled fact. The historical event is solid. The turn happened, roughly when and how described. The claim about its meaning, that it naturalized and depoliticized, is contested by economists who see ordinary scientific progress toward better models. As module 01 insisted: the turn happened is evidence; the turn naturalized the order is a reading you may weigh.

Either way, the direction of travel is not in dispute: a field that began as the open study of wealth and power together became, in name and self-image, just economics, with the political shaved off the front of the word. The map grew more elegant and forgot harder that it was a map.

The word we started with is the most teachable proof of all. Before roughly 1800, economy meant household or estate management, the Greek oikonomia, the running of the oikos, the house, or, by extension, thrift. There was no the economy. That object had to be assembled, and the assembly has a rough date. It was consolidated in the twentieth century, especially the 1930s and 1940s, when economists and governments built the national accounts, the statistical machinery that produces a single number for a nation’s output, the ancestor of GDP. (The historian Timothy Mitchell, in Rule of Experts, 2002, tells this story: “the economy,” as a thing experts measure and manage, is a twentieth-century arrival.) Only once you can measure the economy as one object can you speak of it the way the business page does, as a single entity with a health.

So the most ordinary noun in public life, the thing whose “confidence” must be kept up, is younger than the telephone, and did not exist, as an object of thought, for most of the people whose descendants now treat it as the permanent backdrop of life. That is naturalization caught in a single word: a recently built, deliberately measured thing, experienced as a fact of nature you never chose. Not because anyone lied. Because the map got so good we forgot we had drawn it.

Primary source

Here is the most famous fragment in the founding text of the tradition, the one usually quoted to prove markets work by nature. Read it as module 01 taught: as a specimen, not a slogan.

“It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker, that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest.”

— Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations (1776)

This wording is genuine and very well known; I am confident of it. What I have not done is pin it to a page number, because I will not invent one. You should distrust anyone who supplies a precise citation they cannot check.

Notice first what is true and shrewd: Smith observes that strangers who feel no special love for each other can still, through exchange, feed one another. That is a real insight about commercial life, and no fool’s. Now notice what happens when the sentence is lifted out and made to stand as a law of human nature, when the butcher and baker become Economic Man, the eternal self-interested chooser. That is the leap module 01 warned about and module 08 will take apart: from “people in this arrangement coordinate through self-interested exchange” to “self-interest is what humans economically are.” The first is an observation about a shop in 1776. The second is the map forgetting it is a map. The sentence does not commit that error; the use it is put to does.

Check yourself

Answer from memory before reading on. The word economy once meant something quite different from “the economy” on today’s business page. What did it mean before about 1800, and roughly when did “the economy,” a single measurable national object, come into being?

Show answer

Before about 1800, economy meant household or estate management, running a house or estate without waste (from the Greek oikonomia, the running of the oikos, the house), or, by extension, thrift. There was no “the economy” as a single national thing. That object, one measurable entity a country has, which rises and falls, was consolidated only in the twentieth century, especially the 1930s–40s, with the building of national accounts (the ancestor of GDP). Timothy Mitchell, Rule of Experts (2002), tells this story.

Analysis & interpretation

Hold the seam between evidence and interpretation up to the light. This is a delicate case: a true science doing naturalizing work, with no liar in the room.

The evidence is firm and checkable: the dates, the texts, the shift of the field’s name from “political economy” to “economics,” and the twentieth-century consolidation of “the economy” as a single measured object. None of that is in serious dispute.

The interpretation is the reading this course argues: that across this history a recent, contingent order was progressively re-described as a system of natural laws, the vocabulary of law, nature, equilibrium, and a measurable “the economy” performing naturalization in the prestigious language of theory. That reading is strong, and the word-”economy” evidence supports it about as cleanly as history ever supports a reading. But it is a reading, and honesty means naming its rival: a defender of mainstream economics would say this was a science maturing, finding more general models, cleaner abstractions, as physics did, with nothing sinister in it.

What would help you weigh the two? Largely the history this course keeps putting on the table: whether the order the science described was in fact built, recently, and could have been built differently (modules 04 through 06), or whether it is a natural state a science was right to take as given. Smith was a better thinker than his cartoon; the marginalists did real work; the discipline is no conspiracy. And a recent, made order still got dressed, in the most respectable clothes available, as a law of nature. Keeping both true, refusing both the sneer and the worship, is the work.

Check yourself

From memory: the claim that the marginalist turn “depoliticized” economics, that it dissolved class and power into the study of timeless individual preference, is one of the readings above. Is it presented as established historical fact, or as something else? And which part is treated as solid fact?

Show answer

It is presented as interpretation, not settled fact, a powerful critical reading (the one this course works within), but contested by economists who see the marginalist turn as ordinary scientific progress. What is treated as solid, checkable fact is the historical event: that the turn happened around the 1870s (Jevons, Menger, Walras), recentering economics on individual preference and equilibrium, and that the field’s name and self-image shifted from “political economy” to “economics.” The seam: the turn happened = evidence; the turn naturalized the order = a reading you may weigh.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below describes what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

  1. Read the specimen. Take Smith’s butcher-and-baker line and separate the genuine observation about commercial life it contains from the law of human nature it gets enlisted to prove. Where exactly is the seam, and what work does crossing it do?
  2. Hunt a forgotten map. Find one sentence from today’s news or politics that treats the economy as a single natural thing with needs or moods (“the economy can’t afford it,” “what the markets want,” “you can’t buck the trend”). Make it strange, the way module 01’s anthropologist would, and say what the phrasing hides about the choices and people underneath the word.
  3. Steelman the science. State the strongest honest version of the rival reading: that the abstraction of modern economics is just a science maturing, like physics, and carries no special naturalizing freight. Then say what historical evidence would have to be true for your eventual judgment, for or against, to hold.
  4. Don’t strawman the founder. Knowing that Smith also wrote a whole book on sympathy and worried about merchants and dull labour, argue whether it is fair to call him the father of “the market is natural.” Who did the naturalizing, the man or his readers, and how would you tell?
What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of this module, and not a verdict on whether capitalism is good or bad.
  • Works from the specific words (Smith’s line, a real news sentence), reading the actual language rather than gesturing at “economics” in the abstract.
  • Keeps evidence visibly separate from interpretation: distinguishes what demonstrably happened (the dates, the texts, the shift in the word) from what the writer claims it means.
  • Steelmans the rival reading honestly, gives the “science just matured” case its strongest form before answering it, and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.
  • Avoids the two traps this module guards: strawmanning Smith into a greed-prophet, and treating the “marginalism depoliticized economics” reading as proven fact rather than a contested interpretation.

Recap

We watched naturalization put on a lab coat. A young discipline took an order that had just been built and re-described it as a system of natural laws: Smith’s invisible hand cropped into a slogan, Ricardo’s wage pattern hardened into an iron law, and, after the 1870s, the whole field recentered on timeless individual preference and renamed from political economy to plain economics (that the turn depoliticized the science is a strong reading, but a reading; keep the seam visible). The cleanest proof needed no theory at all: the economy itself, the most ordinary noun on the business page, is a twentieth-century invention, assembled with national accounts in the 1930s and 40s. The map grew so good we forgot we had drawn it. Next the course turns from the order on paper to the order in the body: how clocks, bells, and a particular picture of the self remade the worker from the inside.
