Module 5 of 11

The Polite Origin Story Hides Slavery

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain why Atlantic slavery and colonial extraction were integral to capitalist accumulation rather than a pre-capitalist holdover capitalism outgrew.
  • Distinguish the widely-accepted "integral, not external" claim from the contested strong claim that slave profits financed industrialization.
  • Identify how the "peaceful thrift" origin story naturalizes capitalism by erasing its coercion.

The hook

Picture the portrait of capitalism’s birth that almost everyone has seen, even people who have never read a page of economics. It hangs in textbooks, in op-eds, in the half-remembered things we tell ourselves about where wealth comes from. In the picture, a thrifty man works hard, spends little, saves the difference, and reinvests it. Multiply him by a few generations and a few thousand, and you have the modern economy: capital as crystallized patience, the reward of enterprise and self-denial. It is a flattering portrait. It is also, like most portraits, carefully composed, and the composition is the point. Step close enough to study the brushwork and you notice something at the edge of the canvas, something that was there in the room when the picture was painted and then cropped out of the frame: a plantation, a ledger of human beings, a ship.

Last module we watched part of that cropping happen at home: the dispossession of the commons that made the “free” worker (module 04), the customary user turned out by hedge and law to produce a class with nothing to sell but its labour. This module follows the frame’s other cropped edge, the one that runs across an ocean. The starter capital and the “free” worker were not the whole story of how capitalism got going. The plantation was in the room too.

Context & background

The subject is grave, and the history has to be handled as such. Atlantic slavery was one of the largest sustained atrocities in recorded history. By the most careful estimates, somewhere around twelve million captive Africans were forced onto ships across roughly three and a half centuries, and a great many died in the crossing or in the fields. The aim here is not to shock you with that. You can be shocked elsewhere, and should be. The aim is colder and more specific: to ask what that system was for economically, and where it sits in the story capitalism tells about its own origins. The sobriety is what lets the argument land as argument rather than as outrage.

Start with the thing the origin portrait takes for granted: that slavery and capitalism belong to different worlds. In the flattering version, slavery is something older: a brutal, pre-modern, irrational institution that the rising market economy, with its wage labour and free contract, eventually competed with and outgrew. Capitalism, on this telling, is the thing that ended slavery: the two are opposites on a timeline, and history walks from one to the other.

Against that stands a plainer term. Colonial accumulation, sometimes called Atlantic accumulation, names the claim that slavery and colonial extraction were integral to capitalist accumulation, woven into it from the start, not a separate older system sitting outside it. (Recall that accumulation here means the historical building-up of capital’s preconditions: the concentrated wealth and the workforce; Marx’s sarcastic name for the violent making of capitalism’s preconditions was “so-called primitive accumulation,” the so-called doing the work against the polite story of thrift.) Where module 04 showed dispossession at home, this shows accumulation abroad and in bondage. The second was not a sideshow to the first.

The figure who put this on the map most forcefully was Eric Williams, a Trinidadian historian and statesman whose 1944 book Capitalism and Slavery argued, against the comfortable timeline, that the slave economies of the Caribbean and the Atlantic were not a backward leftover but a central engine of the commercial wealth out of which modern capitalism grew. The book has been argued over ever since. Which parts are accepted and which are still fought about is most of the lesson, and we will get to it. But its central reframing has held.

Narrative / exposition

To see why “integral” is the right word and “holdover” is the wrong one, look at what a plantation actually was.

It is easy to picture a plantation as a farm, a big, cruel farm, but a farm, something agricultural and old. That picture misleads. A sugar plantation in eighteenth-century Barbados or Saint-Domingue was closer to a factory than to a peasant’s field. It existed to produce a single commodity for a distant market it would never consume: sugar, then later cotton, raw material fed into a transatlantic system of trade and manufacture. It ran on the clock and the gang, on measured output and driven, coordinated, deskilled labour: the discipline of the production line before there were production lines. It was financed by European merchant credit, insured by European underwriters, its product shipped to European refiners and sold through European markets. Call it what it was: a coerced-labour engine of commodity production, organized for profit and plugged into the most advanced commercial circuits of its day. The whip did the work that, back home, the dull compulsion of hunger had begun to do, different instrument, same direction. The cruelty is ancient; the organization was new, and it was capitalist in form.

Now hold that against the strongest version of the opposing view, because the steelman is what gives this its credibility. Defenders of the comfortable timeline can point to real things. Slavery is older than capitalism by millennia. Many of the thinkers who built liberal political economy genuinely opposed the slave trade. And in the end it was industrializing, capitalist societies (Britain first) that abolished it. Doesn’t that suggest slavery and capitalism really were at odds, the one growing up to abolish the other? It is not a stupid argument. It has dates on its side.

Here is where the comfortable timeline cracks, and it cracks on a single stubborn fact: slavery in the Americas did not wither as industrial capitalism rose. It grew. The great expansion of plantation slavery, the cotton kingdom of the American South, the sugar of Cuba, the coffee of Brazil, happened in the nineteenth century, the very decades of the steam engine, the factory, and the railway, and slave-grown cotton from the South fed the mills of Lancashire, the showcase of the Industrial Revolution. This expansion inside industrial capitalism, rather than before it, has a name: second slavery, the term associated with Dale Tomich, marking that slavery was not a fading first chapter but a second, larger, modernizing surge bound up with industrial growth. The holdover story predicts slavery should shrink as capitalism rises. The record shows the opposite. That is the kind of fact that breaks a frame.

So the cropping in the origin portrait is not a small omission of detail. The “peaceful thrift” story does not merely understate the violence; it relocates it: files it under “the old world capitalism left behind” rather than “the conditions under which capitalism’s wealth was made.” That relocation is the naturalizing move at the center of this module: by exiling coercion to the past, the story lets present wealth look like the pure fruit of enterprise. Crop the plantation out of the frame, and the portrait becomes a picture of innocent industry. Leave it in, and the same canvas tells you that some of the capital was accumulated at gunpoint.

Primary source

Eric Williams opens his case by attacking the comfortable timeline at its root: the assumption that slavery in the Americas was driven, from the start, by racial contempt rather than by the economics of producing commodities cheaply. He puts the order of causation the other way around:

“Slavery was not born of racism: rather, racism was the consequence of slavery.”

— Eric Williams, Capitalism and Slavery (1944)

Read it the way our anthropologist from module 01 would read a strange artifact, slowly, refusing to take its meaning as obvious. Williams is making an economic-historical claim, not a moral pronouncement. The planters, he argues, wanted the cheapest coerced labour they could get for their sugar; the racial ideology that later justified the system came after the economic demand, as its rationalization, not before it as its cause. Whether he gets the full causal story right is something historians still debate, so hold it as his interpretation rather than settled fact. But notice the shape of the move, because it is the same shape this whole course studies. A brutal economic arrangement, built for profit, gets dressed afterward in a story that makes it look like an expression of how things naturally are. Williams is doing to the slave system what we are learning to do to capitalism: pulling the made thing back out from under the natural-seeming story laid over it.

Check yourself

Before reading on. The “comfortable timeline” says slavery was a pre-capitalist holdover that rising capitalism outgrew and abolished. What single historical fact does the idea of second slavery put in its way?

Show answer

That plantation slavery did not shrink as industrial capitalism rose. It expanded in the nineteenth century, the era of the factory and the steam engine (the cotton kingdom of the American South, sugar in Cuba, coffee in Brazil), and its product fed industrial manufacturing such as the Lancashire cotton mills. “Second slavery” names that surge inside industrial capitalism. If slavery grows alongside capitalism rather than dying before it, the “holdover capitalism outgrew” story can’t be the whole truth.

Analysis & interpretation

Two different claims live in the neighborhood of “slavery and capitalism,” the distinction between them is the easiest thing here to get wrong, and a careful reader must not let them blur into one.

The first is the claim we have been building: that Atlantic slavery and colonial extraction were integral to capitalist accumulation, part of the system, not external to it; that the plantation was a capitalist enterprise and the wealth of the Atlantic economy was, in real part, slave-made. Call this the integral claim. It rests on evidence now broadly accepted across the field: the scale of the trade, the organization of the plantation, the nineteenth-century expansion, the documented flows of slave-grown commodities into industrial production. Few serious historians now defend the picture of slavery as a sealed-off older world.

The second is sharper: that the profits of the slave trade and the plantations were what financed the Industrial Revolution. That British industrialization was, in a measurable causal sense, paid for by slavery. Call this the strong financing claim. This is the contested version, often labelled the “Williams thesis” in its strong form, and it is genuinely disputed. Economic historians have spent decades arguing over the numbers: how large slave-trade profits were relative to the whole British economy, how much of that capital flowed into industry rather than land or luxury, whether the timing even fits. Some find the contribution substantial; others find it too small to have been decisive. The point is not to declare a winner but to see that this is a live debate, and a course that hid it would be committing the exact sin it diagnoses: smuggling a contestable reading in under the cover of plain fact.

Keep the seam visible, then. Integral, not external is well-supported; lean on it. Slavery financed industrialization is contested; flag it as the argument it is. You can accept the first wholeheartedly while suspending judgment on the second, and recognizing that you can is itself a piece of the method this course teaches.

One last guard, the same one we set last time. The argument here is not that capitalism is uniquely evil because slavery stands in its past, nor that every fortune is simply blood money. It is narrower and harder to wave away: that the origin story capitalism tells about itself, patient thrift, peaceful enterprise, is incomplete in a way that flatters. The coercion was not all left behind in a darker age. Some of it was the working condition of the wealth. To say so is not to moralize; it is to put the cropped edge back in the frame and look at the whole picture.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. Write to think; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

  1. Keep the two claims apart. In your own words, state the integral claim and the strong financing claim, and explain why someone could rationally accept the first while doubting the second. What kind of evidence would each one need?
  2. Run the portrait. Take the course’s image, the origin portrait with the plantation cropped out, and apply it to a specific modern object: a brand, a national founding myth, a museum’s account of a fortune. What is in the frame, what has been cropped to the edge, and whose innocence does the cropping protect?
  3. Steelman the timeline. Write the strongest honest version of “capitalism and slavery were opposites, the one ended the other.” Then identify the precise fact (or facts) at which it breaks, and say what work the word holdover was doing.
  4. Test the source. Williams writes that “racism was the consequence of slavery.” What would count as evidence for that ordering, and what would count against it? Why does it matter to his larger argument which way the causation runs?
What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim: not a summary of the module, and not a verdict that capitalism is simply good or evil.
  • Keeps evidence visibly separate from interpretation: distinguishes the documented record (the scale, the organization of the plantation, the nineteenth-century expansion) from the readings built on it.
  • Holds the integral / strong-financing distinction precisely, and does not collapse the contested claim into the accepted one or vice versa.
  • Treats the live debate as live: names it, doesn’t pretend to settle it, doesn’t hide it.
  • Reads Williams as a source with a position and a date, not as a neutral oracle; quotes the actual words.
  • Avoids the twin failures: sensationalism that substitutes horror for argument, and a Eurocentric account that quietly crops the plantation back out.

Recap

The flattering portrait of capitalism’s birth, capital as crystallized thrift, is composed by cropping. Out at the frame’s edge sits the plantation: not an old farm but a coerced-labour engine of commodity production, financed, insured, and sold through the most advanced markets of its day, and expanding rather than fading as industry rose: the surge historians call second slavery. So Atlantic accumulation was integral to capitalism, not a holdover it outgrew; that much the record carries. Whether slave profits actually financed industrialization is a separate, still-contested claim, and the discipline is to hold the two apart. Together with last module’s enclosure, this puts the coercion back into the supposedly peaceful origin story: dispossession at home, accumulation in bondage abroad. Next we leave the making of capital and the worker for the making of the idea itself: how “the market” came to seem like something that grew on its own, when it was built.
