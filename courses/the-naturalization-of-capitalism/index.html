How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural

A historical, critical account of how a recent and deliberately built economic order came to feel like the natural condition of human life, and a method for seeing it as contingent again.

Humanities Beginner 11 modules ~5h Path · Economics as History, Not Nature

“The market demands it.” “You can’t fight economics.” “It’s just human nature.” We talk about capitalism the way we talk about the weather: as a fact we live inside and never voted on. This course is about how that happened: how a way of organizing work, land, and money that most of human history would not recognize came to feel like the floor under our feet.

The approach is historical and critical, and it has a definite point of view: that the naturalness of capitalism is the thing to be explained, not assumed. But this is an analysis, not a sermon. Each step states the strongest version of the “it was always going to be this way” story before taking it apart, keeps what the historical record shows separate from what any thinker claims it means, and names the places where serious scholars still disagree. You do not need any background in economics, history, or theory. Every specialist term is built up from an everyday picture before it is named.

You will move from the lens (what “naturalization” is, and how to make the familiar strange) through the long history of how the order was made (the enclosure of common land, the plantation, the invention of “the market” and “the economy,” the remaking of time and the self, the unpaid labour the whole thing rests on) to how it comes to feel natural and holds, and finally to how the sense that “there is no alternative” was deliberately built within living memory. By the end you will not have a manifesto. You will have something more useful: a method for looking at any arrangement that presents itself as simply the way things are, and asking when it began, whom it suits, and what it is hiding.

Modules

  1. 01 The Lens: How a Made Thing Comes to Seem Natural The tools the whole course runs on: naturalization, contingency, ideology critique, and the trick of making the familiar strange. 24 min
  2. 02 Compared to What? The Embedded Past Before capitalism there was no separate machine called "the economy": producing and exchanging were woven into kinship, status, and obligation, which is why "markets have always existed" proves far less than it seems. 26 min
  3. 03 The Myth of Barter The textbook story that money was invented to fix barter has no historical support. Unpacking why teaches the lens in miniature. 24 min
  4. 04 Enclosure: The Making of the Worker Capitalism needed a class of people with nothing to sell but their labour. That class was produced, by fence and by law, out of the commons. 27 min
  5. 05 The Polite Origin Story Hides Slavery Capitalism's origin tale of thrift and enterprise leaves out the plantation and the slave ship, which were integral to accumulation, not external to it. 26 min
  6. 06 Inventing 'the Economy' and 'the Market' The self-regulating market did not grow on its own: land, labour, and money were turned into commodities, and laissez-faire was built and enforced by the state. 28 min
  7. 07 The Science That Made It Law How a young discipline recast a recently built order as a system of natural laws, and how even the word "economy" only lately came to mean a single national thing you can measure. 28 min
  8. 08 Clocks, Bells, and the Made Self Industrial capitalism remade time and the person, turning hours into something you spend and owe, and the acquisitive individual into "human nature." 26 min
  9. 09 The Hidden Subsidy The "free" worker is sustained by unpaid, largely women's labour (care and the household) made invisible by being coded as nature. 28 min
  10. 10 How It Comes to Feel Natural Three mechanisms turn a made order into common sense: relations between people appearing as relations between things, consent won in culture, and a self that polices itself. 27 min
  11. 11 There Is No Alternative: and How to Doubt It The deepest "natural" common sense (that no alternative is even thinkable) was built on purpose, recently; here is how it was made, and the method for seeing through it. 29 min
