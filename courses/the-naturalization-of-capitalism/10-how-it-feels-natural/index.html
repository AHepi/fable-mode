Module 10 of 11

How It Comes to Feel Natural

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain commodity fetishism: how the people and labour behind a thing vanish behind its price.
  • Explain hegemony: how an order secures consent by becoming everyone's common sense.
  • Explain how discipline produces self-regulating subjects, and distinguish Foucault's account from the "ideology" concept.

The hook

The first module set a price tag in front of the anthropologist and made it strange. A coffee on a shelf: a number, four dollars, and nothing else. No face, no field, no ship, no hands. The tag presents a relationship between people, everyone who grew, picked, shipped, roasted, and stacked those beans, as if it were a fact about a thing. We pocketed a name for that vanishing and promised to return.

We can come back to that price tag now. Every module since has shown how the order was made: the commons fenced, the worker freed of everything but the need to work, the market built and called natural, the clock that turned hours into something you owe. Once an order has been built that thoroughly, by fence and law and force, it comes to feel natural, and the question is how. A thing assembled out of dispossession comes to seem like the floor under your feet, not enforced, not even argued for, just there.

The answer is three mechanisms at three sites, the shelf, the conversation, and the self, the machinery by which a made order wins not your obedience but your consent, then your common sense, then your reflexes.

Context & background

A built order has two ways to hold. It can hold by force, fence, law, bailiff, but force is expensive and brittle; an order held only at gunpoint is at permanent war with the people inside it. The orders that last manage something else: they get people to experience the arrangement as obvious rather than imposed, to want roughly what the order needs them to want, and to find any alternative faintly unthinkable. That is the territory here, how an order holds in the heads and habits of people who were never at the founding and never signed anything.

Three thinkers each found a different gear in that machinery, and we keep them separate on purpose, because they do not agree, and one of them thinks the others are using a broken concept. Carry in one term, ideology, not in the everyday sense of “a party platform” but in this course’s critique sense, the taken-for-granted common sense that serves an order without anyone having to argue for it (module 01). The first two mechanisms below are versions of it; the third is a thinker refusing it.

Narrative / exposition

The shelf

Start where module 01 left off, at the price tag, and give the vanishing its proper name. Commodity fetishism is the term, Marx’s, the 19th-century critic of political economy, in Capital (1867). “Fetish” here means an object treated as if it had powers of its own. Under capitalism, the social relations between people, all the labour and lives that went into a thing, come to appear as relations between things, as properties the object owns, like its weight or colour. The coffee “is worth” four dollars the way it is brown. The price looks like a fact about the object; it is really a frozen summary of a vast chain of human work you cannot see from where you stand. That is the gloss: when the people and labour behind a thing vanish behind its price.

What makes this more than a complaint about ignorance is that the shopper is not lazy and the label is not dishonest. The system itself presents human relations in the form of thing-relations, so you meet other people’s labour only as a number on a tag, a thing confronting you across a counter. The vanishing is structural, not personal.

That hardening has a wider name, the one module 01 slipped into your back pocket and promised to pay off: reification. Reification is human relations hardening into things that seem to have a life of their own, a term coined by the philosopher Georg Lukács. In the 1920s he ran Marx’s point about the price tag across the whole social world. If a single commodity can present human labour as a property of a thing, then a society organized around commodities comes to experience everything that way (“the market,” “the economy,” “demand”) as fixed objects obeying their own laws, that people confront rather than make. Remember how “the market demands” comes out as if a thunderhead had spoken? That is reification in a sentence: we make the thing, then it seems to make us. Lukács called the result a second nature: the social world experienced as if it were as fixed as nature.

Check yourself: A friend says, "Commodity fetishism just means people care too much about buying stuff." Correct them: what does the term actually point to?

It is not about materialism or greed. Commodity fetishism names a structural appearance: under capitalism the social relations between people, the labour and lives behind a thing, show up as properties the object seems to own (its price). The point is not that shoppers are shallow but that the system itself presents human work in the form of thing-facts, so the people behind the price vanish whether you care about stuff or not. Reification (Lukács) is the same move generalized to the whole social world.

Common sense

The shelf explains how the order looks. It does not explain why people defend it, why the arrangement survives in argument long after anyone has stopped pointing a gun. For that, the second site and the second thinker.

Antonio Gramsci was an Italian Marxist imprisoned by Mussolini; he wrote his Prison Notebooks (1929–35) in fragments, under censorship. His question had nagged the whole tradition: why don’t the many, who hold the numbers, remake an order that runs against them? His answer was hegemony: rule secured by consent, when the rulers’ worldview becomes everyone’s common sense. An order becomes hegemonic when its worldview sinks into the unargued frame of ordinary talk, so the rulers no longer have to win each argument: the terms of the argument already belong to them.

The word to weight is consent. A purely coercive order rules over enemies; a hegemonic order rules through people who experience its assumptions as plain reason. Think of the phrases module 01 opened with: “you can’t fight economics,” “of course you’ll borrow to buy a car.” Nobody is forced to say these. People say them freely, often against their own interests, because the worldview that serves the order has become the ordinary water they swim in. That is hegemony: not the silencing of dissent but the narrowing of what counts as sense. And it is maintained nowhere dramatic (in schools, in newspapers, in the offhand thing your uncle says about welfare), which is why it is so durable.

This is the same critique-sense ideology from module 01, now given its political engine. Naturalization, on Gramsci’s account, is not an accident of perception but a political achievement, won and re-won every day in culture, by an order whose worldview holds firmer as common sense than it ever could at the point of a bayonet.

The self

The first two mechanisms still picture power as something that comes at you from outside: a veil over the shelf, a worldview poured into your head. The third thinker rejected that picture, and here you have to slow down, because the temptation to blend him into the others is strong.

Michel Foucault, a French historian-philosopher of power, in Discipline and Punish (1975), studied how modern institutions stopped ruling by spectacle and pain and began ruling by production. Discipline, in his sense, is power that produces self-regulating people, not just punishes. Watch it work in the most ordinary machinery: the timetable that slices the day into measured cells; the school exam that ranks each child against a norm and teaches them to rank themselves; the factory clock from module 08, which did not just fine latecomers but trained them, over generations, to feel the lateness as their own failing. Power of this kind does not stand over you; it works through you, manufacturing the punctual, calculable, productive person the order needs: one who polices themselves, so no one else has to.

Here is the distinction that matters most. Marx and Gramsci both rely, in different ways, on the concept of ideology: that there is a real arrangement underneath which common sense or the price tag masks, and that critique can pull the mask off and show the truth behind it. Foucault is skeptical of that whole picture. He doesn’t think power mainly works by hiding a truth better ideas could expose. He thinks power produces: it makes subjects, makes the desires and norms by which we’d judge it, makes what counts as truth in the first place. There is no clean reality behind the veil for critique to recover, because the “you” doing the recovering was shaped by the same power. The self is not a free thing with ideology laid over it; it is an effect of discipline, produced from the start.

Do not paper over this. Marx and Gramsci say an order masks its real workings and we can unmask them; Foucault says an order makes the people who would do the unmasking, so “mask” is the wrong metaphor. These are rival accounts of one puzzle, and an honest reader keeps the friction visible rather than melting them into a single theory of “how power tricks us.”

Primary source

Here is Marx naming the vanishing at the shelf, in his own words, the formal version of the price-tag intuition you reached in module 01.

“It is a definite social relation between men, that assumes, in their eyes, the fantastic form of a relation between things.”

— Karl Marx, Capital, vol. 1 (1867), Part 1, “The Fetishism of Commodities” (Moore–Aveling translation)

Read it the way the anthropologist of module 01 would read a ritual, slowly, refusing to take any word as obvious. Marx is not saying the relation between things is an illusion you can blink away. He calls it fantastic, but insists it is also definite and real: under these arrangements, that genuinely is the form a social relation takes. The labour really does confront you as a price. This is the whole difficulty of denaturalizing it: the appearance is not a lie laid over the truth, but the shape the truth is built to take.

Analysis & interpretation

Keep the seam this course always keeps. What is evidence here, and what is interpretation?

The evidence is thin and textual, and that is honest to admit: these are concepts, arguments about how experience is structured, not datasets. What is checkable is that Marx wrote those words in 1867; that Gramsci developed hegemony in the Prison Notebooks; that Foucault traced discipline through real institutions (timetable, exam, prison) that exist and demonstrably train behaviour. The factory clock of module 08 is evidence.

The interpretation is the claim each thinker builds on top: that the price masks social relations (Marx); that common sense is an achievement of class rule (Gramsci); that the self is produced by discipline rather than only constrained (Foucault). These are powerful readings, but they are readings, arguable, and argued against. A defender of the order would offer rivals that deserve their day in court: that a price is useful information, not a mask; that common sense about markets persists because markets broadly work; that calling the disciplined self “produced” robs people of the agency they plainly have. The honest response is not a sneer but a question (what would settle it?), and the answer, again, is the historical record this course has been laying down: whether the arrangement really was built, recently, and could have been built differently. If it was, then “this is simply the case” is doing exactly the work these three thinkers describe.

One last guardrail. A theory that explained everything by blending the three into a single machine of deception would be its own kind of naturalization: a story so seamless you stop noticing it is a story. Leave the disagreement standing.

Check yourself: Both Gramsci and Foucault explain how an order holds without constant force. Name the core disagreement between them.

Gramsci works with the concept of ideology: a ruling order’s worldview becomes everyone’s common sense, masking the real interests it serves. Critique can expose the mask. Foucault is skeptical of that “mask over a truth” picture. For him power doesn’t mainly hide reality; it produces: it makes self-regulating subjects, and even makes what counts as truth and as a “self,” so there’s no clean reality behind the veil for critique to recover. Gramsci: consent is manufactured over a real interest. Foucault: the person who’d object was manufactured too. The disagreement is the point.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. Write from a specific example; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

  1. Run the shelf. Take one everyday purchase and trace the chain of people the price tag hides, as far back as you honestly can, marking where your knowledge runs out. Say in one sentence what the gap itself reveals about commodity fetishism.
  2. Catch a common sense. Collect one sentence you have actually heard that treats a contestable arrangement as plain reason (“you can’t fight the market,” “of course you take on debt for a degree”). Argue, using hegemony, why it counts as consent rather than coercion. Then steelman the view that it is just true.
  3. Marx, Gramsci, or Foucault? Take one scene: a performance review, a credit score, a “wellness” app that nudges your steps. Explain it through each of the three mechanisms, then say which account fits best and why the other two fall short here.
  4. Test Foucault against ideology. State the strongest version of the claim that power “masks” a truth we could unmask. Give Foucault’s objection, that the self doing the unmasking was produced by the same power, and say which you find more convincing, and what would change your mind.
What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of the module, and not a verdict that capitalism is good or bad.
  • Works from a specific scene or sentence and reads its actual details, rather than gesturing at “the system.”
  • Keeps evidence separate from interpretation: what demonstrably exists (a clock, an exam, a price) from what a thinker claims it means.
  • Attributes each idea to the right thinker and does not blend them: keeps Foucault’s “power produces” apart from the “ideology masks a truth” framing of Marx and Gramsci.
  • Steelmans the defending view honestly before answering it, and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.

Recap

Three mechanisms, one question: how a built order stops feeling built. At the shelf, commodity fetishism (Marx) makes the labour behind a thing vanish into its price. Reification (Lukács) runs that across the whole social world, until “the market” seems a creature with moods rather than a name for what people do. In the conversation, hegemony (Gramsci) wins the order not obedience but consent, sinking its worldview into common sense until argument itself runs on its terms. In the self, discipline (Foucault) produces people who police themselves: the timetable, the exam, the clock. And here the tradition splits: Marx and Gramsci say the order masks a truth we could unmask, while Foucault says it makes the very self who would do the unmasking. We left that disagreement standing on purpose. Next, the sharpest case of all: an order so thoroughly settled that no alternative seems even thinkable. The discovery that this common sense was manufactured, recently, and on purpose.
