Module 3 of 9

Credit and the Ledger

What you'll be able to do
  • Distinguish a unit of account (the measuring-stick for prices and debts) from a medium of exchange (the thing that changes hands).
  • Explain how Mesopotamian temple and palace accounting used money-of-account with no coin circulating.
  • Explain how a tally records a debt and makes money as a record of who owes whom concrete.

The hook

It is the end of a long evening at a bar where the bartender knows you. Four drinks, none of them paid for, and yet nothing about the night felt like borrowing. You signed nothing, and no coin left your pocket. When you settle up, the bartender does not weigh out what you owe in beer or peanuts. She reads a number off a screen, in dollars, and you hand over the dollars.

Something strange has happened. All night your tab was reckoned in dollars: every drink had a price the instant it was poured. But for most of the evening no dollar moved. The thing that counted the debt and the thing that finally paid it were not the same kind of thing.

That gap, between the money you reckon in and the money you hand over, is the most important idea in this course. The barter myth never sees it. Once you can see it, the myth’s whole sequence falls apart, and it falls apart not in a thought experiment but in the dirt of southern Iraq, five thousand years ago.

Context & background

In module 01 you met the story at its strongest: the barter myth, in which people first swapped goods directly, money was then invented to fix the clumsiness of swapping, and markets and credit grew up afterward on top of money. In module 02 you saw why that sequence cannot be the history: no society has ever been found running on barter and then upgrading to money. What communities actually ran on was credit and debt tracked socially, the two sides of one relationship. People kept a running tab of who owed whom, settled by custom rather than by haggling out a price every time.

If the running tab was already there, it had to be reckoned in something. You can owe someone, but you have to owe them some amount of something. What was the something, before there were coins?

The answer comes from Mesopotamia, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates, roughly where Iraq is now, in the centuries around 3000 BCE: the world of the first cities, temples, writing, and surviving account books. Mesopotamian temples and palaces kept detailed accounts in silver and barley more than two thousand years before anyone struck a coin. There were no coins in this world. There was a great deal of money.

That sentence holds steady once you have one distinction, the one the bar tab was built on.

Narrative / exposition

Go back to the tab. The dollar was doing two different jobs, and the myth’s central mistake is to jam them together.

The first job is measuring. All night, the dollar was the yardstick that said how much each drink was worth and how much, in total, you owed. It did this whether or not a single dollar was in the room. A debt of “twelve dollars” is a perfectly definite thing even while it sits unpaid; the dollar here is a unit you count in, the way a foot is a unit you measure length in. A measure used this way is what economists call a unit of account: the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in (you owe me “twelve shekels”), even when no coin moves.

The second job is changing hands. At the very end, you handed over actual dollar bills, and those dollars did something the measuring-stick dollars never had to: they physically moved, and that movement settled the debt. A thing used this way is what economists call a medium of exchange: the thing that physically changes hands in a deal, the coin or note.

In a modern economy one word does both jobs, which is why they are so easy to confuse. But they came apart on the tab: the measuring went on for hours, the handing-over took a moment at the end. The myth assumes the handing-over thing came first, that money began as some good people passed around and only later got used to keep score. The Mesopotamian record shows the opposite: the score was being kept for two thousand years before there was a coin to hand over.

Look at what the scribes were doing. A temple in a Sumerian city ran like a vast estate: it took in barley, held herds, employed laborers, rented land, lent grain and silver, and tracked all of it on clay. To put a bushel of barley, a day’s labor, and a sheep onto the same page so the books can balance, you need a common measure. Scribes used two: barley, by volume, and silver, by weight, measured in a unit called the shekel (here a weight, long before it was ever a coin). A debt might be written as “so many shekels of silver.” The silver and barley were the units of account, the dollars on the tab.

And much of that silver never moved. A debt could be incurred, carried for a season, and settled all in silver without a lump of silver changing hands: it could be cleared against other debts, paid in barley or labor at a customary rate, or rolled forward on the books. Prices, debts, rents, and wages all reckoned in a standard measure with no coin circulating: that is money-of-account, money that keeps score without any minted thing changing hands. This is money. It is just not the kind the myth taught you to picture.

A second clue sits right alongside the account books. When you ask what the earliest writing was for, the answer is not prayers or poems or the deeds of kings. The earliest cuneiform, the wedge-shaped marks pressed into wet clay, is overwhelmingly administrative: lists, receipts, inventories, ration records, debts. Writing arose largely for accounts. The first thing humans seem to have wanted a durable record of was not their gods or their stories but who owed what to whom. If money were really born as a handy good people passed around, that is a strange thing to find at the dawn of literacy, and exactly what you would expect if money were born as a way of keeping track of debts.

Primary source

This module’s evidence is not a single quotable line but a type of object, so the honest thing is to describe that type faithfully rather than invent one tablet.

Take a small clay tablet of a kind found by the thousands across Mesopotamian sites, a private loan record. Pressed into it, in cuneiform, is roughly this shape of information: a quantity of silver or barley named as the amount owed, the name of the person who owes it and the person it is owed to, often a rate of interest, sometimes a date for repayment, and the names of witnesses. Some such tablets were sealed inside an inscribed clay envelope, so the inner tablet could not be altered without breaking the seal.

Now read it. What is not there is any coin. The silver named on the tablet need never have been seen by the borrower; it is the measure the debt is counted in, not necessarily a thing handed over. The tablet is not money you spend. It is money in a deeper sense: a debt made durable in clay, money-of-account made visible, with no coin in sight.

A reader who wants a named voice for this reading can have one. A. Mitchell Innes, an early-twentieth-century British writer on money whose 1913 essays are the foundational modern statement of this view, argued that credit and debt are older than coined money, and that money is at bottom a matter of credit rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. That last step is interpretation, not a fact the tablet hands us. The tablet is the evidence: debts reckoned in silver, on clay, before coins. What it means is the argument the course takes up next.

Check yourself

Pause before reading on. A friend says: “Fine, but silver was the money. They measured debts in silver and they paid in silver. So money still came first, as a thing, just as the myth says.” Using the distinction from this module, where is your friend going wrong?

Show answer

The friend has collapsed the two jobs back together. Silver was serving as the unit of account, the measuring-stick the debt was reckoned in (“so many shekels of silver”), but it was very often not the medium of exchange, the thing that physically changed hands. Debts in silver were frequently settled some other way, by clearing them against other debts, by paying in barley or labor at a customary rate, or by rolling them forward, with no silver moving at all. So silver as a measure of value came first and did most of the work; silver as a thing you hand over was the lesser, occasional job. The myth needs money to begin as a thing people pass around. The record shows it beginning as a standard for keeping score. Naming the debt in silver is not the same as paying it in silver, and the gap between the two is the whole point.

Narrative / exposition (continued)

A debt can also be a physical thing you carry without being a coin you spend, and the clearest example is the tally: a physical record of a debt, classically a notched stick split in two, with one half kept by each party.

Here is how it works. You lend me ten units of something. We cut notches across a stick to mark the amount, then split it lengthwise through the notches into two halves. You keep one; I keep the other. Because they were one cut, the notches run across both halves identically. When the debt is settled, the halves are brought back together, and they must match, notch for notch and grain for grain, or the record is false. No one can add a notch, because the wood’s own grain would betray the join. (This is not only ancient: the English Exchequer kept its accounts on split tally sticks for centuries.)

Look at what the tally is. It is not the thing owed, and not a medium of exchange you spend on bread. It is the record of who owes whom, made tangible and verifiable. And the half you hold is genuinely worth something, because it is a claim that someone owes you. People could and did pass such claims along to third parties, the first hint of how a tracked personal debt might one day harden into impersonal, transferable money. The tally is the module’s thesis made something you could hold: money here is not a coin.

Analysis & interpretation

Step back and weigh what we have. The evidence and the interpretation built on it are two different things here, and this is exactly the kind of place a course about a theory-driven story could smuggle in one of its own.

Here is the evidence, and it is firm. Around 3000 BCE, Mesopotamian temples and palaces kept detailed accounts of debts, prices, rents, and wages reckoned in silver and barley, more than two thousand years before coinage. Much of the silver was a measure, not a thing that moved. The earliest writing is overwhelmingly administrative. None of that is contested.

Here is the interpretation, a further step. From this evidence, Innes, and later the anthropologist David Graeber in his 2011 book Debt: The First 5,000 Years (an influential synthesis that specialists contest on specifics, so its readings travel under its own name), draw a large conclusion: that money is essentially credit, a measure of debt, rather than a commodity that emerged from barter. That conclusion fits the Mesopotamian record strikingly well, but fitting it is not the same as being proven by it. The evidence shows that money-of-account preceded coinage. Whether money is in its nature debt rather than a commodity is a separate claim about the essence of a thing, and essences are not settled by a single body of evidence, however good.

There is a rival theory of money built around exactly this reading: the credit theory of money, also called chartalism, which holds that money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt, backed by social and ultimately state obligation. It is the direct competitor to the commodity theory you met at the close of module 01, which says money is essentially a special tradeable good that grew out of barter. The Mesopotamian ledgers are the credit theory’s home ground. We will put the two side by side, steelman each, and weigh them in module 06. No winner is crowned here. What the record does settle is narrower and enough: the unit of account came first, and that is what gives the credit theory its footing.

One guard before we close. None of this makes early Mesopotamia a gentle place, a lost age of trust before cold cash. These were stratified societies with temples and palaces commanding labor, and the same loan tablets that prove debt was reckoned in silver also record interest, terms, and the machinery of obligation that could, when a debt went bad, cost a person their freedom. That darker side of debt is the next module’s subject. The point here is not that the past was kinder, only, and it is enough, that money did not begin the way you were told.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, however you come down.

  1. Find the two jobs in your own life. Describe one everyday situation, not the bar tab, in which a unit of account and a medium of exchange come apart, where something is priced or owed in a money that does not physically change hands at that moment. (A rent agreement, a restaurant bill, a phone plan, a loan.) Name which money is doing the measuring and which, if any, does the handing-over, and when each happens.

  2. Read the tablet. Reread the description of the clay loan record in the Primary source section. In a short paragraph, argue for what this object best supports and what it does not support. Be precise about the line between “money-of-account existed before coins” (which the tablet shows) and “money is essentially debt” (which is an interpretation built on top of it).

  3. Why writing, of all things, for this? The earliest writing is overwhelmingly administrative, made for accounts and debts rather than for stories or prayers. Take that fact and argue, in a few sentences, what it suggests about why writing was invented, and how it bears on the barter myth’s claim that money began as a good people passed around.

  4. State the rival, fairly. Without yet having the full case (that comes in module 06), put the credit theory of money into your own words as strongly as you can: money as a unit of account and a measure of debt. Then name one thing you suspect this theory might struggle to explain, something a person who believed money is basically a precious-metal commodity might point to. You are steelmanning a view you have not finished examining.

What a strong response does
  • Uses the hinge terms precisely and keeps them distinct: a unit of account is the measuring-stick a price or debt is reckoned in; a medium of exchange is the thing that physically changes hands. A strong answer shows it can tell which is which in a fresh example, not just in the bar tab.
  • Holds the evidence/interpretation seam. It separates what the Mesopotamian record shows (money-of-account, debts in silver and barley, before coins) from what is argued on top of it (that money is essentially debt), and does not treat the second as proven by the first.
  • Attributes interpretations to their authors. Where it reaches the credit theory, it names it as a theory to be weighed, not as the settled truth, and attributes Innes’s and Graeber’s readings to Innes and Graeber.
  • Resists both myths. It does not slide back into “silver was just the money, so the myth holds” (which collapses the two jobs), and it does not overclaim the credit theory as already won.
  • Stays inside the evidence, and avoids romanticizing: it does not turn early Mesopotamia into a gentle pre-coin Eden, remembering that the same tablets record interest and obligation.

The story

On the night of 16 October 1834, most of the medieval Palace of Westminster, the home of the English Parliament, burned to the ground. The blaze was the worst London had seen between the Great Fire of 1666 and the Blitz, and crowds gathered along the Thames to watch the seat of government go up in flame. The painter J. M. W. Turner was among them, sketching. The cause was money, of a particular kind: the government had been burning its old financial records, and the records were made of wood.

For centuries the English Exchequer, the office that handled the Crown’s money, kept its accounts on tally sticks. A tally was a notched stick of willow, the notches cut across it to record a sum, a debt owed to or by the Crown, a tax payment received. Once notched, the stick was split lengthwise into two unequal parts so that each carried the same notches along the same grain. The larger piece, called the stock, went to one party; the smaller, the foil, to the other. To verify the account, you brought the two halves back together: if they matched, notch for notch and grain for grain, the record was sound, and no one could have shaved a notch off or added one without the join betraying it. This is the tally of this module, scaled up from a private loan to the finances of a kingdom. The Exchequer ran on these sticks from around the twelfth century, and for long stretches almost the entire business of the office was the cutting and matching of tallies. The money of English public finance was, quite literally, a notch in a piece of wood: a record of who owed whom, not a hoard of precious metal.

The system was finally abolished in the early nineteenth century, its last use winding down by the 1820s. That left a problem of disposal. Centuries of tallies had piled up, two cartloads of old notched willow, and in October 1834 officials decided to be rid of them. Rather than give the sticks away as firewood, the Clerk of Works had them stuffed into the two coal-fired furnaces in the basement directly beneath the House of Lords. The furnaces overheated, the flues running under the floor of the chamber caught, and by evening the fire was beyond anyone’s control. The Lords, the Commons, and most of the surrounding medieval buildings were gone by morning. An entire state had run its finances on wooden records for seven hundred years, and the physical destruction of those records took the Houses of Parliament down with them.

Charles Dickens, two decades later, found the whole affair a perfect emblem of official stupidity. In an 1855 speech he mocked the Exchequer for clinging to its “savage” notched sticks long after the practice made any sense, and then, having resolved to be rid of the obsolete bundle, contriving to burn down Parliament in the bargain. The joke lands, and underneath it sits the harder fact. For seven centuries, one of the most sophisticated states in Europe reckoned and settled its debts on split pieces of wood. The value was never in the willow. It was in the record: a matched pair of halves proving who owed what to whom, money working as a unit of account and a measure of debt, with no coin required to make it real.
