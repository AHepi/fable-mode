The Myth of Barter

A history-first account of where money actually came from, not from barter but from credit and debt recorded millennia before the first coin, and why the textbook origin story survives anyway.

Humanities Beginner 9 modules ~4h Path · Economics as History, Not Nature

Open almost any introductory economics textbook to the chapter on money, and you meet the same little fable before a single definition. Long ago, it says, people bartered. Barter was clumsy, because you had to find someone who wanted exactly what you had and had exactly what you wanted. So money was invented to fix it, and from money came markets, banking, and credit. The story is tidy, intuitive, and almost certainly something you believe.

It is also, as history, not what happened. When anthropologists and historians go looking for the barter economy that supposedly became money, they cannot find it. What they find instead, thousands of years before the first coin, is credit: debts reckoned in silver and barley, recorded on clay, governed by written law. Money did not grow out of barter to make trading easier. It began as a way of measuring what people owed one another, and the coin arrived late, carried in on the back of states, armies, and taxes.

This course is history-first. It states the textbook story at its most convincing, then walks the actual record, from Mesopotamian ledgers and Hammurabi’s debt laws to the first coins of Lydia and the periodic cancellation of debts in the ancient world. Along the way it examines the two rival theories of what money fundamentally is, treating them as objects of study rather than the frame, and it asks why a story the evidence does not support is still taught on page one. You need no economics, anthropology, or history to follow it. By the end you will know where money actually came from, and you will carry one sharp question you can put to any origin story: is this remembered, or is it just what some theory needs to be true?

Modules

  1. 01 The Story You Were Taught The textbook origin of money, stated at its most convincing: barter, its frustrations, and money as the invention that fixed them. 24 min
  2. 02 Why It Can't Be the History No society ever ran on barter and then upgraded to money; the record shows credit and obligation came first, and barter turns up only in the margins. 26 min
  3. 03 Credit and the Ledger Thousands of years before the first coin, Mesopotamian scribes were pricing goods and recording debts in silver and barley they never handed over. 28 min
  4. 04 Debt Was Law Before Coins Were Minted A Babylonian king capped interest and set limits on who could be seized for debt a thousand years before anyone struck a coin. 24 min
  5. 05 Coins Are Late: Money as a State Technology The first coins appear thousands of years after the ledgers, and they arrive through armies and taxes, not through barter. 26 min
  6. 06 What Money Actually Is: Two Theories The barter story is the origin myth one theory of money needs; a rival theory, fitting the record better, says money was never a commodity at all. 28 min
  7. 07 The Morality of Debt Before it was a number, a debt was a bond between people, and whole societies once wiped debts clean on purpose, which is why the feeling that you must always pay is history, not a law of nature. 26 min
  8. 08 Why the Myth Survives A false story that keeps getting reprinted is doing work for someone, and the barter myth's work is to make the trading individual look natural and quietly write the state out of the history of money. 24 min
  9. 09 How We Know, and How to Read an Origin Story The tools that overturned the barter myth, and the one question you can now put to any origin story: is it remembered, or only what some theory needs to be true? 24 min
