Module 8 of 9

Why the Myth Survives

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain the ideological work the barter myth performs: naturalizing the market individual, erasing the state and social obligation, and making money look apolitical.
  • Explain how the myth underwrites both the commodity theory of money and a particular politics of debt.
  • Apply the question of whom a story serves without sliding into a claim that anyone deliberately fabricated it.

The hook

By now you have a problem the textbook does not have. You have seen the record. You know there was no barter economy that one day invented money to cure the double coincidence of wants, the need for each side to want exactly what the other has at the same moment (module 01). You know that no ethnographer ever found such an economy (module 02), that Mesopotamian scribes were reckoning debts in silver they never handed over a thousand years before any coin was struck (module 03), that debt was governed law under Hammurabi while bondage was a live threat (module 04), that coins arrive late and arrive through soldiers and taxes (module 05). You know the barter story is the origin a particular theory of money needs (module 06), and you have watched the duty to repay turn from a feeling into a fact of history rather than a fact of nature (module 07).

So here is the problem. If the story is that wrong, and the evidence against it is this old and this clear, why is it still on page one of the textbook?

Not in a forgotten edition. This year’s edition. Open the introductory economics course almost anywhere in the world and you will meet barter, its frustrations, and money as the fix, before you meet a single definition. The error has been pointed out for over a century, by Innes in 1913 and by anthropologists since. It does not go away. A mistake that gets corrected once and stays corrected is just a mistake. A mistake that survives correction, edition after edition, decade after decade, is something else. It is a story doing a job, and the job is what the rest of this follows.

Context & background

Consider a page that keeps getting reprinted. Each new edition of the textbook is reset, recut, reillustrated. The charts are updated, the examples are swapped for fresher ones, the cover is redesigned. And page one of the money chapter says the same thing it said in your parents’ edition and their parents’ edition: long ago, people bartered. The error is not a typo that survived a careless proofreader. It is load-bearing. It is the part of the page nobody thinks to question, because it does not feel like a claim at all. It feels like background, like the way a story has to begin.

A false claim that survives this well is usually not surviving by accident. It survives because it is useful, because it makes something else easy to believe, because pulling it out would mean rebuilding the room it holds up. What it holds up is what comes next.

One distinction does all the work here, and getting it wrong wrecks everything that follows. To say a story does a job is not to say someone wrote it as a job. Naming the work a myth performs is a different act from accusing anyone of forging it. The first is honest analysis. The second is a cheap accusation. The two are easy to blur and ruinous to confuse, so the next section sorts them apart before it does anything else.

Narrative / exposition

A story can serve interests without anyone choosing it for that reason. Everything below turns on that one sentence. Adam Smith, the eighteenth-century Scottish moral philosopher (module 01), did not sit down to write a cover story for anyone. He was reasoning carefully, with the tools and evidence he had, and a good deal of what he wrote is true. The textbook authors who repeat the barter story today are not lying either. Most have simply never been shown the record, or were shown it and judged the story too useful a teaching device to drop. There is no committee, no plot, no smoke-filled room. To imagine one would be to commit the very error this course has warned against from the start: explaining a thing by a story the evidence does not support, because the story is satisfying.

So what is the alternative to a plot? The word for it is function. Asking after a story’s function means asking what it makes seem natural, what it lets you stop questioning, what it removes from view, regardless of whether anyone intended any of that. A custom can hold a community together with no designer. A habit of speech can flatter the powerful with no speaker meaning to. And a story about money can make one picture of human life feel like common sense while another becomes almost unthinkable, with no author ever choosing that result. Function, not intention. That line lets you say sharp things without saying false ones.

The barter myth does at least four jobs, and they reinforce one another.

The first job is the deepest. The story begins with an individual who already wants to truck and barter, already calculates his own advantage, already treats the person across from him as a counterparty in a deal. That figure, the self-interested trader, is placed at the very beginning of the human story, before money, before the state, before society has done anything to him. He is presented as the raw material, the default human, what people simply are once you strip the institutions away. This is a quiet but enormous claim, and the record from module 02 cuts straight against it. Before money, communities did not haggle good for good. They kept running tabs, gave gifts that built obligations, settled by relationship and custom. The person at the origin was embedded in bonds, not standing alone in a market calculating a swap. The myth takes a particular and recent kind of person, the market individual, and slips him in at the start of time as if he were nature itself.

That move has a name, the one this course shares with its companion. Naturalization is making a made, recent arrangement seem like an unchangeable fact of nature. The barter myth naturalizes the trading individual. It takes something historical, the habit of treating exchange as a deal between self-interested strangers, and reads it back into the dawn of the species as the human baseline. Once that baseline is in place, everything built on it looks like the unfolding of human nature rather than the product of particular institutions and a particular history.

The second job follows from the first. If the story starts with lone traders swapping in a market, then the state arrives late, as an outsider. It shows up after money and markets already exist, to tax them, regulate them, sometimes distort them. Money, on this telling, is older than the state and independent of it. It is pre-political. But you have seen the record run the other way (module 05). Coins are late and state-made: minted to pay soldiers, made necessary by taxes payable in coin, with markets partly called into being to provision armies. The myth does not argue against that history. It does something quieter and more effective. It arranges the story so the state never appears in the founding scene at all, so that its later arrival looks like an intrusion onto something that was getting along fine without it. Write the state out of the origin and you make every later claim it has on money look like overreach.

The third job is about debt. A story that begins with even, voluntary swaps between equals, each walking away satisfied, has no room in it for the other history you have seen: debt as a bond between unequal people, debt bondage as a standing threat (module 04, Hammurabi), debt cancelled on purpose when it grew unbearable (module 07, the jubilees). In the barter world, obligation is always settled on the spot. Nobody is ever in anyone’s power. That tidy picture makes a particular intuition feel like simple logic: that a debt is just the unpaid half of a fair swap, and so of course it must always be paid. The course examined that intuition in module 07 and found it contingent, a norm some societies have suspended deliberately, not a law of nature. The myth quietly underwrites the opposite conclusion by never showing you the bondage or the clean slate in the first place.

The fourth job ties the other three to the theory you met in module 06. The barter myth is the origin story the commodity theory of money requires, the view that money is essentially a special tradeable thing, a commodity that grew out of barter to ease exchange. If money is fundamentally a useful good that won out because it was handy, then naturally its birthplace is some clever trader’s market stall, and naturally the state is irrelevant to what money is, and naturally a debt is just deferred barter that has to clear. The myth and the commodity theory hold each other up. The rival, the credit theory or chartalism, which says money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt backed by social and state obligation, needs a completely different origin scene: the ledger, the law code, the tax. The barter myth does not merely accompany the commodity theory. It makes its rival hard to even picture, because it has already filled the founding scene with the wrong furniture.

Put the four together and you can see why the page survives. It is not surviving as a fact. It is surviving as a frame. It seats the self-interested individual at the origin, ushers the state out of the room, makes the duty to pay look like arithmetic, and underwrites one theory of money over another, all of it before the reader has consciously been asked to believe anything. That is a great deal of work for one little fable on page one. Work that useful does not get cut, edition after edition, even when it is wrong.

Primary source

The cleanest evidence that the myth still gets taught is the textbook itself. Paul Samuelson’s Economics, first published in 1948 and the best-selling introductory textbook of the twentieth century, opens its account of money in exactly the conjectural-history voice module 01 described, an origin reasoned out from first principles rather than dug from the ground. What follows paraphrases that framing rather than quoting it, because the wording shifted from edition to edition across the decades the book was in print.

Samuelson’s text presents barter as a clumsy but real early arrangement, an advance on each household having to make everything for itself, and then proposes that if you reconstruct the past along hypothetical, logical lines, an age of barter gives way naturally to an age of commodity money. The phrasing is the tell. The textbook is not reporting what the record shows. It reasons out what should have happened from the logic of convenience and presents that reasoning as the shape of the past. This is conjectural history doing its work in a book that taught introductory economics to many millions of students, the move this course has spent eight modules taking apart.

The reach matters as much as the framing. Edition after edition, through revision after revision over decades, the barter tale opened the treatment of money, and edition after edition it reached a fresh cohort of students worldwide. By the time those editions were teaching it, the case against it was old. A. Mitchell Innes, the early-twentieth-century writer on money, had argued the credit theory in 1913, and the ethnographic record had never turned up a barter economy for the tale to describe. The story did not survive because the evidence backed it. It survived because it framed money the way the textbook wanted money framed.

Check yourself

Pause before reading on. Someone says: “So you are claiming Adam Smith and today’s textbook authors deliberately made up the barter story to serve the powerful.” What has gone wrong in that sentence, in this course’s terms?

Show answer

It has collapsed function into intention. This module analyzes what the myth does, what it makes seem natural, regardless of anyone’s purpose. It does not claim anyone fabricated it. Smith was reasoning carefully, not lying (module 01), and most textbook authors simply repeat a useful teaching device they were never shown the record against. A story can serve interests without anyone choosing it for that reason, the way a custom can hold a group together with no designer. Saying the myth does ideological work is honest analysis. Saying someone plotted it is a different and unsupported claim, and exactly the kind of evidence-free origin story this course teaches you to distrust.

Analysis & interpretation

A careful reader is right to get suspicious of the narrator here, because this is the module where the argument moves furthest from the record. So the line between what the evidence shows and what is being read into it has to stay in plain sight.

The evidence is solid and you have seen it. The textbook still opens with barter: checkable, this year. No barter economy has been found: Caroline Humphrey’s anthropological verdict (module 02). Money-of-account predates coins by millennia: the Mesopotamian record (module 03). Coins are late and state-made: the numismatic and historical record (module 05). None of that is interpretation. It is the ground.

What this module adds is interpretation. “The myth naturalizes the market individual.” “The myth writes the state out of the origin.” “The myth underwrites a politics of debt.” These are readings of what the story does. They are well grounded, they fit the record better than the myth does, and they are the kind of claim that critical scholars from Innes onward have pressed. But they are arguments, not measurements. A careful skeptic can accept every fact in this course and still ask whether the myth’s persistence is really doing all this work, or whether it just survives out of academic inertia, because intro textbooks copy one another and nobody bothers to rewrite chapter one. That is a serious rival reading. Honesty requires putting it on the table.

So weigh them. The inertia reading explains the survival cheaply: textbooks are conservative, the story is easy to teach, change is slow. It is partly right, and it should keep the ideology reading humble. But inertia alone struggles with a particular fact. Inertia preserves things at random, the convenient and the inconvenient alike. It does not explain why this error, of all the simplifications a textbook could keep, is the one that sits unquestioned at the foundation, the one that happens to make a whole picture of money and human nature feel like common sense. When a surviving error is also a load-bearing one, “nobody bothered to fix it” is not the whole story. The function reading does not replace the inertia reading. It explains why inertia met no resistance. The page is easy to keep partly because keeping it costs the prevailing picture nothing and saves it a great deal.

That is as far as the evidence will carry the claim, and not an inch further. We are not measuring anyone’s motives. We are reading a story’s effects and asking which account of its survival fits what we can see. The reading offered here, that the myth survives partly because it does useful work, is the better-supported one. It is still a reading.

All of which lands on the question the course has been circling from the start. Of any origin story, you can ask: whose interests does this story serve? Not “who wrote it as a weapon,” which we have ruled out, but “who is served by its being believed, whoever wrote it and for whatever reason.” Ask it of the barter myth and the answer is not a person or a class. It is a picture. The myth serves anyone, and any argument, that benefits from the trading individual looking natural, from money looking pre-political, and from debts looking like simple arithmetic that must always be paid. You do not need a conspirator for a story to do that. You only need a story that fits a frame people already find comfortable, and that quietly makes the alternatives hard to see.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer here. These are for thinking and writing. The rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Function without a plotter. In your own words, explain how a story can serve particular interests without anyone having designed it to. Give one example from outside economics, a custom, a saying, a piece of folk wisdom, that flatters someone or makes some arrangement look natural, with no author who intended that effect.
  2. The strongest counter. Argue the inertia reading as well as you can: that the barter story survives simply because textbooks copy one another and nobody rewrites chapter one, with no ideological work involved. Then say what the function reading can explain that pure inertia cannot. Where do you actually come down, and why?
  3. Run the question on something else. Pick an origin story you have met outside this course (how a nation, a market, a technology, or an institution “naturally” came to be). Ask of it: who is served by its being believed, and what does it make seem natural that might not be? Keep function and intention separate, the way this module insists.
  4. Where the reading runs out. This module claims the myth naturalizes the market individual and writes the state out of money’s origin. Identify the exact point where that claim stops being a report of evidence and becomes an interpretation. What further evidence, if any, could make you more or less confident in it?
What a strong response does
  • Keeps function and intention rigorously separate. Never slides from “the myth does ideological work” into “someone fabricated it,” and can explain why that slide is both unfair and unsupported.
  • Treats Smith and the textbook authors fairly, as reasoners working from limited evidence or repeating a teaching device, not as liars or schemers (module 01’s steelman still holds here).
  • Keeps the evidence (the textbook still opens with barter; no barter economy found; coins are late) visibly separate from the interpretation (the myth naturalizes the trading individual; it writes the state out), rather than smuggling the reading in as fact.
  • Takes the inertia counter-reading seriously and gives it real force before answering it, rather than knocking down a strawman.
  • Uses the course’s terms precisely: naturalization, the commodity theory versus the credit theory, the unit of account versus the medium of exchange where relevant.
  • Avoids teleology and avoids conspiracy in equal measure: no claim that money was heading anywhere, and no claim that anyone plotted the story.

The story

In 1948 an economist at MIT published a textbook and called it, with no false modesty, Economics. Paul Samuelson was young, brilliant, and about to become the most influential teacher of the subject who ever lived. The book outsold every rival. It went through edition after edition for the rest of the century, was translated into dozens of languages, and put introductory economics in front of many millions of students across the world. For decades, if a person learned what money was from a book, the odds are good the book was this one or one built in its image.

And for decades the book taught money the same way. It reached the subject through barter: people once swapped goods directly, the swapping was clumsy, and money emerged to smooth it. Samuelson was candid that this was reasoning rather than reportage. He framed the sequence as how the past would look if you traced it along hypothetical, logical lines, the age of barter giving way naturally to an age of commodity money. That is conjectural history stated almost in its own name: an origin reasoned out from first principles, not dug from the ground (module 01).

What makes the case sharp is the timing. By 1948 the barter origin was not a fresh hypothesis awaiting test. It had already been challenged in print for thirty-five years. A. Mitchell Innes had laid out the credit theory in 1913, arguing that money begins as a measure of debt rather than as a commodity grown out of trade. The anthropologists had gone looking and come back empty, never finding the barter economy the tale required. The evidence available when Samuelson wrote pointed the other way, and the evidence accumulated against the tale through every edition that followed. The story stayed on the page anyway.

It stayed because it did useful work. The barter opening seats a self-interested trader at the origin of economic life and lets him stand for human nature itself, which is naturalization at its most efficient: a recent and particular kind of person read back into the dawn of the species. It makes money look pre-political, born in private exchange before the state shows up to meddle, which is precisely the origin the commodity theory of money needs and precisely the scene the credit theory cannot use. No one had to plot any of this. A textbook author repeats the opening he was taught, finds it clean and teachable, and passes it on. The story serves a picture of the world without anyone choosing it as a weapon. That is function, not conspiracy, and it is why a tale this convenient gets reprinted whether or not it is true.

One module remains. Having watched the record overturn the myth and asked whose interests its survival serves, the next turns to method: how we know what we know, and the single question worth putting to any origin story you meet again.
