Module 2 of 9

Why It Can't Be the History

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain Caroline Humphrey's finding that no pure barter economy has been ethnographically described.
  • Describe what pre-money communities actually used instead (gift, reciprocity, socially tracked credit).
  • Distinguish genuine barter (between strangers, or when money collapses) from the foundational stage the myth requires.

The hook

The story you were taught (module 01) makes a promise it has never kept. Its shape was simple. First barter, swapping goods directly for goods with no money in between. Then the friction of the double coincidence of wants, the need for each side to want exactly what the other has at the same moment. Then money, invented to end the friction, and after it markets and credit, in that order. It is clean and intuitive, and the last module made the case for it as strongly as it can be made. The story even has a place in the world where it says it happened: long ago, in the moneyless village, the chicken-keeper and the shoemaker.

So go and look. The barter story is a claim about the past. It says there was once a stage of human life that ran on direct swaps and then climbed out of it by inventing money. If that stage was real, it left a world behind, and people who study the actual lives of actual societies should be able to point to it. Anthropologists have been looking, in the field and in the archives, for well over a century, not for an elegant story (the last module granted that it is elegant) but for the barter village itself. When you go looking for it, you do not find it.

Context & background

To see why that is, you need to know who has been looking, and how.

The people who study how living and recently-living societies actually run their economies do ethnography: the close study of how real societies are organized, carried out by observers who live among them, watch what people do, ask them, and write it down. It is unglamorous, patient work. An ethnographer does not deduce from an armchair what a society “must” do. She goes and finds out what it does. Over the last hundred-odd years, ethnographers have described the workings of an enormous range of communities, including many with little or no money in the textbook sense, across every inhabited continent.

This matters because of what kind of claim the barter story is. Last module ended on the distinction the whole course turns on. There is a difference between a thing that is remembered or recorded, attested in some evidence you can point to, and a thing that is required by a theory, believed because the reasoning seems to demand it. The barter story, told as conjectural history, an origin reasoned out from first principles rather than dug up from evidence, lives mostly in the second category. It was deduced, not observed. Ethnography is exactly the kind of evidence that could move it into the first category, if the evidence were there. A field that has catalogued so many moneyless and near-moneyless societies is precisely where the barter economy should turn up, if it ever existed.

The course’s honesty turns on a distinction it never lets blur. There is the evidence, what ethnographers actually found in the field. And there is the interpretation, what that evidence implies about money. The finding that no one has described a barter economy is evidence. The conclusion that money therefore did not come from barter is a reading of that evidence. Strong, but a reading. The two are different acts, and the difference holds all the way through what follows.

Narrative / exposition

Here is what the ethnographers found instead. Not a moneyless world of strangers haggling good-for-good, but something both more ordinary and more interesting: people who already knew each other, keeping track of who owed whom.

Picture a small village where everyone knows everyone. A woman needs a basket, her neighbour makes baskets, and she takes one. No one weighs out a chicken against it on the spot, and no one matches up wants in a single frozen instant. The basket-maker knows that the woman keeps bees, and that honey will come around in season, and that her own roof will need rethatching before winter, and that this woman’s brother is good with a roof. Everyone in the village is carrying, in their head and in the village’s collective memory, a rough running tab of favours given and owed. Nothing is settled today because nothing needs to be. The relationships are long, the memories are good, and the accounts are kept not on clay or paper but in the dense web of who-did-what-for-whom that binds the village together. That is the running tab at a village where everyone knows everyone. People did not barter. They ran tabs.

This is the exact opposite of the barter story’s predicament. The barter story’s whole engine, the double coincidence of wants, only bites if every exchange must close on the spot, fully, then and there. You give me the basket, I must hand you something you want for it right now, or there is no deal. But the running tab dissolves that problem before money ever appears. If I can take the basket now and let the debt stand, then I never need to be holding, at this instant, the precise thing you happen to want. I can settle later, in honey, in labour, in a turn of help when you need it. The “problem money was invented to solve” is not a problem these communities had. They had already solved it, with memory and obligation, long before any coin.

Anthropologists have names for the forms this took. The gift and reciprocity are give-and-take that build and repay a relationship rather than clearing a price: you give, and the giving creates a bond and an expectation, repaid in time and not always in kind. Redistribution is a center, a temple, a chief, a big household, gathering goods in and handing them back out, so the flow runs through the middle rather than directly between two traders. And underneath the gift and redistribution alike runs the thing that matters most for this course: credit and debt, tracked socially. Credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later, debt is what you then owe, and the two are sides of one relationship. The running tab is credit and debt, kept by a community instead of a bank.

So the record does not just fail to show the barter stage. It shows something standing where the barter stage was supposed to be, doing the job the barter stage was supposed to fail at. Within communities, credit came first. That is the positive finding, and it is the spine of everything this course builds next.

Primary source

The sharpest single statement of the negative finding comes from Caroline Humphrey, an anthropologist who set out to examine the barter story against the ethnographic record and reported the verdict in a 1985 essay. The precision of her wording is the whole point. She does not say barter is fictional. She says something narrower and harder to wriggle out of.

“No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described, let alone the emergence from it of money; all available ethnography suggests that there never has been such a thing.”

Caroline Humphrey, “Barter and Economic Disintegration,” Man (1985)

The structure of that sentence does precise work. The claim is not “no one ever bartered.” The claim is that no barter economy has been described, no whole society running on direct swaps as its basic way of getting things, and, the crucial second clause, no case of money emerging from such an economy. The very sequence the textbook teaches, barter society becoming a money society, is the thing the record does not contain. Humphrey is not denying that goods get swapped. She is denying that the swapping was ever the foundation a society stood on, or the soil that money grew out of.

Analysis & interpretation

Evidence and reading part company right here, and this is exactly where a careless reader, or a careless author, ruins the argument.

The evidence is Humphrey’s: no barter economy in the ethnographic record, and no money emerging from one. The interpretation, the reading this course will defend, is that the standard sequence is therefore not history. The barter-to-money story is not something humanity remembers or that the record preserves. It is something a theory needed to be true. (Whether the people who told it ever meant it as literal history, or only as a fable about the logic of money, is the live argument module 01 raised and module 06 will return to. That argument stays open. Either way, the sequence has no foothold in the record.)

But here is the trap, and it is set for you personally. Having heard that the barter economy is a phantom, it is tempting to swing to the far wall and announce: barter never happened, barter is a myth, no one ever swapped a goat for a pot. That is false, and stating it would hand every critic of this course an easy and deserved win. Barter is real. It happens. The precise claim is narrow, and the whole credibility of the argument rides on keeping it narrow. There is no record of a barter-economy-to-money sequence, and no foundational barter stage that societies climbed out of. Barter as such is alive and well. It simply does not appear where the myth puts it.

Where does it appear? In two places, and both are revealing.

The first is between strangers, between people with no ongoing relationship and so no running tab to keep. The village tab works because the people are bound to each other and will meet again. Strip that away, two groups who meet once, who may be rivals or enemies, who share no web of obligation, and there is nothing to track a debt on. So they swap on the spot, good for good, settled in the moment, exactly because they do not trust each other to settle later. Barter, in the record, is overwhelmingly a thing that happens at the edges between communities, not the inside of one. It is what you do with people you cannot run a tab with.

The second place is more striking, because it looks for all the world like the myth coming true. Barter turns up when a money economy collapses. The economist and former prisoner of war R. A. Radford wrote in 1945 a famous account of a wartime POW camp where, with Red Cross rations arriving and no official currency, cigarettes became money. Prices were quoted in cigarettes, a cigarette bought a coffee or a portion of jam, and the camp grew a small economy on a tobacco standard. It is a beautiful little case, and it is constantly misused. People point to it and say: look, money emerging spontaneously from trade, just as the story says. The distinction this course has built reads the same scene the other way. These were not people climbing out of a primitive barter stage. They were modern adults who had grown up with money, dropped into a place with no coin, who promptly reinvented something to do money’s job. The cigarette was a money substitute, reached for by people who already knew exactly what money was for. The same shape appears in post-Soviet Russia in the 1990s, when the ruble economy seized up and firms and households fell back on barter to keep functioning. That is barter after money, in money’s absence, not barter before money on the road to inventing it.

So the over-correction collapses on the same evidence that tempted it. The POW camp and post-Soviet barter are not the barter stage the myth needs. They are its opposite: what happens when a society that has money loses access to it, barter as a fallback reached for when the normal machinery fails. The myth needs barter at the bottom, as the thing that came before everything. The record finds it at the edges and in the wreckage, never at the bottom.

So the honest summary is not “barter is a lie.” It is sharper and harder to dodge. Barter is real, but it is a money substitute or a stranger’s transaction, and the one thing it has never been shown to be is the foundational stage from which money grew. The myth did not get a detail wrong. It put the one form of exchange that happens between and after communities at the origin of community economic life, where the record shows something else standing: the running tab, kept on credit, among people who knew each other.

Check yourself

Pause before reading on. Caroline Humphrey’s verdict is often summarized as “barter never happened.” That summary is wrong. In your own words: what does her finding actually claim, and how is that different from “barter never happened”?

Show answer

Humphrey’s finding is that no barter economy, a whole society running on direct goods-for-goods swaps as its basic system, has ever been described by ethnographers, and that no case of money emerging from such an economy has been described either. The claim is about a missing stage and a missing sequence, not about whether swapping ever occurs. “Barter never happened” is a much stronger and false claim: barter does happen, between strangers and when a money economy collapses. The precise finding leaves room for real barter while still denying the myth its foundational barter-to-money story.

Second check, later in the argument, do this one from memory too. R. A. Radford’s POW-camp cigarette economy is often offered as proof that money arises naturally from trade, just as the barter story says. Why is it actually not an example of the myth’s barter stage?

Show answer

Because the people in the camp were not climbing out of a primitive moneyless stage. They were modern adults who had grown up using money and were suddenly in a place with no official currency, so they reinvented something (cigarettes) to do money’s familiar job. The cigarette was a money substitute, reached for by people who already knew what money was for. It is barter and improvised currency arising after and in the absence of money, not the foundational barter that supposedly existed before money. Same shape: post-Soviet barter in the 1990s, when the ruble economy seized up.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer here. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. State the precise claim without overclaiming. A friend says, “I read that barter is a complete myth and never existed.” In a short paragraph, correct them without swinging to the opposite error. Say exactly what the record shows about barter and exactly what it does not, and explain why the narrow version is the defensible one.
  2. Why credit and not barter? Using the running tab in a village where everyone knows everyone, explain why a small, tightly-bound community would naturally keep track of debts rather than settle every exchange on the spot. What does an ongoing relationship let people do that strangers cannot?
  3. The hardest test case. Take the POW-camp cigarette economy (or post-Soviet barter) and argue both sides: first the case that it looks like the barter story coming true, then the case that it is actually the opposite. Which reading do you find better supported, and what specific feature of the situation settles it for you?
  4. Evidence and interpretation. Find one sentence above that is evidence and one that is interpretation, and explain how you can tell them apart. Why does it matter for a course like this one to keep marking that line?
What a strong response does
  • States the precise claim accurately: no barter economy and no barter-to-money sequence in the record, while granting that barter genuinely occurs between strangers and when money collapses. Does not slide into “barter never happened.”
  • Uses the key terms correctly and keeps them distinct: ethnography, the gift / reciprocity, redistribution, credit / debt, barter as a money substitute. Re-glosses rather than just naming.
  • Attributes the finding to Caroline Humphrey as Humphrey’s, and treats the POW-camp case as R. A. Radford’s account, rather than fusing everything into one anonymous “anthropologists say.”
  • Keeps evidence (what ethnographers found; Humphrey’s verdict; Radford’s camp) visibly separate from interpretation (that the myth’s sequence is therefore not history), and can point to the seam.
  • On the POW-camp prompt, recognizes that the decisive feature is that the participants already knew money, so what they built was a substitute, not a first invention.

The story

Somewhere behind the wire of a German prison camp, an officer reads a price off a notice board. The board lists goods and what they cost, and the cost is in cigarettes. A tin of jam, so many cigarettes. A shave, a slice of someone’s bread ration, a portion of margarine, each carries its number. None of these prices were decreed by anyone. They settled out of thousands of small deals among bored, hungry men with time to trade, and they held steady enough that a prisoner could glance at the board and know, the way a shopper knows the price of milk, what his cigarettes were worth that morning.

The man watching all this was R. A. Radford, an economist in uniform, and after the war he turned what he had seen into one of the most quoted papers in economics, “The Economic Organisation of a P.O.W. Camp,” published in Economica in late 1945. His account is precise because he had nothing to do but observe it. Red Cross parcels came in carrying food, soap, and cigarettes, distributed roughly equally, so men found themselves holding things they did not want and wanting things they did not hold. A non-smoker with a surplus of cigarettes and a craving for chocolate is the double coincidence of wants in miniature, and the prisoners solved it the way money solves it everywhere. One commodity, the cigarette, peeled away from the others and became the thing all prices were quoted in. The camp grew a shop where goods could be left and sold for cigarettes against a posted price. Different huts and different nationalities, cut off from one another, even floated rough exchange rates between their local prices, the way two countries’ currencies trade.

And the cigarette behaved exactly as a currency behaves. When a fresh delivery of parcels flooded the camp with tobacco, prices in cigarettes rose, the camp’s own little inflation, more currency chasing the same scarce food. When deliveries dried up and men burned their savings rather than smoke nothing, the currency literally went up in smoke, the supply shrank, and prices fell. Toward the end, as the parcels stopped and the front drew near, the whole price system shuddered and broke down. Radford had watched a money economy inflate, deflate, and collapse inside a few acres of barracks.

It is irresistible to read this as the barter myth finally caught in the act: trade appears, money emerges from it, just as the textbook swears it happened at the dawn of things. But the camp shows the reverse of what the myth needs. These were not people with no idea of money inventing it from scratch. They were modern adults, an economist among them, who had grown up pricing everything in pounds and shillings and were suddenly dropped into a place with no coin. What they reached for was not a primitive barter stage to be climbed out of. It was a money substitute, a stand-in unit of account improvised within days by people who knew exactly what money was for and only needed something to play its part. Barter flickered at the very start, before the cigarette settled into the job, and then gave way fast, because nobody who has known money will haggle good-for-good for long if they can help it. The same thing happened on a national scale in post-Soviet Russia in the 1990s, when the ruble economy seized up and firms paid each other in goods until something better returned.

That is the shape of it everywhere the record actually shows barter: at the edges, between strangers, or in the wreckage, when money is gone and people scramble to rebuild it. Never at the foundation. The prisoners did not retrace humanity’s climb from barter to money. They showed how quickly people who already have money will reinvent it the moment it is taken away, which is precisely why the moneyless barter village the myth requires has never been found. If credit and account were already there at the start, the next question is what the keeping of those running tabs actually looked like, written down, before there were any coins.
