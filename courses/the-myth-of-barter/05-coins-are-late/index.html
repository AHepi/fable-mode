Module 5 of 9

Coins Are Late: Money as a State Technology

What you'll be able to do
  • State roughly when and where coins first appear, and why the date overturns the myth's sequence.
  • Explain the state-war-tax account of how coinage spread: paying soldiers and taxing them back.
  • Distinguish the solid chronology of coinage from the contested causal claim that states helped call markets into being.

The hook

A coin sits in the palm of a soldier’s hand, small, heavy for its size, stamped on one face with the mark of the authority that made it. He did not get it by trading a chicken. He got it as pay, for marching where he was told and fighting whom he was ordered to fight, and the mark on its face is the reason it will be accepted at the next town, because that same authority will demand it back from the townspeople when the tax falls due. That single object holds almost the whole argument that follows, and almost everything the barter myth says about money runs backward through it.

Here is the thing that should stop you. By the time the first coin like that one was ever struck, money was already thousands of years old.

Context & background

You have spent the last two modules in a world without coins. In module 03 you watched Mesopotamian scribes price goods and record debts in silver and barley around 3000 BCE, keeping a money-of-account, prices and debts reckoned in a standard measure with no coin circulating, on clay tablets that were really receipts. In module 04 you saw that debt by then was governed law: the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE, regulated interest and the debt bondage that forced people into servitude to work off a debt they could not pay, more than a thousand years before anyone struck a coin. Money as a measure, debt as an institution, law to govern it, all of it already standing.

Now place the first coins on the same timeline. Coinage, stamped metal money issued by an authority, first appears around the seventh century BCE, in Lydia (a kingdom in what is now western Turkey) and the neighbouring Ionian Greek cities on the Aegean coast. The earliest were lumps of electrum, a natural alloy of gold and silver, stamped with a design so that their weight and metal need not be checked at every exchange. The date is approximate, and the precision hardly matters next to its distance from everything before it. The clay receipts of module 03 are older than the first coins by roughly two and a half thousand years. That is not a footnote. That is most of recorded history sitting between money and the coin.

So the iconic object, the thing that feels most like money, the disc you can bite and hear ring, turns out to be a latecomer. Money did not begin as a coin and grow into a ledger. It began as a ledger, and coins were added, much later, to a world that already had money. So if coins were not there at the beginning, what were they actually for, and how did they spread?

Narrative / exposition

The textbook teaches a sequence, the barter myth from module 01: people barter, money is invented to ease the swapping, then markets and credit grow on top. In that story coins are money’s natural climax. Barter is clumsy, a common medium of exchange smooths it, the handiest medium wins out, and the winner gets stamped into coins. The coin is where the long convenience-seeking story finally crystallizes. On that account coinage should arrive early, near the dawn of trade, as the obvious fix maturing into its final form.

The record runs the other way. Coins arrive late, suddenly, and in a very particular company. They show up tangled with three things at once: states, war, and taxation. To see why, follow the coin in the soldier’s hand from the moment it is struck.

Start with the army. A state at war needs to feed, equip, and pay a large body of armed men, often far from home, often men with no prior bond to the place they are standing in. Paying them in promises is hard when they may not live to collect, and paying them in a running tab is impossible when they are strangers passing through. What the state needs is something portable, durable, hard to fake, and accepted by anyone the soldier meets on the march. Stamped metal of guaranteed weight does exactly that job. Coinage standardizes pay. One coin is as good as another of the same kind, so the paymaster can count out wages by the piece and the soldier can spend them anywhere the stamp is known.

But why would a farmer two valleys over hand real bread and wine to a passing soldier for a disc of metal he cannot eat? This is the move that makes the whole machine turn, and it is where the state reappears. The same authority that mints the coin to pay its soldiers also levies a tax, and it demands that the tax be paid in that coin, and in nothing else. Now the farmer has no choice. To settle with the tax collector he must get his hands on the official coin, and the readiest way to get it is to sell something, his grain, his labour, his wine, to someone who has it. Who has it? The soldiers, freshly paid. The tax does not merely raise money for the state. It forces an entire population to chase the state’s coin, which means it forces them to produce and sell things in order to earn it.

Out of that comes a market around the army, almost on demand: people bringing goods to sell to the troops, because selling to the troops is how they obtain the coin the tax requires. The state has set up a loop. It pays out coin at one end (wages), pulls coin back in at the other (taxes), and in the gap between the two it has conjured a swarm of buyers and sellers who never had to want each other’s goods at all. The coin in the soldier’s hand is the engine of that loop. It is not the climax of barter. It is a tool a state uses to provision an army by turning its own subjects into a market.

That is why this module calls coinage a state technology: a device deliberately deployed by a ruling power to do a job, the way a road or a standing army or a written law code is. The phrase is precise, not poetic. A technology is something made and used for a purpose. Coinage was made and used to pay soldiers and to be taxed back, and the markets that grew around it grew partly because the state needed them to.

Primary source

The chronology and its company are best stated by a specialist. The numismatist Philip Grierson, a leading scholar of coins and what they reveal, who argued throughout his work that money begins not as a commodity but as an abstract measure, set out the late and deliberate character of coinage. The lines below paraphrase his account of where coins begin rather than quote it, because the exact wording varies between printings and is not worth faking; the substance is his.

On Grierson’s account, coinage is essentially a creation of the Greek world, and the earliest coins we know are those of Lydia. There, electrum, the natural alloy of gold and silver, was minted into small pieces of fixed weight and stamped with a device that marked their origin and guaranteed their value.

Philip Grierson, The Origins of Money (1977)

What the account gives you, firmly, is the where and the what: Lydia, electrum, fixed weight, an official stamp. And the stamp does not merely identify the piece; it guarantees it. The value the stamp vouches for is the value an authority stands behind. Even at the very birth of coinage, the coin already points back at the power that made it, not out at a barter market that made it necessary. That a state’s stamp drove coinage to spread through war and taxes is a further claim, argued in the next section. The account on its own fixes only the date and the maker, and that alone is enough to overturn the myth’s order.

The first coins appear around the seventh century BCE, in Lydia and the neighbouring Ionian Greek cities, made of stamped electrum (a gold-silver alloy). The date is a problem for the barter myth because money-of-account and recorded debt go back to roughly 3000 BCE in Mesopotamia (modules 03 and 04), so coins arrive thousands of years after money already existed. The myth puts coins at the natural end of a barter-to-money story; the record puts them millennia too late to have begun money at all. Coins were added to a world that already had money, not the seed it grew from.

Analysis & interpretation

What is solid here and what is argued sit very close together, and the course’s credibility depends on telling them apart.

What is solid is the chronology and the company coinage keeps. Coins are late (around the seventh century BCE, against money-of-account at around 3000 BCE). That is firm. That early coinage and the paying of soldiers and the taxing of populations turn up together, in Lydia, in the Greek world, and soon after in the empires that minted on a large scale, is also well established. Coins, soldiers, taxes: that trio is evidence. You can point to mints, to military pay, to tax demands in coin. No interpretation required.

What is interpretation is the strong causal claim built on top of that trio. The historian David Graeber, an anthropologist whose Debt: The First 5,000 Years (2011) is an influential but contested synthesis, drew on Grierson and others to argue for what he called a military-coinage complex: the claim that coinage spread because states needed to pay armies and tax them back, a self-reinforcing engine of war, mints, and markets, with mass slavery often feeding the same machine. That is a powerful reading, and it fits the evidence well. Its shape, though, is worth pausing on. “Coins, soldiers, and taxes appear together” is a fact. “Therefore war and taxation are why coinage spread” is a story told about the fact, an attribution of cause. Grierson supplies the careful numismatics; Graeber builds the bold causal arc on top of them. The boldness of the second is not the firmness of the first, and the two belong to the people who made them.

A deeper inversion follows from the same reading, and it carries the same load of interpretation. The barter myth assumes markets came first, growing out of people trucking and bartering, with money arriving later to lubricate them and the state arriving later still to regulate what markets had already built. The state-war-tax account turns that around. States did not stumble upon ready-made markets. By minting coin and demanding it back in tax, states helped call markets into being, at least in part and at least around their armies. That is a striking claim and a well-grounded one, but it remains a claim, an interpretation of the chronology, not a fact you can dig out of the ground. What the evidence forces is narrower and still devastating to the myth: money is older than coins, and coins are bound up with state power from their first appearance. Whatever else money is, it is not the apolitical, pre-state convenience the textbook describes. This is where “money is apolitical” dies.

One temptation to refuse, because it is the very habit this course teaches you to distrust. It is easy to read this module as a tidy upgrade story in reverse: first ledgers, then coins, each a step toward the cash in your pocket, money “maturing” toward its modern form. Resist it. There was nothing inevitable about coinage. Mesopotamia ran a sophisticated money-of-account for two and a half thousand years without ever needing to mint a coin. Coins were one contingent invention, in one corner of the world, for a particular purpose, that happened to spread. They were not money growing up. They were money being put to a new use by states that found them handy. The myth’s deep error is the assumption that money was always heading somewhere. It was not.

  1. The loop, in your own words. Explain the state-war-tax account of coinage as if to a friend who thinks coins were invented to make shopping easier.   3. Why the date stings. A reader who finished module 01 found the barter-to-coins story obvious. What did those modules put in place that makes the date land as a shock rather than a detail?
  4. Apolitical money. The textbook treats money as a neutral tool that exists before and apart from the state. Using only what this module establishes (not the strong causal reading), state the narrowest claim the evidence actually forces about money and political power, and then say what the bolder reading (states calling markets into being) adds and why it is more contestable.
What a strong response does
  • Gets the chronology right and rough: coins around the seventh century BCE in Lydia/Ionia, against money-of-account at around 3000 BCE, so coins are millennia late. No invented precise dates.
  • Reconstructs the state-war-tax loop accurately: mint coin to pay soldiers, demand it back in tax, and the population must sell goods or labour to obtain the coin, seeding a market (often around the army). Names the coin in the soldier’s hand as the engine, not the climax of barter.
  • Keeps evidence and interpretation visibly separate. Treats “coins, soldiers, and taxes appear together” as solid, and “war and taxation drove coinage to spread” / “states called markets into being” as interpretation. Attributes the careful chronology to Grierson and the bold military-coinage complex causal arc to Graeber, without fusing them.
  • Resists teleology: does not narrate coinage as money “maturing.” Notes that Mesopotamia ran money-of-account for millennia with no coin, so coinage was contingent, not inevitable.
  • Draws the defensible conclusion (money predates coins; coins are entangled with state power from the start; money is not apolitical) without overclaiming the contested causal story as settled fact.

The story

A river ran through the Lydian capital, and the river ran with gold.

Lydia sat in western Anatolia, in what is now Turkey, and through its capital, Sardis, flowed a small river called the Pactolus. Down that river, washed out of the hills, came grains of electrum, the pale natural alloy of gold and silver. The Lydians did not have to dig for their wealth; the water brought it to them. Out of that metal, somewhere around the seventh century BCE, the kingdom and its Greek-speaking neighbours on the Aegean coast struck the lumps of stamped electrum that count among the earliest coins anyone has found. The stamp was the point. A lump of electrum varies in its mix of gold and silver, so its worth is hard to judge by eye. A mark pressed into it by an authority said, in effect, this much, guaranteed, take it and move on. The coin was a sovereign’s promise pressed into metal before it was anything else.

Lydia’s last king was Croesus, who reigned in round numbers from about 560 to 546 BCE, and whose name still rides in the phrase “rich as Croesus.” The wealth was real, and the river was part of it, but so was the mint. Croesus is the king associated with the step from those uneven electrum lumps to coins of refined gold and silver, struck to a standard, their weight and purity vouched for by the state that issued them. Here is the order that matters. By the time Croesus was guaranteeing the purity of his gold, money-of-account and governed debt were already old. The Mesopotamian scribes pricing goods in silver, the Code of Hammurabi capping interest and fencing in debt bondage: that world ran for two thousand years and more before a Lydian king ever thought to stamp a coin. The coin did not start money. It joined a story long under way, and it joined it from the top, as an instrument of a treasury and a throne.

What the coin was good for shows in what happened next. Coined money spread fastest where states had armies to pay and taxes to collect, the same loop the module traced: pay the soldier in stamped metal, demand the metal back from everyone else, and a market gathers around the demand. Croesus himself ran into the limits of gold against power. Around 546 BCE he lost his kingdom to Cyrus the Great of Persia, and Lydia became a province of an empire that would mint and tax on a scale he never commanded. The man proverbial for riches could not buy off the end of his reign. The legends Herodotus later told about him, the warnings he ignored, the pyre he was said to have faced, are stories told long after the fact and best left as stories. The hard part stands without them. The first coins were a tool of kings, struck from a river’s gold by a state that wanted its value vouched for, and they came late to a world that had counted and owed and governed debt for millennia without them.
