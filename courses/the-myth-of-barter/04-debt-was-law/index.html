Module 4 of 9

Debt Was Law Before Coins Were Minted

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain what the Code of Hammurabi shows about debt as a governed legal institution.
  • Explain debt bondage and why it marks the credit-first past as no golden age.
  • Distinguish the evidence (the code regulates interest and debt) from the interpretation (what that implies about money).

The hook

A Babylonian king, nearly four thousand years ago, settled the matter of what counted as serious by carving it in stone. Not a private understanding, not a custom that bends with the weather, but a fixed and public rule everyone could be held to: his laws cut into a tall pillar of black stone, set up where people passed, the same for the strong and the weak, outlasting the king who made them.

No one wastes good stone on trivia. What gets carved is what a society cannot afford to leave vague, and a large part of what this stone actually says turns out to be about debt: who may charge what for a loan, what happens when a debtor cannot pay, how long a person handed over against a debt may be held before they must go free. Debt, in other words, was not a soft favour between neighbours in the ancient world. It was hard law, written in stone, governed in detail, a thousand years before the first coin was ever struck.

Context & background

The last module left you somewhere most people never expect to stand. Long before coins, the cities of Mesopotamia, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates in what is now Iraq, were already running on debt and credit: debt is what you owe, credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later, the two sides of one relationship. Scribes priced goods and recorded who owed whom in standard measures of silver and barley, with no coin circulating at all. That is the picture from module 03: a clay tablet that is really a receipt, money working as a measuring-stick before it was ever a thing in the hand.

So debt came first. The obvious next question, and the one this module answers, is: how seriously was it taken? You might guess that without coins, without banks, without a money economy as we know it, debt was a loose and neighbourly thing, a running tab settled by goodwill. That guess is wrong, and the evidence that it is wrong is one of the most famous documents to survive from the ancient world.

That document is the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE that regulates interest, debt, and bondage. Its author, Hammurabi, was a Babylonian king of that era who unified much of Mesopotamia under his rule. The code was inscribed on stone, and a great stele bearing it survives, carved with provision after provision in the wedge-shaped script the Mesopotamians pressed into clay and stone, the script called cuneiform. It is not the first law code from the region, and it was not the only one. But it is full, it is detailed, and it survives, which makes it a window into how an early state thought about the obligations between its people.

One term sits at the dark heart of this module. Debt bondage is being forced into servitude to work off a debt you cannot pay. Not slavery from birth, not capture in war, but bondage that begins with an ordinary loan and an ordinary failure to repay it. A man borrows against the harvest, the harvest fails, and the lender takes what the man has left to give: his labour, or worse, the labour of his wife, his son, his daughter. That is the thing the code is forced to manage, and it is the standing reason not to romanticize the world that credit built.

Narrative / exposition

To say debt was “governed” is to say a society had decided that the relationship between a lender and a borrower was too consequential to be left to the two parties alone, and stepped in to set the terms.

Consider what governing debt actually requires. First, a rule about the price of a loan, the interest, the extra a borrower repays on top of what was lent. If there is no limit, a desperate borrower will agree to almost anything, and a lender can write a loan that can never be repaid. So a state that wants debt not to swallow its people sets a ceiling. The Code of Hammurabi does this. It distinguishes loans of silver from loans of grain and caps what may be charged on each. We will not put a number on the ceiling here, because the exact rates and their reading are a matter for specialists and the translations vary. What matters for our purpose is the bare fact, and the bare fact is striking enough: nearly four thousand years ago, a government was already in the business of telling lenders how much they could charge.

Second, governing debt means rules about what a creditor may seize, and from whom, and for how long. This is where the code turns to debt bondage, and where its provisions become, by turns, harsh and humane in the same breath. The code takes for granted that a person can be handed over against a debt. That is the harshness, and we should not look away from it: a defaulting debtor’s dependants could be given up to work off what was owed. But the code also limits the practice. It sets bounds on how long such a person may be held, and on what may and may not be done to them. The state, in other words, accepts that debt bondage exists and then tries to fence it in.

That double move, accept the institution, then cage it, tells you something precise about the ancient world. Debt bondage was common enough, and dangerous enough, that a king found it worth carving limits on it into stone. You do not legislate against a thing that never happens. The very existence of these provisions is evidence that loans went bad, that people lost their freedom over them, and that the loss was a standing feature of life, not a rare catastrophe.

There is one more thing the existence of this code quietly establishes, and it is the spine of the whole course. None of this needed coins. The cap on interest, the rules on seizure, the limit on bondage: all of it governs debts reckoned in silver and grain, in a world with no coined money at all. Debt was a fully developed legal institution, complete with a price control and a labour regime, while money was still a number in a ledger rather than a disc in a purse. Coins, when they finally arrive in module 05, will be late to a story that was already well under way.

Primary source

Here is a provision of the Code of Hammurabi on debt servitude. It is one of the code’s most frequently translated and discussed clauses, and it concerns exactly the limit we have been circling: how long a member of a debtor’s household may be held before the debt is treated as discharged and the person set free. The wording below follows the sense of the standard translations of this provision; where translators differ on a phrase, I have kept to what they agree on, and I have not attached a clause number, since numbering varies between editions.

If a man has a debt against him and sells or hands over his wife, his son, or his daughter for silver, or gives them into service to work off the debt, they shall work in the house of their buyer or holder for three years; in the fourth year their freedom shall be restored.

The Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE), a provision on debt servitude, paraphrased

A great deal is packed into those few lines. The first thing is an assumption the provision never argues for: a man’s debt can reach past the man himself and fall on his wife and children, who can be handed over, “for silver” or into service, to pay what he owes. It is treated as an ordinary fact of the world, the background against which the rule is written. That single assumption is the anti-romantic core of the module.

Against that background, the rule itself does something narrower. It sets a limit: three years of service, then, in the fourth year, freedom. The debt is worked off and the person released. This is not nothing. It is a state stepping between a creditor and the people in his power to declare that the claim has an end. A debt may take your labour, but not your life.

So the same short passage carries both faces of debt-as-law at once. The harsh face: people were pledged and seized for the debts of their household. The restraining face: the law fenced in how far that could go. Neither alone is the truth of the ancient world; the truth is the tension between them, carved into one stone.

Show answer

The harsh thing: it takes for granted that a debtor’s wife, son, or daughter can be handed over or sold into service to work off a debt. Debt could cost a household its freedom. The provision both accepts debt bondage and limits it.

Analysis & interpretation

What the evidence shows and what we choose to make of it are not the same thing, and a careful reader keeps them apart. The stone is one place that distinction earns its keep.

Start with the evidence, stated as plainly as it can be. A Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE regulates interest on loans, governs the seizure of people for debt, and limits how long debt bondage may last. That is solid. It rests on a surviving document that scholars have read for well over a century. We can point to it. It is, in the language of module 01, remembered, attested in a record we can hold up, not merely reasoned out from what seems likely.

Now the interpretation, which is a separate act. What does that evidence imply about money? Here we are no longer reading the stone; we are arguing about what it means. One reading, and it is the reading that fits this course’s spine, runs like this: if debt was already a central, legally governed institution before coined money existed, then money cannot have begun as a handy commodity that emerged from barter to grease trade. The thing being governed, debt, was there first and mattered enormously, while coins were nowhere in sight. On this view, the code is one more piece of evidence that money is, at bottom, a matter of obligation and account rather than of metal in the hand. That reading has a name we have already met in passing, the credit theory of money, the idea that money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt rather than a special tradeable commodity. We will weigh it properly against its rival in module 06. For now, mark it clearly as an interpretation, a way of reading the evidence, not a fact the stone states.

There is a second interpretive trap here, and it is the one this module was built to spring shut. It is the temptation to read “credit came first” as “the past was kinder.” It is an easy slide. We have spent two modules dismantling a story in which money and markets are the heroes, and it would feel tidy if the world before coins turned out to be gentler, more human, more bound by trust than by cash. Resist that completely. The same record that shows debt coming first shows debt bondage coming with it. People were pledged and seized for what they owed. A bad harvest could cost a family its freedom. The code’s three-year limit is not evidence of a soft world; it is evidence of a world harsh enough that such a limit was worth carving in stone. The point of this history was never that the past was better. The point is that it was different, that money and debt did not arrive in the order or the spirit the myth claims. Contingency, not nostalgia. The credit-first past was not an Eden, and module 07, when it returns to debt’s morality, will lean on exactly this counterweight.

Show answer

The first is evidence: it describes what a surviving document actually does, something we can point to and check, a thing remembered in a record. The second is interpretation: it is an argument about what that evidence means for the nature of money, and a contested one (it is the credit theory’s reading, to be weighed against the commodity theory in module 06). The tell is that the first can be confirmed by reading the document, while the second requires you to reason beyond it, choosing one explanation over another the same facts might support.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Read the limit two ways. The provision caps debt servitude at three years. One reader calls this evidence of a relatively humane state protecting the vulnerable; another calls it evidence of how brutal debt bondage must have been, since it needed limiting at all. Argue for the reading you find better supported, quoting the words of the provision, and say what the other reading gets right.

  2. Mind the seam. Take the bare fact that the code regulates interest on loans. Write one sentence that stays strictly within the evidence, and one sentence that draws an interpretation from it. Then explain, in a line or two, exactly what makes the second sentence an interpretation rather than a fact.

  3. Against nostalgia. This module insists that “credit came first” does not mean “the past was kinder.” Using the primary source, make the case for that insistence in your own words. What in the provision itself blocks the romantic reading?

  4. Why carve it? A society inscribes in stone the things it cannot afford to leave vague. Given that debt and bondage take up so much of this code, what does that tell you about how common and how dangerous debt was in Hammurabi’s Babylon? Build your answer from the fact that the rules exist at all.

What a strong response does
  • Stakes a clear, arguable claim rather than summarizing the source, and grounds it in the specific words of the provision (the seizure of wife or children, the three-year term, the restored freedom), not the gist.
  • Keeps evidence and interpretation visibly separate: states what the code demonstrably does before arguing what it implies, and flags the second as a claim open to dispute.
  • Treats the credit-theory reading as an interpretation attributed to that theory and reserved for module 06, not as a settled fact the stone announces.
  • Refuses the romantic reading: acknowledges the debt bondage the source takes for granted, and does not turn the credit-first past into a golden age.
  • Stays inside the evidence: makes no claim about exact interest rates, clause numbers, or wording that the source as given here cannot bear.

The story

The stone still exists. You can stand in front of it.

It is a pillar of black diorite a little over seven feet tall, narrower at the crown than the base, and it stands today in the Louvre under museum light, polished by close to four thousand years. Near the top, carved in relief, are two figures: a seated god and a standing king. The god is Shamash, the sun, whom the Babylonians held to be the god of justice, with flames rising from his shoulders. He is handing the king a rod and a ring, the tools of a measurer and a builder, the authority to set things straight. The king is Hammurabi. Below the carving, in dense columns of cuneiform running down all four faces, are his laws: close to three hundred of them, and a great many about debt.

The stele was not made for a museum. It was raised in a temple or public square in Babylon around 1750 BCE, where people could see it and scribes could consult it, a king’s justice put permanently on show. Its epilogue says plainly why such a thing should exist: so that the strong might not oppress the weak, and the orphan and the widow get their due. Set against the debt provisions, that is not pious decoration. The three-year cap on debt servitude, the ceiling on interest, the rules on who could be seized are the actual machinery of “the strong shall not oppress the weak,” in a society where the most ordinary way for the strong to swallow the weak was a loan that could not be repaid.

How the stone reached Paris is its own lesson in how any of this is known. Around 1158 BCE, six centuries after Hammurabi, an Elamite king named Shutruk-Nahhunte raided Babylonia and hauled the stele home as plunder to his capital at Susa, in what is now southwestern Iran. He scraped one patch of the inscription smooth, apparently to carve his own name over the Babylonian’s, and never did. There it stayed, buried, until a French expedition dug it out of the ground at Susa in the winter of 1901. The record of how Babylon governed debt survived not because anyone meant to preserve it, but because a looter carried it a thousand years too far away to be lost, and the dirt kept it. The rule that a debt must have a limit outlasted Hammurabi, outlasted Babylon, and outlasted the empire that stole it. It was already old when the first coin was struck.
