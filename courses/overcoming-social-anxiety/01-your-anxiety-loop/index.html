Your Anxiety Loop

What you'll be able to do
  • Name the four parts of the anxiety loop (inward attention, safety behaviors, catastrophic predictions, the post-event replay).
  • Spot your own self-focused attention and one of your own safety behaviors in a real interaction.
  • State plainly what this course is and is not, and when to seek a professional.

You said one sentence in the meeting. You’d rehearsed it four times in your head before it left your mouth, and the whole time your colleague was talking you were busy editing your version instead of hearing hers. You gripped your pen. You kept your eyes on your notepad. When you finally spoke, your voice came out faster and flatter than you wanted, and you spent the rest of the hour replaying it, certain you’d come across as odd. You walked out drained, with a verdict already filed: that went badly.

Here’s the strange part. You have almost no evidence for that verdict. You weren’t watching the room. You were watching yourself. And the watching is the thing that’s keeping you stuck.

That feeling is workable, and it’s the whole subject of this course. Before we go any further, a few honest words about what this is.

This is a skills course, built on solid evidence, and it works by practice. It is not therapy, not diagnosis, and not a substitute for care from a qualified professional. Everyday social nerves are common and very workable with the skills you’ll build here. But social anxiety also runs deeper for some people. When the fear is persistent, out of proportion to the situation, and gets in the way of your work, your relationships, or your daily life, that’s worth more than a self-help course. Seeing a doctor or a therapist for it is a smart, ordinary move, not a failure. Ask for CBT, which has strong evidence behind it for exactly this. And if you ever have thoughts of harming yourself, treat that as urgent and reach out to a professional or your local emergency help right away.

A quick gut-check on when this deserves more than a course: the fear has lasted months and isn’t shifting; it’s holding back parts of your life you care about; the distress is intense; you’re shrinking your world to dodge it; or it comes packaged with low mood, panic attacks, or leaning on alcohol or drugs to get through social things. Any of those, and the move is to find real support where you live. A GP or primary-care doctor is a fine first step. A licensed therapist or psychologist is another. Your country will have a recognized register you can search, and a recognized crisis line for emergencies. None of this is the failure case. It’s just the grown-up version of seeing the right specialist.

With that on the table, let’s look at the machine.

The loop that feeds itself

Researchers who studied social anxiety closely found that it isn’t one big thing. It’s four smaller things wired together in a circle, where each one makes the next more likely. They built a model of it decades ago, and it has held up as the best explanation we have. You don’t need the model in any detail. You need to recognize its four moving parts when they show up in you, because every later module in this course is a tool for cutting one of these wires.

Call the whole thing the anxiety loop. Here are its four links.

The inward spotlight. The moment you sense a social situation might judge you, your attention swings off the room and onto yourself. You start monitoring your own heartbeat, your warm face, the wobble you think is in your voice, a mental picture of how you must look from the outside. Psychologists call this self-focused attention. The trouble is twofold: you treat those inner sensations as hard evidence (“I feel shaky, so I must look incompetent”), and while you’re busy watching yourself, you stop actually taking in the other person. The real, often neutral or friendly faces in front of you go unread.

Crutches. These are the small protective moves you make to keep the feared disaster from happening. The technical name is safety behaviors, and once you know to look, you’ll see your own everywhere: over-rehearsing a line before you say it, gripping a cup or a pen so your hands don’t show, avoiding eye contact, going quiet, having an exit planned, mentally scripting the next thing. We’ll call them crutches. They feel like the very thing keeping you upright. They’re not. They keep the fear alive without ever announcing it, and we’ll come back to why in a moment.

The prediction. Underneath it all sits a specific feared forecast: “If I speak up, I’ll freeze and everyone will think I’m stupid.” A catastrophic prediction, in the jargon. It arrives feeling like a fact rather than a guess, which is exactly the problem, because a guess can be tested and a fact just sits there running the show.

The autopsy. Afterward comes the replay. You lie awake picking over every clumsy second, cataloguing what you got wrong, rehearsing the verdict. Researchers call this post-event processing. We’ll call it the autopsy, because that’s what it feels like: standing over the body of a conversation, looking only for cause of death. It doesn’t sift the evidence fairly. It hunts for failure and files it away as memory, so next time the prediction feels even more certain.

Now watch how they connect, because this is the engine. The prediction (“I’ll come across as weird”) makes you turn the inward spotlight on, scanning yourself for the first sign of weirdness. The inward spotlight makes you reach for crutches, because if you might fall apart, you’d better grip something. The crutches mean you never find out the disaster wasn’t coming, so when you run the autopsy, you “confirm” the prediction. And a confirmed prediction walks straight into the next conversation, more certain than before. Round and round. This is why it’s called a spiral and not just a bad mood. Nothing breaks it from the inside, because every link is busy reinforcing the next.

There’s one more thing the crutches do that’s worth sitting with. Imagine you get through a coffee chat by gripping your mug white-knuckle tight and rehearsing every sentence. You survive it. What does your mind conclude? Not “nothing bad was ever going to happen.” It concludes “I survived because I held the mug and rehearsed.” The crutch steals the credit. It blocks the one lesson that would actually shrink the fear: that you’d have been fine without it. That single fact is the hinge this entire course turns on.

Before you read on: name the four links of the loop from memory.

The inward spotlight (your attention turns onto yourself), crutches (the protective habits like rehearsing or gripping something), the prediction (the specific feared forecast), and the autopsy (the replay afterward). Each one makes the next more likely, which is why it’s a self-feeding loop.

Back to the meeting

Now run the opening scene through the loop, link by link, and watch it light up.

It starts before you even speak, with the prediction: “If I talk, my voice will shake and I’ll sound stupid.” That forecast flips on the inward spotlight. So while your colleague is talking, you’re not hearing her. You’re monitoring your own throat, editing your sentence, picturing how you’ll look. That self-focus reaches straight for the crutches: you rehearse the line four times, grip the pen, lock your eyes on the notepad, say as little as possible. You get through it. But because you got through it gripping and rehearsing, you never learn the room wasn’t out to get you. So on the walk back, the autopsy opens. You replay the flat voice, you skip past the fact that two people nodded, and you file the verdict: weird, badly done. That verdict is just the prediction wearing fresh paint, and it’ll be waiting at the next meeting, louder.

Notice what’s missing from that whole sequence: any actual information about what the other people thought. You never collected any. You were inside the loop the entire time. That’s not a character flaw. That’s the machine doing exactly what it does. And a machine is a thing you can learn to take apart.

Check yourself: in the meeting scene, which link is the gripped pen and the rehearsed line?

Those are crutches (safety behaviors). They feel protective, but because you “got through it” while using them, they rob you of the chance to learn you’d have been fine without them. Spotting your own crutches is half of this module’s drill.

Field drill: watch one loop in the wild

Here’s your work for this week. It’s pure observation. You’re not going to change anything yet, fix anything, or be brave about anything. You’re just going to watch the machine run once, in you, in real life.

The drill: pick one real interaction this week, something ordinary like a chat with a coworker, a checkout, a phone call. During it or right after, notice two things. First, which way your attention pointed the whole time: at yourself (your voice, your face, how you were coming across) or at them (their actual words, their face, the thing you were both talking about). Second, name one crutch you used: one rehearsed line, one gripped object, one avoided glance, one thing left unsaid.

That’s it. Two observations. No grade, no bravery required. You’re learning to see the loop from the inside, which is the thing you’ll need before any of the later tools can land.

A heads-up so the rest of the course doesn’t surprise you. This drill is gentle on purpose, because this week is about noticing. The drills coming later won’t be. They’ll ask you to go do something real with real people and let it be a little uncomfortable, on purpose, because that turns out to be where the change actually lives. For now, just watch.

After your interaction, run through this (self-scored, no right answer)
  • Which way did your attention point most of the time, at yourself or at them? Be honest; both are normal.
  • Catch yourself treating an inner sensation (a warm face, a fast heart) as proof of how you looked? That’s the inward spotlight at work.
  • Name one crutch you used. What were you afraid would happen if you dropped it?
  • Did the autopsy start afterward? What did the replay focus on, the stumbles or the whole picture?

A good rep here isn’t a calm interaction. It’s one where you managed to watch the loop instead of only being swept along by it.

A question for you

Think back to the last social thing you walked away from feeling you’d botched. Replay it once, but this time ask a different question: where was your attention pointed while it was happening? On the room, or on yourself? You may find that the “evidence” for how badly it went was never collected, because you were too busy watching the wrong thing.

Recap

Social anxiety isn’t one overwhelming fog. It’s a loop of four linked parts, the inward spotlight, your crutches, the prediction, and the autopsy, each one feeding the next until the whole thing spins on its own. You can now name those parts, which means you can start to catch them in yourself. That’s the foundation. Everything else in this course is a way of reaching in and cutting one of those wires, one at a time, until the loop has nothing left to run on.
