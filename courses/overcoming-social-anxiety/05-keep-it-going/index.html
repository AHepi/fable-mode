Module 5 of 12

Keep It Going

What you'll be able to do
  • Thread a conversation by asking a follow-up question off the other person's "free information."
  • Use reciprocal self-disclosure to deepen a conversation without dominating it.
  • Explain why question-asking and matched disclosure increase how much people like you.

The part everybody dreads is the easy part

Ask people what scares them about talking to a stranger and most of them skip the opening. The opening they can manage. What they dread is the second after the opening, the silence that opens up when the small talk runs out and it’s on you to say something good. So they spend the whole conversation working a problem in the back of their head: what do I say next, what do I say next. The wittier line, the better topic, the thing that will make them seem worth talking to.

That whole project is a mistake, and it’s an exhausting one. The conversation isn’t waiting for you to produce something brilliant out of thin air. The other person is handing you material the entire time. Your job isn’t to invent. It’s to catch what they’ve already given you and pull on it.

What a conversation actually runs on

Back in module 4 you met free information, the stuff a person hands you without being asked. They don’t just answer “where are you from,” they say “originally Lisbon, but I came here for a job that fell through in the first month.” That second half is free information, and it’s a fistful of threads: Lisbon, the move, a job, a job that fell through. Any one of them is a place to go next.

Threading a conversation comes down to three small moves, and none of them require you to be interesting.

Catch one detail. While they’re talking, you’re listening for the one thing that has some life in it. Not all of it, just one hold worth grabbing. This is the part anxiety wrecks, so it’s worth being honest about why. When you’re busy rehearsing your next line, you literally can’t hear the threads, because your attention is pointed at you instead of at them. This is the same spotlight-outward move from module 3, just aimed at a person rather than a room. Listening is not a separate skill from external focus. Listening is external focus. The thing you trained to lower your own anxiety is the same thing that makes you a good conversationalist. One move, two payoffs.

Ask an open follow-up. Take the detail you caught and ask about it, in a way they can’t answer with one word. Not “so the job fell through?” (which lands with a thud and a yes) but “what happened with the job?” or “so what did you end up doing instead?” Open questions start with what, how, why, or “tell me about.” They hand the floor back. And a follow-up specifically, one that grows out of what they just said, does something a fresh topic can’t: it proves you were listening.

Offer a little back. After they answer, give a small piece of yourself, matched to what they shared. They tell you the move went sideways, you say “I did the same thing in my twenties, packed up for a city where I knew exactly one person.” This is offering something back, which has a clinical name, reciprocal self-disclosure, meaning you take turns opening up rather than one person interviewing the other. The word that matters is matched. A little for a little. You are not taking the floor and keeping it. You’re trading, and then you hand it back, usually with another question.

That’s the loop. Catch, follow up, offer, repeat. The conversation unspools because you keep pulling the thread they’re holding out to you, instead of trying to spin a new one from nothing.

Why this works, and why the dread is backwards

Here’s the finding that should change how you walk into the next conversation. Researchers ran experiments where people chatted in pairs and at speed-dating events, then measured how much partners liked each other. The people who asked more questions were liked more. And the effect was strongest for follow-up questions, the ones that built on what the other person had just said. At the speed-dating events, asking more follow-ups won people more second dates.

Sit with what that means. The thing that makes someone warm to you is not a clever line you produced. It’s the evidence that you were listening and that you cared what they said. A follow-up question is that evidence, made out loud. You can’t fake it, because you can’t ask a real follow-up about something you didn’t hear.

The reciprocal part has its own backing. Across a large body of research, people tend to like those who open up to them, and they like it more when the opening-up goes both ways. We warm to the people we disclose to, and to the people who disclose to us. The catch is the matching. One-sided disclosure, where you dump your life story or where you grill them and reveal nothing, doesn’t build the same closeness. The turn-taking is the engine.

So the dread is exactly backwards. You walk in thinking the burden is on you to be interesting, to perform. The actual move is to be interested. That’s an enormous relief once you believe it, because being interested is something you can simply do. You can’t summon charisma on command. You can always ask one more real question.

Check yourself: name the three moves of threading, in order.
  1. Catch one detail in their free information (listening, which is the spotlight-outward move from module 3).
  2. Ask an open follow-up about that detail, one they can’t answer in a single word.
  3. Offer a little back, matched to what they shared, then hand the floor back.

The order matters: you can’t follow up on a detail you didn’t catch, and the offering only lands as a trade if you’ve actually listened first.

Watching it work

Take a real moment. You’re at a colleague’s leaving drinks, standing next to someone from another team you’ve never spoken to. You’ve done the opener from module 4, asked how they know the person leaving. They say: “Oh, we started on the same week, years ago. I almost didn’t take the job, actually, I’d half-decided to go travelling instead.”

Anxious you hears that and panics: say something interesting about travelling, or about starting jobs, quick. You start drafting. And while you’re drafting, the moment cools.

Threading you does something easier. You caught a detail with some heat in it, “I almost didn’t take the job.” So you follow up, open: “What made you take it in the end?” Notice you didn’t grab travelling, the bigger, flashier topic. You grabbed the choice, because that’s where the person actually was. They tell you a small story about being broke and a friend talking them into it.

Now you offer, matched and small: “I get that, I took this job for almost embarrassingly practical reasons too.” A little of yourself, in proportion to what they gave. Then, because the floor is yours for a second and you don’t want to keep it, you hand it back with another follow-up: “Do you ever wonder about the travelling version of your life?”

You’ve said almost nothing clever. You produced no witty material. And yet the conversation now has somewhere to go, the other person is doing most of the talking, and they’re leaving the exchange with the feeling that you were easy to talk to. That feeling is the follow-ups doing their quiet work. You ran a couple of physiological sighs first (module 2) to walk in, you pointed your attention at them instead of at yourself, and the rest mostly took care of itself.

Check yourself: spot the misstep.

Someone tells you they just got back from caring for a sick parent for three months. You say: “Oh, that’s so hard. My aunt was sick last year, it went on forever, we had this whole nightmare with the insurance, and then my cousin wouldn’t help at all, and honestly I was the only one who…” and you’re off.

What went wrong, and what’s the fix?

Show answer

You hijacked the thread. The opening was a matched offer, “my aunt was sick too,” which is fine. But you kept the floor and turned their moment into your story. That’s disclosure without the reciprocity, the one-sided version that doesn’t build closeness.

The fix is to offer small and hand it straight back with a follow-up: “That’s a lot. My aunt was ill last year so I have a sense of it. How are you doing now that you’re back?” A sentence of yourself, then the floor returns to them.

Field drill: three follow-ups and one piece of you

This week, in one real conversation, run the loop on purpose. The target is simple: ask at least three follow-up questions off what the other person hands you, and offer one genuine piece of yourself back, matched to what they share. A coffee with a colleague, a chat with a neighbour, a call with a friend you usually let lead, any of these will do.

There’s a crutch to drop, and it’s a specific one: over-scripting. Walking in with a mental list of topics, or pre-writing your “interesting” contributions, is the safety behaviour here, and it’s the very thing that stops you hearing the threads. Go in with no script. Trust that the other person will hand you material and that you only have to catch it.

Treat this as a small test of a belief, the way you’ll learn to do formally in module 7. Before you go, predict what you fear will happen. Most people predict some version of I’ll run out of things to say or there’ll be a horrible silence. Then go and watch what threading actually does to that prediction.

Score yourself (the win is what you learned, not whether you felt smooth)

Before you go:

  • Predict. Name the specific fear. “Within five minutes I’ll run dry and we’ll just stand there.” How likely does that feel, 0 to 100%, and how bad would it honestly be?
  • Drop a crutch. Go in without a script of topics or pre-planned clever lines. Over-scripting is the safety behaviour that keeps your attention on yourself instead of on them.

Afterward:

  • Did you do it, three real follow-ups and one matched piece of yourself, with no script?
  • What actually happened, compared to what you predicted? Did you run dry, or did the threads keep appearing once you listened for them?
  • What does that do to the belief that you have to be interesting, even a little?
  • Did you let the nerves come along instead of waiting to feel smooth before you started?

A strong rep isn’t a polished one. It’s one where you stopped performing, listened, and gave the prediction a chance to be wrong.

One honest question

Think back to a conversation that stalled on you recently, the kind you walked away from feeling you’d failed at. Replay it for a second. Were you actually listening, or were you somewhere behind your own eyes, drafting? Most stalls aren’t a shortage of things to say. They’re a moment where attention slipped back onto yourself and the threads went by unheard.

So that’s the move

You don’t have to be interesting. You have to be interested, and interested is something you can just do: catch a detail, ask an open follow-up, offer a little of yourself, hand it back. The conversation carries itself from there, because you’re pulling on what the other person is already holding out instead of spinning something from nothing.
