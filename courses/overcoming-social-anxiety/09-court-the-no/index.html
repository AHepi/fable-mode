Module 9 of 12

Court the No

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain why deliberately seeking small social "failures" reduces the fear faster than avoiding them.
  • Design a small, safe, ethical mishap or rejection-seeking drill (never at others' expense).
  • Run one and record predicted versus actual cost.

A few years ago a man named Jia Jiang walked into a Krispy Kreme and asked the woman behind the counter to make him doughnuts linked together and colored like the Olympic rings. He had no real expectation she’d say yes. The whole point was that she’d say no, because Jiang had set himself a strange project: a hundred days of going out and getting rejected on purpose, one absurd request at a time, to wear the fear down. He’d already been turned away from borrowing a stranger’s dog and refused a “burger refill.” He was braced for another no.

She didn’t say no. She studied the order, pulled out a piece of paper, sketched the rings, thought about which flavors would give her the colors, and about fifteen minutes later handed him a box of Olympic-ring doughnuts. For free. He filmed the whole thing, and millions of people eventually watched it, because it landed on the one nerve this module is about. The disaster he’d braced for, the contempt, the eye-roll, the who do you think you are, simply never showed up. Half the outrageous things he asked for over those hundred days, people just gave him.

That’s the whole lesson, and you don’t need a camera or a hundred days to learn it. You need to go hear a few small noes and discover you’re fine.

Why you go looking for the failure

You already know the move that breaks the loop. In module 7 you learned to treat a feared situation as a behavioral experiment: write the prediction, go test it, let reality have a vote, and remember that success is a surprised prediction, not a calm body. This module takes that engine and points it somewhere that feels, at first, insane. Instead of waiting for a feared situation to come to you, you go out and manufacture one. On purpose. Small.

Here’s why that’s faster than carefully dodging embarrassment forever. Social anxiety runs on two overestimates: how likely a social disaster is, and how bad it would be. Researchers who studied this found the second one, the overblown sense of the cost, does a lot of the heavy lifting. You don’t just think you might fumble. You think the fumble would be catastrophic, that people would think less of you in some lasting way. As long as you never fumble, that price tag never gets checked. It sits in your head, enormous and unargued.

A deliberate small “failure” checks the price tag. You commit a tiny harmless blunder, or ask for something likely to get a no, then watch what actually happens. Almost always: a shrug, a polite no, a moment of nothing, and the world rolls on. The gap between the catastrophe you predicted and the shrug you got is the lesson, and the bigger that gap, the more the fear gives ground.

Think of it as a small safe dose, like a vaccine. A vaccine gives your body a tiny, controlled taste of the thing it’s afraid of, just enough that it learns the threat is survivable, so the real encounter doesn’t lay you flat. A courted no does the same for the fear of rejection, a staged stumble for the fear of looking foolish. You take a trivial dose of the exact thing you dread, your system learns it’s survivable, and the dread loses its grip on the real situations that matter.

Three flavors, graded honestly

This practice comes in three forms. They share one engine and differ in how well the research backs them, so here’s the honest version of each.

Mishap drills. You deliberately commit a minor, harmless social blunder to test the price tag. Mispronounce a word and don’t correct it. Ask an obvious question. Order, then change your mind out loud. What you’re testing is the cost: if I look slightly foolish, what does it actually cost me? Of the three, this one has the most evidence. It comes out of modern cognitive behavioral therapy, where clinicians found that adding deliberate small blunders to treatment, specifically to attack that overestimated social cost, tends to work better than the cognitive work alone. Call it the better-evidenced flavor.

Shame-attacking. The older cousin of the mishap drill, from a clinician named Albert Ellis who built a therapy style partly around it back in the mid-twentieth century. The classic versions: ask a stranger a slightly silly question, wear something mildly odd, announce the time loudly on a train. The aim is identical, to sit in your own embarrassment and prove the catastrophe doesn’t arrive. Be straight about the evidence: this is a long-standing practitioner tradition, not something with a stack of controlled trials behind it. But the mechanism underneath, exposure plus a checked price tag, is the same well-supported engine, so it’s an honest tool as long as you know what it is.

Rejection practice. You make a small, genuine request that’s likely to get a no, and you go for the no. A discount you probably won’t get, a tiny favor. The aim is to hear “no” and notice you’re completely fine. This is the Jia Jiang line from the opening, which began as a game invented by a man named Jason Comely where the winning move was getting rejected. It’s the least formally studied of the three, popular practice rather than clinical protocol, and worth labeling as such. And again, the engine underneath, exposure plus a violated prediction, is the part the science backs.

Notice the through-line. Across all three, the win is hearing the no, or committing the mishap, and being fine. Not getting the yes. If you ask for the discount and they say yes, that’s a pleasant bonus and a sign your cost estimate was even more wrong than you thought, but it isn’t the goal. The goal is to collect proof that the no, the blunder, the flicker of embarrassment, is a thing you can walk through. Chasing the yes puts you back in the old game, where the outcome decides your worth, and the point of this drill is to retire that game.

Check yourself: in rejection practice, what counts as the win?

Hearing the no and noticing you’re fine. The win is getting used to the rejection, not winning the request. A yes is a nice bonus that tells you your predicted cost was too high, but it isn’t the scorecard. If you only feel good when they say yes, you’ve slipped back into letting the outcome judge you, which is the loop this drill exists to break.

The rules that make it safe

Before you design one of these, the boundary, because it’s not optional and it’s what separates a clean drill from a bad idea.

The drills stay small, safe, and ethical. You court trivial social risk and nothing more. You never harm, deceive, frighten, or seriously inconvenience another person. Nothing illegal, nothing dangerous, nothing that could realistically damage someone’s day, a relationship, or your job. The target of every drill is your own discomfort, never another person. The shop assistant, the stranger, the barista are not props in your exposure, and certainly not the butt of it. If a drill would cost someone else something real, it’s the wrong drill. Pick another.

Start tiny, and keep the dial in your own hands. If a drill overwhelms you, the move is to step down a rung, not to flood yourself by pushing harder. Flooding isn’t bravery. It’s a worse, riskier version of the practice, and it can teach the fear rather than shrink it. A drill that leaves you shaken and avoidant taught the wrong thing. Ease off, shrink it, try the smaller version.

And one honest flag, said plainly because it matters. For most everyday social nerves, these drills are safe and genuinely useful. But if your social anxiety runs deep, or if your fear of embarrassment is tangled up with real past wounds, bullying, abuse, a history that put shame in your body for serious reasons, then jumping straight into mishap and rejection drills can backfire and pour salt in a wound instead of healing a habit. If that’s you, build with the earlier skills first, and draw on the self-compassion you’ll learn in module 11 before you come here. And as module 1 laid out, when the anxiety is severe, comes with panic, or is getting in the way of your life, a professional is the right call, not a self-help drill. A therapist who does CBT works with exactly this. None of that is the failure case. It’s just matching the tool to the size of the job.

Designing a tiny one

How do you build one small enough that you’ll actually do it this week? Walk through it the way you’d think it through yourself.

Start with the dread, not the dare. What’s the specific social cost you’re most afraid of? Say it’s looking stupid in front of someone who works in a shop I go to. Now design the smallest thing that pokes that exact fear and nothing bigger. Not “give a speech.” Something like: walk into a café and ask whether they sell something they obviously don’t, a hammer, a haircut, then accept the no and order your coffee like a normal person.

Then make it a real experiment, because that’s what gives it teeth. Run it as a behavioral experiment, the same as module 7, so before you go, write the prediction down. Not “it’ll be bad” but “they’ll give me a weird look, maybe a 70% chance, and I’ll feel humiliated all afternoon.” A number and a cost. That sentence is what reality is about to argue with.

And drop a crutch, the way you practiced in module 8. The classic crutch here is the apologetic exit: the nervous laugh, the “sorry, never mind, I’m being silly,” the rush to explain yourself so nobody thinks you’re actually odd. That little scramble protects you from the very discomfort you came to feel, and it robs the drill of its lesson the same way gripping the mug robbed you in module 1. Leave it behind. Ask the daft question, hear the answer, and let the small silence sit there. That silence is the dose.

Check yourself: someone designs a "drill" where they cut in front of a long line at the pharmacy to practice handling disapproval. What's wrong with it, and how would you fix it?

It breaks the safety rule, because the cost lands on other people. Everyone behind them is genuinely inconvenienced, and some might need that medicine. The target of a drill is always your own discomfort, never someone else’s, and the point is courting trivial risk that you carry yourself.

The fix is a version where you’re the only one paying. Ask a clerk for a small discount “just because” and accept the no. Ask a barista an obvious question. The embarrassment is yours, the inconvenience to anyone else is close to zero, and you still get to test the price tag.

Field drill: go get one no

This week, run one. A single small, safe, ethical request that’s likely to get a no, or one minor harmless mishap, done on purpose. The cleanest starter is a polite, low-stakes ask you’d expect to be turned down on: a small discount you almost certainly won’t get, a tiny favor, a free extra. Make it genuine, polite, and something a stranger could refuse without a second thought.

Hearing the no and noticing you’re fine is the whole point. Not the yes. If you get a yes, smile, take it, and mark down that your cost estimate was even more wrong than you feared.

Run a couple of physiological sighs first (module 2) if you need to take the edge off, then walk in anyway. Don’t wait to feel ready. Ready isn’t coming, and waiting for it is just avoidance wearing a reasonable face.

Score yourself (the point is what you learned, not whether you felt calm)

Before you go:

  • Predict. Write the specific feared outcome, how likely it is (0 to 100%), and how bad it would really be. “I’ll ask for the discount, they’ll look annoyed, maybe 60% likely, and I’ll feel like an idiot all afternoon.”
  • Drop a crutch. Name one safety behavior you’ll deliberately leave behind so the test is clean. The usual one here is the apologetic escape: the nervous laugh, the “sorry, forget it.” Leave it down. Make the ask, hear the answer, let the silence sit.

Afterward, rate yourself:

  • Did you actually do it, with the crutch down? (Hearing the no and staying put is the win.)
  • What actually happened, versus what you predicted? How big was the gap between the catastrophe and the shrug?
  • What does that do to the belief that a small social failure costs you something serious, even a little?
  • Did you let the anxiety be there instead of waiting for it to leave first?

A strong rep isn’t a calm one. It’s one where reality got a clear chance to disagree with the prediction, and the no turned out to be survivable.

One question for you

Think of a small thing you’ve wanted but never asked for, because the asking felt too exposing. A seat swap on a train. A favor from a neighbor. A discount you assumed wasn’t on offer. You’ve been paying the price of the no your whole life, every time, by never asking, to avoid paying it once in reality, where it usually costs almost nothing. What has the imagined no already talked you out of?

So that’s the dare

You spend an enormous amount of energy steering around small social failures, and the steering is what keeps them feeling huge. Go court a few on purpose, in tiny safe doses, and the fear learns what your reasoning never could: that the price tag you’d been carrying around was wildly inflated. The no is the win. The shrug is the lesson. Next we turn to holding your ground and saying what you actually think, which is its own kind of courted risk, with its own quiet payoff.
