Module 6 of 10

Coins, Soldiers, and Taxes

What you'll be able to do
  • State the solid chronology of the first coins: ~7th c. BCE, Lydia and Ionian Greece, electrum, stamped by authority.
  • Explain Graeber's military-coinage complex as an interpretation of how coinage spread, attributed to him.
  • Distinguish the solid co-occurrence of coin, soldiers, and taxes from the contested strong causal claim, and name the critics.

The hook

Coins came thousands of years after the ledgers. The intro course established that much and the reader carried it here: writing, accounting, weighed silver, interest, and debt all run for millennia before anyone stamps a lump of metal and calls it money. Coinage is late. It is also, when it finally arrives, unmistakably a thing of the state. Someone with authority strikes a mark into the metal and stands behind it.

That is the easy part, and it is solid. The hard part is what the coin caused. The first coins appear in the same world as standing armies, war, and taxes, and the temptation is to draw a straight line from one to the others, to say that coinage happened because states needed to pay soldiers and because taxes pulled the coins back in. That line is worth drawing carefully, because most of its length is a guess. There is a datable fact here, and there is a strong story laid over the fact, and the work of this module is to keep them from fusing into a single thing that looks more settled than it is.

Context & background

The reader arrives with two things already in hand. From module 5, the silver problem: an administered standard, the silver-to-barley ratios and capped interest set by temple, palace, and law rather than by a market converging on a price, an arrangement that shifts the case toward the credit and state side without settling it. And from the intro course, the bare chronology, coins late and state-linked, recalled in a clause and not re-derived. This module sharpens that chronology and then weighs the causation that gets hung on it.

The reason coinage matters to the larger quarrel is that it looks, at first glance, like a gift to the commodity theory. A coin is a piece of precious metal you can hold, weigh, bite, and carry across a border. If money is fundamentally a special commodity that trade threw up, the coin is what that commodity looks like once it gets convenient. The metallist can point to a stater and say: there it is, value you can put in your hand. But the timing cuts the other way. Coins do not stand at the origin of money. They arrive late, on top of a thousand years of moneyless accounting, and they arrive bearing a stamp. The interesting question is not whether coins are metal, which is obvious, but what brought the stamp, and what the stamp did once it was there.

Narrative / exposition

Start with what can be dated, because it can be dated firmly, and the firmness is the point.

The first coins appear around the seventh century BCE, in Lydia and the Ionian Greek cities of the western Anatolian coast. They are made of electrum, a naturally occurring alloy of gold and silver, pale and variable in its mix. They are not weighed-out lumps passed by mass alone, which is the older Mesopotamian practice the reader knows from the silver problem. They are struck: an authority impresses a design into the metal, and the impression is a guarantee, a mark that says this piece has been issued and stands behind it. The chronology here belongs to Philip Grierson, the numismatist whose work on the origins of money the course has leaned on before. Grierson read the coins as artifacts and the dates as evidence, and what the evidence supports is narrow and strong: coinage is a late technology, it is a state technology, and it begins in this corner of the world at roughly this time.

Set beside that chronology a second fact, equally solid and equally undramatic on its own. In the centuries when coinage spreads through the Greek and Mediterranean world, three things keep turning up together in the record: coins, soldiers, and taxes. States that mint are states that field armies and states that collect. The mint, the muster, and the tax roll are entangled. This co-occurrence is not a theory. It is a pattern you can read off the evidence, and any account of coinage has to reckon with it.

Now the interpretation, and it must be named as an interpretation from the first word. David Graeber, the anthropologist whose Debt: The First 5,000 Years (2011) the course will return to, read that co-occurrence as a tight causal mechanism, and called it the military-coinage complex (sometimes the military-coinage-slavery complex). The machine, in Graeber’s telling, runs like this. A state with a roving army has a problem its older credit arrangements cannot solve. Soldiers on the move, far from home, among strangers, will not take an IOU. There is no relationship to anchor the debt and no court to enforce it, exactly the stranger-trade condition from module 3 where a thing with intrinsic acceptability does work a promise cannot. So the state pays them in coin, portable metal good anywhere. Then it demands taxes back in that same coin. The population, needing coin to pay the tax, has to obtain it, and the readiest way to obtain it is to sell food and supplies to the soldiers who are carrying it. The tax does not just raise revenue. It manufactures a market, by forcing everyone to chase the token the soldiers hold. And war itself feeds the whole apparatus, supplying the captured bullion to mint and, in Graeber’s fuller version, the slaves whose labor and sale move through the same circuits.

It is an elegant machine, and that is exactly why it has to be handled with care. In Graeber’s image the mint becomes a war-engine, metal struck not to grease trade among neighbors but to move armies and pull a population into a cash economy at the point of a tax demand. The image is doing real explanatory work, tying coinage, the state, war, and the market into one mechanism. The earlier image, the coin struck to pay a single soldier, is the fact. The war-engine is the reading of the fact.

Check yourself

Before reading on, answer from memory. The first coins are dated to roughly the seventh century BCE, in Lydia and Ionian Greece, struck from electrum. Which part of the coins-soldiers-taxes story is that chronology, and which part is Graeber’s military-coinage complex?

Show answer

The chronology is the solid fact: ~7th c. BCE, Lydia and Ionian Greece, electrum, stamped by authority. This is Grierson’s numismatic ground, and it supports only that coinage is late and is a state technology. The military-coinage complex is Graeber’s interpretation of how coinage spread: the causal claim that states minted coin to pay roving armies who would not take credit, then taxed in coin to force a population to obtain it (by feeding the soldiers), seeding markets, with war supplying the bullion and slaves. The dating is fact; the causal machine is a named reading laid over it.

Primary source

The clean statement of the solid part, the part about authority rather than metal, belongs to Grierson. The line below follows the sense of his account of early coinage as a state act; it is given as a labelled paraphrase, not a transcription, because the exact wording is not reproduced here.

What distinguishes a coin from a mere piece of metal of known weight and fineness is the stamp of an issuing authority. The stamp is a guarantee, and behind the guarantee stands the body, the city or the king, that issued it. Coinage in this sense is a public act, not a private commodity, however precious the metal it is struck from.

Philip Grierson, on the nature of early coinage, paraphrased

The paraphrase makes one claim and withholds another. It claims that the defining feature of a coin is the stamp, the act of an issuing authority, not the metal beneath the stamp. That is a strong point for the state theory, and it is solid, grounded in what coins physically are. The claim is also a narrow one. It says coinage is a public act. It does not say why states began to perform that act, and it does not say that armies and taxes drove the practice. The causal story is a further step, and Grierson’s chronology does not take it. The stamp is fact. The reason for the stamp is where the arguing starts.

Analysis & interpretation

Everything turns on the gap between a co-occurrence and a cause.

That coin, soldiers, and taxes appear together is solid. What that togetherness means is not. Graeber’s military-coinage complex reads the togetherness as a single causal mechanism, one engine driving coinage’s spread across the ancient world. The critics of the strong version do not deny that coins, armies, and taxes are entangled. They deny that one mechanism, repeated everywhere, accounts for the entanglement. Their objection has two main edges.

The first is that the causation is over-drawn. Co-occurrence is not proof of a single drive-shaft connecting the parts. Coinage in the early Greek world spread among small city-states, in commerce, in civic display, in religious dedication, and in fines and fees, not only in the pay-chests of mobile armies. To read every appearance of coin back to the soldier’s wage is to fit one story to evidence that supports several. The pattern is real; the claim that one mechanism produced it is the part that outruns the record.

The second edge is that the picture is regionally uneven. The military-coinage complex describes some times and places well and others badly. Where it fits, large empires paying large standing armies and taxing in coin, it fits because those conditions were present, not because the conditions are universal. Other coin-using societies do not show the same machine, and a mechanism that has to be switched on and off region by region is a weaker thing than a law of how coinage works. The strong causal claim wants to be general. The evidence keeps making it local.

So the honest position is precise about what each part can bear. The chronology bears a great deal: coins are late, coins are stamped, coins are a state technology, and where states minted they also fielded armies and collected taxes. The strong causal complex bears less: it is a plausible and influential reading of part of the record, attributed to Graeber, and the critics are right that as a universal mechanism it claims more than the evidence supplies. This does not make Graeber’s reading worthless. It makes it a reading, a bold interpretation set beside a solid chronology, and worth holding precisely because it is bold. The error to avoid is the one the silver problem already warned against in another key: letting an attractive interpretation harden into the fact it is interpreting.

From memory: name the two main criticisms of the strong military-coinage complex, and state what each criticism leaves standing.

Show answer

First, the causation is over-drawn: coin, soldiers, and taxes co-occurring does not prove one causal mechanism produced them, since coinage also spread through commerce, civic life, religious dedication, and fees. Second, the picture is regionally uneven: the complex fits some times and places (large empires with standing armies taxing in coin) and fails for others, so it is local rather than universal. What both criticisms leave standing is the solid core, the chronology and the co-occurrence itself: coins are late and state-stamped (~7th c. BCE, Lydia/Ionia, electrum), and minting states did field armies and collect taxes. The critics contest the strong causal claim, not the dated fact.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Separate the beats. Take the claim “states invented coinage to pay their armies.” Split it into the part that is solid chronology and the part that is contested causation, and write each as its own sentence. Then say what evidence would have to exist to move the second sentence from interpretation toward fact.

  2. A reading worth its boldness. Graeber’s military-coinage complex is an interpretation, not a settled finding. Argue either that the boldness of the reading is a virtue (it explains a real entanglement that cautious accounts leave loose) or a liability (it fits one mechanism to evidence that supports several), and name the precise price of the position you take.

  3. Which theory does the stamp bear on? A coin is precious metal you can hold, which seems to favor the commodity theory, but it carries a state’s stamp and arrives late, which seems to favor the credit and state theories. Decide which feature you weight as more telling about money’s nature, and defend the weighting. Say what the other side sees in the same coin.

  4. The uneven map. The critics say the complex is regionally uneven, fitting empires with standing armies better than small commercial cities. Pick one of those settings and write how coinage might spread there without the military-coinage complex driving it. Then say what your account cannot explain that Graeber’s can.

What a strong response does
  • Keeps the solid chronology (~7th c. BCE, Lydia/Ionia, electrum, state-stamped) genuinely distinct from the contested causal complex, and attributes the complex to Graeber rather than presenting it as established history.
  • States precisely what shifts and what does not: the co-occurrence of coin, soldiers, and taxes is solid; the single-mechanism causation is the contested part.
  • Names a rival reading and what evidence would support each, rather than declaring a winner the module has not earned.
  • Treats the strong interpretation with respect, neither adopting it as fact nor dismissing it, in line with the course’s refusal to sneer at the bold readings it needs.
  • Stays inside the evidence established here and in earlier modules, making no claim later modules have not yet built.

The story

Dig beneath one of the most famous temples of the ancient world and you find, among the foundations, some of the oldest money on earth.

The temple is the Artemision, the great sanctuary of Artemis at Ephesus, on the Ionian coast of what is now western Turkey, one of the largest and most renowned temples of antiquity. Early in the twentieth century, excavators working under the foundations of the structure recovered a deposit of small electrum pieces, lumps and early struck pieces of the pale gold-silver alloy, lying with other dedications in the temple’s earliest layers. These pieces are among the oldest coins, or immediate forerunners of coins, ever found, and they belong to roughly the late seventh or early sixth century BCE, in the Lydian and Ionian world where coinage begins.

The location does the teaching. The earliest money in the Greek world turns up not in a marketplace, not in a merchant’s hoard left on a trade route, but packed into the foundations of a temple, beneath the floor, among offerings to a goddess. That single fact carries most of the argument. The coins are late: they lie above and after a thousand years of moneyless Mesopotamian accounting, the weighed silver and administered standards of the silver problem. They are state- and temple-adjacent: they come into the world bound up with authority and sacred institutions, the very bodies that stamp metal and stand behind the stamp, not with a market quietly converging on its most saleable good. And they are electrum, struck pieces of a real precious alloy, which is why the metallist can still point at them and say there is value you can hold, even as the timing and the setting answer back that this value arrives stamped, late, and lodged under a temple floor.

What the Artemision deposit does not contain is the war-engine. There are no soldiers in the foundations, no tax rolls, no captured bullion freshly minted to pay a marching column. The strong causal story, the military-coinage complex, would have to be argued in from elsewhere, from the later empires where standing armies and coin taxes ran together. Here, at the beginning, in the temple of a goddess on the Ionian shore, what the ground actually holds is the fact and only the fact: the earliest coins, late and struck and sacred-adjacent, and not a single line of the interpretation that would later be hung on them. That is the right place to leave it. The coin is in your hand. The reason for the coin is still being argued.
