Module 4 of 10

Money as Credit, Money as the State's Word

What you'll be able to do
  • Distinguish Innes (money is credit), Knapp (the state names money), Keynes (money of account is primary), and Lerner (tax-acceptance gives money value) as four different claims.
  • Explain Grierson's argument that the abstract unit of account originates in customary compensation tariffs (wergild), not in market haggling.
  • Summarize Ingham's sociological synthesis of money as a social relation and an instituted unit of account.

The hook

There is a word that does more damage to this subject than any other, and the word is “chartalist.” It gets used as a bin. Into it go Innes and Knapp and Keynes and Lerner and Grierson and Ingham, six people who wrote across a century and disagreed with each other about the thing that matters most, and out of the bin comes a single flattened position: money is made by the state, the end. That position is a composite no one ever held. It loses precisely the information this module exists to hand over.

The credit and state theories are the home of Schumpeter’s monetary analysis, the view that money and credit are part of the essential structure of the economy rather than a veil over an essentially barter one. The intro reader arrived here already half-persuaded, because money-of-account is ancient and coins are late. What that reader lacks is the inside of the view: that it is not one view but several, that the people who built it were arguing among themselves, and that the arguments are the substance. Lay six name-cards in a row. Each card carries one claim, in the words of the one person who made it. The work of this module is to keep the cards from sliding into a heap.

Context & background

The commodity theory at strength (module 2) said money is the most saleable good, valued back to a former commodity, and the stranger-trade objection (module 3) gave it its hardest empirical ground, the deal between people with no shared sovereign and no shared law. The credit and state theories answer from the other direction. They start not from a thing that changes hands but from a relation between two people, a debt, and from the measure that debt is reckoned in.

The vocabulary is mostly already in hand. The unit of account, recall, is the measuring-stick that prices and debts are reckoned in, as against the medium of exchange, the object that actually moves. Money-of-account is prices and debts kept in a standard measure with no coin moving at all. Chartalism and the state theory name the position that money is valid because the state declares it so, not because of any metal in it. The word comes from charta, a token or ticket, and the technical adjective is proclamatory, valid by declaration of authority rather than by material. Module 9 will name the one thing this column is most often confused with, Modern Monetary Theory, and fence it off as a present-day policy program rather than a theory of money’s nature. For now the column is being built, not bounded.

What it is built from is six distinct claims. The temptation, the strong one, is to hear them as variations on a theme. They are not variations. They locate the essence of money in six different places: in private debt, in state naming, in the conceptual priority of the unit, in tax-demand, in customary law, and in social relation. Two of these can both be true at once and two of them genuinely compete. Telling which is which is the skill on offer.

Narrative / exposition

Take the cards in order.

The first belongs to A. Mitchell Innes, an early-twentieth-century British writer on money who published two essays in 1913 and 1914. His claim is the bluntest in the column and the foundation the rest sits on: money is credit. A coin is not a commodity that happens to circulate; it is a token of debt, an IOU, an acknowledgment that its issuer owes something, and it passes from hand to hand because a transferable acknowledgment of debt is a useful thing to hold. On this reading the primary fact about money is not a substance but a relation, the relation between a creditor and a debtor. To pay is to hand back a debt-token and cancel an obligation against a credit. Metal is incidental. The IOU is the thing. This card sits at the base of the row, because every other card either sharpens it, narrows it, deepens it, or builds something larger on top of it, and none of them simply repeats it.

The second belongs to Georg Friedrich Knapp, the German economist whose State Theory of Money (1905) gave chartalism its name. Knapp’s claim is not Innes’s. Innes locates money in private debt, the obligation between any creditor and any debtor. Knapp locates it in the state. Money is valid, on his account, because the state names it as the thing it will accept at its own pay-offices. Validity is proclamatory: the authority declares “this is what counts as payment to me,” and that declaration, not the substance of the token, is what makes a money a money. The difference from Innes is exact and worth stating slowly. Innes asks what money is and answers “a debt.” Knapp asks what makes a particular token valid and answers “the state’s naming of it.” A private IOU between two merchants is money on Innes’s account from the moment it is written. On Knapp’s account the decisive thing is the state’s choosing to recognize a token at its counter. One theory centers the debt; the other centers the proclamation.

The third belongs to John Maynard Keynes, writing on money in A Treatise on Money (1930). His claim is narrower and more abstract than either of the first two, and the narrowness is the point. Keynes opens the Treatise by making the money of account the primary concept: the unit that debts and prices are expressed in comes first, and the money-things, the coins and notes that answer to that unit, come second. This is a claim about conceptual priority, about which idea you have to have before you can have the other. It is not Knapp’s claim. Keynes is not saying the state’s power is what makes money; he is saying the unit of account is logically prior to the objects that fill it. A society could have a money of account, a shared measure for debts, before it had any standardized thing to pay them with, and on Keynes’s reading the measure is the foundation and the thing is the superstructure. The strong modern chartalists who came later read more into the state than Keynes did here; his claim, kept to its own size, is about priority, not power. Pinning the full modern thesis onto the Keynes of 1930 reads back a position he did not state.

The fourth belongs to Abba Lerner, a twentieth-century economist whose 1947 formulation is the sharpest mechanism in the row. Knapp said the state names money. Lerner says how the naming bites: the state can give an otherwise worthless token real value by demanding that token back in payment of taxes. A population that owes taxes payable only in the state’s money has to obtain that money, and that standing obligation generates a standing demand for the token, which is what gives it value. This is the phrase the card carries, and it stays on this card: money as a “creature of the state.” Lerner’s claim is sharper than Knapp’s because it supplies the engine Knapp’s naming lacked. Naming alone might leave you asking why anyone accepts what the state names; the tax-demand answers, because everyone needs the token to discharge a debt they cannot escape. This is the cleanest bridge from chartalism toward the modern policy arguments, but it is a bridge toward them, not into them, and module 9 holds the far end. The claim here is about value: tax-acceptance is what makes the token worth holding.

So far the row reads as a deepening sequence on a state-and-debt theme, and a careless eye would now collapse all four. They come apart the moment the four are set as answers to one question. What makes money money? Innes: it is a transferable debt. Knapp: the state names it valid. Keynes: the unit of account is conceptually prior to the thing. Lerner: tax-demand gives the token its value. Those are four different sentences with four different subjects. Debt, naming, priority, demand. They overlap in mood and diverge in claim, and a course that lets them blur has thrown away four-fifths of what it knows.

The fifth card steps outside the state-and-debt frame entirely. It belongs to Philip Grierson, the numismatist, and it asks a question the others assume away: where did the abstract unit of account come from in the first place? A measure of value, a scale on which a cow and a field and an injury can all be priced against each other, is a strange and sophisticated thing. Grierson’s answer is that it could only arise where a society already possessed a fixed scale of equivalences, and he locates that scale not in the market and not in the state’s pay-office but in the compensation tariffs of early law codes, the wergild. A wergild is a tabulated legal price for each injury and death, a list that says this much is owed for a severed hand, that much for a life, settled by custom and law to head off the blood-feud. Here, Grierson argues, is the first place a society fixed abstract equivalences across unlike things, and from this customary-legal scale the unit of account descends. The claim is an origin claim, and its origin is neither Innes’s private credit nor Knapp’s state-naming. It is customary law, the tariff of compensation, older than markets and older than the proclaiming state.

The sixth card holds the others together without pretending to be any one of them. It belongs to Geoffrey Ingham, a present-day sociologist of money, whose synthesis treats money as a social relation and a politically instituted unit of account. Ingham takes the credit insight (money is a claim, an obligation) and the state insight (the unit is instituted by authority) and holds them in one frame, against the commodity theory’s real-analysis picture of money as a veil. The point of the sixth card is that it is a synthesis and not a fifth competitor: Ingham is not offering a rival mechanism to Lerner’s tax-demand or Grierson’s wergild but a way of seeing money, credit and authority and the social relation, as parts of one instituted thing. Set him at the end of the row because he gathers; do not set him in the middle as though he were one more distinct mechanism.

Six cards, then, and a discipline for reading them. Innes and Keynes are compatible and can both be held at once; a transferable debt and a primary unit of account sit together without strain. Knapp and Lerner are close cousins that still differ, naming versus tax-demand. Grierson stands apart with an origin story the others do not supply. Ingham gathers. None of the six is “chartalism, the end.”

Check yourself

Before reading on, answer from memory. Innes and Knapp are the two most often fused into one “chartalist” claim. State each one’s claim in a single sentence, and name the precise thing that separates them.

Show answer

Innes: money is credit, a coin or note is a transferable IOU, and the primary fact is the creditor-debtor relation. Knapp: money is valid because the state names it acceptable at its pay-offices, proclamatory validity by charta, not by metal. What separates them is where the money-making power sits. Innes centers private debt, the obligation between any creditor and debtor, money on his account from the moment the IOU is written. Knapp centers the state’s naming power, the authority’s declaration of what it will accept at its counter. One theory is about what money is (a debt); the other is about what makes a token valid (the state’s word).

Primary source

Innes is the right voice for the primary slot, because his is the foundational card and the one most often swallowed into the composite. The two essays appeared in the Banking Law Journal, “What is Money?” in 1913 and “The Credit Theory of Money” in 1914. The line below renders the core of his claim. It is given as a clearly labelled paraphrase, not a transcription, because the exact wording is not reproduced here from the original pages.

A coin is not a commodity that men accept for the metal in it. It is an acknowledgment of a debt, a token that its issuer is bound to receive back in payment of what is owed to him. What circulates is not gold but obligation, and to pay is nothing other than to return a debt-token and so cancel one debt against another. The eye of the credit theory is fixed on the creditor and the debtor, and on nothing else.

A. Mitchell Innes, “The Credit Theory of Money” (1914), the credit theory’s core claim, paraphrased

The metal sits at the edge of the paraphrase, mentioned only to be set aside. The creditor and the debtor are at the center. That placement is the whole of Innes’s distinctive claim, and it is exactly the claim that the bin-word “chartalist” erases, because it says nothing about the state. Knapp’s state, Lerner’s tax, Grierson’s wergild are not in this passage and should not be read into it. Innes’s card carries one sentence, money is a transferable debt, and the discipline of this module is to let it carry only that sentence and to make the other five cards carry their own.

Analysis & interpretation

The reason the six refuse to collapse is that they answer different questions, and seeing the questions apart is the analytic move the module is built around.

Two of the questions are about the nature of money. Innes answers “what is money?” with “a debt.” Keynes answers “which concept is primary?” with “the unit of account, before the thing.” These are compatible. A society whose money is at bottom transferable debt, and whose unit of account is conceptually prior to any object that fills it, has no contradiction to resolve; Innes and Keynes can be laid one over the other and read together. A reader who treats agreement-in-mood as identity-of-claim will miss that these two cards are doing genuinely different jobs even while pointing the same way, one fixing what money is, the other fixing what comes first.

Two more questions are about the source of validity or value, and here the cards sit close enough to be confused yet differ in ways that matter. Knapp answers “what makes a token valid?” with “the state names it.” Lerner answers “what gives the token value?” with “the state demands it back in taxes.” Knapp’s is a claim about recognition, the state’s choosing to accept a token at its pay-office. Lerner’s is a claim about the mechanism that turns recognition into worth, the tax-debt that forces a population to acquire the token. Lerner is sharper not because he disagrees with Knapp but because he supplies the part Knapp left implicit. Treat them as one and you lose the very thing that makes the second card an advance on the first.

The fifth question is not about present-day money at all but about origins, and Grierson is its only answer in the row. Where does the abstract measure come from? His reply, the wergild, the customary-legal tariff of compensation, is the one claim here that competes with the commodity theory on its own ground, the ground of origins, without invoking either private credit or the proclaiming state. It is worth holding apart from the other five precisely because it is an origin claim and they, mostly, are not. Innes, Knapp, Keynes, and Lerner are telling you what money is or what makes it work; Grierson is telling you where the measuring-stick was first cut.

And Ingham answers a sixth kind of question, the synthetic one: how do these insights fit together? His money-as-social-relation is not a seventh mechanism but a frame for holding the credit insight and the state insight in one hand. The mistake to guard against with Ingham is the opposite of the mistake with Innes and Knapp. There the danger is fusing distinct claims; here the danger is mistaking a synthesis for one more rival, and demanding that Ingham compete with Lerner or Grierson when his work is to gather rather than to compete.

None of this decides the case. The commodity theory at strength is still on the table, and module 5 will bring the two columns to collide on one body of evidence, the silver problem. What this module establishes is narrower and necessary: the credit and state column, given its strongest hearing, is not one claim but six, and a reader who cannot tell them apart cannot weigh them, against the metallist or against each other.

Check yourself

From memory, the harder discrimination: a friend says “Knapp, Lerner, and Grierson all just argue that the state creates money.” Identify the one of the three who does not fit that summary, and say what claim that thinker actually makes.

Show answer

Grierson does not fit. Knapp (the state names money valid at its pay-offices) and Lerner (the state’s tax-demand gives a token its value) are both claims about state power, distinct from each other but both about the state. Grierson is not a state-power claim at all. His claim is about the origin of the abstract unit of account: the measure of value descends from the compensation tariffs (wergild) of early law codes, the customary-legal scale of what is owed for each injury or death, not from market haggling and not from the proclaiming state. Folding Grierson into “the state creates money” loses an origin claim grounded in customary law and replaces it with a power claim he never made.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Sort the four claims onto their questions. Write Innes, Knapp, Keynes, and Lerner each beside the question their claim actually answers (what money is; what makes a token valid; which concept is primary; what gives a token value). Then argue whether any two of the four genuinely contradict each other, or whether they are all compatible parts of one picture, and defend your placement.

  2. Test Grierson against the commodity theory. Grierson and the metallist both offer an origin for the measure of value, one in customary compensation law, one in the market converging on a saleable good. Write the strongest version of each origin story, then say what evidence would tell them apart, without declaring which is right.

  3. Is Ingham a synthesis or a dodge? Ingham holds the credit insight and the state insight together as money-as-social-relation. Argue either that this is a genuine advance that the row of distinct claims was building toward, or that it is a way of avoiding the choice between them, and name the precise price of the position you assign him.

  4. Steelman the bin-word. Make the best honest case that “chartalist” is a useful label rather than a damaging one, that the six really do share a common core that warrants grouping them. Then say exactly what information the grouping costs, so that even your defense of the word shows what it hides.

What a strong response does
  • Keeps the six claims genuinely distinct and attributes each to the right thinker: Innes (money is credit), Knapp (state-naming), Keynes (money of account is primary), Lerner (tax-acceptance gives value), Grierson (wergild origin of the unit), Ingham (sociological synthesis).
  • Sees that Innes and Keynes are compatible (nature and priority), that Knapp and Lerner are close but distinct (naming versus tax-demand), that Grierson stands apart with an origin claim, and that Ingham gathers rather than competes.
  • Does not retroject the strong modern chartalist thesis onto the Keynes of the 1930 Treatise, whose claim is conceptual priority, not state power.
  • Holds the credit/state column’s case at full strength without crowning it over the commodity theory, which has not yet met it on the evidence.
  • Stays inside the claims as stated here, attributing every bold reading to its author and making no argument later modules have not yet built.

The story

He was not an economist, and the profession never forgave him for it.

Alfred Mitchell-Innes was a British official, a man of the diplomatic and civil service rather than the lecture hall, and in 1913 and 1914 he published two essays in a place no economist read, the Banking Law Journal, a trade periodical for American banking lawyers. The essays were called “What is Money?” and “The Credit Theory of Money,” and in them an outsider with no academic standing in the field stated, plainly and at length, a claim that cut against almost everything the textbooks of his day assumed. Money is not a commodity. A coin is an acknowledgment of debt. What circulates is obligation, the relation between a creditor and a debtor, and the metal is beside the point. He marshaled history and law and the everyday workings of credit to argue that the eye should be fixed on the debt and not on the substance.

The reception was silence. There was one notable exception, and it is a telling one: John Maynard Keynes reviewed the first essay and took it seriously, the future author of the Treatise on Money recognizing a serious mind on the unit of account and the priority of credit. But a single respectful notice from a rising economist was not a hearing from the profession, and the profession did not give one. Innes had no university chair, no school of students, no journal of standing carrying his name forward. The two essays sat where he had left them, in a banking-law periodical, while monetary economics went on building its models around money as a commodity and a veil. A heterodox idea, published in the wrong place by the wrong sort of person, simply failed to take. It survived, when it survived at all, on the margins.

Then, decades later, it was pulled back in. Credit and state theorists looking for the roots of their own position found Innes’s two essays, recognized in them an early and unusually clear statement of the view that money is credit, and brought them back into print and into argument. The essays that the profession had ignored in 1913 became, two generations on, a founding document of the credit theory, cited and reprinted and taught. The idea had not changed in the interval. The standing of the man who wrote it had not changed either; he remained the civil-service outsider he always was. What changed was that a part of the field came round to the question he had answered, and discovered that an amateur in a banking-law journal had answered it first.

The card that bears Innes’s name carries one sentence, and it is the sentence the Banking Law Journal printed when no one was reading: money is credit, a transferable debt, the creditor and the debtor at the center and the metal at the edge. It is not Knapp’s state, not Keynes’s priority, not Lerner’s tax, not Grierson’s wergild, not Ingham’s synthesis. It is the first card in the row, set down by a man the profession would not seat at the table, and waiting half a century to be picked up.
