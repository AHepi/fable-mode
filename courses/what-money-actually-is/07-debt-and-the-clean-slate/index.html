Module 7 of 10

Debt, Interest, and the Clean Slate

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain that measured, enforceable, interest-bearing debt predates coinage, and the political danger of debt bondage.
  • Summarize Hudson's case that royal clean-slate decrees and the biblical Jubilee were recurrent tools against debt peonage.
  • Distinguish the attested existence of these decrees from the contested claim about their frequency, enforcement, and reach.

The hook

A debt is a number that grows. That is the part the moral language around debt tends to skip, and it is the part that does the damage. A loan of so much weighed silver does not sit still while the borrower works to repay it. It accrues interest, and the interest compounds, and a bad harvest or a sick ox can turn a survivable obligation into one that outruns anything a household can produce. When that happens the borrower has one asset left to pledge: the labor of the family, and then the family itself. The debt that began as a number ends as a person in bondage.

This is not a late development. The silver problem (module 5) showed an administered standard with the temple as the great creditor, debts reckoned in weighed silver at ratios set by authority rather than by a market. What that record also shows, long before any coin was struck, is interest. The earliest debt was measured, enforceable, and priced to grow. So the clean slate, the deliberate wiping-out of debts that you met in passing in the intro course, was not a quaint ritual. It was an answer to a standing political emergency, and the question this module turns on is how often that answer was actually given.

Context & background

Hold the chronology straight, because it is the load-bearing fact. Coins are late and state-stamped (module 6), a seventh-century technology. Interest-bearing debt is far older. In the Mesopotamian record, debts denominated in silver and in barley were lent at interest, and the obligation to repay with increase was written into clay and enforced by law. The Code of Hammurabi, the Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE that the intro course met as the law of debt, regulates lending: it sets terms, caps what a creditor may take, and provides for what happens when a debtor cannot pay. Debt as a measured, enforceable, interest-bearing instrument predates the coin by more than a thousand years. Whatever money fundamentally is, the priced obligation came first.

That priority connects back to the credit theory (module 4), which makes the creditor-debtor relation the primary thing rather than a layer over commodity exchange. If debt at interest is the oldest financial fact in the record, the credit theorist has a point to press. But the point this module is built around is not which theory wins. It is what debt at interest did to the societies that ran on it, and what those societies did back.

What it did was concentrate. Interest moves wealth from the borrower to the lender by construction, and over a run of bad years it moves land, then labor, then liberty. A free population of smallholders could slide, household by household, into debt bondage: a person pledged against a debt and made to work it off, sometimes for years, sometimes sold abroad and gone for good. A kingdom that let this run unchecked hollowed out its own free population, its tax base, and its army, handing both to a creditor class. Debt was therefore not only an economic instrument. It was a political danger a ruler had reason to fear, and the clean slate was the tool a ruler reached for.

Narrative / exposition

The strongest case that the clean slate was a central, recurring institution belongs to Michael Hudson, a historian of ancient debt who has spent decades reading the Mesopotamian and biblical material as a single tradition. Take his case at full strength first, because it is built on real documents and it explains something real.

Hudson’s claim is that royal debt cancellations were not occasional acts of mercy but a standing instrument of statecraft, repeated across reigns and across centuries. The Sumerian and Akkadian kings issued decrees he reads as exactly this. A misharum decree, from a word for justice or equity, and an andurarum decree, from a word for restoration or release, could annul certain debts, return pledged land to its original holders, and free those who had fallen into bondage for debt. New kings proclaimed them on accession; kings in trouble proclaimed them to relieve pressure. The biblical material, on Hudson’s reading, carries the same institution forward. The Jubilee of Leviticus 25 commands a release every fiftieth year, and the sabbatical-year release of Deuteronomy 15 commands a remission of debts every seventh. For Hudson these are not isolated curiosities. They are the same political technology, the periodic clean slate, by which an ancient state kept its free population from collapsing into peonage and kept the creditor class from becoming the real sovereign.

Read that case generously, because it earns it. The decrees are attested. The texts that command release exist and say what Hudson says they say. The mechanism is coherent: a society that prices debt to grow will concentrate land and people in creditor hands, and a ruler who wants a free population to tax and to conscript has a direct interest in periodically cutting those ties. Hudson assembled a scattered record into an institution with a function, and that is real historical work. The clean slate was a genuine tool of ancient government, and the worst way to read this module would be to come away thinking otherwise.

Now the qualification, which is a different question and has to be kept a different question. That the decrees existed is one claim. That they were recurrent and central, a regular and enforced feature of how these economies actually ran, is a second and much stronger claim, and it is Hudson’s. The strong version is where specialists divide. How often were the decrees actually issued, as opposed to commanded in a text? How far were they enforced once proclaimed, against creditors with every incentive to resist? How widely did they reach, across which classes of debt and which regions, in a record that survives unevenly and by accident? On these questions the evidence thins fast. Some historians read Hudson as building a confident, system-wide institution out of a handful of surviving decrees and two biblical commands whose actual practice is itself debated. The objection is not that he invented the documents. It is that the documents may not bear the weight of recurrent and central, and that a periodic, enforced, society-wide jubilee is a larger thing than scattered royal proclamations can prove.

So the structure of the honest position has two floors. The lower floor is solid: clean-slate decrees are attested, debt bondage was real, and rulers used cancellation as a tool. The upper floor is contested: the claim that this was a regular, enforced, central institution is the strong thesis, it carries Hudson’s name, and the field has not ratified it. The point of keeping the floors apart is not to deflate Hudson. It is that the attested fact and the strong thesis are different sizes, and an undergraduate who flattens them into one has lost exactly the information worth having.

Primary source

The biblical release-law is one of the texts at the center of the dispute, and it is worth reading for what it actually couples together. Leviticus 25 commands the Jubilee, the fiftieth-year release of land and persons. Around that command sits a tighter rule about the thing that drives people into bondage in the first place: interest. The passage below is the King James Version, given exactly.

35 And if thy brother be waxen poor, and fallen in decay with thee; then thou shalt relieve him: yea, though he be a stranger, or a sojourner; that he may live with thee.

36 Take thou no usury of him, or increase: but fear thy God; that thy brother may live with thee.

37 Thou shalt not give him thy money upon usury, nor lend him thy victuals for increase.

Leviticus 25:35-37, King James Version

The text does two things a reader chasing “the clean slate” can miss. It forbids increase on a loan to a poor neighbor, attacking the growth of the debt directly, and it does so inside the same chapter that commands the fiftieth-year release. The Jubilee handles the debts that escaped; the ban on usury tries to stop them forming. Together they describe a whole apparatus aimed at one outcome, that an impoverished member of the community “may live with thee” rather than pass into bondage.

That the apparatus is commanded here is solid; the words are on the page. How regularly the fiftieth-year release was actually observed, and whether a society ever ran a real Jubilee on schedule rather than holding it as an ideal, is precisely the contested question. The command is evidence of the institution as a norm. It is not, by itself, evidence of the practice as a recurrence, and Hudson’s strong reading needs the second to do its work.

Check yourself

From memory: state the difference between the claim that clean-slate decrees existed and Hudson’s claim about them, and say which one the field has not settled.

Show answer

That such decrees existed, that Mesopotamian kings issued misharum and andurarum decrees annulling debts and freeing the debt-bound, and that the Jubilee and sabbatical release were commanded, is the attested, solid claim. Hudson’s claim is the stronger one: that these cancellations were recurrent and central, a regular and enforced instrument by which ancient states routinely kept their free populations out of peonage. The existence is settled; the strong thesis about frequency, enforcement, and reach is what the field has not settled, because the surviving record is thin and uneven and may not bear that much weight.

Analysis & interpretation

Why hold the line between an attested decree and a contested institution so hard? Because the temptation runs the other way, and it runs in both directions at once.

One temptation is to romanticize. “Clean slates existed” slides easily into “the ancient world had a humane answer to debt that we have forgotten,” and from there into a picture of the past as kinder than it was. The record does not support that picture. Debt bondage was real and brutal: people lost land, lost freedom, and were sold away from their families, and the existence of a periodic release does not erase the years of bondage that preceded it or the debts that re-formed after it. A decree that frees the bond-servant every seventh or fiftieth year is evidence of how routinely people fell into bondage in between. The clean slate is a measure of the disease, not proof of a cure, and reading it as a golden age sentimentalizes a system that ground human beings into collateral.

The opposite temptation is to dismiss. Because Hudson’s strong thesis is contested, a careless reader might conclude the whole subject is soft, that the jubilees are a romantic projection and the decrees barely mattered. That is just as wrong. The decrees are documents, not legends. Rulers really did proclaim release, debtors really were freed, and the mechanism really did serve a state’s interest in a free population. The contested part is the scope, not the existence, and collapsing the two by throwing out the institution along with the strong claim discards solid evidence to avoid an over-reach.

The defensible position sits between, and it is precise rather than vague. Interest-bearing debt before coin: established. Debt bondage as a real and dangerous force: established. Royal clean-slate decrees as an attested tool of government: established. The clean slate as a regular, enforced, central institution across these societies: Hudson’s thesis, important, evidence-based, and not ratified by the field. Each of those four sits on its own footing, and the rigor of the module is in not letting a true claim borrow weight from a contested one, or a contested claim borrow respectability from a true one.

Check yourself

Why does the existence of a periodic debt-release decree count as evidence that debt bondage was a recurring problem, rather than as evidence that it was rare?

Show answer

A society does not legislate a periodic release for a problem that does not keep recurring. A decree that frees the debt-bound every seventh or fiftieth year, or that a new king proclaims on accession, is built to address bondage that has accumulated since the last release, which means people were sliding into it continuously in between. The release is a recurring remedy because the affliction was recurring. So the clean slate measures how routinely debt at interest drove free people into bondage; it is a sign of the pressure, not a sign that the pressure was unusual or mild.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Separate the floors. Lay out, in your own words, the attested lower floor (what the documents establish) and the contested upper floor (Hudson’s strong thesis) on the clean slate. Then argue what kind of new evidence, if it existed, would move a claim from the lower floor to the upper one, and why the surviving record makes that hard.

  2. Debt before coin and the credit theory. Interest-bearing debt is older than coinage. Argue whether that chronology strengthens the credit theory’s claim that debt is primary, or whether a metallist can absorb it (recall that the debts were reckoned in weighed silver, a commodity). State precisely what the chronology does and does not settle.

  3. The two temptations. This module warns against both romanticizing the clean slate and dismissing it. Pick the temptation you find more seductive, explain why it is tempting, and write the single sentence that, in your view, most precisely holds the honest middle.

  4. Read the law for what it couples. Leviticus 25 places a ban on charging the poor interest in the same chapter as the fiftieth-year release. Argue what that pairing suggests about how the ancient lawgiver understood the cause of bondage, and whether a command in a text is evidence of a practice in the world.

What a strong response does
  • Keeps the attested existence of the decrees distinct from Hudson’s strong recurrence/centrality thesis, and attributes the strong thesis to Hudson by name rather than treating “regular enforced jubilees” as established fact.
  • Treats the surviving record as evidence to be weighed (thin, uneven, partly normative-not-descriptive), not as either proof or fantasy.
  • Refuses both the romantic reading (the past as humane) and the dismissive one (the jubilees as myth), and can say precisely what each gets wrong.
  • On the chronology, states what “debt at interest before coin” actually bears on, and recalls that the silver denomination keeps a metallist reading available, rather than declaring the credit theory the winner.
  • Stays inside the evidence and the argument as built here and in modules 4, 5, and 6; makes no claim a later module has not yet earned.

The story

Around 594 BCE, in a city already old, an Athenian named Solon was handed power to fix a state coming apart at the seam where debt met liberty.

Attica’s free farmers had been falling, household by household, into the creditors’ hands. Many worked land that was no longer theirs, marked by debt-stones set in the fields, owing a share of what they grew. Others had pledged their own bodies against what they owed, and when the debt outran the harvest they were taken: enslaved on the spot, or sold across the sea to a foreign owner, gone from Attica with no way home. The free population that a city depended on for its farmers and its soldiers was draining into bondage, and the pressure had built toward open conflict between the indebted many and the creditor few. Into that, the Athenians gave Solon authority to legislate a way out.

What he enacted, the sources call the seisachtheia, the shaking off of burdens. It was a clean slate. Solon cancelled the standing debts that had reduced citizens to working others’ land and pledging their persons. He pulled up the debt-stones. He freed Athenians who had been enslaved for debt inside Attica, and he is remembered for working to recover and return some who had been sold abroad. And he did the thing the Mesopotamian decrees did not always reach: he barred the practice going forward, outlawing loans secured on the debtor’s own body, so that an Athenian could no longer be enslaved for debt at all. The remedy was not only retrospective release but a change in the rules that had manufactured the bondage.

The record here is unusually firm, as ancient things go. Solon is a historical figure, his reforms are described in Aristotle’s Constitution of the Athenians and in Plutarch’s life of him, and the seisachtheia is among the better-attested acts of early Greek statecraft. The date is held loosely, around 594 BCE, and the exact figures and any words put in Solon’s mouth are not the solid part; the act and its shape are. What the episode shows is the module’s argument standing outside Mesopotamia entirely. Here is a clean slate in the Greek world, near the very birth of coinage, enacted by a named lawgiver for the named purpose of stopping a free population from collapsing into peonage. It is the strongest kind of confirmation that the clean slate was a real, recurring political tool rather than a single people’s ritual, and it carries the qualification in its own body: even this well-attested case is dated only roughly and known only through later writers, which is exactly the kind of evidence on which the larger, system-wide claim about how regular such releases were has to be built, and exactly why that larger claim stays contested.
