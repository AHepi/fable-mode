Module 1 of 10

The Question We Left Open

What you'll be able to do
  • Restate the open question precisely: is money fundamentally a commodity, or a unit of account and a measure of debt.
  • Distinguish Schumpeter's monetary analysis from real analysis and assign the commodity and credit theories to the right side.
  • Explain the course's contract: steelman both sides at full strength and leave the question open where the field has not closed it.

The hook

The barter story is dead, and you helped bury it. The textbook sequence ran clean: first people swapped goods directly, then, tired of hunting for someone who wanted their grain and happened to be selling the sandals they needed, they hit on money to grease the trade. No society has ever been found that worked that way. The double coincidence of wants, the direct goods-for-goods swap and its central problem, turned out to be a problem invented to be solved, a piece of conjectural history reasoned out rather than dug from the ground. Coins arrive thousands of years after the ledgers. The sequence is a myth.

Killing the sequence felt like killing the commodity theory along with it. It was not. A myth about where money came from is one thing; a claim about what money is is another, and the second claim walked out of the wreckage of the first unharmed. The deeper question, the one the intro course saw clearly and chose not to settle, is still standing exactly where it stood: is money at bottom a special commodity that trade threw up, or is it a unit of account and a measure of debt that law and the state instituted? That is the question this course is for. The work ahead is to take each answer at its strongest, hold them side by side, and find out how far the evidence actually carries.

Context & background

The intro course left two theories on the table, named but not judged. The commodity theory, or metallism, treats money as a special commodity grown out of trade, valued for what it is made of before it is valued as money. The credit theory, or chartalism, treats money as a unit of account and a measure of debt, a way of reckoning obligations that needs no precious substance behind it. Between them sits the hinge the intro course drilled hardest: the unit of account, the measuring-stick that prices and debts are reckoned in, is a different thing from the medium of exchange, the object that changes hands. Mesopotamian scribes kept prices and debts in a standard measure with no coin moving at all. That is money-of-account, and its antiquity is the credit theory’s strongest card.

Here is where the intro reader is most likely to over-reach. It is tempting to read “money-of-account is ancient, coins are late” as “the credit theory wins.” It does not, and seeing why is the work of this course. The Mesopotamian standard was denominated in silver, and silver is a commodity. A metallist looks at the same ledgers and sees a market doing exactly what the commodity theory predicts: settling on its most useful tradeable good as the thing to measure value in. So the early record, which seems to hand the credit theory a victory, hands it instead a harder and more interesting fight. This course is built around taking that fight seriously, which means two commitments that pull against each other and must both hold.

The first commitment is to be sharper than the intro course. Sharper means meeting the strongest form of each argument, not the convenient one. The intro course could afford to leave the silver problem alone; this one cannot, and module 5 is built entirely around it. The second commitment is to be more qualified. Qualified does not mean vague. It means stating with precision what the evidence can bear and what it cannot, attributing every bold claim to the person who made it, and refusing the tidy verdict the field itself refuses. Where two theories each explain part of the record and strain on the rest, the honest move is to say so and to say exactly where each one bends. Confidence about what is known; an equal confidence about what is not.

Narrative / exposition

A course that holds two live theories at once needs a way to keep them straight, or every argument blurs into a single muddle of “money stuff.” The cleanest tool for the job belongs to Joseph Schumpeter, the Austrian-born economist and historian of economics, and it is the frame this whole course will sort its arguments into.

Schumpeter drew a line between two ways of doing economic theory, and named them real analysis and monetary analysis. Real analysis treats money as a veil. Strip the veil away, the thought runs, and underneath you find an economy that is essentially barter: people exchanging goods for goods, with money a convenient technical layer over the top that changes nothing fundamental about how the system works. On this view money is real, useful, worth studying, but theoretically secondary. It sits on top of the real economy without being part of its essential structure. This is the natural home of the commodity theory. If money is a commodity the market converged on to make swapping easier, then money is precisely a tool laid over an exchange economy that could, in principle, be described without it.

Monetary analysis denies the veil. On this view money and credit are part of the essential structure of the economy, not a layer you can peel back to find the “real” thing underneath. The obligations, the units of account, the credit relations are not a convenience bolted onto barter; they are constitutive, woven into what the economy is. Take them away and you do not reveal a barter economy. You reveal nothing, because the economy was monetary all the way down. This is the home of the credit and state theories. If money is fundamentally a unit of account and a measure of debt, then money is not a veil over the real economy. It is part of what makes the economy real.

That gives two columns. Real analysis, money as a veil, the commodity theory’s home. Monetary analysis, money as essential structure, the credit theory’s home. Every argument the course meets can be sorted into one column or the other, and the test of a good argument is often which column it refuses to sit in. The frame is Schumpeter’s, and it is worth stressing that it is a frame, not a verdict. Schumpeter sorted the quarrel; he did not end it. Naming the two camps is not the same as declaring one of them right, and a reader who treats “monetary analysis” as the obviously correct label has already made the mistake this course exists to prevent.

Check yourself

Before reading on, answer from memory. In Schumpeter’s distinction, which kind of analysis treats money as a veil over an essentially barter economy, and which theory of money’s nature does that side house?

Show answer

Real analysis treats money as a veil: peel it away and you find an economy that is essentially barter underneath, with money a convenient technical layer that changes nothing fundamental. That side houses the commodity theory (metallism), money as a special tradeable commodity laid over an exchange economy. Monetary analysis is the opposite side: money and credit are part of the essential structure, not a veil, and that is the home of the credit and state theories (chartalism).

Primary source

Schumpeter set the distinction out in his History of Economic Analysis (1954). The line below follows the sense of how he framed real analysis, the position he was naming in order to argue against it. It is given as a labelled paraphrase, not a transcription, because the exact wording across his long discussion is not reproduced here.

Real analysis proceeds on the principle that all the essential phenomena of economic life can be described in terms of goods and services, of decisions about them and of relations between them. Money enters the picture only as a technical device adopted to facilitate transactions, a device that does not affect the economic process, which behaves in the same way as it would in a barter economy: this is essentially what the concept of Neutral Money implies. Money has been called a “garb” or “veil” over the things that really matter.

Joseph Schumpeter, History of Economic Analysis (1954), on real analysis, paraphrased

The paraphrase states a position whole, in that position’s own terms, before any objection: money is a technical device, the process runs as it would under barter, the metaphor is a veil. That is real analysis given its fair statement. Schumpeter’s own sympathies leaned the other way, toward monetary analysis, but the frame he built is usable by both sides precisely because he stated each one squarely. That is the discipline this course will hold to from here. A position gets its strongest fair hearing before it gets argued with, and the reader who only ever meets a theory through its critics has been cheated.

Analysis & interpretation

The contract this course operates under follows from one fact: the field has not settled the question, and pretending otherwise would be a lie dressed as pedagogy. So the contract has three clauses, and they are binding on every module that follows.

First, steelman before you strike. The strongest honest version of each position comes first, stated in that position’s own terms, before any criticism touches it. Menger and Mises get the commodity theory’s best case before a single credit-theorist replies; Innes, Knapp, Keynes, and Lerner get the credit and state theories’ best case before the metallist rejoinder. A view criticized before it has been fairly stated is a strawman, and a course built on strawmen teaches the reader to win arguments cheaply, which is the opposite of what an education is for.

Second, attribute everything. The temptation in a money course is to flatten six different thinkers into one “chartalist” voice, as though Innes and Knapp and Keynes and Lerner all said the same thing. They did not. Innes put private credit first; Knapp put the state’s naming power first; Keynes argued for the conceptual priority of the money of account; Lerner argued that tax-acceptance gives a token its value. Those are four different claims about what makes money money, and collapsing them loses exactly the information the course is trying to hand over. Each claim stays pinned to the person who made it. The same holds for the bold readings on every side: when an argument outruns the cautious consensus, it is named as someone’s argument, not smuggled in as fact.

Third, crown no winner where the field has not. This is the hardest clause and the one the reader will most want broken. By the end you will know the metallist case and the chartalist case at full strength, and you will want a verdict. On the central question, what money fundamentally is, there is no verdict to give, because the evidence genuinely underdetermines the answer. Each theory explains part of the record and strains on the rest. Some of this course’s conclusions will be firm: the barter sequence is dead, coins are late and state-stamped, banks create money by lending. Others will be an honest, precise “unsettled,” and learning to hold that without flinching is itself one of the course’s objectives. A capable reader can carry two live theories at once. That is the reader this course is written for.

Check yourself

From memory: the course promises to “steelman” each theory. State what that requires in practice, and why a course that skips it discredits itself.

Show answer

Steelmanning requires stating the strongest honest version of a position, in that position’s own terms, before any criticism is brought against it. In practice that means giving Menger and Mises the commodity theory’s best case before any credit-theory reply, and giving Innes, Knapp, Keynes, and Lerner the credit and state theories’ best case before any metallist rejoinder. A course that criticizes a view before stating it fairly is attacking a strawman, which trains the reader to defeat weak versions of real arguments and leaves them unable to judge the strong ones. The credibility of a criticism rests on the strength of the position it answers.

Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

  1. Sort an argument into a column. Take a claim you already hold about money (that it is “really” gold-backed value, that it is “just” numbers in a bank’s computers, anything). Decide whether it belongs to Schumpeter’s real analysis or his monetary analysis, and defend the placement. Then say what the other column would need to be true for your claim to fail.

  2. The cost of a clean verdict. This course refuses to declare a victor between metallism and chartalism. Argue either that this refusal is intellectual honesty or that it is a dodge, and in either case name the precise price the course pays for the position you assign it.

  3. Why the silver problem bites. In your own words, explain why “money-of-account is ancient and coins are late” does not by itself settle the case for the credit theory. What does the metallist see in the same early ledgers, and what would the credit theory have to add to turn a shift into a settlement?

  4. Steelman something you reject. Pick the theory of money you find less plausible and write its strongest honest case, in its own terms, with no rebuttal attached. Then say what writing it changed, if anything, about how plausible you find it.

What a strong response does
  • Uses Schumpeter’s distinction accurately as a sorting tool, not as a verdict, and never treats “monetary analysis” as a synonym for “correct.”
  • Keeps the commodity and credit theories genuinely distinct, and attributes specific claims (saleability, the regression theorem, the priority of the money of account, tax-acceptance) to the right thinkers rather than blurring them into one position.
  • States the strongest honest form of a view before arguing with it, and can tell the difference between steelmanning a position and merely summarizing it.
  • Holds the open question open: states precisely what the evidence shifts and what it does not, rather than reaching for a tidy winner the course has not earned.
  • Stays inside the evidence and the frame as established here, making no claim that later modules have not yet built.

The story

He was working on it when he died, and he had been working on it for years.

Joseph Schumpeter died in January 1950, at his home in Connecticut, with the manuscript of a book unfinished on his desk. The book was History of Economic Analysis, his attempt to trace the whole effort of human beings to understand the economy, from the ancient world forward across the centuries. It was vast, more than a thousand pages even in the state he left it, and it was a mess: major sections scattered across three different studies, drafts overlapping drafts, large stretches written in an archaic German shorthand only he could read fluently. Somewhere in that pile he had drawn the line between real analysis and monetary analysis, the line that names the quarrel this course is built around. He never saw it in print.

The person who got it into print was Elizabeth Boody Schumpeter, his third wife and herself a trained economist. She had been working to bring order to the chaotic manuscript since around 1938, more than a decade before his death, trying to integrate the scattered drafts and polish what could be polished. The task after he died was brutal. She had to reconstruct his intentions from fragments, decide what a half-finished argument was reaching for, and resist the urge to finish his sentences for him in places where finishing them would have meant inventing his views. She did it while she was dying. A malignancy took her before the book appeared. The title page, when History of Economic Analysis was finally published in 1954, reads “Edited from manuscript by Elizabeth Boody Schumpeter,” and by then both Schumpeters were dead.

The book that resulted is unfinished, and it admits it. It breaks off, leaves arguments open, marks where the author would have said more. That is the right shape for the frame this course borrows from it. Schumpeter handed down two columns to sort the quarrel into, real analysis and monetary analysis, money as a veil and money as essential structure, and he handed them down inside a book he could not complete, edited into the world by a woman racing her own death to do it. The frame organizes the question. It does not close it. A monumental, unfinished book, drawing the very distinction that lets us hold the metallist and the chartalist apart and then declining to declare which one is right, is exactly the inheritance a course about an unsettled question should want. The tools are sharp. The verdict is still open. That is where this course begins, and it is, honestly, where it will end.
