What Money Actually Is

A rigorous undergraduate sequel to The Myth of Barter that adjudicates what money fundamentally is, commodity or claim, giving the commodity and credit theories their strongest cases and refusing the verdict the evidence will not support.

Humanities Intermediate 10 modules ~5h Path · Economics as History, Not Nature
Before you start
  • Familiarity with the barter-myth critique and basic money vocabulary (unit of account vs. medium of exchange, money-of-account, commodity theory vs. credit theory, fiat, coinage as late)

The Myth of Barter buried a story: that money began as a fix for the awkwardness of swapping goods. Killing that story felt like settling the question of what money is. It did not. A myth about where money came from is one thing; a claim about what money fundamentally is is another, and the deeper question walked out of the wreckage unharmed. Is money at bottom a special commodity that trade threw up, or a unit of account and a measure of debt that law and the state instituted?

This course adjudicates that question at the level the intro course could not. It takes each answer at its strongest: the commodity theory of Menger and Mises, with its real case for why metal won and why a thing of intrinsic worth does work among strangers that no IOU can; and the credit and state theories of Innes, Knapp, Keynes, Lerner, Grierson, and Ingham, kept carefully distinct rather than blurred into one “chartalist” voice. Then it brings the hardest evidence to bear on both, including the objection the intro course left alone: that the ancient standard was denominated in silver, a commodity, so money-of-account before coins does not by itself decide anything. The answer, that the silver ratios were set by temple, palace, and law rather than by a market, shifts the case without settling it.

You will weigh coinage and the state, debt and the clean slate, the over-reaching claim that society rests on a primordial debt, and the money created in your own bank account when a loan is typed into existence. The reward is not a verdict. It is the rarer thing an education is actually for: the two best theories of money held at full strength, a precise sense of what the evidence can and cannot bear, and the discipline to leave open a question the field has honestly left open. Some of what you learn will be firm. The rest will be a sharp, well-mapped “unsettled,” and knowing the difference is the point.

Modules

  1. 01 The Question We Left Open The intro course killed the barter sequence and left the deeper question open, and a frame from Schumpeter sorts the whole quarrel into two columns. 26 min
  2. 02 The Commodity Theory at Full Strength Money as the most saleable commodity the market converged on, grounded by Mises's regression theorem, with the real case for why metal won. 30 min
  3. 03 Money Among Strangers Where no shared sovereign, no shared law, and no ongoing relationship anchors a debt, a thing with intrinsic acceptability does work an IOU cannot, and that is the commodity theory's strongest empirical ground. 26 min
  4. 04 Money as Credit, Money as the State's Word Six thinkers get mashed into one chartalist voice, and the module's whole job is to lay their distinct claims side by side and keep them apart. 32 min
  5. 05 The Silver Problem The Mesopotamian standard was denominated in silver, a commodity, so unit-of-account-before-coins does not by itself refute the commodity theory, and the reply is that the standard was set by law, not found by a market. 34 min
  6. 06 Coins, Soldiers, and Taxes The first coins are a datable, state-stamped fact, and a bolder causal story about armies, taxes, and slaves is Graeber's reading, not the chronology. 28 min
  7. 07 Debt, Interest, and the Clean Slate Interest-bearing debt is older than coin, and the royal debt cancellations were real, but how regular and far-reaching they were is a claim with one owner's name on it. 30 min
  8. 08 The Debt That Founds the World The boldest claim in the field says society itself rests on an original debt to gods or ancestors, and it is exactly where an attractive reading outruns the evidence the record can supply. 26 min
  9. 09 The Money in Your Account Modern money is created when banks lend, a central bank of record says so, and the contrast with metal-backed money is where the ancient argument reaches the balance in your account, kept clear of policy. 30 min
  10. 10 What Is Settled, What Is Open An honest ledger of what the evidence has settled and what it has not, and a method for weighing the next confident claim about what money really is. 26 min
