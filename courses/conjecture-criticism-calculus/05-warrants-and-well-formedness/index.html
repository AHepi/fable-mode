Warrants and Well-Formedness

What you'll be able to do
  • Distinguish demonstrative from argumentative warrants and state the role each plays in an attack edge.
  • Explain the validity-node closure rule using a concrete worked example before the abstract statement.
  • State the four well-formedness conditions from §3.2.
  • Identify whether a given epistemic state is well-formed, citing which condition would be violated if not.

An attack edge in the system is never a bare accusation. If artifact kkk attacks artifact aaa, something must make the attack stick — a piece of registered, content-bearing, itself-attackable justification called a warrant. Without it, the edge cannot exist. This module defines the two kinds of warrant, explains the subtlest condition they carry, and states when an entire epistemic state qualifies as well-formed.

One callback each, then we build: a verdict (pass / fail / overrun) was introduced in module 2; the attack graph att⊆A×A\mathrm{att} \subseteq A \times Aatt⊆A×A was built in module 4.

Why bare verdicts are not enough

Suppose a commitment κ\kappaκ runs on artifact aaa and the verdict is V(κ,a)=failV(\kappa, a) = \text{fail}V(κ,a)=fail. You might think the verdict alone entitles you to place an edge k→ak \to ak→a in the attack graph. But consider what the defender of aaa would want to challenge. They might grant the verdict and still dispute whether the test was relevant to aaa‘s claim, or whether it was correctly designed. If the verdict is the whole warrant, there is nothing for them to get their hands on. Criticism has no target.

The solution is to make every attack edge carry an artifact — something with content, something with a commitment interface, something that can itself be attacked. Criticism becomes contentful rather than a bare datum, and the uniformity principle from module 3 (“whatever can attack can be attacked”) extends all the way down: even the justification for an attack is fair game.

There are two kinds of warrant, corresponding to two very different styles of criticism.

Definition (Warrants)

Definition 3.4. An attack edge (k,a)∈att(k, a) \in \mathrm{att}(k,a)∈att must carry a warrant of one of two types:

  • Demonstrative: kkk packages a failing verdict — a commitment κ\kappaκ, its trace, and V(κ,a)=failV(\kappa, a) = \text{fail}V(κ,a)=fail — together with a dependence on a validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), an artifact asserting that the test is sound and relevant. By a closure rule: any attacker of ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) attacks kkk.

  • Argumentative: kkk is an explanatory artifact whose commitments include a claim about aaa‘s deficiency.

Both kinds are themselves artifacts and hence attackable, so criticism is always contentful and never a bare verdict.

Bridge: unpacking Definition 3.4

Take each piece in turn.

Demonstrative means the attack is grounded in a test result. The warrant bundles three things: the commitment κ=(τ,β)\kappa = (\tau, \beta)κ=(τ,β) (the test program and its budget), the trace of running it (the evidence that the run happened), and the verdict V(κ,a)=failV(\kappa, a) = \text{fail}V(κ,a)=fail. Together they constitute a self-contained, checkable package — anyone can, in principle, rerun κ\kappaκ on c(a)c(a)c(a) and verify the verdict.

But the package also depends on a validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). This is an artifact whose content asserts something like: “commitment κ\kappaκ is a sound and relevant test for the kind of claim aaa makes.” It is the artifact that vouches for the test itself. Without ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), the demonstrative warrant could be challenged by attacking the test’s soundness — and there would be no artifact in the system representing that challenge target. ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) supplies it.

Argumentative means the attack doesn’t package a test result at all. Instead, kkk is an explanatory artifact — a piece of prose, a formal argument, a citation — whose own commitments include a claim that aaa has some deficiency. The critic constructs an artifact that argues aaa is inadequate, rather than testing aaa and reporting failure.

Both kinds are artifacts: they have content c(k)c(k)c(k) and a commitment interface I(k)I(k)I(k). Both sit inside AAA. Both can be attacked.

The closure rule: a worked example first

The most subtle part of Definition 3.4 is the closure rule for demonstrative warrants:

any attacker of ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) attacks kkk.

Before stating this abstractly, here is the concrete situation that makes it necessary.

Setup. Say artifact aaa is a conjecture: “The reaction rate doubles with each 10°C rise.” Artifact kkk attacks aaa demonstratively: kkk packages a commitment κ\kappaκ (a lab test) with verdict V(κ,a)=failV(\kappa, a) = \text{fail}V(κ,a)=fail, and kkk depends on validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), an artifact whose content asserts: “this lab test reliably measures reaction rate.”

Now suppose artifact jjj attacks ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). Perhaps jjj argues that the lab test is miscalibrated and does not, in fact, reliably measure reaction rate. In plain terms, jjj is saying: the test kkk used to attack aaa was itself unsound.

What should happen to kkk?

If jjj‘s attack on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) succeeds, the whole basis of kkk‘s attack on aaa is undermined. kkk claimed aaa fails a sound test; jjj says the test is not sound. So jjj should count as attacking kkk as well — not just the validity node. The edge j→kj \to kj→k should exist.

This is exactly what the closure rule says: because jjj attacks ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) and kkk carries a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), the system records (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att automatically.

The picture:

j⟶ν(κ)(j attacks the validity node)j \longrightarrow \nu(\kappa) \qquad \text{(}j\text{ attacks the validity node)}j⟶ν(κ)(j attacks the validity node) j⟶k(closure: j therefore attacks k)j \longrightarrow k \qquad \text{(closure: }j\text{ therefore attacks }k\text{)}j⟶k(closure: j therefore attacks k) k⟶a(k’s demonstrative attack on a)k \longrightarrow a \qquad \text{(}k\text{'s demonstrative attack on }a\text{)}k⟶a(k’s demonstrative attack on a)

The defender of aaa does not need a separate artifact disputing kkk directly. If they can attack ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) — the soundness of the test — closure propagates that attack to kkk automatically, and the adjudicator (module 7) will take it into account.

Now the abstract rule. Let kkk carry a demonstrative warrant depending on validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). For any artifact jjj such that (j,ν(κ))∈att(j, \nu(\kappa)) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν(κ))∈att, the closure rule adds (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att. Well-formedness requires this hold in every registered demonstrative attack.

Check yourself: why does closure matter?

Without the closure rule, an attacker of the validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) would undermine the soundness of the test but have no formal connection to the warrant-carrier kkk. The attack graph would represent j→ν(κ)j \to \nu(\kappa)j→ν(κ) but not j→kj \to kj→k, so the adjudicator’s computation would treat kkk as unaffected — even though its evidential basis has been challenged. Closure ensures the attack graph actually reflects the dependency: undermining the test undermines the attestation of failure.

Well-formedness

We now have the ingredients to say when an epistemic state S=(A,Π,addr,att,W)S = (A, \Pi, \mathrm{addr}, \mathrm{att}, W)S=(A,Π,addr,att,W) is well-formed — when the whole configuration is internally consistent.

Definition (Well-Formedness)

A state SSS is well-formed if and only if all four conditions hold:

  1. Warrant registration. Every attack edge (k,a)∈att(k, a) \in \mathrm{att}(k,a)∈att carries a warrant W(k,a)W(k, a)W(k,a) of the kind specified in Definition 3.4 — either demonstrative or argumentative.

  2. Commitment-schema criteria. For every problem π∈Π\pi \in \Piπ∈Π, the criteria set Critπ\mathrm{Crit}_\piCritπ​ consists of commitment schemas — specifications that can be instantiated as commitments against candidate artifacts.

  3. Declared addressing. Every addr\mathrm{addr}addr pair (a,π)(a, \pi)(a,π) is declared by an addressing artifact: some artifact in AAA whose content records that aaa addresses π\piπ. Addressing is not implicit.

  4. Validity-node closure. For every demonstrative warrant carried by some kkk depending on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ): for every jjj with (j,ν(κ))∈att(j, \nu(\kappa)) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν(κ))∈att, we also have (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att.

Bridge: what each condition is doing

Condition 1 is the core of this module: no attack edge exists without a warrant. An edge in att\mathrm{att}att without a corresponding entry in WWW would be a bare accusation — the kind of thing Definition 3.4 rules out.

Condition 2 ensures that problems carry usable criteria. A criterion that can’t be instantiated as a commitment — that can’t produce a verdict — can’t drive the system forward. This is the counterpart to I(a)I(a)I(a) being finite and concrete.

Condition 3 tightens the addressing relation. An artifact can’t silently “count” as addressing a problem; there must be an artifact recording the address. This keeps the system’s bookkeeping transparent and attackable — the addressing claim is itself a piece of content.

Condition 4 is the closure rule, promoted from a property of individual warrants to a requirement on the whole state: not just that individual warrants respect the rule, but that the attack graph has been completed to reflect all closure-implied edges.

Check yourself: which condition does a state violate?

Scenario. You have artifacts {a,k,j,ν}\{a, k, j, \nu\}{a,k,j,ν} in AAA with kkk attacking aaa demonstratively (depending on validity node ν\nuν), and jjj attacking ν\nuν — but the edge (j,k)(j, k)(j,k) is missing from att\mathrm{att}att.

Answer. Condition 4 (validity-node closure) is violated. The state has (j,ν)∈att(j, \nu) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν)∈att and kkk depends on ν\nuν, so closure requires (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att. Its absence makes the state ill-formed.

Worked Example (Closure and Well-Formedness Check)

Setup. Let A={a,k,ν,j}A = \{a, k, \nu, j\}A={a,k,ν,j} with:

  • aaa: a conjecture addressed to problem π\piπ.
  • kkk: a demonstrative attack on aaa, packaging a failing verdict for commitment κ\kappaκ, depending on validity node ν\nuν.
  • ν\nuν: an artifact asserting “commitment κ\kappaκ is a sound and relevant test for aaa.”
  • jjj: an argumentative attack on ν\nuν, arguing ν\nuν‘s content is mistaken — κ\kappaκ is not a reliable test.

Warrant map. W(k,a)=(κ, V(κ,a)=fail, ν)W(k, a) = (\kappa,\, V(\kappa, a) = \text{fail},\, \nu)W(k,a)=(κ,V(κ,a)=fail,ν). W(j,ν)=W(j, \nu) = W(j,ν)= (an argumentative warrant: jjj‘s commitments claim ν\nuν is wrong).

Current attack relation. att={(k,a), (j,ν)}\mathrm{att} = \{(k, a),\, (j, \nu)\}att={(k,a),(j,ν)}.

Well-formedness check.

  • Condition 1: (k,a)(k,a)(k,a) has warrant W(k,a)W(k,a)W(k,a) ✓; (j,ν)(j, \nu)(j,ν) has warrant W(j,ν)W(j,\nu)W(j,ν) ✓.
  • Condition 2: assume Critπ\mathrm{Crit}_\piCritπ​ consists of commitment schemas ✓.
  • Condition 3: assume addr\mathrm{addr}addr pairs are declared ✓.
  • Condition 4: kkk depends on ν\nuν, and (j,ν)∈att(j, \nu) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν)∈att. So closure requires (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att. But (j,k)∉att(j, k) \notin \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈/att.

Verdict. The state is ill-formed — condition 4 fails.

Fix. Add (j,k)(j, k)(j,k) to att\mathrm{att}att with a warrant (the closure rule does not require a new independent warrant for (j,k)(j,k)(j,k); the rule registers the edge as a structural consequence). After adding it:

att={(k,a), (j,ν), (j,k)}\mathrm{att} = \{(k, a),\, (j, \nu),\, (j, k)\}att={(k,a),(j,ν),(j,k)}

Now all four conditions hold. The state is well-formed.

Reading the corrected graph. jjj attacks both ν\nuν (the test’s validity certificate) and kkk (the attack on aaa). If jjj is later found unattacked — or is defended by some further artifact — the adjudicator (module 7) will accept jjj, refute kkk (attacked by accepted jjj), and accept aaa (no longer attacked by an accepted artifact). The closure rule is what makes this chain computable from the graph alone.

Exercises

1. An attack (k,a)(k, a)(k,a) has a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). Artifact mmm attacks kkk directly (with its own argumentative warrant). Does the closure rule add any new edges? Explain.

Show solution

No new edges are added by the closure rule. The closure rule fires only when an artifact attacks ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) — the validity node — not when it attacks kkk directly. Here m→km \to km→k exists by an independent argumentative warrant, not via ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). Closure is a rule about attacks on the validity node propagating to the warrant-carrier; attacks on the warrant-carrier itself are separate events, already recorded in att\mathrm{att}att.

2. A state has three problems π1,π2,π3\pi_1, \pi_2, \pi_3π1​,π2​,π3​. The criteria of π1\pi_1π1​ are commitment schemas. The criteria of π2\pi_2π2​ include a natural-language sentence “the conjecture should be elegant.” The criteria of π3\pi_3π3​ are empty (Critπ3=∅\mathrm{Crit}_{\pi_3} = \emptysetCritπ3​​=∅). Which problems, if any, cause the state to be ill-formed?

Show solution

π2\pi_2π2​ violates condition 2. “The conjecture should be elegant” is not a commitment schema — it cannot be instantiated as a commitment κ=(τ,β)\kappa = (\tau, \beta)κ=(τ,β) that produces a verdict. A criterion must be a schema that a test program can evaluate. (π3\pi_3π3​ with empty criteria does not violate condition 2: an empty set of commitment schemas is still a set of commitment schemas, just vacuously so. A problem with no criteria yet is fine — criteria grow as the problem evolves.)

3. (Conceptual.) A skeptic says: “The closure rule is unfair. If jjj attacks the validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), that’s a separate matter from kkk‘s attack on aaa. Why should jjj get credit for attacking kkk?” Give the best reply.

Show solution

The reply: kkk‘s attack on aaa is not independent of ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ). kkk is a demonstrative warrant precisely because it packages a test result and depends on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) to establish the test’s soundness. If ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) is itself under attack — if there is a live challenge to whether κ\kappaκ is a sound and relevant test — then the evidential basis of kkk‘s attack on aaa is undermined. The closure rule makes this dependency explicit in the attack graph, so the adjudicator can compute statuses correctly. Without closure, kkk would survive as an accepted artifact even when its own foundation is challenged, which would be a bookkeeping lie. “Separate matter” is the misconception: demonstrative attacks are not self-contained; they inherit vulnerability from the validity nodes they rely on.

4. State SSS has att={(k1,a),(k2,a),(j,ν1)}\mathrm{att} = \{(k_1, a), (k_2, a), (j, \nu_1)\}att={(k1​,a),(k2​,a),(j,ν1​)} where k1k_1k1​ is demonstrative (depending on ν1\nu_1ν1​) and k2k_2k2​ is argumentative. Is SSS well-formed? If not, state exactly what edge is missing and why.

Show solution

SSS is ill-formed. The demonstrative warrant of k1k_1k1​ depends on ν1\nu_1ν1​, and (j,ν1)∈att(j, \nu_1) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν1​)∈att. By the closure rule (condition 4), (j,k1)(j, k_1)(j,k1​) must also be in att\mathrm{att}att. It is not, so condition 4 fails. The argumentative attack k2→ak_2 \to ak2​→a is unaffected — k2k_2k2​ does not depend on ν1\nu_1ν1​, so closure generates no obligation for k2k_2k2​. The fix: add (j,k1)(j, k_1)(j,k1​) to att\mathrm{att}att with a warrant recording the structural closure.

5. (Stretch.) Suppose kkk carries a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), and ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) itself carries a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ′)\nu(\kappa')ν(κ′) — the soundness of κ\kappaκ is itself justified by a higher-level test. Now jjj attacks ν(κ′)\nu(\kappa')ν(κ′). What edges does the closure rule require?

Show solution

Apply the rule once for each dependency. ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) carries a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ′)\nu(\kappa')ν(κ′), so: (j,ν(κ′))∈att(j, \nu(\kappa')) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν(κ′))∈att forces (j,ν(κ))∈att(j, \nu(\kappa)) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν(κ))∈att (closure for the warrant carried by ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ)). Now ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) is attacked by jjj, and kkk carries a demonstrative warrant depending on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ), so: (j,ν(κ))∈att(j, \nu(\kappa)) \in \mathrm{att}(j,ν(κ))∈att forces (j,k)∈att(j, k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att. In total, the closure rule requires adding both (j,ν(κ))(j, \nu(\kappa))(j,ν(κ)) and (j,k)(j, k)(j,k) to att\mathrm{att}att. The rule propagates along chains of demonstrative dependency: an attack on a validity node propagates to every warrant-carrier that depends on it, transitively.

Recap

Every edge in the attack graph att\mathrm{att}att must carry a warrant — demonstrative (a packaged failing verdict plus a validity node ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ)) or argumentative (an explanatory artifact claiming deficiency). The validity node is the artifact that vouches for a test’s soundness, and the closure rule makes attacks on ν(κ)\nu(\kappa)ν(κ) automatically propagate to any warrant-carrier that depends on it, keeping the graph honest about evidential dependencies. An epistemic state is well-formed when all four conditions hold: every attack is warranted, problem criteria are commitment schemas, addressing is declared, and closure is complete. These conditions are what module 6’s transition rules (Conj) and (Crit) will be designed to preserve.
