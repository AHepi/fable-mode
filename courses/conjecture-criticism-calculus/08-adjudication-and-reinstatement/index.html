Module 8 of 11

Adjudication and Reinstatement

What you'll be able to do
  • State the (Adj) transition rule in terms of the grounded extension $G$.
  • Prove the Reinstatement Lemma (3.1) by running the iteration from the empty set.
  • Explain why adjudication is recomputed after every registration rather than once at the end.

A criticism can be wrong. We built the whole system on that fact: a criticism is itself an artifact (module 3), so whatever can attack can be attacked. But we never said what happens to the original idea when its critic is itself knocked down. Does the idea limp on, permanently scarred by an attack that has since collapsed? Or does it come back clean?

This module answers that, and the answer is sharper than you might expect. You will not have to decide anything. The grounded extension G we built in module 7 already settles it — and it settles it the only way it can. Refute a criticism, and its target is reinstated automatically. Not as a courtesy, not as a second ruling, but as a forced consequence of the fixed point. That is the content of the Reinstatement Lemma, and it is the one full proof this course carries. Everything you need to prove it, you proved by hand last module.

First, though, a smaller point that turns out to matter: when does the system work out who is accepted and who is refuted?

Adjudication is not a final step

Here is the tempting picture. You build up an attack graph — add conjectures, add criticisms, add criticisms of criticisms — and then, at the very end, you run the adjudicator once to read off the verdicts. Inquiry as a long argument with a ruling at the close.

That picture is wrong, and the system is built to make it wrong. Status is not something the graph earns once it stops growing. Status is a function of the current graph, recomputed every time the graph changes. Add one edge and every label is, in principle, up for re-evaluation. An artifact that was accepted a moment ago can be refuted by the next registration; an artifact that was refuted can come back. There is no settling-down phase, because the system never stops being allowed to grow (that is the no-deletion discipline from module 6 cashed out — nothing is removed, but everything is re-read).

So the right picture is a status that tracks the graph in real time. After each (Conj) or (Crit) step, you recompute the grounded extension G from scratch — iterate from \emptyset again — and relabel. That is exactly what the (Adj) rule says.

The (Adj) rule

Recall from module 7: given the attack graph, the grounded extension GGG is the least fixed point of

F(S)={ a:every attacker of a is attacked by some member of S },F(S) = \{\, a : \text{every attacker of } a \text{ is attacked by some member of } S \,\},F(S)={a:every attacker of a is attacked by some member of S},

computed by iterating ∅, F(∅), F(F(∅)), …\emptyset,\, F(\emptyset),\, F(F(\emptyset)),\, \dots∅,F(∅),F(F(∅)),… until it stops changing. The (Adj) rule takes that set and reads three statuses off it.

(Adj) Adjudication. Given the directed graph (A,att)(A, \mathrm{att})(A,att), let the grounded extension GGG be the least fixed point of FFF. Relabel every artifact:

status(a)={accepteda∈G,refuted∃ b∈G:(b,a)∈att,suspendedotherwise.\mathrm{status}(a) = \begin{cases} \text{accepted} & a \in G, \\[2pt] \text{refuted} & \exists\, b \in G : (b, a) \in \mathrm{att}, \\[2pt] \text{suspended} & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}status(a)=⎩⎨⎧​acceptedrefutedsuspended​a∈G,∃b∈G:(b,a)∈att,otherwise.​

Grounded semantics (Dung 1995) is unique and is recomputed after every registration.

Read the three cases against the picture. Accepted is the clean case: aaa is in the grounded extension, so the iteration from ∅\emptyset∅ reached it — every attacker of aaa was knocked down by something already standing. Refuted is the case where some accepted artifact attacks aaa: there is a bbb in GGG with an edge (b,a)(b,a)(b,a), a standing attacker, so aaa is down. Suspended is everything left over — neither in GGG nor attacked by anyone in GGG. That is the artifact caught in an unresolved tangle: not vindicated, not refuted, just undecided under the current graph (a stalemate in a cycle is the usual cause). The three cases are exhaustive and, for grounded semantics, mutually exclusive.

The clause we will lean on is the middle one. “Refuted” does not mean an attacker exists — every conjecture worth its salt has attackers. It means an attacker exists that is itself accepted. Pull that attacker out of GGG, and the artifact is no longer refuted. That is reinstatement, and now we can state it exactly.

Check yourself

In the (Adj) rule, an artifact aaa with three attackers is labelled refuted under exactly what condition?

Show answer

When at least one of its attackers is accepted — i.e. there is some b∈Gb \in Gb∈G with (b,a)∈att(b,a) \in \mathrm{att}(b,a)∈att. The number of attackers is irrelevant; what matters is whether any attacker is itself in the grounded extension. If every attacker of aaa is itself refuted or suspended (none in GGG), then aaa is not refuted by this clause.

The Reinstatement Lemma

The smallest interesting situation is a chain of three: an idea, a criticism of it, and a criticism of the criticism. We name them aaa (the conjecture), kkk (the criticism attacking aaa), and jjj (the criticism attacking kkk), with jjj itself unattacked. The claim is that aaa survives — its critic kkk is taken out by jjj, and aaa comes back.

Lemma (Reinstatement, 3.1). If kkk attacks aaa, jjj attacks kkk, and jjj is unattacked, then {j,a}⊆G\{j, a\} \subseteq G{j,a}⊆G.

Decode the statement before we prove it. The hypotheses are three edges-and-absences in the attack graph: (k,a)∈att(k,a) \in \mathrm{att}(k,a)∈att (kkk attacks aaa), (j,k)∈att(j,k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att (jjj attacks kkk), and no edge points into jjj (jjj is unattacked). The conclusion {j,a}⊆G\{j,a\} \subseteq G{j,a}⊆G says both jjj and aaa land in the grounded extension — so by (Adj), both are accepted, and the critic kkk, attacked by the accepted jjj, is refuted. In one line: refuting a criticism restores its target automatically. The reinstatement is not a new rule we add to the system; it falls out of the fixed point we already have.

The proof is just the iteration from module 7, run on this graph and watched for two steps.

Proof. F(∅)F(\emptyset)F(∅) contains every unattacked artifact — an artifact with no attackers satisfies “every attacker is attacked by some member of SSS” vacuously, for any SSS, including S=∅S = \emptysetS=∅. Since jjj is unattacked, j∈F(∅)j \in F(\emptyset)j∈F(∅).

Next, consider aaa at the following step. The only attacker of aaa is kkk, and jjj attacks kkk. So every attacker of aaa is attacked by a member of {j}\{j\}{j}, and since j∈F(∅)j \in F(\emptyset)j∈F(∅), we have a∈F(F(∅))a \in F(F(\emptyset))a∈F(F(∅)). Because the iteration is non-decreasing and converges to GGG (module 7), F(F(∅))⊆GF(F(\emptyset)) \subseteq GF(F(∅))⊆G. Therefore a∈Ga \in Ga∈G, and with j∈F(∅)⊆Gj \in F(\emptyset) \subseteq Gj∈F(∅)⊆G we conclude {j,a}⊆G\{j, a\} \subseteq G{j,a}⊆G. □\square□

That is the whole proof, and notice what carried it: nothing but the definition of FFF and the fact that the iteration climbs to GGG. The unattacked artifact enters at the first step; the artifact it defends enters at the second. Reinstatement is the second step of the very construction you ran by hand last module.

Check yourself

In the proof, why is j∈F(∅)j \in F(\emptyset)j∈F(∅) — what is the member of ∅\emptyset∅ that attacks each of jjj‘s attackers?

Show answer

There is no such member, and none is needed. jjj has no attackers, so the condition “every attacker of jjj is attacked by some member of ∅\emptyset∅” is vacuously true — there are no attackers to check. This is the same vacuous-truth move that put the unattacked nodes into F(∅)F(\emptyset)F(∅) in module 7. (Contrast kkk: kkk has the attacker jjj, and jjj is attacked by nobody in ∅\emptyset∅, so the condition genuinely fails and k∉F(∅)k \notin F(\emptyset)k∈/F(∅).)

Worked example

Problem. Take the three-node graph the lemma describes: artifacts A={a,k,j}A = \{a, k, j\}A={a,k,j}, where aaa is a conjecture, kkk is a criticism with (k,a)∈att(k,a) \in \mathrm{att}(k,a)∈att, and jjj is a criticism with (j,k)∈att(j,k) \in \mathrm{att}(j,k)∈att. No other attacks. Compute GGG and the three statuses. Then register one more attack — an artifact mmm with (m,j)∈att(m, j) \in \mathrm{att}(m,j)∈att — and recompute.

Solution (the original graph). This is the grounded extension GGG we built in module 7, so we state the iteration rather than re-derive it:

S0=∅,S1=F(∅)={j},S2=F({j})={j,a},S3=F({j,a})={j,a}=S2.S_0 = \emptyset, \quad S_1 = F(\emptyset) = \{j\}, \quad S_2 = F(\{j\}) = \{j, a\}, \quad S_3 = F(\{j,a\}) = \{j,a\} = S_2.S0​=∅,S1​=F(∅)={j},S2​=F({j})={j,a},S3​=F({j,a})={j,a}=S2​.

The iteration is stable at S2S_2S2​, so G={j,a}G = \{j, a\}G={j,a}. Applying (Adj):

  • j∈Gj \in Gj∈G → accepted (it is unattacked, so nothing in GGG attacks it).
  • a∈Ga \in Ga∈G → accepted (its only attacker kkk is not in GGG).
  • k∉Gk \notin Gk∈/G, and the accepted jjj attacks kkk → refuted.

This is Lemma 3.1 computed: jjj refutes the criticism kkk, so the conjecture aaa is reinstated.

Solution (after registering m→jm \to jm→j). Now mmm attacks jjj, and mmm is unattacked. The graph is a four-node chain m→j→k→am \to j \to k \to am→j→k→a. Re-run the iteration from scratch — this is what (Adj) demands after the new edge:

  • S1=F(∅)={m}S_1 = F(\emptyset) = \{m\}S1​=F(∅)={m} — only mmm is unattacked now; jjj has acquired the attacker mmm.
  • S2=F({m})S_2 = F(\{m\})S2​=F({m}): jjj‘s attacker mmm is not attacked by anyone in {m}\{m\}{m}, so j∉S2j \notin S_2j∈/S2​. But kkk‘s attacker jjj is attacked by m∈{m}m \in \{m\}m∈{m}, so k∈S2k \in S_2k∈S2​. Thus S2={m,k}S_2 = \{m, k\}S2​={m,k}.
  • S3=F({m,k})S_3 = F(\{m,k\})S3​=F({m,k}): aaa‘s attacker kkk is now in the standing set, but is kkk attacked by a member of {m,k}\{m,k\}{m,k}? No — nothing attacks kkk except jjj, and j∉{m,k}j \notin \{m,k\}j∈/{m,k}. So a∉S3a \notin S_3a∈/S3​. We get S3={m,k}=S2S_3 = \{m, k\}= S_2S3​={m,k}=S2​. Stable.

So G={m,k}G = \{m, k\}G={m,k}, and the statuses flip: mmm accepted, jjj refuted (attacked by the accepted mmm), kkk reinstated to accepted, and aaa — attacked by the now-accepted kkk — back to refuted. The conjecture that reinstatement had just rescued is knocked down again, because the artifact that rescued it has itself been refuted.

That flip is the whole point of (Adj) running continuously. We did not “open the old ruling for appeal.” We added one edge and recomputed; the labels followed. Had we treated adjudication as a one-time final step, aaa would be frozen as accepted and the system would be lying about its own graph.

Exercises

Exercise 1 (mechanical). In a graph, artifact aaa has exactly two attackers, k1k_1k1​ and k2k_2k2​. The grounded extension is G={j,k2}G = \{j, k_2\}G={j,k2​}, where jjj attacks k1k_1k1​ (and k2k_2k2​ is unattacked). What is the status of aaa? Justify with the (Adj) clause that applies.

Show solution

aaa is refuted. The refuted clause asks only whether some attacker of aaa is in GGG. Here k2∈Gk_2 \in Gk2​∈G and (k2,a)∈att(k_2, a) \in \mathrm{att}(k2​,a)∈att, so the clause fires. It does not matter that the other attacker k1k_1k1​ is knocked down by jjj; a single standing attacker is enough to refute. (Tempting wrong answer: “accepted, because k1k_1k1​ was refuted.” But reinstatement requires every attacker to be knocked down, not just one.)

Exercise 3 (conceptual). Prove a variant of the Reinstatement Lemma: if kkk attacks aaa, and j1,j2j_1, j_2j1​,j2​ both attack kkk with j1j_1j1​ unattacked (j2j_2j2​ may have attackers), then a∈Ga \in Ga∈G. Where in your proof does j2j_2j2​ play no role?

Show solution

Proof. j1j_1j1​ is unattacked, so j1∈F(∅)j_1 \in F(\emptyset)j1​∈F(∅) (vacuously, exactly as in Lemma 3.1). The only attacker of aaa is kkk, and j1j_1j1​ attacks kkk, so every attacker of aaa is attacked by a member of {j1}\{j_1\}{j1​}; since j1∈F(∅)j_1 \in F(\emptyset)j1​∈F(∅), we get a∈F(F(∅))⊆Ga \in F(F(\emptyset)) \subseteq Ga∈F(F(∅))⊆G. Therefore a∈Ga \in Ga∈G. □\square□

j2j_2j2​ never appears: one knock-down of kkk suffices, and j1j_1j1​ already supplies it. Whether j2j_2j2​ is itself accepted, refuted, or suspended is irrelevant to aaa‘s reinstatement here. (This is the mirror of Exercise 1: refutation needs only one standing attacker, and so does the defense that reinstates a target — one defender that is guaranteed to stand is enough.)

Exercise 4 (conceptual). Why does the (Adj) rule recompute GGG from ∅\emptyset∅ after every registration, rather than once after all artifacts have been added? Give a graph where the order of recomputation would change the eventual labels if we adjudicated only once at the end.

Show solution

Status is defined as a function of the current graph, not of a finished one — and the system never finishes (no-deletion, module 6: the graph only ever grows, and growth never stops). A one-time-at-the-end adjudication would have no “end” to run at. More concretely: in the worked example, adjudicating after j→k→aj \to k \to aj→k→a gives aaa accepted; after also registering m→jm \to jm→j, aaa is refuted. If we had frozen the labels at the first adjudication and never recomputed, aaa would stay (incorrectly) accepted even though its sole defender jjj is now refuted. Recomputing keeps the labels honest to the graph. Adjudication is continuous because the object it reports on is always changing.

Exercise 5 (conceptual). An artifact aaa is attacked only by kkk, and kkk is attacked only by aaa (a two-cycle, a↔ka \leftrightarrow ka↔k, both otherwise unattacked). Compute GGG and the statuses. Does reinstatement apply? Why or why not?

Show solution

S1=F(∅)=∅S_1 = F(\emptyset) = \emptysetS1​=F(∅)=∅: neither aaa nor kkk is unattacked, and neither’s attacker is knocked down by anything in ∅\emptyset∅. So the iteration is immediately stable: G=∅G = \emptysetG=∅. Statuses: nothing is in GGG, so nothing is accepted; and since no member of GGG attacks anything (GGG is empty), nothing is refuted either. Both aaa and kkk are suspended. Reinstatement does not apply: the lemma needs an unattacked artifact to seed F(∅)F(\emptyset)F(∅), and a two-cycle has none. The cycle is the unresolved tangle the suspended status exists to name.

Recap

We pinned down two things. First, (Adj) recomputes the grounded extension after every single registration and reads three statuses off it — accepted, refuted, suspended — so a label is always a live report on the current graph, never a final ruling. Second, the Reinstatement Lemma falls straight out of the iteration from ∅\emptyset∅: an unattacked artifact enters at the first step, and the target it defends enters at the second, so refuting a criticism restores its target with no extra machinery. The no-deletion principle from module 6 has now been cashed in full — nothing is erased, and what was refuted can return the instant its refuter falls. Next we turn from which artifacts stand to what makes an artifact worth standing for: the demarcation between ideas that are live parts of inquiry and ideas that only look like they are.
