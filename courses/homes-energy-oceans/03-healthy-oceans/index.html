Healthy Oceans

What you'll be able to do
  • Explain the mechanism of TOP's Healthy Oceans policy, including the 30% protection target, the bottom-trawling phase-out, ecosystem-based management, the Ocean's Secretariat, and the aquaculture investment
  • Describe the problem the policy targets — low marine protection, destructive trawling, declining fish stocks, and an underdeveloped blue economy
  • Explain why TOP frames ocean protection as economic policy and how New Zealand's ocean zone makes this framing plausible
  • State the main critiques of the policy, including the fishing industry's objections, transition costs, and enforcement challenges
  • Distinguish TOP's attributed claims from independently established facts in the marine policy debate

Picture two patches of seabed off the Northland coast, a few kilometres apart. Over the first, a bottom trawl has passed — a heavy net dragged across the floor, flattening coral fans that took decades to grow, scattering fish that won’t return this season. Over the second, a marine reserve declared fifteen years ago: the coral is intact, the reef fish have rebuilt to densities that spill outward and replenish the surrounding fishery. Same ocean. Different rules. Very different results.

That contrast sits at the heart of what The Opportunity Party (TOP) calls its Healthy Oceans policy — and it raises a question that runs through this entire course: can environmental protection be economic policy? We saw TOP argue yes with housing (protecting land value creates the conditions for productive use) and yes with energy (reliable renewables are the foundation of a productive economy). Oceans is where that framing gets its sharpest test, because it runs headlong into an industry — commercial fishing — with real jobs, real communities, and a real stake in the outcome.

The problem: a vast ocean, barely protected

New Zealand sits at the edge of the South Pacific and governs one of the largest ocean zones on the planet — roughly 4.4 million square kilometres of Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ). To put that in proportion: it is about fifteen times the country’s land area. Yet according to official figures, less than 1% of that zone carries meaningful protection. Most of the rest is open to commercial extraction, including methods that conservationists and many fisheries scientists argue are damaging the very resource base the fishing industry depends on.

The most contested of those methods is bottom trawling — dragging weighted nets along the seabed to catch fish like orange roughy, hoki, and squid. The technique is efficient and, for fishing companies, economically important. It is also, critics argue, ecologically destructive: it disturbs seafloor habitats that can take decades or centuries to recover, catches significant quantities of non-target species (bycatch), and in deep-sea areas may be removing ancient ecosystems before scientists have catalogued them. Fish stocks including orange roughy have historically been overfished, with populations taking years to recover after catch limits were set.

At the same time, NZ’s blue economy — the economic value generated by healthy, productive oceans — is widely described as underdeveloped relative to the potential of that vast EEZ. Aquaculture (farmed shellfish and finfish), marine tourism, and marine science all rely on healthy ecosystems and are constrained, in different ways, by degraded or poorly managed waters.

TOP’s proposed mechanism

TOP proposes a package of interlocking measures. Attribute all of the following to TOP’s 2026 platform.

The 30% protection target. TOP proposes to protect 30% of New Zealand’s ocean by 2035 — up from roughly 0.5% today. “Protection” here means a meaningful designation: marine reserves or equivalent areas where extractive activity is limited or prohibited, not paper parks. The target aligns with the international “30x30” movement (adopted at the 2022 Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework) but would require a dramatic acceleration of the current pace of marine protection in NZ.

Phasing out bottom trawling, with transition support. TOP proposes to end bottom trawling — a significant step, since NZ’s trawl fleet catches hundreds of thousands of tonnes annually and employs thousands of workers directly and indirectly. TOP pairs this with worker transition support: retraining and financial assistance for people whose livelihoods depend on trawl fishing, and support for fishing companies to shift toward less-destructive methods such as line-fishing or midwater trawling (which targets fish in the water column rather than on the seabed).

Ecosystem-based fisheries management. Rather than managing individual fish species in isolation — setting a quota for hoki, a separate quota for orange roughy — TOP proposes to manage the ecosystem as a whole, setting catch limits that account for species interactions, habitat health, and the needs of non-commercial species. This is a shift in the underlying framework, not just the numbers.

An Ocean’s Secretariat. TOP proposes a new government body — an Ocean’s Secretariat — to coordinate marine planning and management across the agencies that currently have overlapping jurisdictions (Fisheries NZ, the Department of Conservation, regional councils). The Secretariat would be responsible for progressing the 30% protection target and integrating Treaty fishing-rights obligations into marine planning.

Aquaculture investment and the blue economy. TOP proposes roughly 500 million dollars in investment in aquaculture — farmed mussels, oysters, salmon, and emerging species like seaweed — as the foundation of a growing blue economy. The party argues this could generate around 500 million dollars per year in economic activity, creating jobs in coastal communities and providing an alternative economic base as the trawl fishery changes. Māori, as Treaty partners with recognised fishing rights and a significant presence in aquaculture, would be active partners in marine planning under this framework.

Treaty fishing rights. The Treaty of Waitangi guarantees Māori fishing rights, and any marine protection framework must respect them. The Māori fisheries settlement (1992) gave Māori interests 50% of quota shares in NZ’s commercial fishery and 20% of Sealord, making iwi significant stakeholders in both the existing commercial fishery and any transition. TOP explicitly includes Treaty fishing-rights protections in its framework, treating Māori as partners in marine planning rather than as one interest group among several.

Check yourself: what is the current level of ocean protection in New Zealand's EEZ, and what does TOP propose to change it to?

According to official figures, roughly 0.5% (or less than 1%) of New Zealand’s EEZ currently has meaningful protection. TOP proposes to increase that to 30% by 2035. Note that these are TOP’s own figures and targets — attribute them to TOP when discussing them, since the exact current figure and what counts as “meaningful protection” are themselves contested.

The NZ fit: environment as economy

Why does TOP frame this as economic policy rather than environmental policy? The argument runs like this.

New Zealand’s EEZ is a natural asset — one of the largest in the world, relatively close to major Asian seafood markets, and governed by a country with a reputation for clean, green produce that commands premium prices. That asset is currently generating far less economic return than it could, because degraded fisheries and poor marine governance limit the productivity of aquaculture, marine tourism, and sustainable fishing. Protecting the ecosystem is, on this argument, investing in the asset — in the same way that a farmer who lets their soil erode is running down capital, not saving costs.

TOP argues that a healthy ocean is more economically productive over time than a degraded one, and that the current management framework is allowing short-term extraction to run down long-term value. The Secretariat and the 30% target are, in this framing, economic infrastructure — as much as the energy strategy we looked at in Module 2, which frames reliable renewables as the foundation of a productive economy.

This is the same logic that ran through our earlier two modules: that what looks like environmental policy is, on closer inspection, also economic policy. Whether that argument is correct is a question worth sitting with.

Check yourself: in TOP's framing, why is ocean protection economic policy rather than (or as well as) environmental policy?

TOP argues that a healthy ocean is a productive economic asset — that aquaculture, marine tourism, sustainable fishing, and marine science all depend on ecosystem health. Degraded fisheries and poorly managed waters reduce the long-run productivity of those industries. So protecting the ocean is, on this view, investing in the underlying asset, not sacrificing economic activity for environmental reasons. The same “environment as economy” framing appears in M1 (land/housing) and M2 (energy).

The critiques: the fishing industry’s side

A fair question here is straightforward: what does the fishing industry say?

The commercial fishing industry does not accept the case for a bottom-trawling phase-out as presented. Its arguments deserve a fair hearing.

The transition cost is real and falls on people, not abstractions. The fishing industry employs thousands of New Zealanders, many of them in coastal communities — Westport, Greymouth, Timaru, Dunedin — where alternative employment is limited. A trawling phase-out, even with transition support, disrupts livelihoods that have been built over generations. The industry argues that retraining workers for aquaculture or other sectors is easier to announce than to deliver, and that TOP’s transition package has not been independently costed to the level of detail that would make it credible. For the families involved, “blue economy jobs” are an abstract future; the fishing job is a present reality.

The environmental case is contested. Industry representatives and some fisheries scientists argue that NZ’s quota management system — introduced in 1986 and among the world’s first — already constrains catch at sustainable levels for most commercially important species, and that the worst overfishing problems belong to the 1970s and 1980s. They would dispute that current bottom-trawling practices are ecologically catastrophic, pointing to areas where trawling has occurred for decades without the collapse of seabed ecosystems. The critique is not that the ocean doesn’t matter, but that the problem diagnosis is overstated.

Enforcement over a vast EEZ is genuinely hard. New Zealand’s 4.4 million square kilometre EEZ is monitored by a small country with limited naval and aerial surveillance resources. Expanding marine protection areas is one thing; enforcing them against foreign vessels and even domestic operators across that space is another. Marine protection that exists on paper but is not enforced can displace fishing effort to the protected zones (since enforcement is weak) while creating compliance costs for law-abiding operators. TOP has not published detailed enforcement costings.

Aquaculture is not a frictionless substitute. Expanding aquaculture raises its own environmental and planning questions: mussel and salmon farming affects local ecosystems, generates conflicts with recreational users and coastal landowners, and requires consenting processes that have their own bottlenecks. The 500 million dollar investment figure is TOP’s stated intention; the conditions for aquaculture growth — consenting reform, disease management, market development — are complex and require sustained policy work beyond the headline number.

Māori interests are not uniform. Iwi with quota holdings have an economic stake in the existing commercial fishery; some will benefit from aquaculture expansion, others may be more cautious about a transition that disrupts quota value. Treating Māori as “partners in marine planning” is correct in principle but leaves open hard questions about which iwi interests prevail when they diverge, and who speaks for coastal hapū without quota allocations.

Check yourself: what is the fishing industry's strongest objection to TOP's bottom-trawling phase-out?

Probably the transition cost argument: that the fishing industry employs real people in coastal communities where alternatives are limited, and that transition support is easier to promise than to deliver at sufficient scale. The environmental-case dispute (NZ already has quota management) is also important, and the enforcement difficulty is a practical concern. There is no single “right” answer here — name the argument you find most substantive and be able to explain it.

Exercises

1. Why does TOP call ocean protection economic policy?

Explain in your own words the argument TOP makes for framing ocean protection as economic rather than purely environmental policy. What does this have in common with the framing in Modules 1 and 2?

Show guidance

A strong answer would note that TOP treats the ocean as a productive economic asset — like land in M1 or the electricity grid in M2 — and argues that degraded ecosystems reduce its long-run economic output. Aquaculture, marine tourism, and sustainable fishing all depend on ecosystem health. Protecting 30% of the ocean is, on this argument, investing in the productive base rather than sacrificing economic activity. The same logic appeared in M1 (land that builds communities generates more long-run value than land that speculates) and M2 (reliable energy is the foundation of a productive economy). A strong answer would also note this argument is contested: protecting the ocean does impose short-run costs on the existing fishing industry, and whether the long-run gains outweigh those costs is a matter of genuine debate.

2. What is the fishing industry’s strongest objection to the bottom-trawling phase-out, and how might TOP respond?

Identify what you take to be the fishing industry’s most powerful argument against a trawling phase-out. Then explain how TOP might respond, and whether you think the response is adequate — without taking a side.

Show guidance

The transition-cost argument is probably the strongest: real jobs in communities with few alternatives. TOP’s response is the transition support package and the promise of blue-economy jobs. The response is plausible in principle but weak on specifics — the package has not been independently costed, and “retraining” for aquaculture assumes those jobs materialise at the scale and in the locations where trawl workers live. A balanced answer would present both positions fairly: the industry’s concern is legitimate and grounded in real people’s lives; TOP’s response is sincere but depends on delivery details that haven’t been settled. Neither side is obviously right.

3. What role does the Treaty of Waitangi play in TOP’s ocean policy, and why does it matter?

Explain why Treaty fishing rights are relevant to any reform of NZ’s fisheries management, and what TOP proposes to do about it.

Show guidance

The 1992 Māori fisheries settlement gave iwi 50% of commercial quota shares and a 20% stake in Sealord — making Māori a major stakeholder in the existing commercial fishery, not just an affected community. Any change to fisheries management (protection areas, quota reallocation, phase-outs) directly affects those economic interests. TOP proposes to include Treaty fishing-rights protections in the framework and treat Māori as partners in marine planning. The complexity is that “Māori interests” are not uniform: iwi with large quota holdings have different interests from coastal hapū without quota, and a trawling phase-out that hurts quota value affects iwi differently from the aquaculture expansion that could benefit others. A good answer names this complexity rather than treating Māori as a single bloc.

4. How does the Ocean’s Secretariat fit into TOP’s broader approach to governance?

What problem is the Ocean’s Secretariat designed to solve, and how does this compare to TOP’s approach to governance in other policy areas covered in this course?

Show guidance

The Secretariat is designed to solve a coordination problem: multiple agencies (Fisheries NZ, DOC, regional councils) have overlapping jurisdictions over the ocean, which can produce inconsistent decisions and gaps in management. TOP’s solution is a dedicated coordinating body with a clear mandate. This is structurally similar to the Urban Development Act proposed in M1 (which aimed to replace the fragmented RMA consenting system with clearer national-level direction) and to the bipartisan energy strategy in M2 (which aimed to lift energy planning out of the short electoral cycle and give it institutional continuity). In each case, TOP argues that the current fragmented, short-term governance structure is failing to manage a long-term national asset — and proposes a more coordinated institutional response.

Recap

Over three modules, a single argument has assembled itself piece by piece. TOP’s “blue-green” positioning — the contested label it uses for itself — rests on one underlying claim: that the country’s built and natural assets (housing land, the electricity grid, the ocean) are being mismanaged by governance structures that reward short-term extraction over long-term productivity, and that fixing that mismanagement is both good environmental policy and good economic policy. In Module 1 we saw that argument applied to housing; in Module 2, to energy; here, to the ocean.

Whether the argument holds depends on a set of empirical questions — whether protection really lifts long-run productivity, whether transition support is deliverable at scale, whether enforcement over 4.4 million square kilometres is feasible — that this course cannot resolve and that reasonable people genuinely disagree on. What the course has tried to do is give you the mechanism, the problem diagnosis, and the strongest version of the critique, so you can hold your own when those questions come up.

New Zealand has the ocean. What it does with it is a choice, and the terms of that choice are now a little clearer.
