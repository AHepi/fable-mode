Affordable Housing

What you'll be able to do
  • Identify the supply-side levers TOP proposes (zoning, consenting, Urban Development Act, government underwriting, infrastructure, modular building) and distinguish them from the demand-side land value tax.
  • Explain the scale of New Zealand's housing shortage and why affordability has become an international outlier.
  • State at least three critiques of TOP's housing supply agenda and explain why each is a genuine concern, not a talking point.

Mere weeks after his thirtieth birthday, Daniel put in an offer on a two-bedroom unit in Tauranga — nothing special, a 1970s weatherboard on a flat section — and lost to a cash buyer at nearly a million dollars. He moved back to his parents’ place. The following year his employer offered him a transfer to Melbourne. He took it.

That sequence — the failed purchase, the retreat, the emigration — is not a personal failure. It is, according to Stats NZ data, the modal experience for New Zealanders under forty. Homeownership in that age group has collapsed by more than twenty percentage points since 1991. The price-to-income ratio sits at roughly seven to one nationally and closer to nine to one in Auckland, where median prices hover around a million dollars. By the standard international measure, anything above five is “severely unaffordable.” New Zealand is at the top of that list — not because wages fell, but because house prices rose faster and kept rising.

This course spans three policy areas: affordable housing, abundant energy, and ocean protection. All three share a framing The Opportunity Party (TOP) calls “blue-green” — the idea that the built and natural world is economic policy, not a separate, softer concern. How we zone land, how we build homes, how we generate electricity, what we do with our ocean: each of these is a decision about which activities the economy rewards, and who gets to participate. This module is about the first of them.

The housing problem, stated plainly: New Zealand has roughly 80,000 fewer homes than it needs. That figure, drawn from analysis by Sense Partners and cited by TOP, is the gap between the stock that exists and the stock needed to house the population at reasonable density. It is not a precise number — estimates vary — but every serious analyst agrees there is a significant shortage, and that it has been building for decades.

Part of the reason is the tax system. As the Essentials course explains, New Zealand taxes wages but not capital gains, which has made housing the national investment vehicle — roughly 48% of household assets now sit in property, concentrating wealth in existing owners and steering money away from productive enterprise. TOP’s housing proposals are a response to that tilt: a housing shortage is not only a construction problem, it is also the predictable result of treating houses as the safest investment available.

Where does a shortage come from? The economics are straightforward. When demand for a good persistently exceeds supply, prices rise. If supply cannot respond — if building is slow, restricted, or unprofitable — prices keep rising. In New Zealand, supply has persistently failed to respond. The question is why.

Two levers on the same problem

Think of housing affordability as having two levers. One is on the demand side: if it becomes expensive to sit on land without using it, landowners have a stronger incentive to build or sell, rather than hold a site empty while prices climb. The Essentials course covers this lever in full — it is the land value tax (LVT), an annual charge on the unimproved value of the land itself, not the building on top. The core argument: land can’t flee, so the tax barely changes behaviour — except to make land-banking more costly. The Essentials course owns that argument; this module only calls back to it.

The other lever is on the supply side: make it easier and cheaper to actually build homes. That is where TOP’s housing agenda sits — and it is the main subject of this module.

Check yourself: what is the difference between the demand-side lever and the supply-side levers?

The demand-side lever (the LVT) works by raising the cost of holding idle or under-used land, pushing owners toward selling or building rather than sitting on appreciating values. The supply-side levers work by removing the rules, costs, and risks that slow or stop construction — making it easier for builders to actually put homes on that land once it’s available. Both aim at the same shortage; they attack it from different ends.

What TOP proposes on supply

TOP argues that New Zealand’s construction bottleneck is largely self-inflicted — a product of rules, not geology. The party proposes several interlocking reforms. All figures and projections below are TOP’s claims, not independently verified targets.

Simpler, nationally consistent zoning. Under the current system, each of New Zealand’s 67 territorial authorities sets its own district plan, with its own rules about what can be built where, how tall, how close to a boundary. The result is a patchwork: a terraced house that is legal in central Auckland is illegal two suburbs over. TOP argues for national zoning standards that make medium-density building — terraces, apartments, townhouses — the default in urban areas, rather than something that requires a special consent. The idea is not new: the previous government’s National Policy Statement on Urban Development moved in this direction, and the current government has retained much of it. TOP wants to go further and make the rules more consistent and more permissive.

Easier consenting for smaller dwellings. Tiny homes, sleepouts, granny flats, and extensions on existing properties could add housing stock quickly and cheaply — if the consent process were not so slow and expensive. TOP proposes streamlining or exempting these smaller projects from the full resource consent process, recognising that a sleepout in a back garden poses a different level of risk than a new apartment block.

A new Urban Development Act. The Resource Management Act (RMA), which governs what can be built where and with what environmental effects, has long been criticised as slow, legalistic, and prone to capture by objectors. Multiple governments have promised to reform or replace it. TOP proposes a dedicated Urban Development Act as the instrument for major housing and infrastructure decisions, separate from the broader environmental management framework.

Government underwriting of affordable developments. Construction in New Zealand lurches in cycles: when the economy is strong and credit flows, developers build; when conditions tighten, projects stall mid-site. TOP proposes that the government underwrite reputable affordable housing developments — essentially backing the project so that finance can flow even when private lenders pull back. This, the party argues, would smooth the boom-bust cycle and keep skilled tradespeople employed between peaks.

Infrastructure for density. One reason councils resist upzoning is that more homes mean more demand on water and wastewater networks — and the council must fund the upgrade. TOP proposes government support for the infrastructure that enables density, particularly water and wastewater, to remove one of the biggest financial barriers councils face when allowing more homes.

Support for off-site and modular building. New Zealand’s construction industry is small, skilled-labour-intensive, and expensive. Prefabricated and modular construction — where components are built in a factory and assembled on site — can be significantly cheaper and faster. TOP proposes support for the development of this sector, which has struggled to scale in New Zealand despite working well in comparable markets like Japan and Scandinavia.

Check yourself: name the supply-side levers TOP proposes, and the one demand-side lever that sits alongside them.

Supply-side: simpler national zoning; easier consenting for tiny homes/sleepouts/extensions; a new Urban Development Act; government underwriting of affordable developments; infrastructure funding (water/wastewater) to enable density; support for off-site/modular builders. Demand-side (covered in the Essentials course): the land value tax, which makes holding idle urban land more expensive and pushes owners toward building or selling.

The NZ fit

Each of these proposals targets something specific about New Zealand’s situation, not just a generic housing problem.

The zoning and consenting rules are genuinely restrictive by international standards. New Zealand’s leaky-building crisis of the late 1990s and 2000s — in which tens of thousands of homes built with inadequate weatherproofing developed serious moisture damage, costing billions in repairs — created a political and professional culture of extreme caution in the building consent process. That caution has costs: more consent requirements, more inspections, more liability aversion among architects and builders, and a generally slower and more expensive path to a completed home. The reforms TOP proposes would need to navigate that legacy.

The boom-bust problem in construction is also a known New Zealand feature. The country’s building industry is small relative to the scale of the shortage. When conditions deteriorate, firms close, workers leave the trade, and it takes years to rebuild capacity. Government underwriting of stable, affordable pipeline projects is one response to that dynamic.

And New Zealand’s urban areas have, historically, spread outward rather than upward — a pattern that makes housing expensive (land near jobs is scarce) and infrastructure costly (services have to reach further). The push for upzoning and medium density is a response to the shape of cities, not just the number of homes.

The critiques — fairly

A policy case for supply reform is not hard to make. The harder question is whether the proposed remedies will work, and who bears the costs.

Upzoning meets strong local opposition. Allowing denser housing in an area consistently attracts opposition from existing residents: concerns about parking, character, sunlight, the value of their own homes. This is not irrational — if you bought a house partly because of the quiet suburban streetscape, an apartment block next door is a genuine change. Local politicians respond to local voters. The result is that upzoning, even when approved in principle, is often watered down or delayed at the local plan stage. The research on this is mixed: some studies find that upzoning does increase supply and moderate prices; others find the effects smaller than advocates expect because other constraints (finance, construction capacity, demand) bind first.

Falling prices hurt recent buyers. TOP — and, notably, the current Housing Minister — accepts that the policy goal is to bring house prices down. That is worth stating clearly, because it is politically unusual. But it creates a real tension: someone who bought a home two years ago at the peak of the market, with a large mortgage, faces the prospect of negative equity if prices fall substantially. Their mortgage does not fall with the market. TOP does not offer a remedy to this problem beyond noting that the alternative — prices that never fall — forecloses ownership for the next generation. That is a defensible position, but the distributional conflict is genuine.

Supply reform is slow. Even if every proposed reform were enacted tomorrow, new homes take years to build. Zoning changes, consent reforms, and infrastructure investments operate on long timelines. A household looking for somewhere affordable to live now does not benefit from reforms that take effect over a decade. The shortage is urgent; the levers are slow.

Government underwriting puts taxpayers on the hook. If the government backs a development that then fails — because the developer becomes insolvent, because construction costs blow out, because the market turns — the taxpayer carries the loss. Underwriting is a form of risk transfer from the private sector to the public. TOP argues the discipline of selecting reputable developers and capping underwriting reduces this risk, but it does not eliminate it. The history of government involvement in housing construction includes both successes (KiwiBuild’s mixed record notwithstanding) and failures.

Check yourself: a friend says "falling house prices are obviously good news for everyone." What's the problem with that claim?

It depends on your position. For a renter or a first-home buyer who hasn’t yet purchased, lower prices are unambiguously better — they mean homeownership becomes reachable. For someone who bought recently and has a large mortgage, falling prices can mean owing more than the house is worth (negative equity) — a serious financial problem. And for an older homeowner who has largely paid off their mortgage, the effect is mainly on paper wealth, not their day-to-day situation. “Everyone” hides three very different groups with very different interests.

Exercises

1. Name the levers.

TOP’s affordable housing agenda involves both demand-side and supply-side measures. List the supply-side levers TOP proposes, describe what each one does in one sentence, and explain why the land value tax sits on a different side of the ledger.

Show discussion

The supply-side levers are: simpler national zoning (makes medium-density building the default, removing local variation); easier consenting for small dwellings (streamlines or exempts tiny homes/sleepouts/extensions from the full consent process); a new Urban Development Act (replaces the RMA framework for major housing decisions); government underwriting of affordable developments (backs reputable projects so finance flows through downturns); infrastructure support (funds water/wastewater to remove the cost barrier for councils); and support for modular/off-site builders (helps scale a cheaper construction method). The land value tax sits on the demand side because it doesn’t directly enable construction — it changes the incentive to hold land without building on it, by making that holding more expensive. Both aim at the same shortage, but from different angles: the LVT squeezes the incentive to land-bank; the supply levers reduce the friction once someone is ready to build.

2. The worried recent buyer.

Imagine someone who bought a home eighteen months ago at close to peak prices, with a ninety percent mortgage. They’ve heard that TOP’s housing policy aims to bring prices down, and they’re worried. Is their worry reasonable? What exactly is the risk they face, and does TOP’s policy response address it?

Show discussion

Their worry is reasonable. If house prices fall significantly, they could end up in negative equity — owing more on the mortgage than the house is currently worth. This does not mean they must sell or that they’ll lose the home immediately, but it does mean they can’t access equity, may struggle to refinance, and face financial stress if they need to sell for any reason (job change, relationship change, etc.). TOP’s position is that prices should fall, and the party does not offer direct relief to recent buyers who bought near the peak. The argument TOP makes is that permanently high prices impose a larger and longer-lasting cost on the generation that can’t enter the market at all — that the alternative (prices that never fall) also has victims, just less visible ones. Whether you find that persuasive depends in part on which group’s interests you weight more heavily. What’s important to note is that this is a real trade-off, not a rhetorical oversight on TOP’s part — the party acknowledges it explicitly.

3. Why might supply reform be slower than its advocates hope?

Even a supporter of the supply-side agenda might concede it could take longer to work than expected. Walk through at least two reasons why the reforms might not translate quickly into more affordable homes, even if enacted in full.

Show discussion

Several constraints operate even after the rules change. First, construction capacity: New Zealand’s building industry is small. Lifting zoning restrictions doesn’t conjure more builders, more materials, or more skilled tradespeople. If capacity is the binding constraint, permitting more density may result in higher construction costs rather than more homes, at least in the short run. Second, local implementation: zoning reform set at a national level still gets applied through local plans, consents, and decisions. Councils that resist upzoning can find ways to comply technically while minimising actual change. Third, financing: even with underwriting, developers still need to find capital, manage risk, and sell or rent finished homes at prices that cover costs. If interest rates are high or demand is uncertain, projects may not proceed even with permissive rules. Fourth, the demand side: supply reform without the land value tax (or some equivalent pressure on holding costs) leaves the incentive to land-bank largely intact. A landowner who benefits from rising land values may still prefer to wait, even if the rules now allow building. None of this means the reforms are wrong — it means the connection between rule changes and outcomes is slower and less direct than the policy case sometimes implies.

4. The leaky-buildings legacy.

The 2000s leaky-buildings crisis shaped New Zealand’s consenting culture. Briefly explain what the crisis was and why it creates a complication for proposals to streamline or ease building consents.

Show discussion

In the 1990s and early 2000s, a large number of New Zealand homes — particularly multi-unit buildings with monolithic cladding (the “plaster homes”) — were built in ways that allowed moisture to penetrate and become trapped in walls. The resulting rot caused severe structural damage to tens of thousands of properties. The repair bill ran to billions of dollars; many homeowners were left with properties worth less than the cost of remediation; councils, builders, and manufacturers faced large legal liabilities. The crisis prompted a major overhaul of the Building Act (2004) and a significant tightening of consenting requirements and inspections. The complication for today’s reform proposals is that the current caution in the consent process has a reason: the last time New Zealand moved quickly on housing with insufficient oversight, the consequences were costly and long-lasting. Streamlining consents requires convincing regulators, councils, and the public that the risks can be managed differently this time — either through better design standards, different materials, or different accountability mechanisms. That is a harder sell because of the legacy, not just a bureaucratic obstacle.

Recap

New Zealand’s housing shortage is deep, historically built, and specific in its causes: restrictive local zoning, slow and expensive consenting, a boom-bust construction cycle, and infrastructure that hasn’t kept pace with urban growth. TOP proposes a set of supply-side reforms — simpler national rules, easier consenting for smaller homes, a new Urban Development Act, government underwriting, infrastructure support, and backing for modular building — alongside the land value tax as the demand-side partner (the Essentials course explains how that works). The strongest critiques are not that the diagnosis is wrong but that upzoning is politically difficult to deliver at scale, that falling prices impose real costs on recent buyers, and that supply reform operates on timelines that offer little relief to households who need housing now. The next module turns from homes to energy — a different policy area, but the same framing: how a physical system, left without long-term investment, becomes an economic constraint.
