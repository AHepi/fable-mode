Symmetries of a Triangle

What you'll be able to do
  • List the six symmetries of an equilateral triangle.
  • Compose two symmetries by performing one move and then another.
  • Show by a worked example that the operation is not commutative.

Cut an equilateral triangle out of stiff paper and drop it into a triangular hole that fits it exactly. Now lift it out, turn it however you like, and set it back. If it drops in clean — every corner in a corner, no overhang — you have performed a symmetry: a way of moving the triangle that leaves the hole looking untouched.

Every group you have met so far has been abelian — the order of combining two things never mattered. On a clock (module 02), 3+53 + 53+5 and 5+35 + 35+3 land on the same hour. Multiply iii by −1-1−1 or −1-1−1 by iii, same answer either way. That has felt like a law of nature. It is not. The paper triangle is about to break it.

Picking up the triangle

Number the three corners of the paper triangle 111, 222, 333, and number the three seats of the hole the same way, with seat 111 at the top, seat 222 at the lower left, seat 333 at the lower right. At the start, corner 111 sits in seat 111, corner 222 in seat 222, corner 333 in seat 333. A symmetry is anything you can do to the paper that lands every corner back in some seat.

There are exactly six. Three of them are rotations — turns about the center:

  • eee: leave it alone (turn by 0∘0^\circ0∘). This is the identity (module 04) — the element that leaves every partner unchanged: combine it with anything and nothing changes.
  • rrr: turn by 120∘120^\circ120∘.
  • r2r^2r2: turn by 240∘240^\circ240∘ — the same as turning by 120∘120^\circ120∘ twice, which is why we write it r2r^2r2. A third turn, r3r^3r3, brings every corner home, so r3=er^3 = er3=e.

The other three are reflections — flips that turn the paper over, each one across a line through one corner and the midpoint of the opposite edge:

  • sss: flip across the line through seat 111, holding the top corner fixed and swapping the bottom two.
  • rsrsrs: a flip that holds the lower-right corner fixed.
  • r2sr^2sr2s: a flip that holds the lower-left corner fixed.

We will see in a moment why the last two earn those compound names. The six together are the cast of this module:

D3={ e,  r,  r2,  s,  rs,  r2s }.D_3 = \{\, e,\; r,\; r^2,\; s,\; rs,\; r^2s \,\}.D3​={e,r,r2,s,rs,r2s}.

Naming the operation: do one move, then another

A group is a set together with an operation — a rule that combines two elements to get a third — obeying four rules (module 04): the operation is closed, associative, has an identity, and gives every element an inverse. The set here is D3D_3D3​, the six symmetries. The operation is composition: do one symmetry, then do another, and ask which single symmetry has the same overall effect.

We write composition by juxtaposition — just putting the two symbols side by side — and we read it right to left, the way function composition runs. So

rsmeans: first do s, then do r.rs \quad\text{means: first do } s, \text{ then do } r.rsmeans: first do s, then do r.

The reflection on the right goes first; the rotation on the left goes second. (Right-to-left feels backward at first. It is the same convention you use without noticing when you write f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)): ggg touches xxx first.)

The operation is closed because a symmetry followed by a symmetry is still a symmetry — the paper still drops in clean. The identity is eee, the turn by 0∘0^\circ0∘. Every move has an inverse that undoes it: a 120∘120^\circ120∘ turn is undone by a 240∘240^\circ240∘ turn (rrr and r2r^2r2), and a flip undoes itself (sss followed by sss is eee, so s2=es^2 = es2=e). Composition is associative for the plain reason that doing moves in a fixed sequence does not care how you bracket them. Run the four-axiom check from module 04 and D3D_3D3​ passes: it is a group.

But it is a group with a twist no earlier one had.

Flip-then-spin is not spin-then-flip

Take the triangle in its home position, corners reading 1,2,31, 2, 31,2,3 in seats 1,2,31, 2, 31,2,3. We will compute rsrsrs and srsrsr by tracking where each numbered corner ends up. Watch only the seats.

Compute rsrsrs (first sss, then rrr).

Start: seats (1,2,3)(1, 2, 3)(1,2,3).

First apply sss — hold the top, swap the bottom two:

(1,2,3)  → s   (1,3,2).(1,2,3) \;\xrightarrow{\,s\,}\; (1,3,2).(1,2,3)s​(1,3,2).

Now apply rrr — turn 120∘120^\circ120∘, so each corner moves one seat around:

(1,3,2)  → r   (2,1,3).(1,3,2) \;\xrightarrow{\,r\,}\; (2,1,3).(1,3,2)r​(2,1,3).

So rsrsrs sends the home triangle to (2,1,3)(2,1,3)(2,1,3).

Compute srsrsr (first rrr, then sss).

Start again: seats (1,2,3)(1, 2, 3)(1,2,3).

First apply rrr:

(1,2,3)  → r   (3,1,2).(1,2,3) \;\xrightarrow{\,r\,}\; (3,1,2).(1,2,3)r​(3,1,2).

Now apply sss — hold the top, swap the bottom two:

(3,1,2)  → s   (3,2,1).(3,1,2) \;\xrightarrow{\,s\,}\; (3,2,1).(3,1,2)s​(3,2,1).

So srsrsr sends the home triangle to (3,2,1)(3,2,1)(3,2,1).

Set the two results side by side:

rs: (2,1,3)sr: (3,2,1).rs:\ (2,1,3) \qquad\qquad sr:\ (3,2,1).rs: (2,1,3)sr: (3,2,1).

They are different arrangements. Therefore

rs≠sr.rs \neq sr.rs=sr.

The order in which you compose the two moves changes the outcome. Flip-then-spin lands the corners one way; spin-then-flip lands them another. A single worked example settles it: D3D_3D3​ is non-commutative — a group where, for at least one pair of elements, ab≠baa b \neq b aab=ba. (A group where order never matters, like all the ones before this, is called abelian, after Niels Abel; D3D_3D3​ is the first group in this course that is not abelian.)

Notice what each result actually is. The arrangement (2,1,3)(2,1,3)(2,1,3) holds seat 333 fixed and swaps the other two — that is a reflection, the one we named rsrsrs. The arrangement (3,2,1)(3,2,1)(3,2,1) holds seat 222 fixed — the reflection we named r2sr^2sr2s. So the two computations also prove a clean relation:

sr=r2s.sr = r^2 s.sr=r2s.

That single equation — “a flip after a turn equals a different flip” — is the engine of the whole group. Every product in D3D_3D3​ can be untangled with r3=er^3 = er3=e, s2=es^2 = es2=e, and sr=r2ssr = r^2ssr=r2s.

Check yourself

Using sr=r2ssr = r^2ssr=r2s, what is sr2sr^2sr2? (Hint: r2=rrr^2 = rrr2=rr, and you can slide an sss past one rrr at a time.)

Show answer

sr2=rssr^2 = rssr2=rs. Slide the sss past the first rrr: sr2=(sr)r=(r2s)r=r2(sr)=r2(r2s)=r4ss r^2 = (sr) r = (r^2 s) r = r^2 (sr) = r^2 (r^2 s) = r^4 ssr2=(sr)r=(r2s)r=r2(sr)=r2(r2s)=r4s. Since r3=er^3 = er3=e, r4=rr^4 = rr4=r, so sr2=rssr^2 = rssr2=rs. The rule is that pushing sss to the right of rkr^krk flips the power: srk=r−kss r^k = r^{-k} ssrk=r−ks.

The Cayley table

A Cayley table (first named in module 05) lays out every product at once: read the row label first… no — read the row element as the move done second and the column element as the move done first, matching rsrsrs = “first sss, then rrr.” Each entry is the single symmetry equal to (row) after (column).

The table makes the non-commutativity visible: it is not symmetric across its diagonal. Look at the sss row against the sss column. The rrr-column of the sss-row reads r2sr^2sr2s (that is srsrsr); the sss-column of the rrr-row reads rsrsrs. Two different boxes, two different answers — exactly sr≠rssr \neq rssr=rs caught in a grid.

What the table still respects is the fingerprint of every group’s Cayley table, the one from module 05: every element shows up exactly once in each row and exactly once in each column. Non-commutativity bends the table; it does not break that law.

Check yourself

From the table, what is (rs)(rs)(rs)(rs)(rs)(rs)? What does that tell you about the element rsrsrs?

Show answer

Find the rsrsrs row and the rsrsrs column: the entry is eee. So (rs)(rs)=e(rs)(rs) = e(rs)(rs)=e, meaning rsrsrs is its own inverse. That fits — rsrsrs is a reflection, and flipping the same way twice returns the triangle home. All three reflections are their own inverses.

Exercises

1. List all six elements of D3D_3D3​ and say, for each, whether it is a rotation or a reflection.

Show solution

Rotations: eee (0∘0^\circ0∘), rrr (120∘120^\circ120∘), r2r^2r2 (240∘240^\circ240∘). Reflections: sss, rsrsrs, r2sr^2sr2s. Three of each, six in total. The rotations turn the paper in place; the reflections flip it over.

2. Compute r⋅sr \cdot sr⋅s and s⋅rs \cdot rs⋅r directly by tracking the seats (1,2,3)(1,2,3)(1,2,3), and confirm they disagree.

Show solution

rsrsrs is first sss then rrr: (1,2,3)→(1,3,2)→(2,1,3)(1,2,3) \to (1,3,2) \to (2,1,3)(1,2,3)→(1,3,2)→(2,1,3). srsrsr is first rrr then sss: (1,2,3)→(3,1,2)→(3,2,1)(1,2,3) \to (3,1,2) \to (3,2,1)(1,2,3)→(3,1,2)→(3,2,1). The results (2,1,3)(2,1,3)(2,1,3) and (3,2,1)(3,2,1)(3,2,1) differ, so rs≠srrs \neq srrs=sr. This is the same computation as the worked example — running it yourself is the point.

3. Use the relations r3=er^3 = er3=e, s2=es^2 = es2=e, and sr=r2ssr = r^2 ssr=r2s to simplify r2srr^2 s rr2sr.

Show solution

Work from the inside: sr=r2ssr = r^2 ssr=r2s, so r2sr=r2(sr)=r2(r2s)=r4sr^2 s r = r^2 (sr) = r^2 (r^2 s) = r^4 sr2sr=r2(sr)=r2(r2s)=r4s. Since r3=er^3 = er3=e, r4=r3r=rr^4 = r^3 r = rr4=r3r=r. Thus r2sr=rsr^2 s r = rsr2sr=rs. (Check against the Cayley table: the r2sr^2sr2s row, rrr column reads rsrsrs. It matches.)

4. A tempting wrong answer: a student says ”D3D_3D3​ is non-commutative, so ab≠baab \neq baab=ba for every pair of elements.” Find two elements of D3D_3D3​ that do commute, and explain why one example of disagreement does not mean total disagreement.

Show solution

rrr and r2r^2r2 commute: r⋅r2=r3=er \cdot r^2 = r^3 = er⋅r2=r3=e and r2⋅r=r3=er^2 \cdot r = r^3 = er2⋅r=r3=e, so rr2=r2rr r^2 = r^2 rrr2=r2r. (Any two rotations commute, since they all just add up turns.) Also eee commutes with everything. “Non-commutative” means there exists at least one pair that disagrees — not that all pairs do. One pair, rrr and sss, disqualifies the group from being abelian; it says nothing about the rest.

5. Find the inverse of each reflection and of each non-identity rotation in D3D_3D3​.

Show solution

The inverse of aaa is the element a−1a^{-1}a−1 with a⋅a−1=ea \cdot a^{-1} = ea⋅a−1=e (module 04). Each reflection is its own inverse: s2=(rs)2=(r2s)2=es^2 = (rs)^2 = (r^2s)^2 = es2=(rs)2=(r2s)2=e, because flipping the same way twice undoes itself. For the rotations, rrr and r2r^2r2 undo each other: r⋅r2=r3=er \cdot r^2 = r^3 = er⋅r2=r3=e, so r−1=r2r^{-1} = r^2r−1=r2 and (r2)−1=r(r^2)^{-1} = r(r2)−1=r. Read off the Cayley table, this is just where each row meets eee.

6. (Stretch.) The square has eight symmetries — four rotations (by 0∘,90∘,180∘,270∘0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ0∘,90∘,180∘,270∘) and four reflections. Without building the full table, argue that this group is also non-commutative. (Hint: pick a rotation and a reflection and track one corner.)

Show solution

Label the square’s corners 1,2,3,41,2,3,41,2,3,4. Let ρ\rhoρ be the 90∘90^\circ90∘ turn and σ\sigmaσ a flip across a diagonal. Track where corner 111 lands. Doing σ\sigmaσ then ρ\rhoρ sends 111 to one corner; doing ρ\rhoρ then σ\sigmaσ sends it to a different corner — the same flip-then-turn versus turn-then-flip split that broke D3D_3D3​. So the square’s symmetry group is non-commutative too. Mixing a rotation with a reflection is what creates the disagreement; this is a general feature of symmetry groups of shapes, not a quirk of the triangle.

Recap

The six symmetries of an equilateral triangle — three rotations and three reflections — form a group, D3D_3D3​, under composition: do one move, then another. We named the moves, composed them by juxtaposition reading right to left, and built the full Cayley table. The headline is the relation sr=r2ssr = r^2ssr=r2s, which means rs≠srrs \neq srrs=sr: order matters now, and it will not stop mattering. Every group from here on must answer the commutativity question fresh — the clock’s easy symmetry was the exception, not the rule.
