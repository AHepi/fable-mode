Statistics & Data

Lines Through Data

How to read a relationship between two measurements: scatter plots, what correlation does and doesn't capture, the best-fit line as a summary, and the most important caution in statistics — correlation isn't causation.

Statistics & Data Beginner 3 modules ~0.8h Path · Statistical Literacy

  • The Statistics Essentials course is the ideal warm-up, but this course stands on its own

Two measurements, side by side: hours studied and exam score, temperature and ice-cream sales, a city’s library count and its test results. Do they move together? By how much? And — the question that trips up everyone from headline writers to working scientists — does one cause the other? This short course teaches you to read a relationship between two things without being fooled by it.

It’s the third mini-course in the Statistics, From the Ground Up series, and it stands on its own: where it leans on shared ideas — the natural wobble in data, telling a real signal from random noise — it reminds you in a line. (The Statistics Essentials course is the full warm-up if you want it.) Across three short modules you’ll learn to read a scatter plot, measure how tightly its cloud of points hugs a line with correlation, summarize the trend with a best-fit line (and see why it predicts averages, not individuals), and then confront the headline lesson: two things moving together can be coincidence, reverse cause, or the work of a hidden third thing. Correlation isn’t causation — and by the end you’ll know exactly why, and what to ask instead.

No formulas to derive here; correlation and the line are taught as ideas you can see. The reward is a kind of statistical self-defense: the refusal to be talked into a cause by a chart that only shows a pattern.

Modules

  1. 01 Two Things at Once Plot two measurements against each other and a cloud of points appears: how to read its tilt and tightness, what correlation captures on a −1 to +1 scale, and the trap hidden in the number zero. 15 min
  2. 02 The Line Through the Cloud The single straight line that comes closest to all the points at once: what it summarizes, why it describes averages and not individuals, and why trusting it past the edge of the data is a mistake. 14 min
  3. 03 Together Isn't Because Two measurements rising together can be coincidence, reverse cause, or both pulled by a hidden third thing — and how to ask which before you believe a headline. 14 min
