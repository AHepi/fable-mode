A Citizen's Income

What you'll be able to do
  • Describe the mechanism of TOP's Citizen's Income — the weekly rate, who qualifies, and what it replaces
  • Explain the welfare problems it targets, including abatement traps and means-testing complexity
  • Lay out the main critiques from both left and right, including the adequacy gap for disabled people and the question of who may not qualify
  • Distinguish TOP's claims from independently verified facts

Sione works twenty hours a week at a Countdown in Porirua and takes home just enough to cover rent, power, and a small contribution to his kids’ food. But when his hours creep up — a few extra shifts here, a holiday cover there — his Jobseeker benefit gets clawed back almost dollar for dollar. He earns more and ends up roughly where he started. His accountant would call it a near-100% marginal rate. Sione calls it a trap.

The Opportunity Party (TOP) — which stood as “The Opportunities Party” until its recent rebrand — has built its most prominent 2026 proposal around exactly this kind of situation. Its answer is a Citizen’s Income: a regular, near-unconditional payment to almost all adults, designed to sit underneath everything else so that earning more always leaves you better off.

If you have worked through the Essentials course in this series, you have already met the idea: a Citizen’s Income is a near-unconditional payment to almost all adults — a floor everyone stands on. This module takes apart exactly what TOP is proposing, the logic behind it, and why some of the fiercest arguments in NZ welfare policy are about precisely this floor.

The mechanism

TOP proposes paying approximately 370 dollars a week — around 19,400 dollars a year — to almost every adult in New Zealand. The payment is near-unconditional: it does not shrink because you pick up extra shifts, and it does not require proving you are actively looking for work. Means-testing only kicks in for those earning above roughly 350,000 dollars a year — a threshold that sits far above the incomes of the vast majority of New Zealanders.

TOP claims the payment would be near-tax-free in recipients’ hands, which matters most for people on low incomes who currently juggle income tax and benefit abatement at the same time.

The Citizen’s Income would replace most existing welfare payments — Jobseeker Support, Student Allowance, and similar benefits — and, crucially, NZ Super, the universal pension currently paid to New Zealanders over 65. Current superannuitants would receive a transitional top-up so no one in retirement takes an immediate income cut during the changeover.

Alongside the main payment, TOP proposes a unified Child Support Income of approximately 350 dollars a week in a child’s first year, folding in Best Start payments, the Family Tax Credit, and Paid Parental Leave into one simpler stream.

The gross cost of the Citizen’s Income, by TOP’s own modelling, is approximately 62.8 billion dollars a year. The land value tax — covered in the next module — is the primary funding mechanism. These are TOP’s figures; they have not been independently verified by Treasury or the IRD.

The problem it targets

New Zealand’s current working-age benefits are means-tested: payments shrink as your income rises, which sounds sensible until you run the numbers. The combined withdrawal rate from income tax and benefit abatement can reach extremely high effective rates for low-income earners, creating what policy analysts call abatement traps — situations where working more leaves you little or no better off in cash terms.

Sione’s situation is not unusual. The system also requires claimants to actively prove eligibility, meet stand-down periods, submit to regular reassessments, and navigate separate agencies for different payments. This complexity imposes real costs — not just in time, but in the stigma that attaches to being scrutinised. People often leave money on the table because the process is daunting, or because the abatement arithmetic makes the effort unrewarding.

Beyond welfare mechanics, TOP points to NZ’s cost-of-living pressures: high rents, household debt, and stagnant wages for lower-income workers. A universal floor, TOP argues, would give everyone a stable base regardless of employment status, and let people take risks — starting a business, retraining, caring for a family member — that the current system actively discourages.

TOP claims the Citizen’s Income would lift approximately 50,000 families out of poverty. This is TOP’s own claim, reflecting their modelling of who currently falls below income thresholds and would be brought above them by the payment floor.

Check yourself

Before reading on: what is an abatement trap, in your own words, and why would a near-unconditional payment sidestep it?

Show answer

An abatement trap occurs when earning more from work causes a benefit to reduce so quickly that the worker keeps little or nothing of the extra income. A near-unconditional payment sidesteps it because it does not reduce when you earn: the floor stays at approximately 370 dollars a week regardless of how many hours you work, so every additional dollar of wages is a real gain rather than an offset against a shrinking benefit.

NZ fit

New Zealand’s welfare system is relatively patchwork compared to a flat universal payment: a different payment for being unemployed, for being a student, for being a sole parent, for being in early childhood with a new child. Navigating between them as life circumstances change involves bureaucratic friction at every transition.

TOP argues that a single near-unconditional floor removes most of that friction at once. In a high-cost economy — where housing alone consumes a large share of household income — a simple baseline is a different kind of instrument from a safety net with separate eligibility tests for each hole. The floor metaphor from the Essentials course captures what TOP is aiming at: not a net to catch people who fall, but a surface they are already standing on. The guard that travels with that metaphor is equally important: a floor’s height matters, and whether 370 dollars a week actually constitutes an adequate base is precisely what the debate is about.

The critiques

Both sides of NZ’s political spectrum have serious objections to the Citizen’s Income — but they are objecting to different things.

Adequacy for disabled people — the sharpest challenge. The Citizen’s Income’s rate of approximately 370 dollars a week sits near the current Jobseeker Support rate. But Jobseeker is not the floor of the welfare system. Supported Living Payments — for people with significant long-term illness or disability — can reach approximately 800 dollars a week today, accounting for the higher costs of disability. TOP proposes that the Citizen’s Income replaces most welfare, including Supported Living. Disability advocates and critics on the left argue that replacing an 800-dollar payment with a 370-dollar floor is not simplification; it is a cut. The transitional arrangements for this group are not fully detailed in TOP’s published platform. This is arguably the hardest unresolved tension in the proposal.

Replacing NZ Super — the political third rail. NZ Super is universal, near-unconditional, and currently pays approximately 496 dollars a week for a single person living alone. For most current superannuitants, the Citizen’s Income represents a reduction. TOP proposes a transitional top-up for existing retirees, but the mechanism and duration are not fully specified in its published material. Replacing universal Super — a payment that has been politically sacrosanct in NZ for decades — is sometimes called the “third rail” of NZ politics because touching it tends to end the conversation. Critics across the political spectrum, including some who are broadly sympathetic to universal payments in principle, regard this as the policy’s biggest political liability.

A fair question: if the transitional top-up protects current retirees, is there actually a problem? The concern is the long-run direction — that over time, the level would remain at 370 dollars a week while Super’s historical level has been significantly higher, and that means-testing for high earners represents a structural shift away from universality for older New Zealanders.

The eligibility gap. An independent analyst at Good Ideas NZ examined TOP’s costing assumptions and found something that has not been publicly explained. TOP’s figures appear to imply approximately 3.2 million Citizen’s Income recipients, against an adult population of approximately 4.1 million — a discrepancy of around 900,000 people. TOP has not detailed what criteria exclude this group: whether they are recent migrants, people above the income threshold, people with some status not yet specified, or the result of a modelling assumption that has not been made public. This figure is an analyst’s inference from the published numbers, not a statement TOP has confirmed or addressed, and the exact explanation remains unclear.

The left critique: removing statutory entitlements. Some progressive critics are wary of trading means-tested welfare — which, for all its flaws, involves statutory entitlements and legal rights — for a universal payment that is set by policy and could, in principle, be trimmed by a future government. Paradoxically, the universality that makes the Citizen’s Income politically attractive might also make it easier to reduce quietly: a targeted benefit for a named vulnerable group attracts more public resistance when cut than a universal payment that flows to everyone.

The right critique: poor targeting. From the other direction, critics who favour tightly focused welfare argue that paying 370 dollars a week to every adult regardless of income is an inefficient use of public money. Their argument is that the answer to abatement traps and complexity is to design better targeted benefits — not to abandon targeting altogether. A dollar paid to a household earning 100,000 dollars a year is a dollar not directed to someone in genuine hardship.

Both critiques are coherent. They reflect different underlying values about what welfare is for: the left’s concern is that the floor is too flat and too low for people with the greatest needs; the right’s concern is that the floor is too broad for people with no needs at all.

Check yourself

The political left and right both object to the Citizen’s Income — but they are objecting to different things. What, at root, separates their critiques?

Show answer

The left’s concern is primarily about adequacy and legal protection: a flat universal payment may be too low for people with the greatest needs (especially disabled people), and replacing statutory entitlements with a single payment reduces the legal protections vulnerable groups currently have. The right’s concern is about targeting efficiency: paying everyone, including those who do not need support, wastes money that could be concentrated on those in genuine hardship. Both can sincerely oppose the proposal while disagreeing completely about what welfare should do.

Exercises

1. Map the mechanism

Without looking back at the module, write two or three sentences describing: what does TOP’s Citizen’s Income pay, to whom, and what does it replace?

Show solution

A strong answer includes: approximately 370 dollars a week, to almost all adults, near-unconditional (no work test for the vast majority; means-tested only above roughly 350,000 dollars annual income), replacing Jobseeker, Student Allowance, most working-age welfare, and NZ Super — with a transitional top-up for current superannuitants. The Child Support Income of approximately 350 dollars a week for a child’s first year is a separate companion payment.

A common gap: confusing “near-unconditional” with “completely unconditional.” The income cutoff above 350,000 dollars is a form of means-testing — it just affects very few people. The distinction matters when evaluating TOP’s claim that this payment removes abatement traps.

2. Steelman a critic

Choose one of the critiques in this module — adequacy for disabled people, replacing NZ Super, the eligibility gap, or poor targeting — and write the strongest version of that objection you can manage. Aim for a version a sincere, well-informed critic would actually make, not a caricature of one.

Show solution

There is no single correct answer here; both left and right critiques are legitimate. Two examples of what a strong response looks like:

Disability adequacy (steelmanned): Supported Living recipients currently receive approximately 800 dollars a week — more than double the proposed floor — because the costs of living with a significant disability are genuinely higher: medical equipment, personal care, housing modifications, transport. Replacing an 800-dollar payment with a 370-dollar floor is not welfare simplification for this group; it is a 430-dollar-a-week cut dressed up as universality. A reform that claims to lift people out of poverty while halving the income of those who cannot supplement it through work has not solved the problem — it has moved it somewhere less visible.

Poor targeting (steelmanned): The problem the Citizen’s Income claims to solve — abatement traps, complexity, cost-of-living pressure — affects low-income New Zealanders. Paying 370 dollars a week to every adult, including those in comfortable households, means the majority of the policy’s 62.8 billion dollar gross cost flows to people who do not need a welfare floor. Better targeted reforms — higher Jobseeker rates, fixed abatement thresholds, simplified entitlements — could address the real problem at a fraction of the cost, leaving more fiscal room for housing, health, or education. Universality is a solution looking for a problem at the top of the income distribution.

A strong response picks one, engages with the actual evidence, and does not misrepresent TOP’s position (for example, do not omit the transitional top-up when critiquing the Super replacement).

3. What would you need to know to judge whether the floor is high enough?

The Citizen’s Income sets a floor at approximately 370 dollars a week. What information would you need — and what questions would you ask — to assess whether that level is adequate for different people?

Show solution

Good answers might include any of the following:

  • Regional housing costs. A 370-dollar floor has very different real value in Westport versus central Auckland, where rent alone can exceed that amount for a single person.
  • What happens to disability-specific costs. If the Citizen’s Income replaces Supported Living Payments without supplementary support for disability costs, the adequacy question is answered mainly by asking what happens to those costs — which don’t go away.
  • How 370 dollars compares to NZ poverty measures. MSD publishes household income poverty thresholds. Whether the Citizen’s Income crosses the poverty line depends on household size, housing costs, and whether the threshold is measured before or after housing costs.
  • Whether the payment is indexed over time. A floor that is adequate in 2026 but is not indexed to wages or inflation becomes less adequate every year. Does TOP propose indexation, and to what?
  • What supplementary payments remain. If accommodation supplements, disability supports, and similar payments continue to exist on top of the Citizen’s Income, the effective floor is higher than 370 dollars for some groups. If they are abolished, the floor is exactly 370 dollars — which matters enormously for people who currently receive more than that.
  • Who the roughly 900,000 unaccounted adults are. The eligibility gap identified by an independent analyst means the policy’s actual reach is uncertain until TOP explains who falls outside the payment and why.

4. Attribute the claim

Read the following three statements. For each, identify whether it is (a) TOP’s own claim or modelling, (b) an independently verified fact, or (c) an analyst’s inference from published data.

  • “The Citizen’s Income will lift approximately 50,000 families out of poverty.”
  • “Supported Living Payments can reach approximately 800 dollars a week.”
  • “TOP’s published costings appear to imply only approximately 3.2 million recipients, against 4.1 million adults.”
Show solution

  • “Lift approximately 50,000 families out of poverty”: (a) TOP’s own claim. This is the party’s modelling of its policy’s distributional effect. It has not been independently verified by Treasury or any external body. Report it as “TOP claims” or “by TOP’s modelling.”

  • “Supported Living Payments can reach approximately 800 dollars a week”: (b) an independently verifiable fact. MSD publishes benefit rates; the figure here can be checked directly on the MSD website or in published benefit schedules. It is not TOP’s claim and not an analyst’s inference.

  • “TOP’s costings appear to imply approximately 3.2 million recipients against 4.1 million adults”: (c) an analyst’s inference. This finding comes from an independent commentator (Good Ideas NZ) examining TOP’s published figures. TOP has not confirmed or explained this discrepancy. It is a legitimate concern worth tracking, but it is not a fact TOP has stated and not a number that has been independently audited.

The distinction matters throughout: conflating TOP’s own modelling with independently verified data understates how much of the claimed impact is still contested.

Recap

TOP’s Citizen’s Income is a near-unconditional 370-dollar weekly payment to almost all adults, designed to dissolve welfare abatement traps by keeping the floor fixed regardless of what you earn from work. The case for it is structural: the current system penalises people for earning more, and a flat floor removes that penalty. The case against it comes from two directions — from the left, that a flat floor is too low for people whose current payments substantially exceed it, especially disabled New Zealanders; from the right, that paying everyone regardless of need is an inefficient way to help those who need help most. The sharpest unresolved questions are about disabled people whose current payments exceed the proposed floor, and about the roughly 900,000 adults whose absence from TOP’s costings has not been publicly explained.
