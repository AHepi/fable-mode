Economics as History, Not Nature

Three critical histories that take what feels most natural about money and markets and show, from the record, that it was made, finishing with the deeper argument about what money actually is.

We are taught to treat the economy the way we treat the weather, as a fact we live inside and never voted on. Money replaced clumsy barter. Markets are what people do when left alone. There is no real alternative. This path takes two of those settled feelings and does the one thing that dissolves them: it goes to the historical record and asks when they began.

History is the throughline here, not abstract models. The Myth of Barter is the concrete starting point: it follows the actual evidence, from Mesopotamian debt tablets to the first coins of Lydia, to show that money grew not from barter but from credit and obligation, and it examines the rival theories of money as objects of study rather than as the frame. How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural widens the lens to the whole order, tracing how enclosure, the invention of “the market,” and the remaking of work and time turned a recent arrangement into common sense. What Money Actually Is is the advanced finish, an undergraduate sequel to the first course that stops dodging the hard question and adjudicates it: is money a commodity the market threw up, or a unit of account and a measure of debt instituted by law and the state? It gives each theory its strongest case, confronts the objection the intro course left alone (the ancient standard was silver, and silver is a commodity), and refuses the tidy verdict the field itself refuses. Each course stands on its own and can be taken first. Together they hand you a single discipline: when something presents itself as simply the way things are, ask when it started, whom it suits, and what the record actually shows.

By the end you can
  • Tell a remembered history from a story that a theory simply needs to be true.
  • Explain where money really came from: credit and debt, recorded long before the first coin.
  • See how a made economic order comes to feel natural, and hold the tools to question it.
  • Hold the commodity and credit theories of money at full strength, and judge precisely how far the evidence carries each.

The path

  1. 1
    The Myth of Barter
    Start concrete: where money actually came from, and why the barter origin story is a myth.
  2. 2
    How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural
    Then the whole system: how a recent, built economic order came to feel like human nature.
  3. 3
    What Money Actually Is
    Finish with rigor: the sequel to The Myth of Barter, adjudicating commodity versus credit theory and refusing the verdict the evidence will not support.
