We are taught to treat the economy the way we treat the weather, as a fact we live inside and never voted on. Money replaced clumsy barter. Markets are what people do when left alone. There is no real alternative. This path takes two of those settled feelings and does the one thing that dissolves them: it goes to the historical record and asks when they began.

History is the throughline here, not abstract models. **The Myth of Barter** is the concrete starting point: it follows the actual evidence, from Mesopotamian debt tablets to the first coins of Lydia, to show that money grew not from barter but from credit and obligation, and it examines the rival theories of money as objects of study rather than as the frame. **How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural** widens the lens to the whole order, tracing how enclosure, the invention of "the market," and the remaking of work and time turned a recent arrangement into common sense. Each course stands on its own and can be taken first. Together they hand you a single discipline: when something presents itself as simply the way things are, ask when it started, whom it suits, and what the record actually shows.
