The Myth of Barter buried a story: that money began as a fix for the awkwardness of swapping goods. Killing that story felt like settling the question of what money is. It did not. A myth about where money came from is one thing; a claim about what money fundamentally *is* is another, and the deeper question walked out of the wreckage unharmed. Is money at bottom a special commodity that trade threw up, or a unit of account and a measure of debt that law and the state instituted?

This course adjudicates that question at the level the intro course could not. It takes each answer at its strongest: the commodity theory of Menger and Mises, with its real case for why metal won and why a thing of intrinsic worth does work among strangers that no IOU can; and the credit and state theories of Innes, Knapp, Keynes, Lerner, Grierson, and Ingham, kept carefully distinct rather than blurred into one "chartalist" voice. Then it brings the hardest evidence to bear on both, including the objection the intro course left alone: that the ancient standard was denominated in silver, a commodity, so money-of-account before coins does not by itself decide anything. The answer, that the silver ratios were set by temple, palace, and law rather than by a market, shifts the case without settling it.

You will weigh coinage and the state, debt and the clean slate, the over-reaching claim that society rests on a primordial debt, and the money created in your own bank account when a loan is typed into existence. The reward is not a verdict. It is the rarer thing an education is actually for: the two best theories of money held at full strength, a precise sense of what the evidence can and cannot bear, and the discipline to leave open a question the field has honestly left open. Some of what you learn will be firm. The rest will be a sharp, well-mapped "unsettled," and knowing the difference is the point.
