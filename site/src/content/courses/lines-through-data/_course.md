Two measurements, side by side: hours studied and exam score, temperature and ice-cream sales, a
city's library count and its test results. Do they move together? By how much? And — the question
that trips up everyone from headline writers to working scientists — does one *cause* the other? This
short course teaches you to read a relationship between two things without being fooled by it.

It's the third mini-course in the *Statistics, From the Ground Up* series, and it stands on its own:
where it leans on shared ideas — the natural wobble in data, telling a real signal from random noise —
it reminds you in a line. (The *Statistics Essentials* course is the full warm-up if you want it.)
Across three short modules you'll learn to read a **scatter plot**, measure how tightly its cloud of
points hugs a line with **correlation**, summarize the trend with a **best-fit line** (and see why it
predicts averages, not individuals), and then confront the headline lesson: two things moving together
can be coincidence, reverse cause, or the work of a hidden third thing. **Correlation isn't causation**
— and by the end you'll know exactly why, and what to ask instead.

No formulas to derive here; correlation and the line are taught as ideas you can see. The reward is a
kind of statistical self-defense: the refusal to be talked into a cause by a chart that only shows a
pattern.
