A topologist, the joke goes, can't tell a coffee mug from a doughnut. Each is a single lump of
stuff with exactly one hole — the mug's hides in its handle — and you can squash one into the other
without ever tearing or gluing. That joke is the whole subject in one sentence: topology studies
what survives when you allow any amount of stretching and bending but forbid cutting and pasting.
Size, angle, and straightness all dissolve; what remains are the stubborn features of a shape, like
how many pieces it has and how many holes run through it.

This is a first course in topology that assumes only high-school math — algebra, functions,
coordinates, and a little set notation. No calculus, and no prior experience with proofs. We start
from the rubber-sheet intuition, then build the precise language that backs it up: open sets, what
makes a collection of them a *topology*, and a definition of continuity that needs no limits. From
there we forge the tools that let you *prove* two shapes are genuinely different — connectedness,
the cut-and-count argument, counting holes, the one-sided Möbius band, and the Euler characteristic,
the little number you get by counting corners, edges, and faces that is 2 for any sphere and 0 for
any doughnut.

By the end you will be able to say exactly what it means for two shapes to be "the same," compute
an invariant with your own hands, and use it to settle the question we opened with — why the mug
really does equal the doughnut, and why neither will ever equal a sphere.
