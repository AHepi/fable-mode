## The hook

Give a child a rubber stamp and an empty page, and watch what happens. One stamp, pressed again and again, fills the whole sheet. Nobody handed them a second stamp; the whole pattern grew out of a single mark, repeated.

A group can behave the same way. Pick one element, apply the operation to it over and over — itself, then itself again, then again — and watch what spills out. Sometimes you get a small, tidy patch. Sometimes the single element, stamped enough times, covers the entire group. The element that can do that has a name, and so does the group it fills. This module is about starting with one and seeing how far it reaches.

## Intuition

In the last module we learned to spot smaller groups living inside bigger ones — subgroups. Here is the laziest possible way to build one: grab a single element $g$ and just keep hitting it with the operation.

Write $g^2$ for "do $g$, then $g$ again" — exactly the power notation we used for the triangle's turns, where $r^2$ meant *turn, then turn*. Then $g^3$ is three copies of $g$ combined, $g^4$ is four, and so on. List them out:

$$
g,\quad g^2,\quad g^3,\quad g^4,\quad \ldots
$$

This list cannot run forever in a *finite* group — there are only so many elements to land on, so sooner or later the walk has to revisit somewhere it has already been. And when it does, something clean happens: it comes home to the identity $e$, and then the whole pattern repeats from the top, like the child's stamp marching in a loop. The collection of everything you can reach this way — all the powers of $g$, together with $e$ and the inverses that walk the steps backward — turns out to satisfy all four group rules. It is a subgroup, and we say $g$ **generates** it.

Two questions fall out immediately. *How many* distinct stamps do you make before the pattern closes and loops? And *which* part of the group does the loop cover — a small corner, or the whole thing? The first question is the **order** of the element; the second decides whether the element is a **generator** of the entire group. Let us pin both down.

## Definition (Order of an element)

Let $g$ be an element of a group with identity $e$. The **order of $g$** is the smallest positive integer $k$ for which

$$
g^k = e.
$$

If no such $k$ exists — if no number of repetitions ever returns to $e$ — we say $g$ has **infinite order**.

## Definition (Cyclic subgroup generated by an element)

Let $g$ be an element of a group. The **cyclic subgroup generated by $g$**, written $\langle g \rangle$, is the set of all powers of $g$:

$$
\langle g \rangle = \{\, \ldots,\ g^{-2},\ g^{-1},\ e,\ g,\ g^2,\ \ldots \,\}.
$$

When $g$ has finite order $k$, this set is exactly $\{\, e,\ g,\ g^2,\ \ldots,\ g^{k-1} \,\}$, and it has $k$ elements.

## Definition (Cyclic group and generator)

A group $G$ is **cyclic** if there is some element $g$ in $G$ with $\langle g \rangle = G$ — that is, the powers of $g$ produce every element of $G$. Any such $g$ is called a **generator** of $G$.

## A note on notation: powers versus multiples

One snag before the examples. The word "power" and the symbol $g^k$ make sense when the operation is multiplication-flavored, the way composing turns of a triangle is. But when the operation is **addition** — as on the clock, in $\mathbb{Z}_n$ — combining $g$ with itself $k$ times is not a power, it is a *multiple*: you write it $kg$, not $g^k$, because $g + g + g$ is plainly $3g$.

These are the same idea in two costumes. "Apply the operation to $g$ a total of $k$ times" is written $g^k$ when the operation is multiplicative and $kg$ when it is additive. The identity is $e = 1$ under multiplication and $e = 0$ under addition, so the order of an additive element $g$ is the smallest positive $k$ with $kg \equiv 0$. Keep the picture — *one element, stamped $k$ times* — and the notation will sort itself out.

## Worked example (1 generates all of Z_6)

**Problem.** In $\mathbb{Z}_6 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ under addition mod $6$, show that $1$ generates the whole group, and find the order of $1$.

**Solution.** The operation is addition, so we form *multiples* of $1$, reducing mod $6$ each time:

$$
\begin{aligned}
1\cdot 1 &= 1, & 2\cdot 1 &= 2, & 3\cdot 1 &= 3, \\
4\cdot 1 &= 4, & 5\cdot 1 &= 5, & 6\cdot 1 &= 6 \equiv 0.
\end{aligned}
$$

The multiples of $1$ run through $1, 2, 3, 4, 5$ and then land on $0$, the identity. Along the way they hit every single mark on the six-mark clock, so

$$
\langle 1 \rangle = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\} = \mathbb{Z}_6.
$$

Since the powers (here, multiples) of $1$ fill the whole group, $\mathbb{Z}_6$ is **cyclic** and $1$ is a **generator**. The smallest positive $k$ with $k \cdot 1 \equiv 0$ is $k = 6$, so the order of $1$ is $6$ — the same as the size of the group, which is exactly what we expect from a generator. $\square$

## Worked example (the order of 2 in Z_6)

**Problem.** In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, find the order of the element $2$ and the subgroup it generates.

**Solution.** Form multiples of $2$, reducing mod $6$:

$$
2,\qquad 2+2 = 4,\qquad 2+2+2 = 6 \equiv 0.
$$

We reach the identity $0$ after three steps, and no sooner, so the order of $2$ is $3$. The subgroup it generates is

$$
\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4\},
$$

a tidy patch of three elements — not the whole group. So $2$ is **not** a generator of $\mathbb{Z}_6$. Notice the order of the element, $3$, equals the size of the subgroup it builds, $\{0, 2, 4\}$. That is no accident: an element of order $k$ generates a subgroup of exactly $k$ elements, a thread we will pull on hard in the next module. $\square$

## Worked example (r generates the rotations but not all of D_3)

**Problem.** In the triangle group $D_3 = \{e, r, r^2, s, rs, r^2 s\}$, find the order of $r$ and the subgroup it generates. Is $D_3$ cyclic by way of $r$?

**Solution.** The operation here is composition, so we take genuine *powers* of $r$. From the triangle module we know $r^3 = e$:

$$
r,\qquad r^2,\qquad r^3 = e.
$$

The identity first appears at the third power, so the order of $r$ is $3$, and

$$
\langle r \rangle = \{e, r, r^2\}.
$$

These are precisely the three rotations of the triangle — a subgroup of size $3$ sitting inside a group of size $6$. The powers of $r$ never produce a single flip; no amount of turning will ever flip the triangle over. So $\langle r \rangle \neq D_3$, and $r$ is **not** a generator of $D_3$.

In fact $D_3$ has no generator at all. Every rotation only ever makes more rotations, and every flip $f$ satisfies $f^2 = e$, so its powers give just $\{e, f\}$ — two elements, nowhere near six. No single element of $D_3$ can reach the whole group, so $D_3$ is **not cyclic**. (This is the price of non-commutativity we met last time: a cyclic group, built from powers of one element, always commutes with itself, so a group where order matters cannot be cyclic.) $\square$

## Exercises

1. **(Mechanical.)** In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, find the order of the element $3$ and write out $\langle 3 \rangle$. *Hint: form $3, 3+3, \ldots$ mod $6$ and stop when you first hit $0$.*

2. **(Mechanical.)** In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, find the order of $5$ and the subgroup $\langle 5 \rangle$. Is $5$ a generator? *Hint: add $5$ repeatedly mod $6$ — equivalently, you are stepping backward by $1$ each time.*

3. **(Mechanical.)** In $D_3$, find the order of the flip $s$ and write out $\langle s \rangle$. *Hint: recall $s^2 = e$, so the list is very short.*

4. **(Conceptual.)** Looking at exercises 1 and 2, list all the elements of $\mathbb{Z}_6$ whose order is $6$ — that is, all of its generators. What do the generators seem to have in common with the number $6$? *Hint: check which of $1, 2, 3, 4, 5$ share no common factor with $6$ other than $1$.*

5. **(Conceptual.)** Explain in a sentence or two why a cyclic group can never be non-commutative. *Hint: every element is a power of one generator $g$; show that $g^a$ combined with $g^b$ gives the same thing in either order.*

6. **(Stretch.)** Back in the clock module you stepped around $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ by repeatedly adding $3$ and asked whether you visited every mark. Reframe that question here: what is the order of $3$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, what is $\langle 3 \rangle$, and is $3$ a generator of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$? *Hint: add $3$ mod $12$ until you return to $0$; the number of distinct landings is the order.*

## Recap

Hand a group one element and let the operation run, and you carve out the cyclic subgroup $\langle g \rangle$ — every power (or, under addition, every multiple) of $g$. The number of steps before the pattern loops home to the identity is the **order** of the element. When a single element's powers fill the entire group, the group is **cyclic** and that element is a **generator**: $1$ does this for $\mathbb{Z}_6$, while $2$ only reaches the patch $\{0, 2, 4\}$ and $r$ only reaches the rotations of $D_3$. Watch what kept happening: the order of an element matched the size of the subgroup it generated, every time. That quiet coincidence is the doorway to counting inside groups — to Lagrange's theorem, where we discover that the size of a subgroup can never be just anything, but must divide the size of the whole group.
