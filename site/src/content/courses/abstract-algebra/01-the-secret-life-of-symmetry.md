## The hook

Pick up a square coaster and turn it a quarter turn. It looks exactly the same as before. Turn it
again — still the same. Flip it over left to right — same again. You have just done something, and
the coaster refuses to admit anything happened.

That refusal is the whole subject. Abstract algebra is the study of actions that leave something
unchanged, of the rules those actions obey, and — this is the surprising part — of how the *same*
rules show up in places that look nothing like a coaster: in arithmetic, in the solutions of
equations, in the shuffles of a deck of cards. Learn to see the rules, and a square coaster and a
clock and the number line all turn out to be telling the same story.

## Intuition

In school, algebra means *numbers and the letters that stand in for them*. You solve for $x$. You
factor. The objects are numbers, and the game is finding the ones that make an equation true.

Abstract algebra moves the spotlight. It stops asking "what are the objects?" and starts asking
"what can you *do* to them, and what rules do those doings obey?" The objects could be numbers. They
could just as easily be the turns and flips of a coaster, or the ways to rearrange three books on a
shelf. What matters is the *combining*: do this action, then that one, and you get a third action.
That act of combining is called an **operation**, and it is the real star of everything ahead.

Here is the move that makes the subject powerful. Once you describe a situation purely in terms of
its operation and the rules that operation follows, you can forget what the objects ever were. Two
situations that obey the same rules are, for an algebraist, the same situation wearing different
costumes. The quarter-turns of a square and the hours on a clock are not similar by coincidence —
they are running identical machinery underneath. Spotting that shared machinery is what this course
trains you to do.

Think of it like learning the rules of chess. You don't need to know whether the pieces are carved
from ivory or printed on cardboard; the game is the same. Abstract algebra studies the *rules of the
game*, and lets the pieces be whatever they like.

## What we are after

Most of this course studies one kind of structure, the **group** — a set of things together with an
operation that follows four simple rules. That sounds modest. It is not. Groups are the
mathematician's universal tool for measuring symmetry, and symmetry, it turns out, is hiding inside
arithmetic, geometry, and even the formulas you used to solve quadratic equations.

We will build groups from the ground up. No proofs are assumed — you have probably never written
one, and that is fine. When we need to justify a claim, we will walk through it slowly and say why
each step is allowed. The only tools you need on day one are the algebra and geometry you already
own: solving simple equations, working with functions, picturing triangles and squares.

## The road ahead

- We warm up with **clock arithmetic**, a finite world of numbers that wrap around, to get a feel
  for an operation that isn't ordinary addition.
- We distill arithmetic down to **four rules** worth caring about.
- We bolt those rules together into the definition of a **group**, then go hunting for groups in the
  wild — in numbers, in fractions, and in the symmetries of a triangle.
- We learn to see groups *inside* other groups, to build them from a single element, and to discover
  when two groups are secretly identical.
- We close by widening the lens to **rings and fields**, the structures that organize all of modern
  algebra.

## Recap

Abstract algebra studies operations and the rules they obey, not the particular objects being
operated on. That shift — from "what is it?" to "what can you do to it, and how does the doing
behave?" — is what lets one set of ideas explain symmetry, arithmetic, and structure all at once.
Next, we step onto the clock and meet our first operation that isn't the addition you grew up with.
