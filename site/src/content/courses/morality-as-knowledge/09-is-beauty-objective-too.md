## The hook

Pick the thing you are surest is just a matter of taste. Not morality, which by now has given you trouble. Something safer. A flower is beautiful, you think, the way chocolate is good: pleasant to you, nothing more, and a person who found a rose drab would not be *wrong*, only differently wired. Beauty is the home territory of "just opinion." Even people who waver about ethics rarely waver here. The eye of the beholder owns it outright.

So here is a fact that should not fit. Humans across the world, in cultures that never met, reliably find flowers beautiful. And flowers did not evolve their colors and shapes for us. They evolved them to attract insects, bees and moths and beetles, species with which we share no taste, no upbringing, no agreement about what looks good. A flower is a signal aimed at a creature whose nervous system parted from ours hundreds of millions of years ago. Yet the signal lands on us too, a third party it was never sent to. If beauty were only in the eye of each beholder, the flower would have no way to please an eye it never evolved to please, and certainly no way to please two such different eyes at once. Something is being got *right* by that flower, across a gap, and the rightness reaches even the strangers.

## Context & background

This argument is **David Deutsch's**, the physicist and philosopher whose extension of Popper's epistemology this course has followed throughout. He makes it in *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011), in a chapter titled "Why Are Flowers Beautiful?" It is not the load-bearing beam of the case for objective morality. It is a parallel, a second site where the same surprising thing can be seen, and seeing it twice in two such different domains is what makes it convincing. Objectivity-without-a-foundation turns up here where no one expects it, in the most subjective-seeming subject there is, and the work below is to follow how it gets there and to weigh honestly how much that does and does not show.

Recall the tool the parallel uses. In the course's sense, a claim is objective when it is answerable to more than opinion, able to be got wrong and improved. And a good explanation, in Deutsch's criterion from the previous module, is one that is **hard to vary**: its parts are locked together by the work they do, so none can be swapped without wrecking it. The myth that winter comes because a grieving goddess withdrew can be moved to any season and explains nothing; the axial tilt cannot be moved at all without breaking. Hard-to-vary is what makes an explanation answerable to the world rather than to the teller's convenience. The flowers argument runs that same criterion through biology and lands, startlingly, on beauty.

One distinction has to be set down before the argument, because the word "beauty" carries more than the argument claims. Not all beauty is in question here. Much of what any one culture finds beautiful is local and learned, the cut of a coat, the curve of a script, the chord that sounds resolved to an ear raised on one tradition and unfinished to another. Deutsch is not denying that. The claim is narrower and stranger: that *some* beauty, the kind that crosses the widest gaps, is objective in the same sense a sound explanation is. A flower's beauty is the test case precisely because it crosses the widest gap imaginable, from one kingdom of life to another.

## Narrative / exposition

Begin with the problem the flower has to solve, stated as the flower's engineering problem, because that is what it is.

A flower needs a pollinator. It cannot move, so it must persuade an insect to come, carry its pollen to another flower of the same kind, and come back. The insect, for its part, is busy and has no reason to help. So the flower offers a reward, nectar, and the trouble is advertising it. The flower has to send a signal an insect can find, recognize, and trust, across a distance, in competition with every other flower and every distraction in the meadow. The signal must be easy for the right insect to detect and hard for anything else to fake, because a cheap signal that any weed could mimic would be worthless, drowned in noise, leading the insect nowhere.

Consider who the two parties are. The flower and the insect are not partners who sat down and agreed on a code. They are separate species, shaped by separate histories, with no shared language and no way to negotiate. They share no prior agreement about what counts as a clear, attractive, trustworthy signal. Whatever standard the signal meets, it cannot be a standard internal to one of them, a private taste the flower happens to have, because the flower's private taste is exactly what the insect has no access to. A signal tuned to the flower's own idiosyncratic preferences would be invisible to the creature it needs to reach.

This is the gap. And here is Deutsch's move. To send a signal that works reliably across that gap, a signal hard to fake and hard to vary, the cleanest available solution is to converge on criteria that are *not internal to either species*: criteria that are, in a word, objective. A pattern that is genuinely, non-arbitrarily striking, hard to produce by accident and hard to counterfeit, is a pattern both sides can lock onto without ever having agreed on anything, because its appeal does not depend on agreement. It is the aesthetic analog of a hard-to-vary explanation. Just as a good explanation earns its standing not by anyone's say-so but by being the kind of thing that cannot be cheaply varied to say something else, an objectively beautiful signal earns its reach not by shared taste but by being the kind of thing that cannot be cheaply faked. The flower and the insect did not invent a private code. They discovered a public standard, the way two mathematicians who never met arrive at the same theorem.

The clincher is the third party. We never entered this negotiation. Humans are not pollinators of orchids; the flower never evolved to signal to us, and we never coevolved to read it. If a flower's beauty were merely a key cut to fit one particular insect's private lock, we would have no reason to find it beautiful at all, any more than we find a radio frequency we cannot receive to be music. But we do find flowers beautiful, reliably, across cultures, as outsiders to the whole arrangement. The most economical explanation of *that* is that the flower converged on something objectively attractive, criteria not internal to any one species' taste, and that those criteria are available to any sufficiently capable mind that comes along, including ours. Beauty, on this account, is got right by the flower the way a theorem is got right, without a vote and without a foundation, and a stranger can recognize it for the same reason a stranger can check the proof.

So the thing readers were surest was mere preference turns out to wear the same signature as moral and scientific knowledge. It can be answerable to more than opinion. It can be got right, by hard-to-vary design, tuned across a gap to a partner that could not be consulted. And it needs no foundation to be objective, only the capacity to be got right or wrong, which the flower demonstrates by getting it right well enough that a species it never met still finds it beautiful.

Why is *that gap* doing the work? Why would the argument be weaker if the flower were signalling to itself, or to a near-identical partner it had long agreed with?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The gap is what rules out a subjective explanation of the signal. If the flower were signalling to itself or to a partner it shared a taste with, the signal's appeal could be a private, agreed-upon convention, beauty "internal to" that pair, settled by their shared preference and answerable to nothing further. That would look exactly like "just taste." But there is no shared preference to lean on across a species gap, and no negotiation could install one. So a signal that nonetheless reaches across the gap, reliably and hard-to-fake, cannot be running on private taste; it must be meeting criteria that are not internal to either party, criteria that are objective. The wider the gap with no prior agreement, the more the reliable signal forces the objective reading. The human, a third party the flower never evolved to signal, is the widest gap of all, which is why our finding flowers beautiful is the sharpest evidence.

## Primary source

The argument is best read in Deutsch's own framing of the puzzle. Confirming his exact wording across these sentences from memory is beyond what this draft can do honestly, so the passage below is a clearly labelled paraphrase of his case in "Why Are Flowers Beautiful?" rather than a direct quotation. The structure is his; the words are a reconstruction, not a transcription.

> *Paraphrase of David Deutsch, "Why Are Flowers Beautiful?", in* The Beginning of Infinity *(2011).* A flower must signal across the gap between two different species, its own and that of its insect pollinator, which share no prior agreement about what is attractive. To be reliable, that signal has to be hard to fake. The most economical way to be hard to fake across such a gap is to meet a standard of beauty that is objective, not one internal to either species. That humans, who are not the intended audience, also find flowers beautiful is evidence that the standard really is objective, as objective as the criteria of mathematics.

Read it as the same shape of claim the course has been making about knowledge, now wearing biology. "Hard to fake" is the aesthetic cousin of "hard to vary." A signal a weed could cheaply counterfeit is easy to vary and worthless, the meadow's equivalent of an explanation you can adjust to fit any fact. A signal that holds up across a species gap is hard to vary, locked to criteria outside any one taste, and that is what lets it be objective. Then comes the harder claim, in the last clause: as objective as the criteria of mathematics. Deutsch is not saying flowers are pretty and leaving it there. He is putting beauty, on its best examples, in the same epistemic category as the most objective knowledge there is.

## Analysis & interpretation

What does this reinforce, and exactly how far does it carry? The honest accounting matters here more than the rhetoric, because a parallel oversold is a parallel that discredits the case it was meant to support.

What it reinforces is the course's central reframe, in the least likely place. Objectivity does not require a foundation, a proof, or an authority. It requires only that a thing be answerable to more than opinion, able to be got right or wrong. The flowers argument shows that even *beauty*, the reigning example of mere preference, can meet that bar, got right by a process with no foundation under it and no agreement behind it. If objectivity-without-a-foundation can be found in aesthetics, the reader's lingering suspicion that it was a special pleading invented to rescue morality loses its last foothold. The same signature, hard-to-vary design answerable to more than taste, shows up in science, in ethics, and now in the petals of a flower. That recurrence is the reinforcement: not a new proof, but the same true thing seen from a third angle, which is how a reader comes to trust that it was true and not a trick.

Now the limits, stated plainly, because the analogy has them and a careful reader will already feel the strain. First, the flower's "objective beauty" is a claim about a signal that reliably attracts, hard to fake across a gap. That is a thinner notion than the full-blooded beauty an art critic argues over, and thinner still than the moral rightness the course is really after. To grant that the orchid's signal is objectively attractive is not yet to grant that a Rembrandt is objectively beautiful, let alone that cruelty is objectively wrong. The argument earns the narrow claim cleanly and gestures at the wider ones; it does not carry them on its back.

Second, and more important, this is a parallel, not a link in the moral argument. Nothing about morality is *proved* by flowers. The case that moral knowledge is objective was made earlier, on its own ground: no special is/ought problem, good explanations that survive criticism and are hard to vary, moral progress that reads as the correction of an error rather than a change of fashion. The flowers argument adds none of those bricks. It does something else, which is to make the conclusion they reached feel less lonely, by exhibiting objectivity-without-a-foundation in a domain no one suspected of harboring it. A reinforcing instance is worth having. It is not a substitute for the argument it reinforces, and reading it as one would be exactly the overreach this course has tried to avoid. This is a parallel, offered as encouragement to a conclusion already earned, not a proof smuggled in to do work the real arguments must do.

There is a rival reading worth naming, since interpretation here is argued, not decreed. A skeptic can grant every biological fact and resist the aesthetic conclusion. Perhaps, the skeptic says, what coevolution selected for is not *objective beauty* but merely *salience*, signals that happen to grab the particular sensory and neural machinery that pollinators and humans, as it turns out, partly share by common descent or by convergent wiring. On that reading the cross-species hit is explained by shared biology, not by an objective standard, and "beauty" is doing no work the word "conspicuous" could not do. Deutsch's reply is that mere salience is cheap and easy to fake, the loud and the garish, and that what flowers achieve is the harder thing, a signal that holds up as hard-to-counterfeit design, which is the aesthetic property and not merely the attention-grabbing one. Which reading is right is not settled by the flower alone. What the example does settle is that the question is a real one, not foreclosed by "beauty is obviously subjective," and that is already more than most readers granted at the start.

## The story

In 1862 a parcel of orchids reached Charles Darwin from Madagascar, and among them was one that stopped him: *Angraecum sesquipedale*, a white star of a flower with a nectar spur of extraordinary length hanging from its base, a green tube running down nearly a foot, with the nectar pooled only in the last inch or two at the very bottom. Darwin turned it over and reasoned about it the way the flowers argument reasons. The spur was not decoration. It was a piece of engineering aimed at a partner the flower would never negotiate with. For the orchid to be pollinated, some animal had to push its head deep enough that it reached that distant nectar, brushing the pollen on the way, and to reach the bottom of a foot-long spur an insect would need a tongue of roughly that astonishing length. No such insect was known. Darwin predicted it anyway. In Madagascar, he wrote, there must live a moth with a proboscis that could drain that spur, and he was willing to stake the orchid's whole design on the existence of a creature no one had ever caught.

The prediction had the shape the module has been describing. It was hard to vary. Darwin could not loosen it to fit whatever turned up, because the flower's measurements pinned it: a spur that length demands a tongue that length, or the coevolution makes no sense. A vaguer naturalist could have said the flower was simply lovely and left the spur unexplained. Darwin let the flower's form make a risky, specific claim about a partner it was tuned to across the gap between an orchid and an insect, two lineages with no shared anything except the fit between a tube and a tongue, a fit neither could have arranged by agreement.

The moth was slow to appear, and the people who first matched it to the prediction did so after Darwin's death. In 1903 the entomologists Walter Rothschild and Karl Jordan formally described a Madagascan hawk moth whose proboscis could drink from that spur, and gave it a subspecies name that recorded the whole episode: *praedicta*, the predicted one. The flower had been advertising, across a gulf between kingdoms of life, the precise dimensions of a stranger it needed, and the stranger was real, carrying a tongue cut to fit a lock it had never seen described. The signal and the spur were not arbitrary taste. They were design tuned across a gap, hard to fake and hard to vary, answerable to a partner that could not be consulted and a world that could prove the guess wrong. That such a guess could be made from the shape of a flower, and come out right decades later, is the natural history under Deutsch's argument: beauty and attraction, on their best examples, are not the soft opposite of objective knowledge. They are made of the same thing, got right across a distance, by a design that could not have been cheaply otherwise.
