## The hook

Two centuries ago, the buying and selling of human beings was legal, profitable, and respectable across much of the world. Now it is none of those things. Something happened in between, and the question is what kind of something it was.

There are two ways to describe the change, and they sound similar until you press them. The first: people used to keep slaves, and then they stopped, the way they used to wear powdered wigs and then stopped. Customs turn over. A practice that suited one age fell out of favor in the next, and the codes shifted, as codes always do. The second description uses a different word. People used to keep slaves, and they were *wrong* to, and then enough of them came to see it, and the practice was abolished because it was an injustice and had always been one. On the first description, a fashion changed. On the second, an error was corrected.

Everything turns on the gap between those two words, *change* and *correction*. Almost everyone, including almost every relativist, reaches for the second when the case is slavery. They do not say the abolitionists merely had a different taste in social arrangements. They say the slaveholders were wrong, the way a person can be wrong about a fact. That ordinary reaction is not loose talk. It commits you to something, and what it commits you to is exactly the objectivity the relativist set out to deny.

## Context & background

The challenge this module answers was built, at full strength, several modules back. The relativist's claim was that morality is only local: a moral claim is true relative to a culture or a speaker, with no fact of the matter standing above them to settle which culture is right. The evidence was real and ancient. Cultures have differed, deeply and sincerely, about what may be done, and each has been certain of itself. The relativist took that diversity and drew a conclusion from it: if there were a single objective moral truth, surely we would have converged on it, and since we have not, there is none. There is only the patchwork of local codes, each valid where it lives.

That view makes a clear prediction about history, and it is worth stating in the relativist's own favor before testing it. If moralities are just local codes, then over time they should *change*, the way languages and fashions and cuisines change, drifting with circumstance and taste and the accidents of who conquered whom. Diversity across space implies diversity across time. The relativist predicts that moral codes will shift, and they have shifted, and so far the relativist is winning. The mere fact that our moral code differs from that of the Roman slaveholder is no embarrassment to relativism at all. It is precisely what relativism expects.

So the bare fact of moral change proves nothing, and a reply that pointed only to change would be no reply. The relativist has already accounted for change. To answer the relativist you have to show something stronger: not that codes changed, but that at least one of those changes was an *improvement*, a getting of something right that had been gotten wrong. That is a claim relativism did not predict and, as the argument will show, cannot accommodate. The whole reply lives in the difference between a code that merely became different and a code that became *better*.

One term from an earlier module does the load-bearing work here. A moral explanation counts as objective, on the account already built, insofar as it solves real problems about what to do and how to live, survives criticism, and is hard to vary, the kind of explanation whose parts are locked together so that it cannot be adjusted to taste without breaking. Progress, if it is real, is movement toward explanations of that kind, and the asymmetry between change and correction is what comes apart next.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the asymmetry, because the whole argument is built on it, and it has to be argued rather than assumed.

That a moral code changed over time is, by itself, trivial. Codes change. Relativism predicts it, history confirms it, and nobody disputes it. If "moral progress" meant only "moral change," there would be nothing to argue about and nothing for relativism to fear. The interesting claim, the one relativism cannot have, is that some particular changes were *corrections*: cases where the later code is not merely different from the earlier one but *better*, closer to getting something right, an improvement and not a swap.

Take the load-bearing case. We do not, when we look back at the abolition of slavery, regard ourselves as a generation that merely happened to develop a distaste for an arrangement our ancestors happened to relish, the way one century favors ornament and the next favors clean lines. If that were all we thought, we would hold the slaveholders blameless, as we hold the wig-wearers blameless. A change of fashion carries no verdict. Nobody was *wrong* to wear a powdered wig, and nobody who stopped had *corrected* anything; they just moved on. But that is not how anyone, relativist included, actually regards slavery. We hold that the slaveholders were *wrong*, that owning human beings was an injustice while it was happening and not only by the lights of a later taste, and that the people who saw this and ended it were *right*. We treat abolition as a case of someone being mistaken about something and someone else getting it right. We treat it, in a word, the way we treat being wrong about a fact.

That word *wrong* is doing more than it looks. To say the slaveholders were wrong, and not merely differently inclined, is to say there was something they were wrong *about*: some matter on which the truth ran one way and their conviction ran the other. And once there is a matter on which they could be wrong, there is a matter on which the abolitionists could be right, a standard the one was missing and the other was tracking. You cannot be in error unless there is something to be in error about. The reaction everyone has to slavery, the refusal to file it under "changed taste," is already a commitment to a standard above both codes, the slaveholders' and the abolitionists', against which the first can be judged worse and the second better.

This is the move worth slowing down for, because it is the heart of the matter. *You cannot improve toward nothing.* The very idea of improvement requires a direction, a respect in which the later state is better than the earlier one, and "better" is a comparison, and a comparison needs something to be measured against. If there is no standard at all, there is no better and no worse, only different and different. So anyone who says abolition was an improvement, and not merely a change, has already granted that there is a standard the improvement moved toward. The abolitionists were tracking something the slaveholders missed. That something is what the earlier module called an objective moral standard: not a foundation, not a proof, not a commandment from any authority, but a fact of the matter about how human beings may be treated, the kind of thing a moral explanation can get right or wrong, hard to vary once you have seen it clearly. The reality of the correction *implies* the reality of the standard. The standard is not assumed in advance and then used to license the verdict; it is read off the verdict everyone already makes.

Now the relativist is in a vise, and it is worth turning the screws slowly, because the trap is the relativist's own. The relativist holds that all moralities are equally valid, each true relative to its own culture, with no standard above them. Apply that consistently to slavery. The slaveholding society had its code, on which slavery was permitted, true-for-them. The abolitionist society had its code, on which slavery was forbidden, true-for-us. Two codes, each valid in its own place, neither answerable to anything higher. On that picture, what happened between them? Not an improvement, because improvement needs a standard the relativist has denied, and without a standard there is no fact about which code is better. All the relativist can say is that the code *changed*. One arrangement gave way to another, the way Greek funeral custom gave way to Persian, each horrified by the other, neither right. The relativist cannot say abolition was *progress*. The relativist can only say it was *change*. The word *better* is not available, because the relativist sawed off the branch the word sits on.

But here is what relativism cannot escape: abolition *was* an improvement, and the relativist, off duty, believes this as firmly as anyone. No one with the doctrine fresh in mind actually holds that the end of slavery was a lateral move, a matter of one era's arrangements yielding to another's with nothing gained. Pressed on the actual case, the relativist condemns the slaveholders and honors the people who fought them, which is to say the relativist applies a standard above both codes, the very standard the doctrine forbids. So relativism collides with a belief in moral progress that the relativist genuinely has and cannot give up. Either the relativist abandons the doctrine, or the relativist abandons the belief that abolition was an improvement, and almost no one can bring themselves to do the second. The doctrine and the conviction cannot both stand. This is what it means to call relativism self-undermining: not that it is contradicted from outside, but that the people who hold it cannot live inside it, because their own confident judgment that some moral changes were corrections is a judgment relativism has no room for.

So the reply to the relativist, owed since the module that built the challenge, is finally paid. The relativist was right that codes change and right that cultures differ, and those facts are no threat to objectivity, because change is not correction. The relativist was wrong to think diversity was the end of the story. Some of the changes were corrections, abolition chief among them, and a correction is movement toward a standard, and a standard above all the local codes is exactly the objective something the relativist denied. Moral error is real: the slaveholders were in it. Moral progress is real: the abolitionists climbed out. And the reality of both, error and the correction of error, is the reality of the objective standard that error and correction require.

## Primary source

Deutsch makes this argument in *The Beginning of Infinity*, in the course of his case that moral knowledge is objective in the same way scientific knowledge is. No verified verbatim passage is reproduced here; what follows is a clearly-labelled paraphrase of his position, not his words.

> Paraphrase of David Deutsch, *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011): There has been real moral progress over the course of history, and the abolition of slavery is among its clearest instances. To call it progress is to say more than that customs changed. It is to say that the earlier moral knowledge was defective and the later moral knowledge is better, that an error was found and corrected. Moral philosophies, like scientific theories, are problem-solving conjectures open to criticism, and they improve when criticism exposes their errors. So moral knowledge can grow, and the claim that it cannot, that there is only the parade of incommensurable local codes, is itself a conjecture that the actual history of moral improvement refutes.

The shape of Deutsch's move is the one this module has been building toward. He does not begin by assuming an objective moral standard and then ranking the codes against it. He begins where everyone already stands, with the shared recognition that abolition was an improvement and not a mere turnover of fashion, and he reasons from the improvement to the standard it required. The objectivity is the conclusion, not the premise. That is why the argument has force even against a reader who arrived a relativist: it does not ask you to accept a moral foundation up front. It asks only whether you really believe abolition was progress, and then shows you what believing that already commits you to.

## Analysis & interpretation

The argument is strong, but honesty requires marking what it does and does not deliver, in keeping with the discipline this course has held throughout.

What it delivers is this. If you grant that abolition was an improvement and not merely a change, you are committed to an objective standard above the local codes, and so to the reality of moral error and moral progress. That conditional is tight. The escape route is correspondingly narrow: to keep relativism, you must deny the antecedent, must hold with a straight face that the abolition of slavery was no improvement, only a change of arrangements between equally valid codes, the slaveholders no more mistaken than a person who prefers a different century's clothes. The argument's power is that it makes the relativist pay this price out loud. Most will not pay it. The cost of consistency is a verdict on slavery that almost no human being can sincerely endorse, and a doctrine you cannot sincerely apply to the clearest case is a doctrine in serious trouble.

There is a more interesting objection, and it deserves its strongest form rather than a brushing-off. A determined relativist might accept the cost and bite down: yes, strictly, abolition was only a change, and our sense that it was an *improvement* is itself just our local code talking, the parochial conviction of a culture that happens to forbid slavery, with no more standing than the slaveholder's conviction that it was fine. This is at least consistent. But look at what it costs the one who says it. It requires treating one's own deepest moral certainty, that owning human beings is a wrong and not a preference, as a mere artifact of upbringing, no more answerable to anything than a taste for a local dish. Few can hold that under their own weight, and the ones who say they can tend to drop it the moment the case is concrete and close. More to the point, the bitten-down position does not refute the module's argument; it confirms its structure. It concedes that *if* abolition was a genuine improvement, *then* there is an objective standard, and it escapes only by denying the improvement. The disagreement has been driven all the way down to a single question, whether the end of slavery was progress or only change, and that is exactly where this course wants the question to rest. The reader who cannot honestly call it a mere change has, by that very judgment, granted the standard.

What the argument does not deliver should be marked too, and it sets up the course's honest close. Showing that moral progress is real, and that its reality implies a standard being approached, is not the same as settling every question in metaethics about what that standard ultimately is or how mind-independent it runs. A sophisticated anti-realist can agree that abolition was an improvement, can agree there is something we were getting better at, and can still dispute the deepest account of what that "something" amounts to. That debate is genuine and is not closed here. The claim of this module is bounded and strong: moral progress is real, change is not correction, and relativism, the view that there is no standard above the local codes, cannot survive contact with the one correction nobody is willing to call a mere change.

## The story

John Newton spent the early 1750s as the master of slave ships. He had been pressed into the navy, deserted, been flogged, and ended up in the trade himself, eventually captaining the *Duke of Argyle* and then the *African* on voyages that bought human beings on the West African coast and carried them across the Atlantic to be sold. He kept careful logs. He recorded the numbers loaded and the numbers who died in the hold, the way a man records cargo. At the time he saw nothing in it that troubled him much. The trade was legal, ordinary, and lucrative, the settled practice of his world, and he was a working part of it.

He left the sea after an illness, took work onshore, and years later was ordained in the Church of England, serving for decades as a parish minister. For a long time he said nothing public about the trade he had once worked. Then, in 1788, more than thirty years after his last slaving voyage, he broke the silence and published a short pamphlet, *Thoughts upon the African Slave Trade*. In it he set down what he had seen with his own eyes: the conditions in the holds, the chains, the deaths, the routine violence of the business, described not by an abolitionist who had read about it but by a man who had run the ships. And he turned the account on himself. "I hope it will always be a subject of humiliating reflection to me," he wrote, "that I was, once, an active instrument, in a business at which my heart now shudders." He added that he felt bound in conscience to make a public confession, late as it came, since it could no longer prevent or repair the misery he had been party to.

The interesting thing, for this module, is what changed and what did not. The facts of the trade were the same in 1788 as in 1752. The holds had been just as crowded, the deaths just as numerous, the chains just as real, while Newton was filling his logs and seeing nothing wrong. What changed was not the world but his knowledge of it, his understanding of what those facts meant and of his own place among them. He came, under the pressure of reflection and the criticism of a growing public argument, to see the thing he had done as a wrong, and to say so against his own younger certainty and his own former interest. That is not a man swapping one fashion for another. A man who merely changed his taste in social arrangements would feel no shame, write no confession, call nothing a thing his heart now shuddered at. Newton's revulsion makes sense only on the assumption he himself came to hold: that the trade had been wrong all along, wrong while he profited from it and believed it ordinary, and that he had finally gotten right what he had earlier gotten wrong.

He did not stop at the pamphlet. The young member of Parliament William Wilberforce, in doubt about whether to stay in public life and pursue the cause, consulted Newton, who urged him to remain and press on. The parliamentary campaign ground forward across two decades, and in 1807 Britain abolished its slave trade. Newton lived to hear of it. He died that December, an old man who had begun as one of the trade's instruments and ended among the witnesses against it. The arc of his life is the module's claim in a single person: not a change of custom but the correction of an error, made under criticism and hard experience, by someone who had every interest in not making it, and who could give no account of his own shame except that he had once been wrong about something it was possible to be wrong about.
