## The hook

Why is it colder in winter?

A child asks it, and the answer everyone half-remembers is that the Earth is farther from the Sun in winter. The trouble is that this is false, and obviously so the moment you say it out loud to anyone in the southern hemisphere, where July is the cold month and the Earth is at exactly the same distance from the Sun as it is in a freezing northern January. When it is winter in Buenos Aires it is summer in Berlin, on the same planet, at the same distance. Whatever causes the seasons cannot be distance, because distance is the same for both hemispheres at once and the seasons are opposite.

The real cause is that the Earth's axis is tilted. The planet spins about an axis that does not point straight up out of its orbit but leans over by about twenty-three degrees, and it keeps pointing the same way all year as it goes around the Sun. For half the orbit the northern end of the axis leans toward the Sun, so the northern hemisphere catches the Sun's rays more directly and for more hours, and it is summer there while the southern hemisphere, leaning away, has winter. Half an orbit later the arrangement is reversed. That is the whole of it.

Now look at what just happened. The distance answer could be patched. Tell its defender about Argentina and they might say the seasons are caused by something else entirely, or that distance matters in some places and not others, or that the hemispheres are exceptions. The story bends to absorb the trouble. The axial-tilt answer cannot bend like that. Change the tilt to nothing and you lose the seasons altogether; change which way the axis points and you predict summer in the wrong month; detach the explanation from the geometry of a tilted, orbiting sphere and it stops explaining anything at all. Its parts are locked to each other and to the observed pattern. You cannot move one piece without wrecking the whole. That difference, between a story you can bend to fit anything and one you cannot bend at all without breaking it, is the subject of this module, and it turns out to be what objectivity was made of the entire time.

## Context & background

The course has cleared a path to this point. Module 01 replaced one idea about objectivity with another: objective in the course's sense does not mean certain or proven, it means answerable to more than opinion, able to be got wrong and improved. Module 04 took away the foundations: Popper's point that no knowledge, not even science, rests on a base that justifies it, and that knowledge grows instead by conjecture and criticism, by guessing and then hunting for what is wrong with the guess. Module 05 made the decisive move: morality has no foundation, but neither does science, so the lack of one is the universal condition of knowledge rather than a special defect of ethics. Morality stands on science's footing.

That move clears away the obstacle, but it leaves a hole where an answer should be. If proof and foundations are not what make scientific knowledge objective, then *something* does, because physics plainly is objective and the seasons really are caused by axial tilt and not by Demeter's grief. What is the something? Saying "not foundations" tells you what objectivity is not. It does not yet tell you what it is. Until that hole is filled, the parity won in module 05 is a little hollow: morality has been placed on the same footing as science, but the footing itself has not been described.

The physicist and philosopher **David Deutsch**, who took Popper's epistemology and pressed it further than Popper did, gives the missing account in his 2011 book *The Beginning of Infinity*. His answer is that what separates good knowledge from bad, in the absence of any foundation, is the quality of its **explanations**, and that the mark of a good explanation is a precise and demanding property he calls being **hard to vary**. This is Deutsch's idea, built on Popper's but not found in Popper. Popper supplied conjecture and criticism and the death of foundations; Deutsch supplied the criterion that says which surviving conjectures are any good. The seasons example above is his, and it is worth seeing exactly what it shows.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the old Greek myth, because Deutsch does, and because the contrast is the lesson. The ancient story of the seasons is that Persephone, daughter of the harvest goddess Demeter, was carried off to the underworld and is allowed back to the surface for only part of each year. While she is gone, Demeter grieves, and in her grief the world goes cold and barren; when her daughter returns, the goddess rejoices and the warmth comes back. That is winter: a goddess mourning. It is a real explanation, in the sense that it does set out to account for the thing it is about, the yearly turn from cold to warm and back. The question is whether it is a *good* one.

Watch how easily it bends. Suppose you discovered that winter falls in different months in different places. The myth does not even flinch: the goddess's moods, you can say, simply reach different lands at different times, or there are local goddesses, or the grief moves around. Suppose the cold came in autumn instead. Fine, then Persephone leaves in autumn. Suppose there were no seasons at all on some planet. Then Demeter never lost her daughter there. There is no possible pattern of warmth and cold that the grief story cannot be adjusted to fit, because the connection between the grief and the cold is not forced by anything. Why should a goddess's sadness make the air cold rather than hot, or rainy, or louder? It is simply asserted. And because it is merely asserted, it can be unasserted and reasserted differently the instant the facts press on it. The myth explains the seasons in the way it would equally well explain their opposite. A story that fits any outcome tells you nothing about why *this* outcome happened. It is **easy to vary**, and that is precisely why it is empty.

The axial-tilt explanation has the opposite character. Every part of it is doing load-bearing work, and the work locks the parts together. The tilt has to be a real tilt of a definite angle, or the directness of the sunlight does not change through the year. The axis has to hold its direction as the planet orbits, or the hemispheres would not trade places. The sphere has to be lit from one side by a distant source, or "more direct rays" would mean nothing. Pull out any one of these and the explanation does not get weaker, it collapses, because each piece is needed to deliver the very pattern you are trying to account for, opposite seasons in the two hemispheres, the cold months six apart, the long summer days and short winter ones. There is nowhere to insert a fudge. If the observed seasons had been different, this explanation could not have been bent to cover them; it would simply have been refuted, and you would have needed a different one. That is the strength hiding inside the apparent weakness. An explanation you cannot adjust to fit any outcome is an explanation that *commits*, and a claim that commits can be answerable to the world. **A good explanation, then, is one that accounts for what it sets out to account for and is hard to vary while still doing so.** Its parts are constrained by the problem they solve, so tightly that none of them can be swapped out without ruining the fit.

This is why hard-to-vary is not the same as simple, or elegant, or merely satisfying. A bare assertion can be simple. "Winter happens because it does" is as simple as you like and explains nothing. Hard-to-vary is a more exacting property: it asks whether the explanation's details are pinned down by the work they do, so that the explanation could have been wrong and isn't. The seasonal myth is easy to vary because its details float free of any constraint; the axial-tilt account is hard to vary because its details are nailed to the phenomenon at every point.

A good explanation also tends to do something its maker never asked of it. Deutsch calls this **reach**: the tendency of a good explanation to solve problems beyond the one it was invented for. The tilted axis was brought in to explain the seasons, but once you have it, you have also explained why the midnight sun shines all night in the far north in June, why the length of the day changes through the year and changes more the farther you are from the equator, why the Sun climbs higher in the summer sky, why the Arctic and Antarctic have their long polar nights. Nobody added those to the theory. They fell out of it, because a true explanation describes something real, and a real thing has more consequences than the one you happened to be looking at. The grief story has no reach at all; it explains the one pattern it was tailored to and casts no light on anything else, because there was never anything real behind it to have further consequences. Reach is a signature of getting something right. An explanation that solves problems it was not built for has latched onto more of the world than its author knew.

Now bring this back to morality, which is where the course has been heading since module 05 put ethics on science's footing. If a good *scientific* explanation is one that solves its problem and is hard to vary, and if morality is not epistemically worse off than science, then the same standard should tell good moral knowledge from bad. And it does. A moral claim is a kind of explanation too: an account of what to do, what to want, how to live, and why. "Keep your promises" is, unpacked, an explanation of how people who depend on each other can coordinate, trust, and plan, and of what goes wrong when they cannot. Such a claim is objective, in the course's sense, insofar as it solves a real problem, survives criticism, and is hard to vary, just as a scientific explanation is. It is answerable to something beyond opinion, the actual problem of living together, and it can be got wrong and improved.

Set against that, look at a bad moral "explanation," the kind the relativist mistakes for all there is. A taboo defended only by "that is just how we do things here" is the moral twin of the Persephone myth. Ask why the practice is required and you get no account of any problem it solves; ask what would happen if it were dropped and the defense bends to absorb the question, the way the grief story bent. The taboo can be attached to any practice and detached from any other, because it was never constrained by a problem in the first place. It is infinitely easy to vary, and like the myth, it explains nothing. A good moral claim, by contrast, is constrained, hard to vary, because it is doing real work on a real difficulty, and you cannot alter it freely without that work falling apart. "Do not enslave people" is not a taboo you could just as easily have pointed the other way; it answers to facts about what slavery does to the people inside it, and those facts hold the claim in place. This is the constructive core the course has been building toward: objectivity without foundations is good, hard-to-vary explanation plus the standing capacity to find and correct error. It is what the parity of module 05 was parity *of*.

## Primary source

The criterion is Deutsch's, set out in the opening chapter of *The Beginning of Infinity*, "The Reach of Explanations." What follows is a faithful paraphrase rather than a transcribed quotation, because the exact words and punctuation of the passage are not reproduced here from the page; the position, the phrase "hard to vary," and the seasons example are all genuinely his.

> Deutsch's claim (paraphrase, *The Beginning of Infinity*, 2011, ch. 1, "The Reach of Explanations"): A good explanation is one that accounts for what it sets out to account for and is *hard to vary* while still doing so, meaning its details cannot be readily changed without spoiling its ability to explain. He uses the seasons to draw the contrast. The myth that winter comes from a goddess's grief is easy to vary, since the grief could just as well have been made to produce any other effect, so it explains nothing; the axial-tilt account is hard to vary, since each part is forced by the work it does. He adds that the test of a prediction is not, by itself, what makes an explanation scientific, because a bad explanation can be patched to pass a test; what does the work is the demand for explanations that are hard to vary, and that quest for good explanations is the regulating principle of science and of the Enlightenment more broadly.

The first half of this tears down the same idol the course met in module 01, knowledge as justified true belief resting on an authority. The second half names what stands in its place. Deutsch does not say a good explanation is one that has been *proved*, or one that has *never* been contradicted. He says it is one that is hard to vary while still doing its explanatory job. That property is available without a foundation, which is exactly why it can carry the weight that foundations were once thought to carry, and why it can carry that weight in morality as readily as in physics.

## Check yourself

Before reading on, answer from memory. Here are two accounts of the same fact. Fact: a certain bridge collapsed. Account A: "The bridge fell because its main cables, designed for a load they were never going to face in that wind, resonated with the gusts until the deck tore itself apart, which is also why it had been swaying visibly for months." Account B: "The bridge fell because it was unlucky; bridges fall when their luck runs out." Which account is hard to vary and which is easy to vary, and what makes the difference?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Account A is hard to vary; Account B is easy to vary. In A, the details are locked to the phenomenon: the cables, the load, the wind, the resonance, and the swaying are all parts that do explanatory work, and you cannot change one (say, remove the wind, or deny the resonance) without breaking the fit to what actually happened. It even reaches beyond the collapse to explain the months of visible swaying, which nobody added on purpose. In B, "luck" is constrained by nothing; it could be attached to any collapse and any survival equally, explains the opposite outcome just as comfortably, and so explains nothing. The difference is whether the explanation's parts are pinned down by the problem they solve (A) or float free of it (B). Only the hard-to-vary account is answerable to the world and can be got wrong.

## Analysis & interpretation

The criterion repays a close reading, because its real force is easy to miss and easy to overstate, and the honest version of the course needs it stated exactly.

What hard-to-vary does is explain how objectivity survives the loss of foundations, which module 05 left as an open debt. A foundation was supposed to be what made a claim answerable to more than opinion: the proof, the bedrock, the thing that forced the conclusion. Take it away and the worry is that nothing is left but assertion, one person's say-so against another's. The reply is that a hard-to-vary explanation is answerable to more than opinion without any foundation at all, because it has staked itself on the details being a certain way, and the world can refuse those details. The axial-tilt account could have been wrong; it made claims that reality was free to contradict, about opposite hemispheres and six-month gaps and lengthening days, and it is held to its objectivity precisely by being thus exposed. Opinion, by contrast, stakes nothing and exposes nothing; the seasonal myth risks no detail and so answers to nothing. The capacity to be got wrong, which module 01 named as the whole of objectivity, just *is* the property of being hard to vary, looked at from the other side. A claim that cannot be got wrong, because it bends to fit any outcome, is not more secure than the others. It has opted out of objectivity altogether.

That is the strong reading, and it is the course's reading, but a fair interpreter should mark where the analogy with science is doing more work than it has yet earned, because the course has promised not to overclaim. In physics, the test that a hard-to-vary explanation invites is often an experiment with a number at the end, a measurement that either matches the risky prediction or does not. The moral case is harder, and an honest critic will press exactly here. What is the experiment for "do not enslave people"? When we say a good moral explanation must "survive criticism," what is the moral analog of the wind hitting the bridge, the recalcitrant fact that can refuse the details? Deutsch's answer is that the relevant problems are real, the problems of what to do and how to live together, and that bad solutions to them fail in discoverable ways, through the suffering they cause and the contradictions they generate and the criticism they cannot answer, while good solutions have reach into new problems the way good physics does. A rival reading, which module 10 will give its full hearing, holds that this moral "test" is too unlike empirical test to secure the same kind of objectivity, that calling a moral failure a refutation trades on a metaphor. That debate is genuine and the course does not pretend to close it here. What this module establishes is narrower and solid: *if* there is objective moral knowledge, this is the shape it has, good, hard-to-vary explanation answerable to real problems and open to correction, and not proof from a foundation, because proof from a foundation was never what objective knowledge was, anywhere, even in physics. That much is earned. Whether it carries all the way to mind-independent moral facts is a further question, and one the course leaves open until the end.

## Check yourself

From memory: a friend says, "A good explanation is just one that fits the facts. The Persephone myth fits the facts of the seasons fine, so by your standard it should count as good." Using this module's criterion, say what has gone wrong in the objection.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The objection mistakes "fits the facts" for the criterion, when the criterion is "fits the facts *and is hard to vary while doing so*." The Persephone myth does fit the seasons, but only because it would equally well fit any other pattern; it can be bent to cover seasons in different months, no seasons, opposite seasons, anything, since nothing in it forces the grief to produce cold rather than heat. That endless adjustability is what makes it a *bad* explanation, not a good one. A good explanation is constrained: its parts are pinned to the phenomenon so that it could have been wrong and isn't, like the axial-tilt account, which collapses if you alter any piece and even reaches to explain the midnight sun and the lengthening days it was never built for. Fitting the facts is cheap when you are allowed to vary the story freely. Being hard to vary is what is not cheap, and it is what the objection leaves out.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Find an easy-to-vary explanation in the wild.** Take a claim you have heard offered as an explanation, in politics, medicine, your own field, anywhere, that turns out to be easy to vary, able to absorb any result without changing its mind. Lay out how it bends, and then say what a hard-to-vary explanation of the same thing would have to commit to instead.

2. **Test a moral claim against the criterion.** Pick a moral claim you hold (keep your promises; do not be cruel; tell the truth). Treat it as an explanation of some real problem of living, and ask whether it is hard to vary: could it be adjusted freely to license its own opposite, or are its details held in place by the problem it answers? Say what you find, and whether the exercise changed your confidence in the claim.

3. **Press the disanalogy honestly.** The module grants that the moral analog of an empirical test is disputed. State the strongest version of the worry that "surviving criticism" in morality is too unlike a physics experiment to secure real objectivity. Then say whether you think Deutsch's reply (real problems, discoverable failures, reach) meets it, and where you draw the line.

4. **Reach, for and against.** Reach is offered as a signature of a good explanation. Give one example, in any domain, where an explanation solved problems it was not built for, and then consider whether reach can ever mislead, an explanation that seems to reach but is really being stretched. How would you tell the two apart?

## The story

In 1915 Albert Einstein finished the general theory of relativity, and the theory said something specific and dangerous about light. Gravity, in Einstein's account, is not a force reaching across space but a bending of space and time around mass, and a ray of starlight passing close to the Sun would have to follow that bending and arrive at Earth slightly deflected from a straight line. There was already a way to ask whether starlight bends near the Sun under the old Newtonian picture too, treating light as if it had weight, and that calculation gave a deflection as well. But Einstein's theory predicted a deflection twice as large as the Newtonian value, a definite number, not a vague "some." The two theories did not merely disagree about whether light bends. They disagreed about exactly how much, by a factor of two, and the difference was measurable.

This is the position a hard-to-vary explanation puts itself in. Einstein could not, having built the theory, halve its prediction to be safe, or claim the bending applied only on clear days, or attach a caveat to escape a bad result. The deflection followed from the geometry. The number was nailed to the structure of the theory the way the seasons are nailed to the tilt, and that meant the theory had handed the world a weapon against itself: measure the bending, and if it came out Newtonian, general relativity was wrong. The only moment the test could be run was during a total solar eclipse, when the Moon blots out the Sun's glare and the stars whose light grazes the Sun become briefly visible, so their apparent positions can be compared with where those same stars sit in the night sky months later.

The Astronomer Royal, Frank Watson Dyson, identified the total eclipse of 29 May 1919 as an unusually good chance, with the eclipsed Sun set against a rich field of bright stars. Arthur Eddington, the Cambridge astronomer who had grasped Einstein's theory early and championed it in Britain, helped organize two expeditions to catch it from opposite sides of the Atlantic, one to the island of Príncipe off the west coast of Africa, where Eddington himself went, the other to Sobral in northern Brazil. Two teams, two continents, one question, because a result from a single cloud-covered station would have settled nothing. The point of going twice was the point of the whole enterprise: to expose the prediction to every chance of failing.

The eclipse came, the plates were exposed, the measurements were made and argued over through the summer, and in November 1919 Dyson and Eddington announced that the deflection of starlight matched Einstein, not Newton. The light had bent by the larger amount. A theory that could have died on a photographic plate off the coast of Africa had instead survived a test that a vaguer theory could never even have faced, because a vaguer theory predicts no precise number and so risks nothing. Einstein, until then known mainly to physicists, became world-famous almost overnight; the news that the heavens had ruled for him ran in the papers. What had actually happened was smaller and larger than the fame suggested. An explanation hard to vary had made a risky, exact claim, had been put where reality could refuse it, and had not been refused. That is what objective knowledge looks like when it works, with no foundation under it and no proof behind it, only a guess that staked everything on the details and a test that could have killed it. It is the model the course holds up for moral knowledge: not certainty, but an explanation that commits, answers to a real problem, and stands open to the correction that, this time, did not come.
