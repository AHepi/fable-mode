Priya is twenty-four. She has a wisdom tooth that has been quietly nagging for two years. She knows what the dentist would say. She also knows what the dentist would charge — somewhere between three hundred and six hundred dollars for an extraction, more if it turns complicated. She has rent, a student loan, a job that doesn't come with sick leave, and a savings account that could cover the bill but would take months to rebuild. So she waits. She takes ibuprofen. She hopes the tooth gets bored and stops.

Priya is not unusual. She is the problem The Opportunity Party (TOP) is trying to solve with what it calls the **Teal Card**.

---

If you have read the Essentials course in this series, you'll recognise the backdrop: a cost-of-living squeeze where income is taxed heavily and wealth is largely not, and where young people face an economic start their parents didn't. This module sits inside that same diagnosis — but instead of tax architecture, it's about what TOP proposes to do for the specific cohort bearing the most acute pinch.

## What the Teal Card is

TOP proposes a Teal Card for every New Zealander **under 30**. It bundles five things:

1. **Fully funded GP visits, dental care, eye checks and glasses, and mental-health support.** The card covers these as primary-care services — not means-tested, not capped by parental income. If you're under 30, you go.

2. **Free public transport.** Any trip on a public system — bus, train, ferry — at no fare for the cardholder.

3. **A 1,500-dollar credit toward a bike, e-bike, or e-scooter.** A one-off credit to offset the upfront cost of active or electric micro-transport.

4. **A 5,000-dollar savings boost at 18**, deposited into the young person's account when they complete the National Civic Service (described below).

5. **The National Civic Service** — an Outward-Bound-style programme covering conservation work, civil defence training, civics, and personal finance. Completing it is what unlocks the 5,000-dollar savings deposit.

TOP puts the cost of the package at approximately **1.5 billion dollars per year**. The Teal Card is carried forward from earlier TOP work under prior leadership; the current platform retains it as written.

## The problem it's pointing at

The Teal Card is a response to two overlapping gaps.

**Health access.** In New Zealand, free GP visits are already extended to under-14s, and some subsidies exist up to 24. But adult dental care is mostly unsubsidised for everyone outside children and some low-income holders of community service cards — and the cliff between those limited coverages and full adult pricing is steep. According to health surveys, New Zealanders consistently report cost as the main reason they delay or skip dental care. Mental-health services have long waiting lists; the publicly funded tier is rationed, and private care is expensive. TOP argues these gaps fall disproportionately on young adults, who are most likely to be earning entry-level wages, carrying student debt, and not yet covered by workplace health benefits.

**Economic footing.** Homeownership among under-40s has fallen sharply since the 1990s. Younger cohorts face higher rent-to-income ratios, larger student loan balances, and less accumulated savings than the same age cohort a generation ago. TOP argues that 18 is a particularly exposed moment — old enough to be legally and financially independent, young enough to have almost no financial cushion. The 5,000-dollar savings boost is designed to give something concrete at that threshold.

The transport credit sits at the intersection: a bike or e-scooter reduces commuting costs, which matter more at low incomes.

<details><summary>Check yourself: why does TOP single out dental care?</summary>

TOP's argument rests on a specific gap in NZ's public health coverage: dental care is not covered for most adults, and cost is the leading reason New Zealanders skip it. Young adults — on low wages, often without employer health benefits — are among those most likely to delay treatment. The Teal Card's dental component is meant to remove the price barrier specifically for the under-30 cohort. Note this is TOP's framing; the underlying coverage gap is a real feature of NZ's health system, but how much it harms outcomes, and whether this is the most efficient fix, are contested.

</details>

## NZ fit

Primary dental care for adults is one of the most glaring omissions in New Zealand's publicly funded health system by OECD comparison. The country covers a great deal — hospital care, primary GP visits for low-income adults, ACC injury treatment — but adult teeth largely fall outside the public net. TOP argues the Teal Card addresses a structural gap rather than a luxury.

The transport credit fits an NZ-specific pattern too: public transport networks are concentrated in the main centres, and upfront costs for alternatives (a decent e-bike runs 1,000 to 2,500 dollars) can be prohibitive at entry-level wages.

The National Civic Service draws on models from elsewhere — Outward Bound, AmeriCorps, Singapore's national service — but TOP's version is designed to be shorter, skills-focused rather than military, and oriented toward environmental and community work.

## The critiques, fairly

**Cost.** 1.5 billion dollars per year is real money. Critics ask whether universal benefits for an entire age cohort — regardless of income — are the most targeted way to spend it. Under the Teal Card, a 28-year-old lawyer who can easily afford dental care gets the same card as Priya. A more targeted approach (subsidised care only for lower-income under-30s) would cost less and, arguably, help more. TOP's counter is that means-testing carries administrative overhead and stigma, and that universality avoids the gaps and cliffs that targeted schemes tend to produce.

**Is the savings boost really universal?** A fair question: the 5,000-dollar deposit is tied to completing the National Civic Service. That means it is conditional — not every 18-year-old will complete the programme, whether through disability, caring responsibilities, being in remote areas, or simply choosing not to. Critics argue this makes the savings boost not a universal economic foundation but a reward for participation, and that the programme's Outward-Bound character may not suit everyone. If the civic service is genuinely optional — no participation, no money — then "universal" is a stretch. TOP has not fully addressed what happens to young people who cannot participate.

**The 30 cut-off.** Why 30 and not 31? The boundary is administratively necessary but arbitrary in human terms. A 30-year-old in a casualised job with no savings has the same structural problem as a 29-year-old. Supporters of the policy tend to respond that some boundary is inevitable and that 30 roughly tracks the period of most acute economic vulnerability for early-career adults; critics note that arbitrary cut-offs always create cliff edges, and people just over the line often resent them.

**Is this the best use of 1.5 billion?** Broader health economists and commentators have asked whether primary dental care for all under-30s addresses the highest-priority gaps. Some argue the money would go further improving adult mental-health services for all ages, or addressing the primary-care shortage in rural areas. These are not arguments that the Teal Card's goals are wrong — they are arguments about what the same money could alternatively do.

**Exercise 2 — Is tying money to civic service fair? Argue both sides.**

Write two short paragraphs: one arguing that linking the 5,000-dollar deposit to completing the National Civic Service is reasonable, and one arguing it is not. Try to make each as strong as you can before reading the discussion below.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

**The case for conditionality:** Civic service offers skills — civil defence, conservation, personal finance — that have independent value. Tying a financial reward to completion gives an incentive for participation, which TOP argues increases the programme's take-up and social benefit. Many social programmes attach conditions (KiwiSaver requires contributions; student loans require study); this is in that tradition. The argument is that the state gets something back — a more civically prepared cohort — in exchange for the investment.

**The case against:** A universally available payment that requires a specific programme to unlock is not truly universal. An 18-year-old with a disability that prevents outdoor conservation work, or one who is the sole carer for a younger sibling, or one in a rural area far from programme facilities, cannot access the same deposit as a healthy, mobile, urban peer. The savings gap the Teal Card is meant to address exists regardless of whether someone can complete an Outward-Bound-style programme. Critics argue the civic service is a worthwhile idea that could stand on its own, but attaching it as the condition for a financial transfer punishes those already in harder circumstances.

Neither reading is definitively wrong. The question of when conditionality is a reasonable design choice and when it becomes a barrier is live in social-policy debates everywhere.

</details>

**Exercise 3 — Identify the strongest critique**

Of the four critiques raised in this module — cost, conditionality, the age cut-off, and opportunity cost — which do you think poses the hardest question for TOP's proposal, and why? Write two or three sentences before reading the note below.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

There is no single correct answer here, but consider the structure of each critique:

- **Cost** is a critique every policy faces; TOP's counter (that universality avoids administrative cost and means-testing traps) is a standard social-policy response with genuine evidence behind it.
- **Conditionality** strikes at the internal logic of the policy: if the savings boost is the most economically significant component and it is conditional, the universalism TOP claims is only partial. This is arguably the critique most difficult to answer without changing the policy's design.
- **The age cut-off** is real but manageable — all age-bounded policies face it, and the question is whether the boundaries are set sensibly, not whether they exist at all.
- **Opportunity cost** depends on what you value and what you think the counterfactual spending would achieve — it is a genuine debate but requires comparing policies rather than examining this one.

Noticing which critiques target a policy's internal logic (conditionality here) versus its external tradeoffs (cost, opportunity cost) is a useful analytical habit for any policy debate.

## Recap

The Teal Card bundles five things TOP proposes for under-30s: free primary health care (including dental and mental health), free public transport, a transport credit, a savings deposit at 18, and a civic service programme that unlocks that deposit. The problem it targets is real — dental coverage gaps, mental-health access, and a thin economic start are documented features of life for young adults in New Zealand. The critiques are also real: 1.5 billion dollars is a significant spending commitment; the conditionality built into the savings boost is in tension with the policy's universalist framing; the age cut-off is arbitrary at the margin; and there are genuine questions about whether the same money might do more elsewhere. The module that follows moves to two more policies that touch everyday life: transport and drug law.
