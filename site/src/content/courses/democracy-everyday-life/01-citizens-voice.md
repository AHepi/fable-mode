In October 2020, shovels broke ground on the Auckland light rail project. It had taken years of planning, more than a billion dollars in scoping work, and the better part of a parliamentary term to reach that moment. Eighteen months later, a new government cancelled it. The sunk cost — roughly 229 million dollars of public money — went nowhere. No track was laid.

This is not a story about light rail. It is a story about a pattern.

**The Opportunity Party (TOP)** — note that it was formerly called "The Opportunities Party" — argues that New Zealand politics runs like a pendulum: policies swing one way under one government, then swing back under the next, leaving behind waste, uncertainty, and an inability to make progress on anything that takes longer than three years to deliver. (The pendulum metaphor is established in the Essentials course in this series; the guard is worth repeating here: not every reversal is waste — some policy change is exactly what democracy is for. The concern is specifically with the *costly cycle* of building, cancelling, and re-building the same infrastructure and institutions.)

TOP proposes two linked responses: **citizens' assemblies** to break gridlock on big issues, and a suite of **clean-up politics** reforms to reduce the influence of money in political decisions.

## The mechanism — a randomly chosen jury, but for policy

Think of the way a jury works. You do not elect your peers for criminal trials, and you do not vote on who will serve. Twelve people are drawn at random from the electoral roll, asked to step away from their ordinary lives for a defined period, hear evidence from both sides, and reach a reasoned verdict. The randomness is the point: it is a cross-section of the community, not a room of insiders.

TOP proposes the same basic logic applied to policy. A **citizens' assembly** (TOP's term) would be a group of New Zealanders — **randomly selected**, paid for their time, and made representative of the country in age, gender, ethnicity, and geography — convened by the government to study a policy problem that Parliament has been unable to resolve. Members would hear from experts and from people directly affected, deliberate across multiple sessions, and then make formal recommendations.

The government would be required to **formally respond** to those recommendations. It would not be required to adopt them — Parliament still decides. That distinction matters and is worth holding onto: a citizens' assembly only *recommends*. It has no power to legislate, and no recommendation it makes becomes law without going through the ordinary parliamentary process.

Overseeing the process would be a new office: a **Parliamentary Commissioner for Citizens' Voice**, modelled on existing commissioners (the Ombudsman, the Children's Commissioner) and responsible for ensuring the assemblies are conducted fairly and that governments actually respond rather than quietly filing the recommendations away.

The **clean-up politics** package sits alongside this. TOP proposes a mandatory **lobbying register** — so the public can see who is meeting with ministers and on what topics — **limits on political donations**, and an explicit anti-corruption focus in government procurement and decision-making. The argument is that citizens' assemblies are undermined if the political system around them remains porous to influence money cannot touch.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what is the key limit on what a citizens' assembly can do?</summary>

A citizens' assembly makes *recommendations* — it cannot legislate. Parliament still has the final say. The government must formally respond to the assembly's recommendations, but it is not bound to adopt them. This is what distinguishes an assembly from a referendum (which produces a binding outcome, at least politically) and from Parliament itself (which actually passes law).

</details>

## The problem — where pendulum politics is most expensive

TOP's case for citizens' assemblies rests on identifying a class of problems that the pendulum actively makes worse. These are issues where (a) the stakes are high, (b) the decision requires a long time horizon, and (c) the policy is politically toxic enough that no government wants to move first.

TOP points to three recurring examples:

**Infrastructure that gets cancelled.** Auckland light rail (~229 million dollars in sunk costs), cancelled after the 2023 election. Ferry replacement (~300 million dollars committed, then cancelled under the same National-ACT coalition in 2024). Polytechnic reform (~297 million dollars into the unified Te Pūkenga network, then reversed). Each of these involved real spending on planning, procurement, or early construction before the project was scrapped. TOP argues that in a longer-cycle system — or with cross-party buy-in generated by a deliberative process — at least some of that waste could have been avoided.

**Gridlock on structural issues.** New Zealand Superannuation (the pension) has been fiscally unsustainable for years — everyone in Treasury agrees that an aging population makes the current settings unaffordable, but no government has been willing to raise the age or means-test it, because the political penalty is immediate while the fiscal relief is twenty years away. Housing and climate have similar profiles: long-horizon problems that require durable policy, but short electoral cycles that reward reversal.

TOP argues that an assembly — whose members are ordinary citizens rather than career politicians with something to lose — can study these questions, reach conclusions that carry legitimacy precisely because they are not made by the people who benefit from the status quo, and create political cover for governments to act.

## New Zealand's fit — and international models

The short three-year electoral cycle matters here. Most Westminster democracies run on four- or five-year terms; New Zealand is unusual even among its peers. A policy that takes four years to show results will, by definition, be evaluated by a government that did not make it. That structural fact is part of why the pendulum swings as hard as it does.

TOP cites two international examples as evidence that citizens' assemblies can work:

**Ireland.** The Irish Citizens' Assembly, established in 2016, produced recommendations on same-sex marriage (the 2015 referendum had already passed that question, but a later assembly on abortion reform is the cleaner example) and on repealing the Eighth Amendment — the constitutional provision restricting abortion access. The assembly's recommendation in favour of repeal was followed by a referendum in 2018, in which 66% of voters agreed. The key point is sequencing: the assembly did not bypass the democratic process — it *fed into* it, and the political confidence to hold the referendum came partly from the assembly's deliberations.

**France.** The Citizens' Convention on Climate (Convention Citoyenne pour le Climat), convened in 2019–2020, brought together 150 randomly selected citizens to develop proposals for reducing France's greenhouse gas emissions. Many of its recommendations were incorporated into the Climate and Resilience Law of 2021. Critics noted that some of the assembly's more ambitious proposals were watered down; that is, in fact, the critique — the government was not bound to adopt everything.

Neither example is a simple success story. Both generated genuine results *and* genuine arguments about implementation. TOP is drawing on real precedents, but the precedents include complications.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what role did Ireland's citizens' assembly play in the abortion referendum — and what did it not do?</summary>

The Irish assembly studied the question, deliberated, and recommended repeal of the Eighth Amendment. It did not hold the referendum itself — that went to the full Irish electorate, which voted 66% in favour of repeal. The assembly's role was to produce a credible, considered recommendation that gave political cover to the government to hold the referendum. It did not bypass voters; it informed them.

</details>

## The critiques — fairly

**Assemblies are advisory, and can be ignored.** This is the structural critique, and it is real. A government that dislikes what an assembly recommends can respond formally — technically satisfying the requirement — without acting on anything. The French experience with the climate convention illustrates this: President Macron initially said the assembly's proposals would go "without filter" to Parliament or a referendum; in practice, they were filtered. The value of an assembly depends significantly on whether the government that convenes it is willing to be bound in spirit, not just in procedure.

**Risk of being captured or stacked.** Random selection is only random if the process is genuinely independent. Critics ask: who chooses the question? Who designs the process? Who selects the facilitators? A cleverly framed question can pre-determine the range of answers; a sympathetic facilitator can steer deliberation. These risks are not hypothetical — they are the same risks that affect any expert advisory process. The Parliamentary Commissioner is meant to guard against this, but a commissioner appointed by the government faces obvious independence questions.

**Adds machinery and cost.** Convening a genuinely representative, paid assembly — flying members from Invercargill and Kaitāia to Wellington, providing expert testimony, funding facilitators, running the process over weeks — is not cheap. TOP does not publish a cost estimate for a standing assembly programme; critics note that the benefit of breaking gridlock needs to be weighed against the administrative overhead, and that the overhead is real.

**"Who picks the question?" is a political act.** Every assembly is convened to study something specific. Choosing to study superannuation rather than, say, property taxation is itself a political choice that reflects the priorities of whoever controls the process. The assembly then deliberates within that frame. This is not a fatal objection — all policy processes have a frame — but it means that the question-setting stage needs its own transparency and accountability.

A fair question: if assemblies are advisory only, what prevents a government from treating them as a performance of consultation rather than a genuine input?

The honest answer is: not much, structurally. The force of an assembly depends on the political cost of ignoring it — which in turn depends on public awareness and media scrutiny. That is a softer guarantee than a legal one.

## Exercises

**1. How is a citizens' assembly different from a referendum?**

Try to articulate the difference in your own words before looking at the discussion below.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

A referendum asks the full electorate to vote yes or no on a specific proposition. The outcome is a popular mandate, which is politically (and sometimes legally) binding. A citizens' assembly does not put a question to all voters — it convenes a representative *sample* of citizens to study a question in depth, hear evidence, deliberate, and make a recommendation. The recommendation then goes back to Parliament or, in some designs, becomes the basis for a referendum. The key differences: (1) Scale — a referendum involves millions of voters; an assembly involves dozens or hundreds. (2) Process — referendum voters usually receive a leaflet and a ballot; assembly members spend weeks with experts and affected parties. (3) Bindingness — referendum results carry direct political weight; assembly recommendations are advice that the government must respond to but need not follow. TOP's proposal sits in the assembly model, not the referendum model. Critics sometimes note that this makes assemblies weaker; supporters argue that the depth of deliberation makes recommendations more considered and harder to dismiss than a blunt yes/no.

</details>

**2. What is the strongest worry about giving assemblies significant power in the policy process?**

Think about the critiques above — which one do you find most substantive?

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

Different readers will weigh these differently, which is the point. The structural critique — that assemblies are advisory and can be ignored — is the most fundamental: it goes to whether the mechanism can deliver what it promises. But some readers will find the capture/stacking risk more urgent, because it concerns the integrity of the process rather than just its outputs. The "who picks the question" concern is arguably the deepest, because it sits upstream of everything else: an assembly that studies the wrong question, or studies the right question in a loaded frame, will produce a tainted result no matter how well it deliberates. A strong answer identifies one worry, explains *why* it is the most serious (not just that it exists), and considers what safeguards might address it and whether those safeguards are sufficient.

</details>

**3. NZ's three-year cycle is often cited as a cause of pendulum politics. Is a shorter cycle the only explanation?**

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

The three-year cycle is a contributing factor but probably not sufficient on its own. Countries with longer cycles (Australia's three-year House terms; the UK's five-year terms) also experience policy reversal. What seems to matter more is the combination of: the *margin* of change between governments (how different their policy platforms are — MMP has moderated this somewhat in NZ), the *concentration* of executive power (a unicameral Parliament with no upper house and a government that usually commands a majority), and the *political incentives* around long-horizon policy (the cost of addressing Super or housing falls on voters now; the benefit accrues to voters in twenty years). TOP's citizens' assembly proposal is partly about the cycle but more directly about the political-incentive problem: giving politicians a credible external mandate to act on hard questions.

</details>

**4. Is the lobbying register part of the citizens' assembly mechanism, or is it a separate reform?**

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

It is a separate reform, bundled under what TOP calls "clean-up politics." A lobbying register and donation limits are transparency and integrity measures aimed at reducing the influence of money in political decisions — they do not change how citizens' assemblies work. They belong in the same module because TOP presents them as complementary: the argument is that deliberative democracy (assemblies) and transparent democracy (lobbying/donation reform) reinforce each other. An assembly that produces good recommendations is undermined if a well-funded lobby can quietly pressure the government to ignore them. Whether you find that link persuasive is a separate question from whether each reform has merit on its own.

</details>

## Recap

Citizens' assemblies — randomly selected, paid, and required to produce recommendations that government must formally answer — are TOP's proposed mechanism for breaking gridlock on big issues that the pendulum cycle cannot resolve. The examples TOP cites (abandoned Auckland light rail, cancelled ferries, structural deadlock on Super and climate) illustrate the problem; the examples it draws on internationally (Ireland, France) show the idea has produced real results, alongside real limitations. The core critiques — that assemblies are advisory, that they can be captured, that the question-setting is itself political — are substantive, not trivial, and the mechanism's effectiveness depends heavily on the political will of whoever convenes it. Clean-up politics (a lobbying register, donation limits) sits alongside assemblies as a transparency complement rather than an alternative.
