Who really wins?

That is not a rhetorical question. It is the question at the centre of every serious argument about the Tax Reset — and it turns out to be two separate questions tangled together. The first is mechanical: once you run the numbers, does a given household pay more or less? The second is philosophical: even if the numbers say you win, is this the right way to redesign a tax system?

TOP has an answer to the first question. The critics have answers to both. By the end of this module you should be equipped to argue any side — not because the evidence is weak, but because the disagreement is genuine and the people holding each position are not fools.

---

## TOP's claim: 70/20/10

TOP's own modelling — not independently audited — produces what has become the package's headline number: roughly **70% of households end up better off, about 20% are largely unchanged, and about 10% pay more**. TOP also calls the income-tax simplification, taken together with the Citizen's Income, "the largest tax cut for low-income workers in history." That is TOP's slogan, quoted in their voice, not this course's assessment.

A few things worth holding onto when you see this number:

These figures come from TOP's modelling, not from an independent body such as the Treasury or an academic research group. The Citizen's Income, the Land Value Tax, and the restructured income-tax brackets are all moving at once, and the net effect for any individual household depends on which combination of wages, land ownership, and existing welfare entitlements they happen to have. Modelling integrated packages is hard, and small assumption changes can shift the distribution significantly. The figure is plausible — it follows logically from the mechanics we have already worked through — but "plausible" and "verified" are different things.

With that caveat noted, we can still use the 70/20/10 claim as a map to navigate by. The question is: which households land in which bucket, and why?

## Four households, four stories

The Tax Reset is a swap — less tax on wages, more on land, plus a floor payment under nearly everyone. Whether that swap suits you depends almost entirely on three variables: how much land you sit on, what you earn, and whether you currently receive a welfare payment or New Zealand Super.

**The renter on a low income.** Aroha works in hospitality and rents a flat in Wellington. She owns no land. Under the Tax Reset, the Citizen's Income — roughly 370 dollars a week — goes to her near-unconditionally, replacing the Jobseeker support she currently receives. The restructured income-tax brackets (28% from the bottom, but applied to a smaller effective base once the CI is counted) generally leave wage earners at this level better off overall, at least according to TOP's modelling. She is squarely in the "70%" bucket. The floor she was already standing on gets a little higher, and the abatement trap — the income threshold at which her current benefit starts to be clawed back — disappears. The strongest caveat from critics on the left: if Aroha has a serious disability, the Citizen's Income at roughly the Jobseeker rate may be substantially less than what she can access today through Supported Living, which can reach around 800 dollars a week. The floor is higher for some people; for others, it may be lower.

**The young family on a median home.** Jess and Mike own a three-bedroom house in Hamilton. The land under it is worth something — perhaps 350,000 dollars on a recent council valuation. At 1.75%, the Land Value Tax on that land comes to roughly 6,100 dollars a year: more than rates, less than a mortgage payment. Both Jess and Mike earn wages. They each receive the Citizen's Income. Their income-tax position simplifies. In TOP's modelling this household tends to come out net positive, because two Citizen's Incomes plus a lower effective tax burden on wages outweighs the LVT bill on a median suburban section. But the margin is not huge, and it depends heavily on whether both adults qualify and receive the CI — which circles back to the eligibility questions raised in Module 1 (the approximately 900,000 adults whose qualification status is unclear in the published costings).

**The asset-rich, cash-poor retiree.** Pita is 73 and lives alone in a house in Remuera where the land is valued at 1.4 million dollars. He owns no other assets of note. His income is New Zealand Super. Under the Tax Reset, Super is replaced by the Citizen's Income — a transition that TOP says will be managed with a top-up for current recipients, though the long-run trajectory is means-tested rather than universal. More immediately, the Land Value Tax on his section comes to around 24,500 dollars a year. TOP's deferral scheme lets him accrue that bill against the title and pay it when the property is sold, so no cash leaves his pocket today. But the deferred balance grows, the property has effectively been pledged as collateral, and the implicit pressure to downsize — or to let the bill accumulate through his children's inheritance — is real. "Land can't run away" is the correct observation about why an LVT is administratively tidy; it does not mean a large LVT bill is painless for the person whose wealth happens to be locked in the land.

This is the package's most politically charged case. Critics call it the "asset-rich, cash-poor problem," and it is not a fringe concern: a significant fraction of older New Zealanders, particularly in Auckland, sit on high-value land while living on fixed incomes. Supporters respond that deferral solves the cash-flow problem and that taxing land value — rather than wages — is fairer over the long run. Both positions have genuine substance.

**The high-value landowner.** Ngaio owns several Auckland properties as investment assets, with combined land value well above two million dollars. Her rental income is substantial. Under the Tax Reset, her LVT bill is large. She also receives the Citizen's Income — the payment is near-universal — but the CI does not come close to offsetting an LVT liability at this level. She is in the "10% pay more" bucket, and by design. The scales tilted toward property over work — the structural fact that taxing land has been lighter than taxing wages in New Zealand — would tilt the other way. Whether that is the right correction, and whether an LVT is the right instrument for it, is precisely what the critiques below contest.

## The critiques, organised

It is tempting to line these up left vs right and call it a debate. That works as a rough map but misses some crosscutting concerns that come from neither direction. Here is the full set, grouped as fairly as possible.

### Concerns that come mostly from the left

**The Citizen's Income is not adequate for people who need more.** The payment is pegged near the current Jobseeker rate. People receiving Supported Living today can receive substantially more, in recognition that disability and chronic illness carry costs a poverty-level floor does not cover. A payment that is unconditional and simple is also, necessarily, one-size. Critics argue this trades away targeted adequacy for universality — and that the people who lose most from that trade are already the most vulnerable.

**The package taxes only one asset class.** Land is a big store of wealth in New Zealand, but it is not the only one. A high-net-worth family that holds most of its wealth in shares, managed funds, or overseas assets faces no new tax under the Tax Reset — because those assets are not land. The very rich described in IRD's 2023 research, whose effective tax rates averaged around 8.9% against a median household's roughly 20%, hold a large proportion of their wealth as capital gains on assets broader than land. An LVT addresses the land component of that gap; it does not address the financial-wealth component. Critics on the left — and some economists — argue this makes the Tax Reset a partial fix at best, and that a broader wealth tax or a capital gains tax would do more for distributional fairness. This connects to the scales metaphor from the Essentials course: the scales are tilted, but the LVT only pushes on one part of the beam.

**Replacing universal Super is a political and human trade-off.** New Zealand Super is universal and generous by developed-world standards. The Citizen's Income, in TOP's design, replaces it with a lower base payment plus a means-tested top-up. For many retirees that is a cut in real income; for those with other assets, it phases out faster than Super does today. The left critique is that this erodes a cornerstone of retirement security that took decades to build.

### Concerns that come mostly from the right

**Universal payments misallocate.** The classical efficiency argument against a near-universal income is that it sends money to people who do not need it. A well-targeted welfare system, the argument goes, concentrates support where it does the most good per dollar. Universal payments are administratively simple, but simple is not the same as efficient. The NZ Initiative and related critics have made this argument explicitly.

**Compulsory saving is a squeeze, not a gift.** KiwiSaver 2.0 — compulsory at 6% employee and 6% employer — is framed by TOP as building a productive capital pool. Critics on the right, and some workers, frame it differently: it is a mandatory deduction from take-home pay, phased in over years, that reduces consumption choices for households that might otherwise prefer to spend or save differently. For low-income earners, the CI is meant to offset this; whether it does depends on the same household-by-household arithmetic that drives the 70/20/10 debate.

**A land tax on the family home is politically toxic and arguably unfair.** Voters who own their home do not think of themselves as landlords; they think of themselves as families who bought a house. An annual tax on the land under that house — even with deferral — reads as a new and compulsory obligation on the primary residence. The right's objection is partly principled (the family home should be protected) and partly practical (it is very hard to win an election on a platform that includes an annual tax on the place most voters live, however well the economics work).

**Valuation is harder than it looks.** The LVT applies to the *unimproved* land value — the spot, not the house on it. Separating land value from building value is not trivial. Council valuations already do something like this, which is why TOP argues the administrative machinery exists; critics point out that disputed valuations would be frequent, especially at the high-value end where the differences are large, and that the appeals system would be under sustained pressure. Māori land in collective title adds further complexity that TOP's published material does not fully resolve.

### The rent pass-through debate

This one sits in the middle, because it concerns tax incidence — the question, introduced in the Essentials course, of who *actually* bears a tax, as opposed to who writes the cheque.

LVT theory holds that a landlord cannot pass an LVT to tenants, because land supply is fixed. If a landlord raises the rent to cover the LVT, some tenants will leave, and eventually the landlord absorbs the cost rather than lose rental income. This is the standard economic argument. Critics point out that the theory assumes competitive rental markets and reasonable tenant mobility — neither of which fully describes Auckland. Whether rent pass-through happens, to what degree, and over what time horizon is genuinely contested among housing economists. TOP argues the LVT will *lower* rents by making it expensive to hold land idle; critics are not convinced the mechanism is fast or reliable enough to matter.

## Exercises

**1. Steelman the package.**
Make the strongest possible case *for* the Tax Reset. What problems does it genuinely solve that the existing system does not? Which households benefit most, and why might those benefits matter for New Zealand? Use only the mechanisms we have worked through — do not invent claims.

<details><summary>Show response criteria</summary>

A strong steelman would cover: (a) the floor effect — a near-unconditional CI eliminates abatement traps and reduces welfare complexity, providing real security for people cycling in and out of work; (b) the deadweight argument — an LVT taxes something that cannot flee or shrink in response to the tax, making it less economically distorting than taxing wages; (c) the distributional logic — 70% better off according to TOP's modelling, with the gains concentrated at lower income levels; (d) the NZ fit — NZ lacks a CGT or wealth tax, so the LVT partially addresses the untaxed-capital-gain problem without requiring a CGT's administrative complexity; (e) the administration case — NZ councils already do land valuations. A strong response cites these without endorsing them as correct.

</details>

**2. Steelman the strongest critic.**
Choose the single critique you find most compelling from the list above — left or right — and make its strongest version. What does the package fail to do that it should? Who is harmed, and how? What would a better policy look like?

<details><summary>Show response criteria</summary>

Any of the major critiques can support a strong response if developed honestly: the disability-adequacy gap (the CI is one-size and may be less than what some vulnerable people receive today); the single-asset-class problem (LVT doesn't touch financial wealth, where the effective-rate gap is concentrated); the retiree problem (a fixed-income household on a high-value section faces a structurally new obligation, deferral notwithstanding); the misallocation critique (universal payments are administratively efficient but not targeted). A strong response proposes a concrete alternative — a supplementary wealth tax, a higher CI floor for disability, a CGT alongside the LVT — and explains why that alternative would do better on the dimension the critic cares about.

</details>

**4. The rent pass-through disagreement.**
Two economists disagree about whether landlords will pass the LVT to tenants. One cites fixed land supply; the other cites Auckland's constrained rental market. What is each person's argument? What evidence would, in principle, settle the dispute? What does the disagreement suggest about the limits of the theory of tax incidence in this context?

<details><summary>Show response criteria</summary>

Economist A (LVT theory): land supply is fixed, so a landlord who raises rent to cover the LVT will face tenant exit and lose rental income — the rational response is to absorb the cost, not pass it on. This is the standard tax-incidence logic for a factor in perfectly inelastic supply. Economist B (market structure): Auckland's rental market is not perfectly competitive; tenant mobility is constrained by cost, geography, and housing supply; landlords may be able to raise rents without losing tenants if the alternative (buying) is even more expensive. Evidence that could settle it: long-run rent-to-income ratios after an LVT is introduced in a comparable market (Australia and parts of Europe have some analogue data); natural experiments from local rating changes in NZ. The disagreement illustrates a general point about tax incidence: the theory gives a clear result in idealised conditions, and real markets depart from those conditions in ways that require empirical observation, not just logic, to resolve.

</details>

**5. LEFT or RIGHT — which critique lands harder?**
Pick either the "taxes only one asset class" critique (often from the left) or the "universal payments misallocate" critique (often from the right). Explain the strongest version of each — then explain what evidence or value judgment you would need to hold in order to find the other side's critique more compelling than the one you picked.

<details><summary>Show response criteria</summary>

This exercise tests whether the reader can hold two opposing frameworks at once. A strong response to "taxes only one asset class": the LVT addresses land wealth but leaves financial wealth — where the effective-rate gap documented by IRD is concentrated — untouched; someone who cares most about reducing wealth concentration would want the package extended. A strong response to "universal payments misallocate": the CI sends money to people who do not need it; a means-tested system with higher payments for those who need more would do better per dollar on welfare outcomes. To find the other side more compelling: on the left critique, you would need to believe targeting efficiency matters less than simplicity and universality; on the right critique, you would need to believe the effective-rate gap on financial wealth is the primary injustice.

</details>

## Recap

The Tax Reset's central claim — 70% of households better off, on TOP's own modelling — follows logically from its mechanics: wage earners below high-land-value thresholds tend to gain from the Citizen's Income and income-tax changes more than they pay in LVT, while those who hold large land assets bear the new burden. The asset-rich/cash-poor retiree is the package's hardest case, and the deferral scheme is the answer the critics keep examining. The left's deepest objection is not that the package goes too far but that it stops too short — one asset class, one floor that is not high enough for everyone. The right's deepest objection is that universal payments and a land tax on the family home are both bad politics and questionable economics.

None of these arguments is trivial, and none is settled. What this course has given you is the machinery to follow them: the Citizen's Income as a floor and its eligibility complications (Module 1), the LVT's mechanics and why land makes an unusual tax base (Module 2), and here the distributional picture and the four strongest objection families. That is enough to sit down at the debate — not to end it.

"Homes, Energy and Oceans" follows, carrying the same framework into housing supply, energy, and the environment. The argument continues.
