Picture the most expensive piece of land in your street. Nothing on it — just the bare section, valued as if the buildings were gone. Now imagine a small annual charge on that value, replacing a chunk of what you'd otherwise owe in income tax. The house you've built is irrelevant. Your renovations are irrelevant. Only the location counts.

That, in outline, is the land value tax TOP proposes as the engine behind the Citizen's Income we looked at in the last module. The floor everyone stands on needs something to hold it up; this is TOP's answer.

If you've read the Essentials course, you'll already know the toolkit: a land value tax is an annual levy on the *unimproved value of land* — the worth of the spot, not whatever sits on it — and the central argument for taxing it, rather than wages or sales, is that land can't flee. NZ taxes wages but has no comprehensive capital gains tax (one of the few OECD countries in that position), so the tax base leans heavily on work. This module looks at how TOP would shift that base, what comes with it, and where the critics push back hardest.

---

## The mechanism: taxing the spot, not the house

TOP proposes an annual charge of **1.75% of urban land value** — on the *unimproved* value of the land, not the building on it — and **0.5% on rural land**. The rate is applied to the land as if it were a bare section: your house, your renovations, the sleepout you added last summer — none of it counts.

The tax is assessed using the land valuations councils already produce. NZ revalues land triennially as a standard part of the rating system, which means the administrative scaffolding largely exists. Councils already separate land value from improvements on every rating notice. TOP argues this makes an LVT administratively feasible in a way that a capital gains tax on property is not — there is no need to track purchase prices, calculate gains, or wait for a sale.

The shorthand that recurs in this series is worth keeping close: **taxing the worth of the spot, not the house on it**. That metaphor has a guard built in — it does not mean you're taxed for having a home; it means you're taxed on the locational value the community's investment in roads, schools, and services has created, not on what you've built.

TOP projects this tax would raise approximately **24 billion dollars a year**. To put that in context: the gross cost of the Citizen's Income is roughly 62.8 billion dollars, but most of that replaces existing welfare and Super spending. TOP claims a net surplus of around **4 billion dollars** after the Citizen's Income is funded. These are TOP's own modelling figures and have not been independently verified.

### The deferral provision

The immediate objection is obvious: what if you live in a house worth a lot but earn very little? An elderly Aucklander sitting on a section valued at, say, 800,000 dollars faces a 1.75% annual charge — roughly 14,000 dollars a year. That is serious money on a fixed income.

TOP's answer is a **deferral mechanism**. Owners who are "land-rich, cash-poor" can elect to accrue their LVT liability against the title of the property rather than paying it annually in cash. The debt accumulates and is paid when the property is eventually sold. The effect is that no one is forced to sell a home simply because they can't write the annual cheque — but the liability doesn't disappear; it's deferred until there's a liquidity event.

The precise interest rate applied to deferred balances has not been fully confirmed in TOP's published platform at the time of writing, so treat the deferral's total cost as provisional.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what makes LVT different from a property tax on the whole house value?</summary>

A standard property tax (like many US-style rates) is levied on the assessed value of the whole property — land plus buildings. An LVT is levied only on the land's *unimproved* value — what the section would sell for as bare dirt. That means your house, your renovations, and any structures you've added are excluded from the tax base. The practical difference: under an LVT, improving your home does not increase your tax bill, whereas under a property tax it typically would. This is why LVT proponents argue it doesn't discourage building or renovating.

</details>

## The wrapper: income tax and KiwiSaver 2.0

TOP doesn't propose the LVT in isolation. It comes as part of a three-piece package called the Tax Reset, and the other two pieces matter for understanding why it's designed the way it is.

### Simplified income tax

TOP proposes replacing NZ's current five-bracket income tax with **three brackets**:

- **28%** on income up to 50,000 dollars
- **34%** on income from 50,000 to 200,000 dollars
- **39%** on income above 200,000 dollars

These thresholds have not been fully confirmed in TOP's published materials, so treat them as indicative. The direction is clear: flatten the system and raise the bottom rate.

That last point is where a significant critique lives, and we'll come to it shortly. But TOP's argument is that the Citizen's Income functions as an effective tax-free floor — everyone receives approximately 370 dollars a week before any income tax applies — so the 28% rate hits a different starting point in practice than it would if the CI didn't exist.

### KiwiSaver 2.0

TOP proposes making KiwiSaver compulsory, with combined contributions of **6% from the employee and 6% from the employer — 12% total** — phased in over approximately eight years. The stated ambition is to build a "trillion-dollar" pool of domestic capital available for productive investment and infrastructure.

### Why bundle these?

The three pieces are designed to work as a system, not independently. The logic TOP offers runs roughly like this:

The current tax system puts a heavy signpost on wages (income tax up to 39%) and consumption (GST at 15%) while leaving land value largely untaxed. That signpost steers capital toward land and away from productive enterprise — the tilt toward property the Essentials course maps in detail. The Tax Reset tries to shift the base: tax land annually, simplify and slightly flatten income tax, and channel compulsory savings into a domestic capital pool. Less tax on the wage; more on the section; more productive investment.

Taken together, TOP calls this combination "the largest tax cut for low-income workers in history" — a claim they attribute to their own modelling and which is contested (the critiques below explain why).

<details><summary>Check yourself: why does TOP propose compulsory KiwiSaver alongside the LVT?</summary>

The KiwiSaver change is part of the same logic as the LVT: shift resources toward productive capital and away from land speculation. If more household saving flows into KiwiSaver rather than investment property, that pool of capital can be deployed into infrastructure and enterprise. TOP argues that NZ's current voluntary, low-contribution KiwiSaver produces thin savings and a thin domestic capital market. Making it compulsory at a higher rate is meant to build the capital base the economy needs — independent of whether any individual retiree *wants* to save at that rate.

</details>

## Why an LVT fits New Zealand

TOP argues the LVT is especially well-suited to NZ for several reasons that don't apply equally everywhere.

**No capital gains tax.** NZ is one of very few OECD countries with no comprehensive CGT. The 2019 Tax Working Group recommended one; it was vetoed by NZ First and then abandoned. A CGT taxes gains on property sales — politically toxic, practically complex, and (as the Essentials course explains) repeatedly killed at the ballot box. An LVT sidesteps all of that: it applies annually, doesn't require tracking a purchase price, and doesn't depend on a sale. TOP frames it as doing much of the same redistributive work as a CGT without the political baggage.

**Existing land valuations.** Because NZ's councils already assess land values separately from improvements, the data infrastructure for an LVT already exists. A new system would require setting rates and integrating them with existing billing, but it wouldn't require building a valuation regime from scratch.

**Land can't run away.** This is the third metaphor from the series canon, and it earns its place here. Unlike a company's profits or a person's income — both of which can be restructured, relocated, or minimised — land can't be moved offshore or hidden. A landowner who dislikes the LVT cannot pick up a valuable Auckland section and fly it to Singapore. The immobility of land as a tax base is the core argument that the LVT is hard to avoid, which makes it a relatively stable revenue source. The guard on this metaphor matters though: "can't flee" does not mean a big bill is painless, especially for someone with little income.

**NZ already taxed land this way.** Land-value rating was common in NZ for roughly 90 years before being largely abandoned in the 1980s and 1990s. It is not a novel experiment but a reversion to a tool NZ once used widely — a point TOP uses to argue it's administratively feasible rather than untested.

## The critiques

Every policy in this series is paired with its strongest objections. The LVT draws serious fire from several directions, and they deserve weight.

**The asset-rich, cash-poor problem.** The deferral provision addresses this but does not dissolve it. An elderly person on a fixed income who owns a high-value section in an area where land prices have risen sharply may face a deferred debt that grows substantially over time. When the property is eventually sold — often by an estate — that debt is repaid, which can mean significantly less passes to heirs than would have otherwise. For some, deferral feels less like a relief and more like a slow forced sale on a delayed timer. This is the most politically resonant critique.

**Taxing only one asset class.** An LVT leaves shares, bonds, business equity, and other financial wealth untaxed. If the goal is to tax accumulated wealth more fairly, a tax that only reaches land ignores a large portion of what the very wealthy actually hold. Critics on the left — and some economists — argue that a more comprehensive wealth or capital-income tax would do the redistributive job more evenly. TOP's answer is that land is the specific distortion it wants to fix, but the objection that it leaves financial wealth untouched is legitimate.

**Land valuation is hard and disputed.** Councils produce land valuations, but those valuations are based on comparable sales — and for urban land, comparable sales of genuinely bare sections are rare. Most land trades with a building on it, so valuers must strip out the building value, which involves judgement. Disputes over valuations are common even under the current rating system. A 1.75% annual charge makes any valuation error matter much more: an overvaluation of 200,000 dollars is a 3,500-dollar annual overcharge. The system for contesting valuations would need to be robust, fast, and accessible.

**The rent pass-through question.** Standard LVT theory holds that landlords cannot pass the tax to tenants, because the supply of land is fixed: raising rent to cover the LVT would simply push tenants away, and a landlord who can't sell or move the land has no alternative but to absorb the cost. But in a very tight rental market — like Auckland or Wellington — landlords may have enough pricing power to pass some of the cost on anyway, at least in the short run. The theory is sound in a competitive market; how much of NZ's rental market is competitive enough for it to hold is contested.

A fair question that comes up often: *doesn't LVT just become a higher rent for tenants?* The honest answer is that theory says no, but tight markets complicate the picture in ways economists still debate.

**The bottom income-tax rate.** The proposed 28% bottom rate is higher than today's 10.5% (on the first 14,000 dollars of income) and 17.5% (up to 48,000 dollars). TOP's case is that the Citizen's Income offsets this — a low-income earner receiving approximately 370 dollars a week is in a fundamentally different position than one who isn't. But the net benefit depends heavily on where exactly the thresholds land, how the CI interacts with the tax brackets for each income level, and whether the transition is smooth. Critics argue the offset is real but may not be complete for everyone, and that the headline income-tax rate rising for low earners is a genuine risk for those who fall through the eligibility gap.

**Compulsory KiwiSaver squeezes take-home pay.** Raising contributions to 12% of salary — half from the employer, half from the employee — means a worker on 60,000 dollars a year contributes an extra 3,600 dollars annually that is not available as cash. For households already stretched by high rents and food costs, mandatory savings can feel like a constraint rather than a benefit, even if the long-run retirement security is genuine. The eight-year phase-in softens this, but it doesn't eliminate it.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what is TOP's answer to the asset-rich, cash-poor problem, and what does the critique of that answer say?</summary>

TOP's answer is the **deferral provision**: land-rich, cash-poor owners (including retirees on fixed incomes) can elect not to pay the LVT annually. Instead, the liability accrues against the property title and is settled when the property is sold or transferred.

The critique is that deferral doesn't eliminate the burden — it moves it. The deferred debt grows (potentially with interest), reducing what the owner or their estate ultimately receives when the property is sold. For some retirees, this can feel like being slowly dispossessed of intergenerational wealth. The provision makes an immediate cash crisis avoidable, but it doesn't make a high-value section free to hold indefinitely. Critics argue it eases the problem without solving it.

</details>

## Exercises

**1. Why does TOP tax land rather than buildings?**

Think about what this choice implies — who benefits, who pays, and what behaviour it encourages or discourages.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

TOP taxes the *unimproved land value* rather than the full property value for several interconnected reasons.

First, **incentive alignment**: if the tax fell on the whole property — land plus building — then improving your home would increase your tax bill. An LVT removes that disincentive: you can renovate, extend, or develop intensively without triggering a higher charge. The tax falls on what the *community* has created (a valuable location, near services, schools, and infrastructure) rather than on what the *owner* has built.

Second, **targeting the specific distortion**: TOP's diagnosis is that NZ's tax system steers capital into land rather than productive investment. By taxing land value specifically — not shares, not business investment — the LVT is aimed at that distortion rather than being a general wealth tax.

Third, **administrative simplicity relative to CGT**: taxing the building would require tracking improvements and valuations in ways that resemble capital gains accounting. The unimproved land value is already separately assessed by councils.

The tradeoff is that buildings and other assets are untaxed, which critics argue leaves a large part of wealth accumulation outside the tax base.

</details>

**2. Explain the asset-rich, cash-poor problem and TOP's answer — and name one limitation of that answer.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The **problem**: An LVT charges a percentage of land value annually. If someone owns a high-value section — say, a retired couple in an area where land prices have risen sharply — but has little liquid income, the annual bill can be substantial and unaffordable from ordinary income. The LVT is tied to an asset's value, not the owner's cash flow.

**TOP's answer**: A deferral provision allows "land-rich, cash-poor" owners to accrue their LVT liability against the property title rather than paying it in cash each year. The debt is settled when the property is sold or transferred, at which point the accumulated amount is deducted from the proceeds.

**One limitation**: Deferral moves the burden rather than removing it. The deferred liability grows over time, and depending on the interest rate applied, can become a significant debt. When the property eventually sells — often on the owner's death, as part of an estate — the LVT debt is repaid first, reducing what passes to heirs. Critics argue that for homeowners whose main asset is a family property they planned to pass on, this is functionally similar to a forced partial sale — just delayed.

</details>

**3. NZ doesn't have a capital gains tax. How does an LVT relate to that gap — and how does it differ from a CGT?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The **gap**: NZ taxes wages and consumption but largely leaves capital gains untaxed (no comprehensive CGT, no land tax since 1991, no inheritance tax). This means a landlord who buys a property, holds it while it appreciates, and sells it pays no tax on the gain. As the Essentials course explains, this tilts the system toward property ownership and away from productive enterprise.

**How LVT relates**: A land value tax targets the same underlying issue — the untaxed value locked in land — but does so differently. Instead of waiting for a sale and taxing the gain, the LVT charges annually on the current estimated value of the land. In theory, this captures the ongoing economic rent from holding a valuable location, rather than the windfall at the point of sale.

**Key differences from a CGT**:
- *Timing*: CGT is triggered by a sale; LVT is charged every year regardless of whether the land is sold.
- *Base*: CGT taxes the increase in value (the gain); LVT taxes the current estimated value of the unimproved land, not the gain above purchase price.
- *Political profile*: A CGT requires tracking purchase prices, defining which assets are in scope, and calculating gains — administratively complex and politically toxic in NZ. An LVT uses existing council valuations and applies a flat rate. TOP frames this as a practical advantage.
- *Who pays*: A CGT only falls on people who sell. An LVT falls on everyone who holds land, every year — including landowners who never plan to sell.

</details>

**4. What does "land can't run away" mean, and what does it not mean?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The phrase captures a key economic property of land as a tax base: **immobility**. Unlike income — which can be restructured, shifted into trusts, or earned in a lower-tax jurisdiction — and unlike financial capital, which can be moved offshore, a section of land in Auckland cannot be relocated to avoid a tax. The landowner who dislikes the LVT cannot pick up the section and fly it to Singapore. This makes land a relatively hard-to-avoid tax base compared to income or capital.

**What it does not mean**: The phrase does not imply that a large tax bill is painless. "Can't flee" means the government can collect the tax; it says nothing about whether the owner can easily *afford* it. An elderly person on a fixed income sitting on a high-value section cannot relocate the land, but they also cannot necessarily pay 14,000 dollars a year from their pension. The immobility argument is about revenue stability and avoidance difficulty, not about the human cost of a large annual charge.

</details>

## Recap

TOP's Land Value Tax is a 1.75% annual charge on the unimproved value of urban land — taxing the worth of the spot, not the house on it — with a deferral mechanism for owners who cannot pay in cash. TOP projects this raises approximately 24 billion dollars a year, enough to fund the Citizen's Income from the previous module and leave a surplus. It comes bundled with a simplified three-bracket income tax and compulsory 12% KiwiSaver contributions, all aimed at shifting the tax base from wages toward land and building a domestic capital pool.

NZ's lack of a CGT, its existing land valuations, and land's inherent immobility make the LVT a plausible fit for NZ's specific circumstances — though the critiques are serious: the asset-rich, cash-poor problem is real even with deferral; the tax reaches only one asset class; valuation disputes will be significant; rent pass-through in tight markets is genuinely contested; and the higher bottom income-tax rate carries risk for anyone the Citizen's Income doesn't fully offset.
