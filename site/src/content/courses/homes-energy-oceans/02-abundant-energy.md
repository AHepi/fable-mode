Open your last power bill. Now imagine receiving one during a dry summer when the hydro lakes are low — the sort of drought that used to arrive every decade but is arriving more often. Prices spike. Factories run night shifts to dodge peak rates. A dairy farmer in Southland considers going back to diesel. The electricity was supposed to be the clean, cheap backbone of a modern economy. Instead it feels like the weather.

The Opportunity Party (TOP) argues this is what under-investment looks like at a national scale, and that fixing it is inseparable from the rest of the productive economy it wants to build. In the previous module we saw how TOP frames housing supply as economic policy — the same logic runs here. Energy, in TOP's telling, is the infrastructure that everything else plugs into, and NZ has been under-building it for decades.

---

## The mechanism: a 25-year plan and a different grid

TOP proposes a **25-year bipartisan energy strategy**, developed with industry, that would grow New Zealand's renewable electricity capacity to roughly **300% of its current level — around 30 gigawatts — by 2050**. The headline number is TOP's own target; attribute it there.

Three moving parts:

**1. The capacity build-out.** Predominantly wind and solar, added at a pace that — TOP argues — would require the kind of cross-party commitment NZ has never quite managed on energy. The point of "bipartisan" is stability: investors won't commit to decade-long projects if the rules change every three years with a new government.

**2. Market reform.** TOP argues the current electricity market structure discourages large-scale renewable investment. The precise reform mechanism is described in outline rather than detail in the 2026 platform; the diagnosis is that regulatory uncertainty and thin profit margins have kept private capital on the sidelines.

**3. Household and ratepayer electrification assistance.** TOP proposes a scheme of **low-interest loans** to help households and businesses switch from gas, coal, or petrol to electricity — heat pumps, induction cooktops, electric vehicles. The goal is to make decarbonisation affordable at the household level rather than leaving the cost entirely to individuals.

### The grid idea: overbuild, then hold back the lakes

This is the most technically interesting part of TOP's proposal, and worth slowing down for.

The conventional worry about wind and solar is intermittency — the wind stops blowing, the sun sets, and you need something else to fill the gap. The usual answer is battery storage, which is expensive. TOP's answer for NZ is different: **overbuild wind and solar so heavily that even on a bad day enough is produced**, and hold the hydro lakes in reserve to respond when wind and solar dip.

Think of it this way. NZ's hydro lakes — Manapōuri, Pūkaki, Hāwea — can be held at a higher level than normal if wind and solar are covering most of the load day-to-day. When the wind drops or demand spikes, you release the water. The lakes act as a **responsive reserve**, not a baseload source. You draw on them when you need to, and refill them (in the hydrological sense) when you don't. This is sometimes described as using hydro as a "battery," but that metaphor is loose in one important way: the lakes are a *reserve held back*, not a literal storage technology. The water is already there; the strategy is about when you choose to use it.

This makes NZ's position unusual. Most countries building out renewables face a harder storage problem because they lack large hydro capacity. NZ, with roughly 57% of its electricity already coming from hydro and around 80–85% of total electricity from renewables, has an existing physical infrastructure that the overbuild strategy can lean on.

## The problem TOP is responding to

NZ's electricity system has a specific history. The 1990s market reforms introduced competition but also created price volatility — generators profit more in dry years (when hydro output falls and prices spike) than in wet ones, which creates perverse incentives against building too much new capacity. Investment cycles have been lumpy and politically reactive, swinging between building booms and long pauses.

The result, TOP argues, is that **business can't plan on affordable electricity**. In surveys of what investors and businesses most want from government, energy reliability and price predictability consistently rank at the top. A manufacturer building a new plant needs to know what power will cost over the next twenty years. A data centre making a site decision needs the same. Uncertainty about energy costs is, in TOP's framing, a direct drag on the productive economy.

The household dimension compounds this. Power bills are a significant cost-of-living item for low-income households, and electrification — converting to heat pumps, electric vehicles, induction cooking — requires upfront capital that many households don't have, even when the long-run economics are favourable.

## NZ's unusual starting point

The case for ambition here is partly that NZ has a structural advantage most countries lack. At roughly 80–85% renewable electricity already, NZ is not starting from a coal-and-gas grid that needs rebuilding from scratch. The main tasks are:

- **Electrifying the things that aren't yet electric** — process heat in industry, transport, and low-temperature heating — which requires more electricity, not a different kind.
- **Building enough generation** to supply that expanded demand.
- **Managing the grid** through the variability that comes with higher wind and solar shares.

NZ's hydro endowment makes the third task more tractable. The 2026 platform presents this as an opportunity: the country is closer to a deeply renewable, electrified economy than almost anywhere else, and a deliberate strategy could close the gap rather than drifting toward it.

"Already renewable" describes the electricity grid; the wider economy runs on fossil fuels in places electricity hasn't yet reached.

## The critiques, fairly

### Capital cost

Tripling renewable capacity is not cheap. Thirty gigawatts of generation — wind farms, solar arrays, grid upgrades, new transmission — would require investment at a scale NZ has not attempted in living memory. TOP's platform attributes the benefits but does not provide a detailed public breakdown of the capital cost or who bears it (government, generators, consumers, or some combination). A fair question is whether the cost estimates are fully worked through and who specifically is on the hook.

### The ambition of the target

"300% of current capacity by 2050" is a striking number, and it is worth being precise about what makes it hard. Thirty gigawatts by 2050 is roughly 25 years away, but the consent processes, supply chains, workforce training, and grid investment needed to get there would have to begin immediately and run continuously. NZ's consenting system — which M1 covered in the housing context — has a track record of slowing large infrastructure projects. Critics point out that the gap between a stated target and a built megawatt is substantial, and the history of NZ energy planning includes aspirations that stalled.

### Bipartisanship is hard to guarantee

A 25-year strategy survives roughly seven or eight election cycles. New Zealand's political history — what the series-canon describes as "pendulum politics," the lurch-and-reverse pattern that has characterised everything from road funding to polytechnics — suggests that locking in any policy for 25 years is more aspiration than contract. A bipartisan agreement requires the ongoing consent of parties that may not share power, and successive governments have reversed energy-sector settings when it suited them. TOP's platform acknowledges this as the goal without fully explaining how the commitment would be institutionalised or enforced.

### Overbuilding has its own costs

The overbuild strategy works by building more generation than the grid needs on an average day, so there is always a surplus to cover low-wind or low-sun periods. But that surplus capacity isn't free — it sits idle much of the time, and the cost of building it is recovered somewhere. If generators bear it, returns fall and investment incentives may weaken. If consumers bear it through higher line charges or capacity payments, the electrification assistance scheme needs to be large enough to offset the bills. The strategy is coherent as engineering; whether it is coherent as economics at the proposed scale is a live question.

**Check yourself:** TOP's plan is described as bipartisan, but NZ has a three-year electoral cycle. What's the tension there?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A 25-year strategy spans roughly seven or eight elections. Each new government inherits the plan but is not bound by it — no Parliament can bind a future Parliament in NZ's constitutional framework. "Bipartisan" in practice means a political agreement, not a legal lock. Parties that weren't in the coalition that signed the strategy might repeal or redirect it once in government. The challenge is less technical (you can write a plan) than political (you can't easily write a plan that future governments must keep).

</details>

## Exercises

**1. Explain the overbuild-and-hold-hydro strategy in your own words.**

Write two or three sentences explaining why overbuilding wind and solar makes the hydro lakes more useful, as if explaining it to a friend who asked "but what happens when the wind stops?"

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

A strong response would make three moves: (1) identify that wind and solar covering most demand most of the time means the hydro lakes aren't being drawn down constantly; (2) explain that this leaves more water in reserve; (3) explain that when wind and solar fall short, the hydro can respond quickly because the reserve is full. The key word is *reserve*: the lakes are held back, not used as baseload. A common error is to say the lakes "store" solar or wind energy — they don't; they're a separate water resource that is managed to complement the intermittent sources.

**2. Is a 25-year bipartisan energy strategy realistic in NZ's political system?**

Draw on what the series has said about "pendulum politics" and NZ's electoral cycle. What would make such a strategy more durable, and what makes it fragile?

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

A strong response would identify the tension between a 25-year commitment and NZ's three-year cycle (roughly seven or eight elections), and note that in NZ's constitutional framework no Parliament legally binds the next. For durability, you might point to independent statutory bodies (like the Reserve Bank or the Climate Change Commission) that create accountability even across governments, or to cross-party consensus documents that at least raise the political cost of reversal. For fragility, the history of energy-sector reversals — and the series-canon's "pendulum politics" point — suggests good intentions don't survive a change in coalition reliably. Candidates should note that the Essentials course covers pendulum politics in the context of Citizens' Voice, which is TOP's proposed structural fix for exactly this problem.

**3. Who might bear the cost of the overbuild strategy, and why does it matter for the electrification assistance scheme?**

Think through which actors in the electricity sector could be asked to fund spare capacity — and what happens if the answer is consumers.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

The key actors are generators (who build and own the plant), the grid operator (who manages transmission), and end consumers (who pay through power bills). If generators build surplus capacity and don't recoup costs through energy sales (because prices fall when supply is plentiful), they need some other revenue stream — either a capacity payment from the government or higher regulated returns, both of which ultimately flow from taxpayers or ratepayers. If consumers bear it through higher bills, the electrification assistance scheme (the low-interest loans) needs to be substantial enough that households still find switching worthwhile. A weaker scheme might leave lower-income households paying more for electricity without being able to afford the upfront cost of switching appliances. This is a real design challenge, not a dealbreaker, but it means the two parts of the policy — the overbuild and the assistance scheme — need to be sized together.

</details>

**4. Compare TOP's energy plan with what you know about NZ's current policy settings. What is TOP actually changing?**

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

A strong response would identify that the current NZ electricity market is lightly regulated and largely market-driven — generators decide when to invest based on expected returns. TOP's plan introduces two things the market hasn't provided: a *binding long-term target* (300%/30 GW) and *direct consumer-side support* (the electrification loans). The bipartisan framing is also a structural change — it explicitly tries to remove energy from the normal political cycle rather than leaving it to each government's preferences. Worth noting: NZ already has a renewable electricity target and a Climate Change Commission with statutory authority; TOP's plan would sit alongside or above that existing framework, though the 2026 platform doesn't specify precisely how. That's a reasonable area to flag as detail that needs fleshing out.

</details>

## Recap

TOP's energy plan rests on a single bet: that committing to three times the current renewable capacity, held in place across seven or eight governments, would let NZ run a deeply electrified economy on power that is both cheap and clean. The grid mechanism — overbuild wind and solar, hold the hydro lakes as a reserve — is a plausible answer to the intermittency problem that most countries don't have available to them. The genuine difficulties are the ones that dog long-horizon infrastructure planning everywhere: capital, consent, and political durability. Whether a bipartisan commitment is a realistic solution to the last of these — or whether it requires something like the Citizens' Voice mechanism the next module in the series explores — is a question this course can only pose, not settle.
