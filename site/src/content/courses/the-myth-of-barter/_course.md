Open almost any introductory economics textbook to the chapter on money, and you meet the same little fable before a single definition. Long ago, it says, people bartered. Barter was clumsy, because you had to find someone who wanted exactly what you had and had exactly what you wanted. So money was invented to fix it, and from money came markets, banking, and credit. The story is tidy, intuitive, and almost certainly something you believe.

It is also, as history, not what happened. When anthropologists and historians go looking for the barter economy that supposedly became money, they cannot find it. What they find instead, thousands of years before the first coin, is credit: debts reckoned in silver and barley, recorded on clay, governed by written law. Money did not grow out of barter to make trading easier. It began as a way of measuring what people owed one another, and the coin arrived late, carried in on the back of states, armies, and taxes.

This course is history-first. It states the textbook story at its most convincing, then walks the actual record, from Mesopotamian ledgers and Hammurabi's debt laws to the first coins of Lydia and the periodic cancellation of debts in the ancient world. Along the way it examines the two rival theories of what money fundamentally is, treating them as objects of study rather than the frame, and it asks why a story the evidence does not support is still taught on page one. You need no economics, anthropology, or history to follow it. By the end you will know where money actually came from, and you will carry one sharp question you can put to any origin story: is this remembered, or is it just what some theory needs to be true?
