## The hook

Picture a coin in the palm of a soldier's hand. It is small, heavy for its size, stamped on one face with the mark of the authority that made it. He did not get it by trading a chicken for it. He got it as pay, for marching where he was told and fighting whom he was ordered to fight, and the mark on its face is the reason it will be accepted at the next town, because that same authority will demand it back from the townspeople when the tax falls due. Hold that picture. The coin stamped to pay a soldier is the whole of this module compressed into a single object, and almost everything the barter myth says about money runs backward through it.

Here is the thing that should stop you. By the time the first coin like that one was ever struck, money was already thousands of years old.

## Context & background

You have spent the last two modules in a world without coins. In module 03 you watched Mesopotamian scribes price goods and record debts in silver and barley around 3000 BCE, keeping a **money-of-account**, prices and debts reckoned in a standard measure with no coin circulating, on clay tablets that were really receipts. In module 04 you saw that debt by then was governed law: the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE, regulated interest and **debt bondage**, being forced into servitude to work off a debt you cannot pay, more than a thousand years before anyone struck a coin. Money as a measure, debt as an institution, law to govern it, all of it already standing.

Now place the first coins on the same timeline. **Coinage**, stamped metal money issued by an authority, first appears around the seventh century BCE, in Lydia (a kingdom in what is now western Turkey) and the neighbouring Ionian Greek cities on the Aegean coast. The earliest were lumps of electrum, a natural alloy of gold and silver, stamped with a design so that their weight and metal need not be checked at every exchange. Keep the date rough; what matters is its distance from everything before it. The clay receipts of module 03 are older than the first coins by roughly two and a half thousand years. That is not a footnote. That is most of recorded history sitting between money and the coin.

So the iconic object, the thing that feels most like money, the disc you can bite and hear ring, turns out to be a latecomer. Money did not begin as a coin and grow into a ledger. It began as a ledger, and coins were added, much later, to a world that already had money. Which raises the question this module exists to answer: if coins were not there at the beginning, what were they actually for, and how did they spread?

## Narrative / exposition

Recall the sequence the textbook teaches, the **barter myth** from module 01: people barter, money is invented to ease the swapping, then markets and credit grow on top. In that story coins are money's natural climax. Barter is clumsy, a common medium of exchange smooths it, the handiest medium wins out, and the winner gets stamped into coins. The coin is where the long convenience-seeking story finally crystallizes. On that account coinage should arrive early, near the dawn of trade, as the obvious fix maturing into its final form.

The record runs the other way. Coins arrive late, suddenly, and in a very particular company. They show up tangled with three things at once: states, war, and taxation. To see why, follow the coin in the soldier's hand from the moment it is struck.

Start with the army. A state at war needs to feed, equip, and pay a large body of armed men, often far from home, often men with no prior bond to the place they are standing in. Paying them in promises is hard when they may not live to collect, and paying them in a running tab is impossible when they are strangers passing through. What the state needs is something portable, durable, hard to fake, and accepted by anyone the soldier meets on the march. Stamped metal of guaranteed weight does exactly that job. Coinage standardizes pay. One coin is as good as another of the same kind, so the paymaster can count out wages by the piece and the soldier can spend them anywhere the stamp is known.

But why would a farmer two valleys over hand real bread and wine to a passing soldier for a disc of metal he cannot eat? This is the move that makes the whole machine turn, and it is where the state reappears. The same authority that mints the coin to pay its soldiers also levies a tax, and it demands that the tax be paid in that coin, and in nothing else. Now the farmer has no choice. To settle with the tax collector he must get his hands on the official coin, and the readiest way to get it is to sell something, his grain, his labour, his wine, to someone who has it. Who has it? The soldiers, freshly paid. The tax does not merely raise money for the state. It forces an entire population to chase the state's coin, which means it forces them to produce and sell things in order to earn it.

Watch what that produces. A market springs up around the army, almost on demand: people bringing goods to sell to the troops, because selling to the troops is how they obtain the coin the tax requires. The state has set up a loop. It pays out coin at one end (wages), pulls coin back in at the other (taxes), and in the gap between the two it has conjured a swarm of buyers and sellers who never had to want each other's goods at all. The coin in the soldier's hand is the engine of that loop. It is not the climax of barter. It is a tool a state uses to provision an army by turning its own subjects into a market.

That is why this module calls coinage **a state technology**: a device deliberately deployed by a ruling power to do a job, the way a road or a standing army or a written law code is. The phrase is precise, not poetic. A technology is something made and used for a purpose. Coinage was made and used to pay soldiers and to be taxed back, and the markets that grew around it grew partly because the state needed them to.

## Primary source

The chronology and its company are best stated by a specialist. The numismatist Philip Grierson, a leading scholar of coins and what they reveal, who argued throughout his work that money begins not as a commodity but as an abstract measure, set out the late and deliberate character of coinage. The lines below paraphrase his account of where coins begin rather than quote it, because the exact wording varies between printings and is not worth faking; the substance is his.

> On Grierson's account, coinage is essentially a creation of the Greek world, and the earliest coins we know are those of Lydia. There, electrum, the natural alloy of gold and silver, was minted into small pieces of fixed weight and stamped with a device that marked their origin and guaranteed their value.
>
> Philip Grierson, *The Origins of Money* (1977)

Read it as evidence, not yet as interpretation. What the account gives you, firmly, is the *where* and the *what*: Lydia, electrum, fixed weight, an official stamp that guarantees the piece. Notice the word "guarantee." The value the stamp vouches for is the value an authority stands behind. Even at the very birth of coinage, the coin is already pointing back at the power that made it, not out at a barter market that made it necessary. The strong causal story, that war and taxes *drove* coinage to spread, is the next step, and it is interpretation. The account itself simply marks the date and the maker, and that is already enough to overturn the myth's order.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now separate carefully what is solid from what is argued, because this module sits right on the seam, and the course's credibility depends on marking it.

What is solid is the chronology and the company coinage keeps. Coins are late (around the seventh century BCE, against money-of-account at around 3000 BCE). That is firm. That early coinage and the paying of soldiers and the taxing of populations turn up together, in Lydia, in the Greek world, and soon after in the empires that minted on a large scale, is also well established. Coins, soldiers, taxes: that trio is evidence. You can point to mints, to military pay, to tax demands in coin. No interpretation required.

What is interpretation is the strong causal claim built on top of that trio. The historian David Graeber, an anthropologist whose *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) is an influential but contested synthesis, drew on Grierson and others to argue for what he called a *military-coinage complex*: the claim that coinage spread *because* states needed to pay armies and tax them back, a self-reinforcing engine of war, mints, and markets, with mass slavery often feeding the same machine. That is a powerful reading, and it fits the evidence well. But notice its shape. "Coins, soldiers, and taxes appear together" is a fact. "Therefore war and taxation are *why* coinage spread" is a story told about the fact, an attribution of cause. The seam between them is real, and an honest reader keeps it visible. Grierson supplies the careful numismatics; Graeber builds the bold causal arc. Attribute each to the one who made it, and do not let the boldness of the second be mistaken for the firmness of the first.

There is a deeper inversion here, and it is interpretation too, so handle it with the same care. The barter myth assumes markets came first, growing naturally out of people trucking and bartering, with money arriving later to lubricate them and the state arriving later still to regulate what markets had already built. The state-war-tax account turns that around. On this reading, states did not stumble upon ready-made markets. By minting coin and demanding it back in tax, states helped *call markets into being*, at least in part and at least around their armies. That is a striking claim and a well-grounded one, but it is a claim, an interpretation of the chronology, not a fact you can dig out of the ground. Mark it as such. What the evidence forces on you is narrower and still devastating to the myth: money is older than coins, and coins are bound up with state power from their first appearance. Whatever else money is, it is not the apolitical, pre-state convenience the textbook describes. This is where "money is apolitical" dies.

One temptation to refuse, because it is the very habit this course teaches you to distrust. It is easy to read this module as a tidy upgrade story in reverse: first ledgers, then coins, each a step toward the cash in your pocket, money "maturing" toward its modern form. Resist it. There was nothing inevitable about coinage. Mesopotamia ran a sophisticated money-of-account for two and a half thousand years without ever needing to mint a coin. Coins were one contingent invention, in one corner of the world, for a particular purpose, that happened to spread. They were not money growing up. They were money being put to a new use by states that found them handy. The myth's deep error is the assumption that money was always heading somewhere. It was not.

## Recap

The most coin-like thing about money turns out to be its newest part. Money as a measure and debt as a governed institution were thousands of years old before the first **coinage**, stamped metal issued by an authority, appeared around the seventh century BCE in Lydia and the Ionian Greek world. And coins did not spread by easing barter. They spread through the loop you can read off the coin in a soldier's hand: a state mints coin to pay its army, then demands the same coin back in tax, forcing a whole population to sell goods and labour to obtain it, conjuring a market where none had to exist. The chronology and that trio of coins, soldiers, and taxes are solid; the strong claim that war and taxation *drove* coinage and that states thereby called markets into being is interpretation, Grierson's careful record carrying Graeber's bolder arc, and the seam between them stays visible. None of it was money maturing toward its modern form; coinage was contingent, not destined. What it kills is the idea that money is apolitical. The next module steps back from the record to ask the question all of it has been circling: what, then, is money actually, and which theory of it does the evidence support?
