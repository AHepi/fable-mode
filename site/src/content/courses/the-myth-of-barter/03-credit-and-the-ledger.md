## The hook

Picture the end of a long evening at a bar where the bartender knows you. Four drinks, none of them paid for, and yet nothing about the night felt like borrowing. You signed nothing. No coin left your pocket. When you settle up, the bartender does not weigh out what you owe in beer or peanuts. She reads a number off a screen, in dollars, and you hand over the dollars.

Something strange has happened. All night your tab was reckoned in dollars: every drink had a price the instant it was poured, your debt grew drink by drink. But for most of the evening no dollar moved. The thing that counted the debt and the thing that finally paid it were not the same kind of thing.

That gap, between the money you reckon in and the money you hand over, is the most important idea in this course. The barter myth never sees it. Once you can see it, the myth's whole sequence falls apart, and it falls apart not in a thought experiment but in the dirt of southern Iraq, five thousand years ago.

## Context & background

Two modules stand behind this one. In module 01 you met the story at its strongest: **the barter myth**, in which people first swapped goods directly, money was then invented to fix the clumsiness of swapping, and markets and credit grew up afterward on top of money. In module 02 you saw why that sequence cannot be the history: no society has ever been found running on barter and then upgrading to money. What communities actually ran on was **credit and debt tracked socially**, the two sides of one relationship. People kept a running tab of who owed whom, settled by custom rather than by haggling out a price every time.

If the running tab was already there, what did it get reckoned in? You can owe someone, but you have to owe them *some amount of something*. What was the something, before there were coins?

The answer comes from **Mesopotamia**, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates, roughly where Iraq is now, in the centuries around 3000 BCE: the world of the first cities, temples, writing, and surviving account books. Here is the fact the module turns on. Mesopotamian temples and palaces kept detailed accounts in silver and barley more than two thousand years before anyone struck a coin. There were no coins in this world. There was a great deal of money.

To hold that sentence steady you need one distinction. It is the distinction the bar tab was built on.

## Narrative / exposition

Go back to the tab. The dollar was doing two different jobs, and the myth's central mistake is to jam them together.

The first job is measuring. All night, the dollar was the yardstick that said how much each drink was worth and how much, in total, you owed. It did this whether or not a single dollar was in the room. A debt of "twelve dollars" is a perfectly definite thing even while it sits unpaid; the dollar here is a unit you count in, the way a foot is a unit you measure length in. A measure used this way is what economists call a **unit of account**: the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in (you owe me "twelve shekels"), even when no coin moves.

The second job is changing hands. At the very end, you handed over actual dollar bills, and *those* dollars did something the measuring-stick dollars never had to: they physically moved, from you to the bartender, and that movement settled the debt. A thing used this way is what economists call a **medium of exchange**: the thing that physically changes hands in a deal, the coin, the note, the bill.

In a modern economy one word does both jobs, which is why they are so easy to confuse. But they came apart on the tab: the measuring went on for hours, the handing-over took a moment at the end. The myth assumes the handing-over thing came first, that money began as some good people passed around and only later got used to keep score. The Mesopotamian record shows the opposite: the score was being kept for two thousand years before there was a coin to hand over.

Look at what the scribes were doing. A temple in a Sumerian city ran like a vast estate: it took in barley, held herds, employed laborers, rented out land, lent out grain and silver, and tracked all of it on clay. To put a bushel of barley, a day's labor, and a sheep onto the same page so the books can balance, you need a common measure. Scribes used two: barley, by volume, and silver, by weight, measured in a unit called the **shekel** (here a weight, long before it was ever a coin). A debt might be written as "so many shekels of silver." The silver and barley were the units of account, the dollars on the tab.

And much of that silver never moved. A debt could be incurred, carried for a season, and settled all in silver without a lump of silver changing hands, because it could be cleared against other debts, paid in barley or labor at a customary rate, or rolled forward on the books. Prices, debts, rents, and wages all reckoned in a standard measure with no coin circulating: that is **money-of-account**, money that keeps score without any minted thing changing hands. This is money. It is just not the kind the myth taught you to picture.

A second clue sits right alongside the account books. When you ask what the earliest writing was *for*, the answer is not prayers or poems or the deeds of kings. The earliest cuneiform, the wedge-shaped marks pressed into wet clay, is overwhelmingly administrative: lists, receipts, inventories, ration records, debts. Writing arose largely for accounts. The first thing humans seem to have wanted a durable record of was not their gods or their stories but who owed what to whom. If money were really born as a handy good people passed around, this is a strange thing to find at the dawn of literacy. It is exactly what you would expect if money were born as a way of keeping track of debts.

## Primary source

This module's evidence is not a single quotable line but a *type* of object, so the honest thing is to describe that type faithfully rather than invent one particular tablet.

Picture a small clay tablet of a kind found by the thousands across Mesopotamian sites: a private loan record. Pressed into it, in cuneiform, is roughly this shape of information: a quantity of silver or barley named as the amount owed; the name of the person who owes it and the person it is owed to; often a rate of interest; sometimes a date for repayment; and the names of witnesses. Some such tablets were sealed inside an inscribed clay envelope, so the inner tablet could not be altered without breaking the seal.

Now read it. What is *not* there is any coin. The silver named on the tablet need never have been seen by the borrower; it is the measure the debt is counted in, not necessarily a thing handed over. The tablet is not money you spend. It is money in a deeper sense: the record of a debt, made durable in clay, money-of-account made visible, a debt in silver with no coin in sight.

A reader who wants a named voice for this reading can have one. **A. Mitchell Innes**, an early-twentieth-century British writer on money whose 1913 essays are the foundational modern statement of this view, argued that credit and debt are older than coined money, and that money is at bottom a matter of credit rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. That last step is *interpretation*, not a fact the tablet hands us. The tablet is the evidence: debts reckoned in silver, on clay, before coins. What it *means* is the argument the course takes up next.

## Check yourself

Pause before reading on. A friend says: "Fine, but silver *was* the money. They measured debts in silver and they paid in silver. So money still came first, as a thing, just as the myth says." Using the distinction from this module, where is your friend going wrong?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The friend has collapsed the two jobs back together. Silver was serving as the **unit of account**, the measuring-stick the debt was reckoned in ("so many shekels of silver"), but it was very often not the **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changed hands. Debts in silver were frequently settled some other way, by clearing them against other debts, by paying in barley or labor at a customary rate, or by rolling them forward, with no silver moving at all. So silver as a measure of value came first and did most of the work; silver as a thing you hand over was the lesser, occasional job. The myth needs money to begin as a thing people pass around. The record shows it beginning as a standard for keeping score. Naming the debt in silver is not the same as paying it in silver, and the gap between the two is the whole point.

## Narrative / exposition (continued)

There is one more object to put in your hands, because money-of-account can feel abstract. A debt can be a physical thing you carry without being a coin you spend, and the clearest example is the **tally**: a physical record of a debt, classically a notched stick split in two, with one half kept by each party.

Here is how it works. You lend me ten units of something. We cut notches across a stick to mark the amount, then split it lengthwise through the notches into two halves. You keep one; I keep the other. The notches now run across both halves identically, because they were one cut. When the debt is settled, the halves are brought back together, and they must match, notch for notch and grain for grain, or the record is false. No one can add a notch, because the wood's own grain would betray the join. (This is not only ancient: the English Exchequer kept its accounts on split tally sticks for centuries.)

Look at what the tally is. It is not the thing owed, and not a medium of exchange you spend on bread. It is the *record* of who owes whom, made tangible and verifiable. And the half you hold is genuinely worth something, because it is a claim that someone owes you. People could and did pass such claims along to third parties, the first hint of how a tracked personal debt might one day harden into impersonal, transferable money. For now the tally makes the module's thesis something you could hold: money here is not a coin, it is a record of who owes whom.

## Analysis & interpretation

Step back and weigh what we have, keeping the seam between evidence and interpretation in view. This is exactly the kind of place a course about a theory-driven story could smuggle in one of its own.

Here is the **evidence**, and it is firm. Around 3000 BCE, Mesopotamian temples and palaces kept detailed accounts of debts, prices, rents, and wages reckoned in silver and barley, more than two thousand years before coinage. Much of the silver was a measure, not a thing that moved. The earliest writing is overwhelmingly administrative. None of that is contested.

Here is the **interpretation**, a further step that must be marked as one. From this evidence, Innes, and later the anthropologist David Graeber in his 2011 book *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (an influential synthesis that specialists contest on specifics, so its readings travel under its own name), draw a large conclusion: that money is *essentially* credit, a measure of debt and an obligation, rather than a commodity that emerged from barter. That conclusion fits the Mesopotamian record strikingly well. But fitting the evidence is not the same as being proven by it. The evidence shows that money-of-account preceded coinage. Whether money is *in its nature* debt rather than a commodity is a separate claim about the essence of a thing, and arguments about essences are not settled by a single body of evidence, however good.

So plant a flag. There is a rival theory of money built around exactly this reading: the **credit theory of money**, also called **chartalism**, which holds that money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt, backed by social and ultimately state obligation, rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. It is the direct competitor to the commodity theory you met at the close of module 01, which says money is essentially a special tradeable good. The Mesopotamian ledgers are the credit theory's home ground. We will put the two side by side, steelman each, and weigh them in module 06. Do not let this module talk you into crowning a winner. It has done its job if you can see that the unit of account came first in the record, and that this is what gives the credit theory something to stand on.

One guard before we close. None of this makes early Mesopotamia a gentle place, a lost age of trust before cold cash. These were stratified societies with temples and palaces commanding labor, and the same loan tablets that prove debt was reckoned in silver also record interest, terms, and the machinery of obligation that could, when a debt went bad, cost a person their freedom. That darker side of debt is the next module's subject. The point here is not that the past was kinder. The point is only, and it is enough, that money did not begin the way you were told.

4. **State the rival, fairly.** Without yet having the full case (that comes in module 06), put the credit theory of money into your own words as strongly as you can: money as a unit of account and a measure of debt. You are steelmanning a view you have not finished examining.

## Recap

Money did not begin as a thing people passed around. It began as a way of keeping score. The hinge that lets you see this is the difference between a **unit of account**, the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in ("you owe me twelve shekels"), and a **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changes hands to settle a deal. The bar tab pulls them apart: the dollar measures all night, but the dollar bill moves only at the end. Mesopotamia pulls them apart by two thousand years. Around 3000 BCE, temple and palace scribes were pricing goods and recording debts in silver and barley, **money-of-account** with no coin circulating, and the earliest writing we have was made largely to keep exactly these accounts. The **tally**, a notched stick split in two whose halves must match, makes the same truth something you can hold: money as a record of who owes whom. All of that is evidence, and it is firm. The further claim that money is therefore *essentially* debt is interpretation, the home ground of a rival to the commodity theory called the **credit theory of money**, or **chartalism**, which we will weigh in module 06. The next module stays in this world and asks a harder question. If debt was already this central, this early, how was it governed, and what happened to people who could not pay?
