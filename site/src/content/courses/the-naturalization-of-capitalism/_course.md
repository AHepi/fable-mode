"The market demands it." "You can't fight economics." "It's just human nature." We talk about capitalism the way we talk about the weather: as a fact we live inside and never voted on. This course is about how that happened: how a way of organizing work, land, and money that most of human history would not recognize came to feel like the floor under our feet.

The approach is historical and critical, and it has a definite point of view: that the naturalness of capitalism is the thing to be *explained*, not assumed. But this is an analysis, not a sermon. Each step states the strongest version of the "it was always going to be this way" story before taking it apart, keeps what the historical record shows separate from what any thinker claims it means, and names the places where serious scholars still disagree. You do not need any background in economics, history, or theory. Every specialist term is built up from an everyday picture before it is named.

You will move from the lens (what "naturalization" is, and how to make the familiar strange) through the long history of how the order was *made* (the enclosure of common land, the plantation, the invention of "the market" and "the economy," the remaking of time and the self, the unpaid labour the whole thing rests on) to how it comes to feel natural and holds, and finally to how the sense that "there is no alternative" was deliberately built within living memory. By the end you will not have a manifesto. You will have something more useful: a method for looking at any arrangement that presents itself as simply the way things are, and asking when it began, whom it suits, and what it is hiding.
