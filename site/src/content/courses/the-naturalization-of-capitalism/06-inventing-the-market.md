## The hook

Two pictures of where the free market came from, and you have almost certainly been handed the wrong one.

In the first, the market is a garden that grew wild. Left alone by kings and councils and meddling officials, people just *traded* (the way water finds its level) and out of millions of small bargains a vast self-running system grew up on its own, the way a forest grows where no one planted it. The state, here, is the gardener who keeps wanting to prune and fence and intervene, and the wisdom of the free market lies in telling him to put the shears down. Market: spontaneous. State: intrusion.

In the second, the market is a machine someone built. Every part of it (that land can be bought, that a person's working hours can be rented by the hour, that money is a thing you can lend at interest rather than a token of who owes whom) every part was made, by specific people, through specific laws, often against fierce resistance, and is *maintained* by constant effort the way any built engine is. The state, here, is not the gardener standing outside the garden. It is the engineer who built the engine and keeps it running, and who had to clear the ground first, by force, to lay the foundation.

This module is about why the second picture is the true one. Built, not grown. The man who saw it most clearly wrote it down in the middle of the Second World War. Polanyi again, the Hungarian economic historian of the 1940s, whose *The Great Transformation* (1944) is the keystone of everything that follows. One caution we set down in module 02 and keep here: Polanyi is *not* a Marxist, and his quarrel was as much with socialist orthodoxy as with the free-market kind, and some of his specific history has dated. But his central diagnosis is the cleanest tool we have for taking apart the garden picture.

## Narrative / exposition

To use the tool you need one thing from earlier. In module 04 we watched the commons get fenced and a whole class turned, by enclosure and law, into the **"free" labourer**, free of feudal bonds and of any land to live on, and so compelled to sell the only thing left, their working time. That dispossession made the human material. This module is about the machine that material was fed into, and the astonishing claim that someone had to *design* it and pass laws to keep it fed.

The thing Polanyi anatomizes is the **self-regulating market**: the idea of a market that runs society on its own, needing no governance, running like a thermostat no one has to touch. Not a marketplace, not a fair on a Tuesday; module 02 already drew that line: *markets are ancient; a market society is not*. A self-regulating market is the stranger thing: a whole society organized so the market is the master institution, the one everything else bends around. Polanyi's claim, the one this module exists to deliver, is that such a thing has never once arisen on its own, and could not.

Start with the foundation, the part that sounds most like common sense and is in fact the founding fiction. A market needs things to sell. A **commodity** is something produced in order to be sold (a chair, a loaf, a barrel of nails) and the genius of a market economy is that it coordinates the production of millions of such things with no one in charge, by the signal of price. So far the garden picture holds. If everything traded were a chair or a loaf, you could almost believe the system grew itself.

But a market society needs to buy and sell three things that are not commodities at all, because none of them was produced to be sold. These are Polanyi's **fictitious commodities**: land, labour, and money treated as for-sale though none was made for sale, and naming them is the hinge of his whole argument.

**Land** is the first. Land is just another word for nature: the earth, its rivers and soil and the weather over it. Nobody manufactured it for sale; it existed before there were markets and would exist if every market vanished tomorrow. Yet a market society must treat land as real estate: a thing with a price, bought, sold, mortgaged, and parcelled like crates of nails.

**Labour** is the second, and the strangest. "Labour" is just another word for human beings going about the activity of living: a person's hours, energy, attention, the use of their own body. No one is born in order to be hired. Your working time is not a product that rolled off a line; it is your life, the only one you get, sliced into rentable hours. Yet a market society must treat labour as a commodity with a wage-price, bought and sold like the loaf.

**Money** is the third. Money, in origin, is a token of trust and obligation: a way of keeping track of who owes what to whom, as module 03 found when it took apart the barter myth and discovered debt running underneath the coins. Nobody produces money the way a baker produces bread; a society conjures it into being. Yet a market society must treat money, too, as a commodity to be bought and lent at a price called interest.

Look at what that treatment does. To price land, labour, and money as goods made for sale is to take three things that are nothing of the kind (nature, human life, social trust) and *force them to appear* as commodities, with prices, behaving like objects on a shelf. A relation among people, and even the living earth, is made over into a thing with a market rate, as fixed and impersonal as the tides. That hardening of human relations into things with a life of their own is **reification**, the idea module 01 slipped into your back pocket, Lukács's name for it. Polanyi's fictitious commodities are reification with the receipts: the precise points where a market society pretends that what was never a commodity is one, and gets a whole civilization to believe it.

Call it fiction and you might hear an accusation: that the whole thing is fake, a con to be swept away by anyone clear-eyed enough. That is not the claim, and the careful reader holds the line. The fictions are *real in their effects*: wage labour, real estate, and the bond market are as solid as anything in your life, and they organize the planet. Polanyi's word is exact: a fiction in the legal sense, a thing we agree to treat *as if* it were what it is not, because the treating-as-if does enormous work. The market society is not unreal. It rests on a decision, not on the nature of things, and a decision could have been, and was, made differently.

Which brings us to the engine, and to the single most important sentence in this course.

If land, labour, and money are not commodities by nature, then somebody has to *make* them into commodities and *force* them to behave like commodities, and somebody did. The fences of module 04 turned land into property and people into wage-seekers; that took acts of Parliament and, often, soldiers. But the harder work was keeping the engine running once it was built, and that work the state did, deliberately, at enormous scale, in the very decades the free market was supposedly being *left alone*. Polanyi's name for the result is the one to carry out of this module:

**"Laissez-faire was planned"**: the free market was made and enforced by the state, not left alone.

Sit with the paradox, because the paradox is the point. *Laissez-faire* is French for "let it be," the doctrine that the market works best when government keeps its hands off. So "laissez-faire was planned" sounds like a contradiction: how can leaving-alone be planned? Polanyi's answer is that the leaving-alone never happened. Building a society in which the market runs the show took relentless, coordinated state action: writing the property law that lets land be sold, the contract law that makes a wage agreement enforceable, the enclosure acts that cleared the commons, and, most tellingly, *reforming the laws that had protected people from the market* so they would have no choice but to enter it.

The English Poor Law reform of 1834 is Polanyi's sharpest example, so make it concrete. The old system had given the poor a floor: relief that meant a labourer did not absolutely have to take any wage on offer to avoid starvation. To make a true labour market, that floor had to go: only a person facing real hunger will reliably sell their hours at whatever price the market sets. So the law was rewritten, on purpose, by a powerful centralized state, to make poverty frightening enough that no one could refuse the market. That is not the state stepping back. That is the state stepping in, hard, to *manufacture* the market, the **dull compulsion of economic relations** module 04 named, where once alternatives are gone, hunger does the work the whip used to, and it all looks like free choice.

So the garden picture has it backwards. The "non-interfering" market was itself one of the most ambitious interventions any state has ever undertaken: a mountain of planning, legislation, and force to build a system that could then be described as self-running. Markets and states did not arrive as enemies, one natural and one intrusive. They arrived together, the second building the first. This is the course's single most powerful answer to the deepest piece of common sense it confronts. *Markets are natural, the state is an intrusion on them.* No: the market was built, not grown, and the builder was the state.

(Polanyi pairs this with the **double movement**: as the market tears at ordinary life, society throws up protections against it, so the free market and the resistance to it arrive together; we leave it there and hold the sharper fact: the market itself was planned.)

## Primary source

Polanyi states the claim more plainly than any paraphrase, in the most-quoted line in the book:

> "Laissez-faire was planned; planning was not."
>
> — Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944)

The second half is the twist of the knife. The free market, the thing presented as spontaneous, as what happens when no one plans, was the planned thing, built by design. What was *not* planned, what arose from below, was society's reaction against it: the labour laws, the protections, the unscripted defending of ordinary life. The order we call natural was engineered, and the disorder we call interference was the closest thing to a natural, unplanned response.

A note on integrity, in the spirit of this course. That sentence is genuine and famous, from Polanyi's chapter on the rise of the market economy; I am confident of the wording but have not pinned it to a page number, and I will not invent one. Take the rest of his case (the Poor Law, the fictitious commodities) as accurately rendered argument rather than direct quotation.

## Analysis & interpretation

Keep the seam between evidence and interpretation visible, because here it matters.

The **evidence** is hard and checkable: the English Poor Law was reformed in 1834 to cut the old relief; enclosure proceeded by thousands of specific acts of Parliament; property and contract law was built and extended across the same period; the modern state grew, it did not shrink, during the rise of "free" markets. These things demonstrably happened, and reasonable historians do not dispute the record.

The **interpretation** is Polanyi's framing of what the record means: that all of it adds up to the deliberate manufacture of a self-regulating market, and that "laissez-faire was planned" is the law underneath the facts. That is a reading (this module argues it is the right one) but it is a reading, and it has serious critics worth taking on their strongest ground.

The sharpest objection is to Polanyi's idea of *disembedding*, his claim that the market society tore the economy loose from the social relations that had always held it (module 02's embedded past) and set it floating free as an autonomous sphere. Critics answer: no economy floats free. Every economy, the market included, is **embedded** in *some* set of institutions, and the market society sits in a dense thicket of them: property and contract law, the courts, the central bank, the very apparatus Polanyi himself describes. So "disembedding" is too strong. The market did not escape society into a state of nature; it was *re-embedded* in new institutions rather than freed from all of them.

Pressed honestly, that objection does not weaken Polanyi's main point. It sharpens it. If the market society is held up by an enormous structure of law, it is even *less* self-running than the garden picture claims, not more. The criticism cuts against the loose word "disembedding," not against "laissez-faire was planned." And it gives us a more precise account of naturalization. The market society's institutions did not disappear; they became invisible, so woven into the floor of ordinary life, so much the unquestioned background, that we stopped seeing them as institutions and began experiencing them as nature. Naturalization, on this reading, is not the economy escaping into thin air. It is *re-embedding in institutions that have learned to hide themselves*, an apparatus so well built and so familiar that we mistake it for a garden.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Pick a fiction and test it.** Choose one of the three fictitious commodities (land, labour, or money) and describe a real situation from your own world where treating it as an ordinary good produces something that feels strange once you look (a layoff described as "cutting costs," a forest priced for sale, a payday loan). Say what the commodity-fiction makes visible and what it hides.
2. **Steelman the garden.** State the strongest honest version of the view that the free market is a spontaneous order the state only disturbs, including its best evidence and its real appeal. Then say exactly where Polanyi's record meets it, and where you think the disagreement actually lies.
3. **Weigh the criticism.** The critics say "disembedding" is too strong, since every economy, the market included, is embedded in *some* institutions. Does that objection wound Polanyi's "laissez-faire was planned," leave it standing, or strengthen it? Argue your answer using the difference between *no institutions* and *institutions that hide themselves*.
4. **Run the lens.** Take the claim "you have to work for a living, that's just reality." Make it strange the way module 01's anthropologist would, then use this module to separate what is genuinely necessary in it from what was built by law and could have been built otherwise.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of the module, and not a verdict that markets are good or bad.
- Works from a **specific** case (a real layoff, a real law, a real price) and reads its details, rather than gesturing at "the market" in the abstract.
- Keeps **evidence separate from interpretation**: distinguishes what demonstrably happened (the 1834 reform, the enclosure acts) from Polanyi's reading of what it means.
- Steelmans the garden picture, or the disembedding critics, honestly before answering, never substituting a sneer for an argument.
- Uses *fictitious commodity*, *self-regulating market*, and *laissez-faire was planned* precisely, and avoids the trap of treating "fiction" as "fake," or treating the made character of the market as proof it is worthless.

## Recap

You now hold the course's hardest blow against its central misconception. The self-regulating market, a whole society run by the market, is not a garden that grew wild but a machine someone built and maintains: **built, not grown**. Its foundation is three **fictitious commodities**, land, labour, and money, none of them made for sale, all of them forced to *appear* as goods on a shelf, reification with the receipts. And it was kept running by the state, not in spite of it: **"laissez-faire was planned,"** while the resistance to it was the unplanned thing. The deepest piece of common sense this course confronts, that markets are natural and the state is an intrusion, turns out to have the arrow pointing backwards. Next we follow the market into the lecture hall, where a young science took this freshly built order and rewrote it as a system of natural laws.
