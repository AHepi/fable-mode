## The hook

Run the numbers on a worker and you will find a column missing.

A factory needs a worker who arrives fed, rested, clothed, and reasonably sane, and who, decades from now, will be replaced by someone the same is true of. None of that comes from the wage. The wage buys the hours on the floor; it does not cook the dinner, raise the child who will one day take the worker's place, nurse the fever, or hold the household together on the days the worker cannot. That work is done (it has to be, or the whole arrangement stops within a generation), but it is done off the books, mostly by women, mostly for nothing. The paid economy runs a profit in part because a second economy, unpaid, never sends an invoice.

That missing column is the work ahead. Earlier we watched the commons fenced and a propertyless worker produced by force and law, the dispossession that made the "free" worker (module 04). True, and half a story: the worker it describes had to be sustained somewhere and replaced somewhere. The somewhere is the household, and the labour that runs it has been so thoroughly recoded as "what women are for" that we forget to count it as labour at all. That recoding, work disguised as nature, is naturalization (the dressing-up of a made arrangement as an unchangeable fact, module 01) operating on the most intimate scene there is.

## Context & background

The name for the missing column is **social reproduction**: the largely unpaid, gendered work of sustaining and raising the workforce. Not "reproduction" in the narrow sense of giving birth, though that is part of it, but the whole daily and generational labour of keeping human beings alive and able to work: feeding, cleaning, caring for the sick and the old, raising children to adulthood, and patching back together each evening the people the workday wears down. Production makes the goods. Reproduction makes (and re-makes, every day) the producers.

Hold the two side by side and the asymmetry jumps out. The producing side is paid, counted, and called "the economy"; its hours show up in wages and national accounts. The reproducing side is unpaid, uncounted, and called "home" or "family" or "love": anything but work. Yet the first cannot run for a week without the second. A wage economy that had to *pay* for all the care it consumes, every meal, every night of nursing, every hour of raising the next generation of workers, would look very different, and might not close its books at all.

So this course owns a phrase for what is happening: the **hidden subsidy**. A subsidy is support that lets an enterprise charge less than its true cost because someone else covers the difference. The paid economy receives exactly that from the unpaid household, and the canonical image to keep in view is **the subsidy that never appears on the invoice**. The bill the factory pays for labour is short by the entire cost of producing and maintaining the labourer, because that cost was met, unwaged, in the home. The subsidy is real, large, and continuous; what makes it *hidden* is that it has been coded as nature rather than as economics. That coding is the part this module takes apart.

A note in the spirit this course keeps. To call care "labour" and "subsidy" is not to say it is *only* that, or that the love in a kitchen is a sham. People genuinely love and care, and the analysis does not deny it. The point is sharper: whatever else this work is, it is *also* work the wage economy depends on and refuses to count, and the refusal is not an accident.

## Narrative / exposition

The spine the course has followed (Marx on the dispossession of the worker, Polanyi on the building of the market) runs through the paid economy and the wage-worker, and treats the household as background scenery. The feminist tradition this module draws on pulls it into frame and asks the question the spine skips: where does the "free" worker come from each morning, and who makes him?

The answer is that the same historical process that built the paid worker also built the unpaid one: that the disciplining of work and the disciplining of reproduction happened together, in the same centuries, by related means. The sharpest version belongs to **Silvia Federici**, an Italian-American feminist scholar, in her book *Caliban and the Witch* (2004). Her claim: the period of enclosure and primitive accumulation, the often-violent making of capitalism's preconditions (module 04), was also the period in which women were stripped of older economic footholds, pushed out of trades and the commons, and reassigned to unpaid reproductive labour in the home. As the male worker was "freed" from the land into the wage, the female worker was, in Federici's reading, enclosed into the household.

The strangeness of the arrangement is the whole point, and it is exactly the kind habit hides. Step back the way the anthropologist of module 01 stepped back from the job interview, and look at the claim that household and care work is "women's nature." A vast quantity of genuinely demanding labour, skilled, exhausting, indispensable, is reclassified as *not labour* by appeal to biology. Because a woman can bear a child, the reasoning runs, it is her nature to also cook, clean, nurse, soothe, and raise, and what flows from nature is not *work*, for which one might be paid, but simply what one *is*. The leap is the one the lens taught you to catch: from a real fact about bodies ("women bear children") to a sweeping claim about one social arrangement ("therefore the unpaid household is women's natural place"). The first does not entail the second any more than "humans have hands" entails "humans must work for wages." A contingent division of labour, decided by power and history, is dressed as a fact of biology, and once it wears that costume, the question of paying for it does not even arise. You do not pay the rain for falling.

That is naturalization at its most efficient. Enclosure (module 04) needed fences, deeds, and acts of Parliament: visible machinery. The hidden subsidy needs almost none once the coding holds, because work turned into "nature" defends itself: to ask for a wage for it sounds, inside the coding, like asking to be paid for breathing. The subsidy stays off the invoice not because anyone forges the books but because, to the naturalized eye, there is no work there to bill.

## Primary source

Federici puts the comparison starkly. Where the male waged worker confronts the factory as the site where his labour is extracted, she argues, women came to confront their own bodies as the site where theirs was.

> "The body has been for women in capitalist society what the factory has been for male waged workers: the primary ground of their exploitation and resistance, as the female body has been appropriated by the state and men and forced to function as a means for the reproduction and accumulation of labor."
>
> — Silvia Federici, *Caliban and the Witch* (2004)

Read it as a specimen, the way module 01 read Thatcher, not a slogan to cheer but a claim to weigh. Federici performs an act of estrangement: she takes something coded as the most natural thing in the world, a woman's body and what it does, and re-describes it in the vocabulary of the factory floor, as a site where labour is appropriated and where it might be resisted. The move is meant to jolt, and it earns the jolt by making the hidden subsidy suddenly visible as *labour*. Whether you find the comparison illuminating or overdrawn, notice what it accomplishes: it drags reproduction out of the category "nature" and into the category "work," which is precisely where the naturalizing move had refused to let it go.

*(Confidence on the quotation: high. This formulation, the body as, for women, what the factory is for male waged workers, is among the most-cited lines from* Caliban and the Witch*, drawn from its introduction; the wording above is rendered faithfully to that well-established passage. No page number is asserted. Where exact phrasing is uncertain anywhere in this course, the rule is to paraphrase and label rather than invent.)*

## Check yourself

Before reading on: in your own words, what makes the hidden subsidy *hidden*? It isn't that the work is secret: everyone knows the cooking and caring happen. So what does the hiding?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The work is visible; what is hidden is that it is *work the paid economy depends on and is not paying for*. The hiding is done by the coding of household and care labour as "women's nature" rather than as labour. Once it counts as nature, just what women are, it drops out of the economic ledger, and the question of who is subsidizing whom never comes up. The disguise, not secrecy, keeps the subsidy off the invoice.

## Analysis & interpretation

Federici's larger and more startling claim is about the witch hunts, and here the course has to be especially careful, because this is exactly the kind of place where evidence and interpretation must be kept visibly apart (module 01), and where a powerful reading can outrun what the record can bear.

Start with the evidence, the part historians broadly accept. Across Europe, roughly the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, tens of thousands of people were tried for witchcraft and many were executed. The accused were overwhelmingly women. This persecution overlapped in time with the upheavals of enclosure, dispossession, and the early reorganization of work and property that earlier modules traced. Those are checkable facts, and grim enough without embellishment; handle them with sobriety, not as a horror-show.

Now the interpretation: Federici's reading of what those facts *mean*. She argues that the witch hunts were not a stray outbreak of superstition but were bound up with the disciplining of women's reproductive labour: that the women most often targeted included healers, midwives, and others who held knowledge of bodies, contraception, and birth, and that the persecution helped break older forms of female autonomy over reproduction at the moment capitalism needed reproduction harnessed and unpaid. On this reading the witch hunt is a chapter of primitive accumulation written on women's bodies, the reproductive counterpart to the enclosure of the fields.

It is a powerful reframing. It takes an episode usually filed under "religion" or "madness" and asks an economic question of it, and in doing so it makes the hidden subsidy's history visible. But (and the course's credibility lives in this *but*) Federici's **causal and chronological claims are contested by historians of witchcraft**. The disputes are specific: the persecutions did not line up neatly, in place or timing, with the spread of capitalist relations; the accused were not predominantly midwives, and in places midwives were more often witnesses for the prosecution than its victims; the causes specialists emphasize, religious conflict, war, local feuds, weak or zealous legal institutions, panic, are many, and most are not economic. Mainstream witchcraft scholarship does not read the hunts as primarily a tool of capitalist labour discipline.

So what should a careful reader do with a reading that is illuminating and contested at once? Not pick a team and stop thinking. Hold the seam open. Federici's *interpretation*, that the control of reproduction was central to the making of capitalism and that women paid a distinct and brutal price in that making, can carry real force even if her *specific causal story about the witch hunts* is, as many specialists argue, too tidy. The first is a claim about a structure and can be supported from the wider history of women's exclusion and unpaid labour; the second is a claim about a particular cause of a particular wave of killings, and that is the one the record strains against. A reading can reframe a question brilliantly and still overstate its causal link, and saying both at once, rather than swallowing the package whole or dismissing it whole, is the discipline this course keeps. To wave the contestation away because the reframing is exciting would be to commit, on this topic, the exact sin the course diagnoses: smuggling a contestable reading in under the cover of fact.

## Check yourself

Federici reads the witch hunts as part of capitalism's making. Separate the two things a careful reader must keep apart here: which part is widely accepted, and which part do historians of witchcraft contest?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Widely accepted (closer to evidence):** that the witch persecutions happened across roughly the 16th–17th centuries, that the accused and executed were overwhelmingly women, and that this overlapped in time with enclosure and the early reshaping of work and property. Also broadly defensible: the structural claim that controlling reproduction and excluding women from paid economic life mattered to capitalism's development.

**Contested (Federici's interpretation):** the *causal* claim that the witch hunts were primarily a mechanism for disciplining women's reproductive labour as part of primitive accumulation, including the chronological fit with capitalism's spread and the emphasis on midwives/healers as targets. Specialists point to many largely non-economic causes (religious conflict, war, local feuds, legal institutions, panic) and dispute the neat alignment. The reframing can be valuable while that specific causal story is judged too tidy.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Run the method on care.** Take one piece of household or care work you have done or received: a parent's cooking, an unpaid stretch of looking after a sick relative, the daily logistics of keeping a home running. Describe it the way the anthropologist of module 01 described the job interview, refusing to take "that's just family" as obvious. Then say in one sentence what the estrangement reveals about its place in the wage economy.
2. **Steelman the coding, then test it.** State the strongest honest version of the claim that the household division of labour reflects something natural rather than imposed. Then locate the exact point where it slides from a fact about bodies to a claim about one social arrangement, and say what evidence would be needed to defend the second step.
3. **Weigh the reframing.** Federici's witch-hunt argument is illuminating *and* contested. Argue for how a reader should hold a reading like that: when does a powerful reframing earn a place in how we think even though its causal claim is disputed, and when does the dispute sink it? Use the evidence/interpretation distinction explicitly.
4. **Find the subsidy elsewhere.** The hidden subsidy is not only the household. Identify one other kind of uncounted, unpaid, or underpaid work that the paid economy leans on without counting, and argue whether the same naturalizing move ("it's just what those people do") props up the wall that keeps it off the invoice. Resist romanticizing: name the work honestly, not as noble suffering.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of this module, and not a verdict that capitalism is good or bad.
- Works from a **specific** scene, case, or text and reads its actual details, rather than gesturing at "women" or "society" in the abstract.
- Keeps **evidence separate from interpretation**, especially on the witch hunts, distinguishing what is broadly accepted from Federici's contested causal claim.
- Steelmans the "it's natural" coding honestly before answering it, and never substitutes a slogan for an argument.

## Recap

The course's spine showed how the propertyless worker was made; this module supplied the column that spine leaves out. **Social reproduction**, the unpaid, mostly women's labour of feeding, caring, and raising the next workforce, is a continuous **hidden subsidy** to the paid economy, support that never appears on the invoice because it has been recoded from labour into "women's nature." That recoding is naturalization working on the household, and it defends itself: work disguised as nature does not get billed. Federici sharpens the point with the witch hunts, a reframing worth taking seriously, and contested in its causal detail, which is why we held the evidence and the interpretation apart rather than swallowing or dismissing the whole. The worker was made; so, by the same history and the same disguise, was the unpaid maker behind him. Next the course turns from how the order was built to how it is *held*: the machinery by which a made world passes for common sense.
