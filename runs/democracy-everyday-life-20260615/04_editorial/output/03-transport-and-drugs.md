## Transport: free fares, road charges, and the long view

### The problem

The cost-of-living diagnosis runs through this whole series — the Essentials course sets it out, and M2's Teal Card addressed the pinch for under-30s. Transport is one more place where cost falls on households. For those without a car, or with several people paying fares every day, the bill accumulates. Congestion costs time. And New Zealand's record on major infrastructure has been, by most assessments, stop-start: projects launched, reversed, relaunched, cancelled as governments rotate. TOP argues this leaves a transport network that costs too much to use and gets too little long-term investment.

### What TOP proposes

TOP's transport package, as stated in its 2026 platform, has three parts.

**Permanent free public transport nationwide.** Not just for under-30s — that was the Teal Card in M2. For everyone, permanently. You board a bus or train, you pay nothing. The aim is to shift commuters and daily trips off congested roads, lower household transport costs, and make public transport a realistic option regardless of income.

**Road-user charging (RUC) to replace the fuel tax.** At the moment, NZ drivers pay a fuel tax at the pump — a portion of the petrol price goes to roading. TOP proposes replacing this with a road-user charging system: you pay by how far you drive, not by how much fuel you burn. (Diesel vehicles in NZ already operate on a RUC basis; the proposal extends something like this model to all vehicles.) Road-user charging revenue would fund, among other things, the free public transport.

**Long-horizon infrastructure planning.** TOP argues for "intergenerational" infrastructure — long-term commitments to transport projects that survive electoral cycles rather than being launched and cancelled with each new government. This connects directly to M1's theme of pendulum politics: the lurching that wastes money on policy can waste it on infrastructure too.

### The critiques

**Who pays for free transport?** Nothing is actually free — someone funds it. TOP's answer is road-user charging revenue. But critics point out that free public transport is a universal benefit: wealthy people who ride the train also travel for nothing. More targeted uses of the same money — reduced fares for low-income households, better service in underserved areas — might deliver more benefit per dollar to those who need it most. A bus that is free but infrequent and unreliable may not shift many car trips.

**RUC complexity and privacy.** Road-user charging means measuring how far vehicles travel. For diesel vehicles, this already works via odometer-based distance licences. Extending it to all petrol vehicles raises questions about how distances are tracked, whether that involves location data, and who administers it. A system based on GPS raises privacy concerns; one based on odometer readings is harder to monitor. The transition from a fuel-tax system involves implementation costs and potential evasion risks.

**Rural households, urban benefits.** A fuel tax falls harder on people who drive older, less fuel-efficient vehicles — often lower-income households in areas without good public transport. Road-user charging, charged by distance, has a similar profile. Critics argue that the combination of free public transport (which benefits urban users most) and road-user charging (which burdens rural drivers who have no alternative to a car) could disadvantage people in regions where buses are sparse or do not run at all.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what is road-user charging, and how does it differ from a fuel tax?</summary>

A fuel tax is levied on the fuel itself — you pay it at the pump, embedded in the price of petrol or diesel. The more fuel you use, the more you pay, but the charge is bundled into the price and invisible at the point of filling up.

Road-user charging charges drivers directly for the distance they travel, not the fuel they burn. An electric car, which uses no petrol, pays nothing under a fuel-tax system. Under RUC, it pays by how far it goes. The practical differences: RUC can be applied equally to all vehicle types regardless of fuel, and rates can in principle vary by road type or time of day. The drawbacks: it requires a mechanism to measure distance, which raises administrative complexity and potential privacy questions.

</details>

## Drug law: harm minimisation over criminal justice

### The problem

New Zealand's current approach to most illegal drugs — including cannabis — treats possession and use primarily as criminal matters. People found with small quantities can be charged, fined, and convicted; a conviction can affect employment, travel, and housing for years. TOP's starting premise is that addiction is better understood as a health problem than a crime. A criminal-justice-first approach, TOP argues, does not reduce drug use — it attaches a legal burden to people who are using, which can worsen their situation and make it harder to seek help.

### What TOP proposes

TOP's platform describes a **harm-minimisation** approach — a term from public health meaning that when a risk cannot be eliminated, the goal shifts to reducing the damage it causes. TOP attributes three main elements to this approach.

**Legalise cannabis.** Under TOP's proposal, cannabis would be legal for adult use, regulated similarly to alcohol: age limits, licensed sellers, product standards. The argument is that prohibition has not stopped cannabis use but has pushed it into unregulated markets with no quality control and no age verification. Legalisation and regulation, TOP argues, would reduce some of those harms while generating tax revenue and allowing public-health messaging.

**Decriminalise possession of small quantities of other drugs.** For drugs that would remain controlled — methamphetamine, heroin, MDMA, and others — TOP proposes decriminalising possession of small personal-use amounts, meaning possession would not result in a criminal charge, though supply and distribution would remain illegal. Someone found with a small amount would typically be referred to health services rather than prosecuted. Portugal adopted a similar model in 2001; its experience is frequently cited in this debate, though NZ's context differs.

**Let doctors prescribe for addiction treatment; fund kaupapa Māori services.** TOP proposes that doctors be able to prescribe drugs — including heroin substitutes and cannabis — to people with addictions, treating addiction through the health system. The platform also specifically funds **kaupapa Māori addiction services**: services designed and delivered within a Māori cultural framework. This recognises that Māori communities have been disproportionately affected by both addiction and criminal-justice responses to drug use, and that culturally grounded services may be more effective and more accessible for those communities.

<details><summary>Check yourself: what is the difference between legalising cannabis and decriminalising it?</summary>

**Legalisation** means that the activity — producing, selling, possessing, using cannabis — is no longer prohibited by law, subject to whatever regulations apply (age limits, licensed sellers, quantity limits). Cannabis becomes a legal product in a regulated market.

**Decriminalisation** means that possession of small quantities is still technically against the law but is no longer treated as a criminal matter. You will not be charged or convicted. You might receive a fine or a health referral, but you do not accumulate a criminal record. Supply and distribution typically remain criminal offences under decriminalisation.

TOP's platform proposes full legalisation for cannabis and decriminalisation for small-quantity possession of other drugs. They are different instruments aimed at reducing the criminal-justice burden in different ways.

</details>

### The 2020 referendum context

New Zealand already voted on cannabis legalisation — and rejected it. In the October 2020 general election, a non-binding cannabis referendum asked whether recreational cannabis should be legalised and regulated. The result was **50.7% No, 46.1% Yes** (with informal votes making up the remainder). That is a narrow margin, but a majority voted against. The referendum was non-binding, so it did not automatically change the law, but it is the clearest direct expression of public opinion on the question. TOP's policy proposes to proceed with legalisation regardless. Critics argue that revisiting a recent referendum result without a clear mandate is democratically questionable; supporters of TOP's position argue a 4-point margin in a single vote does not permanently foreclose a policy question.

### The critiques

**Public safety and youth use.** The concern most often raised against cannabis legalisation is that wider legal availability increases use, including among young people. Evidence from US states that have legalised cannabis (Colorado, California, and others) shows mixed results on youth use rates — this is a genuinely contested empirical area, not a settled one. Supporters of legalisation point to regulated markets restricting sales to adults; opponents argue legal availability normalises use and that regulated markets do not eliminate illegal supply.

**The referendum result.** The 2020 vote is the sharpest political constraint. Proponents of TOP's policy argue that a near-even split is not a decisive rejection, and that the public debate in 2020 was compressed and poorly resourced — the government released its implementation framework very late in the campaign. Critics counter that a recent democratic vote should not be set aside by a party that holds no seats in parliament and is polling at a few percent. Both readings are defensible.

**Implementation questions.** Who licences sellers? How are products tested? What happens to existing convictions for cannabis offences? Decriminalisation models also raise questions about where the line between personal use and supply is drawn, and whether health services are adequately funded to handle increased referrals. TOP has outlined the direction; the operational detail of implementation is less fully specified.

## Exercises

**1. Road-user charging vs. fuel tax**

Who benefits and who is burdened differently under each system? Think about: an electric vehicle owner in Auckland; a rural household that commutes 80 km each way; someone who never drives but uses public transport.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

Under the **fuel tax**, electric vehicle owners pay no contribution to roading (they use no petrol). Petrol and diesel drivers pay in proportion to fuel burned — which roughly tracks distance but is also affected by engine efficiency. Older, less fuel-efficient vehicles pay more per kilometre.

Under **road-user charging**, electric vehicle owners pay like everyone else, by distance. This resolves the "EV free rider" problem but removes any environmental incentive built into the fuel tax. Rural commuters who drive long distances pay more in proportion — and if they have no viable public transport alternative, they cannot reduce their bill by switching modes, unlike urban drivers. Someone who does not drive pays nothing under either system; under TOP's plan, they also have free public transport.

Whether the shift from fuel tax to RUC — combined with free public transport — is broadly progressive or regressive depends on geography and circumstance. Urban non-drivers gain the most; rural long-distance drivers with no transit alternative face higher costs without a straightforward way to offset them.

</details>

**2. Argue both sides on cannabis legalisation**

Construct the strongest case for TOP's legalisation proposal, then construct the strongest case against it. Stay factual and attributed — do not campaign for either position.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

**For legalisation (as TOP frames it):**
Prohibition has not stopped cannabis use; NZ has consistently high use rates by international standards despite the drug being illegal. Criminalisation imposes lasting costs — employment barriers, fines, a record — that fall disproportionately on young people and Māori communities. A regulated market sets age limits that black-market suppliers ignore, allows product quality standards, and generates tax revenue that could fund addiction services. Portugal's decriminalisation (though not full legalisation) is often cited as evidence that health-focused approaches can reduce harm without increasing overall use.

**Against legalisation (critics' strongest case):**
The 2020 referendum produced a majority against, providing a clear and recent democratic signal. Legalisation may increase total use, including among young people, even with age restrictions — regulated markets do not eliminate illegal supply. Social and health costs, including mental-health effects associated with high-potency cannabis, may offset or exceed any reduction in criminal-justice costs. Evidence from US states is mixed and contested.

A strong discussion holds both sets of arguments simultaneously, without treating either as decisive.

</details>

**3. How does harm minimisation differ from the current approach?**

In plain words, describe the philosophy of harm minimisation and explain how TOP's drug proposals embody it.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

The current criminal-justice approach treats drug use primarily as behaviour to be punished and deterred: possess an illegal drug, face prosecution. The theory is that the threat of criminal consequences reduces use.

Harm minimisation starts from a different premise: some people will use drugs regardless of legal status, and the policy goal should be to reduce the damage that use causes — to users, their families, and communities — rather than to punish the behaviour itself. In practice this means treating addiction as a health problem (hence medical prescriptions for addiction treatment), removing criminal barriers to seeking help (hence decriminalisation, so someone with a problem does not risk prosecution by going to a doctor), and reducing harms associated with unregulated supply (hence legalisation of cannabis, with quality and age controls).

TOP's kaupapa Māori addiction services sit within this framework: effective harm reduction requires services that people will actually use. Culturally grounded services may be more accessible to Māori communities that have historically had reason to distrust mainstream institutions — a recognition that neither the problem nor its solution is culturally neutral.

</details>

**A fair question: if NZ voted 50.7% against cannabis legalisation in 2020, how much weight should that result carry for policy in 2026?**

Consider the arguments on both sides before reading the discussion.

<details><summary>Show discussion</summary>

**Arguments for treating the referendum as a strong constraint:**
It was a direct public vote, held at a general election with high turnout. Majorities — however narrow — matter in democratic systems. A party polling at a few percent proposing to revisit a recent majority verdict is open to the charge that it is substituting its own judgment for the public's. If the result were reversed and a narrow majority had voted Yes, critics of legalisation would not accept a proposal to ignore that result either.

**Arguments for treating the result as one input among others:**
The margin was extremely narrow — around 4 percentage points — and opinion can shift over six years. The 2020 vote was preceded by a compressed and poorly resourced public debate; the government released its implementation framework very late in the campaign. Non-binding referenda inform but do not constitutionally constrain future parliaments — every parliament elected after a referendum is free to legislate differently. Policy is made by elected governments, not fixed by advisory votes.

Neither reading is obviously wrong. The honest position is that the referendum matters as evidence of public opinion at a point in time, but does not permanently foreclose the question.

</details>

## Recap

This module covered two places where TOP argues everyday life can look different: transport that is free to use and funded by road-user charges, and drug law reoriented around health rather than punishment. For each policy, there is a genuine problem in the target — transport costs and stop-start infrastructure; the harms of treating addiction as crime — and genuine objections that do not dissolve on inspection: who funds universal free transport, and whether it is well-targeted; the 2020 cannabis referendum result, and the public-safety concerns that produced it.

Together with M1's democracy reforms and M2's Teal Card, these three modules trace TOP's argument that the same diagnosis — a system tilted toward property over people, and politics too short-sighted to fix it — runs beneath everything from how Parliament deliberates to what a young person pays at the dentist to who gets a criminal record for drug use. Whether the remedies are proportionate to those problems, well-designed, and worth their costs is exactly what a policy wonk needs to be able to argue — in both directions.

The series closes here. TOP is a small party with large ambitions and, so far, no seats in parliament. The policies in this series are proposals, not laws. But the problems they point at — inequality, cost of living, environmental degradation, short political horizons — are not in serious dispute, even among critics. What remains disputed is everything else: the mechanisms, the costs, the trade-offs, and who ultimately benefits. That is what politics is for, and now you are equipped to be part of it.
