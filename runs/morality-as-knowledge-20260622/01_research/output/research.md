# Research Dossier — *Morality as Knowledge*

**Run:** `morality-as-knowledge-20260622` · **Kind:** humanities (philosophy) · **Level:** undergrad
(lower-to-mid; motivated general reader, no prior philosophy coursework) · **Math:** none.

This is a designer's dossier, not learner prose. It maps the territory, fixes the attributions, supplies
the real story material, and flags the traps. The curriculum stage decides the module shape; this file
gives it accurate raw material and an honest accounting of what the argument does and does not prove.

---

## Orientation — the house discipline (carry this into every later stage)

The course **defends one thesis at full strength**: *morality is objective — there are moral truths and
we make real progress toward them — not because morality rests on a foundation, a proof, or an authority,
but because moral knowledge grows the way all knowledge grows: by conjecture and criticism,
error-correction, and the search for good (hard-to-vary) explanations.* That is the spine. The course
argues *for* it, persuasively, in Deutsch's own accessible register.

But it argues honestly, and four disciplines are non-negotiable. They are constraints on every downstream
stage, not slogans to recite to the reader (enact, do not announce):

1. **Steelman first.** Every objection is stated in its strongest form, sympathetically, *before* any
   reply. The reader should feel the pull of relativism, of Hume's gap, of Mackie's queerness, before
   seeing how a Deutschian answers. A strawman is a fabrication of a different kind and is barred.

2. **Attribute, never blend.** Hume ≠ Moore ≠ Mackie ≠ Ayer ≠ Popper ≠ Deutsch. Each claim is pinned to
   the person who made it. The most important internal seam: **Popper owns** critical rationalism /
   fallibilism, the dissolution of induction, World 3 / objective knowledge, and the open society.
   **Deutsch owns** the "hard to vary" criterion, *reach* in its Beginning-of-Infinity sense, the
   beginning-of-infinity optimism, and the *explicit extension to moral (and aesthetic) objectivity via
   good explanations*. Deutsch builds **on** Popper; do not credit Popper with "hard to vary" or the
   moral-objectivity move, and do not credit Deutsch with dissolving induction or inventing the open
   society. (See the attribution ledger, §"Sources".)

3. **Do not overclaim; name the live debate.** What the argument plausibly establishes: morality is
   *epistemically on a par with science* (no special is/ought problem), and moral progress and moral
   error are real. What it does **not** simply settle: whether that secures full **moral realism**
   (mind-independent moral facts). An anti-realist or quasi-realist can accept fallibilism, error-
   correction, and even moral progress while still denying mind-independent moral facts; the moral analog
   of empirical *test/prediction* is itself disputed; some hold Deutsch's "objectivity" is thinner than
   realism. The course leads with the case and **ends honestly** on the open metaethical debate, crowning
   no winner there. This is a defense, not a proof, and it says so.

4. **No fabrication.** No invented quotes, dates, or page numbers. Deutsch and Popper are quotable, but
   wording is verified or clearly labelled paraphrase. Everything in the closing stories is real attested
   detail (this is the highest-fabrication-risk section). Anything uncertain is marked `[verify]`.

The intellectual rigor of this course lives in the **structure of the argument** (full-strength objection,
then attributed answer), in **accurate attribution**, and in **honest qualification** — not in formalism.

## Scope

### In
- **The challenge, steelmanned:** why morality is widely thought *not* to be objective.
  - Moral **relativism** ("morality is just culture / opinion / taste").
  - **Hume's is/ought gap** (you cannot validly derive an "ought" from an "is").
  - **Moore's naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument** (good is not definable as any natural
    property).
  - **Mackie's argument from queerness + moral error theory** (objective values would be metaphysically
    and epistemologically "queer," so there are none; "There are no objective values").
  - **Ayer's emotivism** (moral sentences express emotion, state no fact, are neither true nor false).
  - The general worry: there are no moral *facts* and no way to *test* a moral claim.
  - (Optionally surfaced as a modern objection: **evolutionary debunking arguments** — Street, Joyce.)
- **The Popperian epistemology underneath:** critical rationalism; **fallibilism** (all knowledge is
  conjectural and corrigible); the rejection of **justified true belief / justificationism /
  foundationalism** (knowledge has no foundations and is never *justified*, only criticized); the
  **problem of induction** and Popper's **dissolution** of it (we do not derive theories from
  observation; we conjecture and test by attempted refutation); **objective knowledge / World 3**;
  the **open society** as political error-correction.
- **The decisive move (Deutsch's):** the is/ought gap is *not a special problem for morality*, because
  nothing is derived from a foundation — not science either (induction is the exact parallel). "Morality
  has no foundation" therefore puts it on the **same footing** as science, not a worse one.
- **Objectivity without foundations (Deutsch's):** **good explanations that are hard to vary**; **reach**;
  objectivity as the capacity for **error-correction and progress**, not certainty. Applied to morality:
  objective moral knowledge = moral explanations that solve real problems (what to do, what to want, how
  to live), survive criticism, and are hard to vary.
- **Moral progress is real:** abolition of slavery (and similar) as the *correction of an error*, not a
  change of fashion; the reality of moral error and improvement implies an objective standard being
  approached; relativism cannot account for progress and is self-undermining.
- **Knowledge and the good (Deutsch's deeper move):** persons as **universal explainers / knowledge-
  creators**; the moral weight of sustaining the conditions for unbounded error-correction; **optimism**
  ("problems are inevitable; problems are soluble"; "all evils are due to insufficient knowledge"); the
  **good** bound up with the growth of knowledge and the open society; **evil / the bad** as the
  entrenchment of error and the destruction of the means of correcting it.
- **The aesthetics parallel (Deutsch, optional module):** beauty is *also* objective — the flowers
  argument (a hard-to-fake, hard-to-vary attractive signal aimed across a species gap) — as a reinforcing
  case for objective-knowledge-without-foundations.
- **The honest close:** what is established vs. what remains a live metaethical debate (realism vs.
  anti-realism / quasi-realism; companions-in-guilt; the status of moral disagreement; whether there is
  a moral analog of empirical test). The method as the takeaway: an error-correcting stance toward one's
  own morality.

### Out
- A full survey of **normative ethics** (no systematic utilitarianism vs. deontology vs. virtue-ethics
  comparison; these appear only as *examples of moral conjectures to be criticized*).
- A balanced **metaethics textbook** (rival positions appear as steelmanned objections, not as a neutral
  survey for its own sake).
- **Religious / authority-based** grounding of morality (the view explicitly rejects it; named, not
  argued at length).
- **Deutsch's physics** (multiverse, quantum computation) beyond what the epistemology needs.
- **Partisan applied-ethics culture-war cases.** Examples of moral progress are chosen for being
  historically settled and verifiable (slavery), not live partisan flashpoints.
- A defense of any *particular* first-order moral code. The thesis is about the **objectivity and growth**
  of moral knowledge, not which specific maxims are correct.

## Core concepts and dependency sketch

The argument is a chain; later links genuinely require earlier ones. Concepts in dependency order:

1. **Objective vs. subjective / "just opinion."** The thesis word. Objective = answerable to something
   beyond what any individual or culture happens to feel; admits of being *got wrong* and *improved*.
2. **Moral relativism.** The default opposing view: moral claims are true only relative to a culture or
   speaker; no cross-cultural fact of the matter. *(Depends on 1.)*
3. **The is/ought gap (Hume).** No valid inference from purely descriptive ("is") premises to a normative
   ("ought") conclusion. *(Depends on 1.)*
4. **The naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument (Moore).** "Good" cannot be *defined* as any
   natural property; for any natural property N, "this is N, but is it good?" stays an open question.
   *(Depends on 1; distinct from 3 — Moore is about definition, Hume about inference. Keep them apart.)*
5. **Emotivism (Ayer).** Moral sentences *express emotion / attitude*; they state no fact and are
   neither true nor false. *(Depends on 1; a semantic thesis, sharper than relativism.)*
6. **Error theory + argument from queerness (Mackie).** Moral claims *purport* to state objective facts
   but *all are false*, because objective values would be metaphysically and epistemologically "queer"
   (unlike anything else; knowable only by some special faculty). *(Depends on 1, 5; Mackie agrees moral
   claims aim at fact, unlike Ayer — a crucial contrast.)*
7. **The "no facts / no test" worry.** Synthesis of the above as the live problem the course must answer:
   there seem to be no moral facts and no way to test a moral claim. *(Depends on 2–6.)*
8. **Fallibilism / critical rationalism (Popper).** All knowledge is conjectural and corrigible; certainty
   is not available anywhere. *(Foundation of the reply.)*
9. **Anti-justificationism / anti-foundationalism (Popper).** Knowledge is not *justified true belief*;
   it has no foundations and is never *justified*, only *criticized*. We reject the demand for a base.
   *(Depends on 8.)*
10. **The problem of induction and its dissolution (Popper).** We do not derive theories from
    observation; observations never *prove* a general theory. Instead we **conjecture and test by
    attempted refutation**. This is the model for *all* knowledge growth. *(Depends on 8, 9. This is the
    hinge: it makes 11 possible.)*
11. **The decisive move (Deutsch): no special is/ought problem.** Since *nothing* is derived from a
    foundation — not science either (10) — "morality has no foundation" is not a special defect of
    morality; it is the universal condition of knowledge. Morality is in the *same* position as science.
    *(Depends on 3, 10. The course's central pivot.)*
12. **Good explanations / "hard to vary" (Deutsch).** A good explanation accounts for what it sets out
    to account for and is *hard to vary* while still doing so — its parts cannot be swapped without
    wrecking the explanation. This is what distinguishes good from bad explanations *without* appeal to a
    foundation or to proof. *(Depends on 10; Deutsch's, not Popper's.)*
13. **Reach (Deutsch).** A good explanation tends to solve problems beyond the ones it was invented for.
    *(Depends on 12.)*
14. **Objectivity as error-correction, not certainty.** Objectivity is the capacity to be *wrong and to
    find out* and improve — not the possession of a proof. *(Synthesis of 8–13.)*
15. **Objective moral knowledge.** Apply 12–14 to ethics: a moral explanation is objective insofar as it
    solves real problems (what to do, want, how to live), survives criticism, and is hard to vary.
    *(Depends on 11–14. The constructive core of the thesis.)*
16. **Moral progress and the reality of moral error.** Some moral changes are *corrections of errors*
    (slavery), not changes of fashion; their reality presupposes an objective standard being approached.
    *(Depends on 14, 15.)*
17. **Relativism is self-undermining and cannot account for progress.** If all moralities are equally
    valid, "we improved" is meaningless — yet abolition was an improvement, not a swap. *(Depends on 16;
    closes the loop back to 2.)*
18. **Persons as universal explainers; the open society (Popper) as political error-correction.**
    Institutions that let us remove bad rulers/policies *without violence* are the political form of
    fallibilism. *(Open society is Popper's; the link to knowledge-creation is developed by Deutsch.)*
19. **Optimism; the good and the bad in terms of knowledge (Deutsch).** "Problems are inevitable;
    problems are soluble"; "all evils are due to insufficient knowledge." The **good** is bound up with
    the growth of knowledge and the conditions for error-correction; **evil/the bad** is the entrenchment
    of error and the destruction of the means of correcting it. *(Depends on 15, 18. Flag the bold "all
    evils" framing as Deutsch's own and contestable — see pitfalls.)*
20. **The aesthetics parallel (Deutsch).** Beauty is *also* objective; the flowers argument as a
    reinforcing analogy for objectivity-without-foundations. *(Optional; depends on 12, 14.)*
21. **The honest close: established vs. contested.** Epistemic parity with science and the reality of
    moral progress: plausibly established. Robust moral realism: still contested. Name realism vs. anti-
    realism/quasi-realism, companions-in-guilt, moral disagreement, and the disputed moral analog of
    test. *(Depends on everything; crowns no winner.)*

**Shape of the chain:** challenge (1–7) → epistemology (8–10) → the pivot (11) → objectivity without
foundations (12–15) → progress and the failure of relativism (16–17) → knowledge and the good (18–19) →
the aesthetics parallel (20) → the honest close (21).

## Prerequisite analysis (what to teach from scratch at this level)

Assumed background: a motivated reader, comfortable with the everyday idea of "objective vs. just
opinion," willing to follow a sustained argument, but **with no prior philosophy**. At `undergrad` the
dial allows standard vocabulary after a brief gloss, but per the brief, *every* technical term is
introduced in plain words at first use. Nothing below may be used un-glossed.

**Teach from scratch (all of these — no philosophy is assumed):**
- *objective / subjective* (in the precise sense: answerable to something beyond opinion; can be got
  wrong and improved).
- *relativism* (moral claims true only relative to a culture/speaker).
- *the is/ought gap* (Hume) — and the *separate* point of —
- *the naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument* (Moore). Teaching these as *distinct* is itself a
  pedagogical task; learners conflate them.
- *emotivism* (Ayer; "boo/hurrah" is a real, usable gloss).
- *error theory* and *the argument from queerness* (Mackie).
- *fallibilism* / *critical rationalism*.
- *justificationism / foundationalism* / *justified true belief* (and why the view rejects them).
- *the problem of induction* and *Popper's dissolution* of it.
- *conjecture and criticism* (compatible with the existing canon vocabulary; see continuity note).
- *good explanation / hard to vary*.
- *reach*.
- *World 3 / objective knowledge* (use lightly; it is the most abstract Popper concept and can be glossed
  briefly rather than developed in depth — see open questions).
- *the open society*.
- *universal explainer / knowledge-creator*.

**Can be taken as known (everyday, not technical):**
- The historical fact *that* slavery was once legal and widespread and was later abolished (the moral
  *interpretation* is the work; the bare fact is common knowledge).
- That cultures differ in their moral codes (the datum relativism leans on; needs no teaching, only
  framing).
- The everyday distinction between an opinion and a fact (the course sharpens it; it does not start from
  zero).
- Basic comfort with following a multi-step argument and with the words *premise / conclusion / valid*
  (gloss *valid* in the logician's sense at first use to be safe).

**No silent gaps check:** every concept on the dependency chain (1–21) appears above as either
"teach from scratch" or "known (everyday)." The payoff concepts (15, 16, 21) rest only on items taught
earlier in the same chain. Confirmed: no orphan terms.

**Continuity with the existing canon (`conjecture-criticism-calculus`).** That course formalizes
*conjecture*, *criticism*, *hard to vary*, and *reach*. This course is **standalone** and prose-only (no
formal calculus), but should keep the *plain-language* senses compatible: a **conjecture** is a guess/idea
offered to solve a problem; **criticism** is an attempt to find what is wrong with it; **hard to vary**
means the explanation's parts cannot be swapped without wrecking it; **reach** is solving problems beyond
the original one. Use these glosses; do not import the symbols (`a = (c(a), I(a))`, the grounded
extension, etc.) — those belong to the formal course and would mis-level this one.

## Hooks and examples

Motivating openings and illustrations the curriculum/authoring stages can draw on. (Closing-story
material with full verifiable detail is in its own section below; these are lighter hooks and analogies.)

- **The dial test.** "Is the Holocaust wrong, or do you just dislike it?" Almost no one will say it is
  *merely* a matter of taste — yet most people also say morality is "subjective." The course lives in
  that gap between what we *say* about morality and what we *do* with it. (Strong cold open; use once.)
- **Two columns.** A column of claims everyone treats as objective (the Earth orbits the Sun) and a
  column treated as "just opinion" (chocolate is the best flavor). Where does "slavery is wrong" go — and
  why does it feel wrong in the *third* category? (Sets up the whole problem.)
- **The science parallel, made vivid.** No experiment ever *proved* Newton's laws; no number of white
  swans *proves* "all swans are white" (and there are black swans). If "unprovable from observation" does
  not make physics "just opinion," why should "underivable from facts" make ethics "just opinion"? (The
  hook for the decisive move, item 11.)
- **Hard-to-vary, concretely.** The Greek myth that winter comes because Demeter mourns Persephone vs.
  the axial-tilt explanation: you can move the myth's misery to any season; you *cannot* move the tilt
  explanation without breaking it. Deutsch's own contrast (seasons/axis) — verify wording but the example
  is his. (Hook for item 12.)
- **"Could we be wrong about our morality the way past ages were wrong about theirs?"** If yes, there is
  something to be wrong *about* — an objective standard. (Hook for items 14–16.)
- **The flowers puzzle.** Why do humans find flowers beautiful, when flowers evolved their signal for
  *insects* of species they never met? (Hook for item 20; full account below.)
- **Removing rulers without bloodshed.** A vote that ends a government is a society *correcting an error*
  without violence — the political mirror of conjecture-and-refutation. (Hook for item 18, Popper's.)
- **The error-correcting stance, turned on the reader.** The payoff hook: not "here is the correct
  morality" but "here is how to hold *any* morality — as a conjecture open to criticism." (Hook for 21.)

Real, attested episodes for the fact-dense **closing story** that replaces the recap in each humanities

- **John Newton — the slave-ship captain who reversed his view (→ moral progress as error-correction;
  a person changing a moral conviction; items 16–17).**
  English slave-ship captain and investor in the Atlantic trade who later became an Anglican clergyman
  and abolitionist; wrote the hymn "Amazing Grace" (written ~1772–73 for a sermon, published in the 1779
  *Olney Hymns*); in **January 1788** published the pamphlet *Thoughts upon the African Slave Trade*
  describing horrors he had witnessed, and explicitly calling it "a subject of humiliating reflection to
  me, that I was once an active instrument in a business at which my heart now shudders" (his words —
  attested, but **[verify exact wording]** before quoting). Mentored **William Wilberforce**; the British
  **Slave Trade Act 1807** ended the trade; Newton died **December 1807**, months after. *Points true:*
  a single person *correcting* a moral error under criticism and experience — not swapping a fashion. The
  bare reversal is the safest spine; keep the religious-conversion framing light to avoid the "religious
  grounding" the course rejects.

- **The British abolition campaign as institutional error-correction (→ moral progress; the open society;
  items 16–18).**
  The decades-long parliamentary campaign culminating in the **Slave Trade Act 1807** and later the
  **Slavery Abolition Act 1833**. *Points true:* a settled, legal, profitable institution overturned as
  the *correction of an error* through open criticism and political institutions — relativism's "just a
  change of fashion" reading cannot survive contact with the campaigners' own language of *wrong*. Use
  verifiable specifics only (dates, the Acts); avoid mythologizing any single hero.

- **Popper flees Vienna and writes *The Open Society and Its Enemies* in New Zealand (→ fallibilism made
  political; the open society; item 18; also a Popper-life beat).**
  Popper, of Jewish descent, left Austria ahead of the Anschluss and took a post at **Canterbury
  University College, Christchurch, New Zealand**. There, during **WWII**, he wrote *The Open Society and
  Its Enemies* (published **1945**), which he described as his **"war effort."** *Points true:* the moral/
  political application of fallibilism — institutions that let a society remove bad rulers without
  violence — written, literally, in exile from a regime that had abolished exactly those institutions.
  (Source: Te Ara Dictionary of New Zealand Biography; widely attested.)

- **Ignaz Semmelweis and handwashing (→ fallibilism, entrenched error, the cost of rejecting criticism;
  items 8–10).**
  Vienna General Hospital, **1847**: Semmelweis found that doctors coming from the dissection room to the
  maternity ward carried something deadly; chlorinated-lime handwashing dropped maternal mortality on his
  ward dramatically (roughly **~13%** to **~2%** — the figures are real but vary by ward/period;
  **[verify the exact figures]** before stating them precisely). Colleagues, offended, **rejected** it;
  he published late, was committed to an asylum, and **died there in 1865**, aged 47. *Points true:* a
  *correct* conjecture rejected because the community would not subject itself to criticism — entrenched
  error, the failure mode the epistemology diagnoses. **`[verify it points true to the right module]`:**
  this story illustrates *error-correction delayed by social resistance*, not the is/ought move; slot it
  with the fallibilism module, not the decisive-move module.

- **Continental drift / Wegener, or the H. pylori ulcer reversal (→ objectivity as error-correction, not
  certainty; fallibilism in real science; items 10, 14 — a scientific parallel for the moral case).**
  *Option A — H. pylori (cleanest, best-dated):* **Robin Warren** and **Barry Marshall** (Perth) proposed
  that a bacterium, not stress/acid, caused most peptic ulcers; first cultured it in **1982**; in **1984**
  Marshall **drank a culture of *H. pylori*** to demonstrate causation, fell ill, then treated it; the
  medical establishment resisted, then accepted; they received the **2005 Nobel Prize in Physiology or
  Medicine**. *Points true:* a "settled truth" (ulcers = stress) overturned by conjecture, test, and
  error-correction — the exact shape the course claims for moral knowledge. *Option B — continental
  drift:* Wegener's drift hypothesis (proposed ~1912) long rejected, vindicated decades later by
  plate-tectonics evidence — same lesson, fuzzier dates; prefer A.

- **Deutsch's flowers argument (→ the aesthetics parallel; objective beauty; item 20).**
  In *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011), chapter **"Why Are Flowers Beautiful?"**, Deutsch argues that
  humans reliably find flowers beautiful despite flowers having evolved their signal to attract **insects
  of species with which humans share no relevant prior "knowledge."** To signal *across that gap* with a
  hard-to-fake, hard-to-emulate pattern, the cleanest solution is an **objective** standard of beauty —
  suggesting aesthetic criteria as objective as those of math or physics. *Points true:* objectivity
  without a foundation, in a domain ("beauty") people are *even more* sure is "just taste" than morality —
  a reinforcing analogy. This is **Deutsch's** argument; attribute it to him by name and book.

  - *Hume's life-and-text beat* (→ the is/ought gap, item 3): the is/ought passage is in **Book III, Part
    I, Section I** of *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40). Safe to cite the location; **[verify any

  - *Mackie's opening line* (→ error theory, item 6): *Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong* (**1977**) opens

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

### Learner-facing (teach against these)
- **Conflating Hume and Moore.** The is/ought gap (Hume: an *inference* fails) and the naturalistic
  fallacy (Moore: a *definition* fails) are different objections. They often get fused into a vague "you
  can't get morality from facts." Teach them apart.
- **Conflating emotivism, relativism, and error theory.** Ayer says moral claims *state nothing* (no
  truth-value); Mackie says they *state something but it's all false*; the relativist says they're *true,
  but only relative to a culture*. These are three different views with three different replies. The
  course's clarity depends on keeping them distinct.
- **Hearing "no foundation" as "anything goes."** The whole point of the decisive move is that *lack of
  a foundation is universal* (science has none either) and yet science is objective. "No foundation" ≠
  "no objectivity." This is the single most important misconception to dismantle.
- **Mistaking fallibilism for relativism or skepticism.** "All knowledge is corrigible" is *not* "no
  knowledge is better than any other." Fallibilism is *compatible with* — indeed grounds — objective
  progress. Learners routinely slide from "we could be wrong" to "it's all just opinion."
- **Thinking objectivity requires certainty/proof.** The course's redefinition (objectivity =
  error-correctability, not certainty) is counterintuitive; readers arrive equating "objective" with
  "proven." Surface and replace this explicitly.
- **Reading moral progress as mere change.** That codes *changed* is trivial; that some changes were
  *corrections* is the claim. Do not let "progress" smuggle in the conclusion — argue it (the
  asymmetry: we say abolition *improved* things; relativism cannot license "improved").
- **Treating "hard to vary" as "simple" or "elegant" or "unfalsifiable-but-nice."** Hard-to-vary is a
  precise notion: the explanation's parts are *locked together* by their explanatory work. A vague or
  endlessly adjustable story (the seasonal myth) is *easy* to vary and explains nothing.

### Author-integrity (defects in the *making* of the course)
- **Strawmanning the objection.** The cardinal sin here. If relativism, Mackie, or Ayer is stated weakly,
  the whole course is dishonest and the reply is worthless. Steelman first, every time.
- **Blending attributions.** Crediting Popper with "hard to vary," or Deutsch with the open society or
  the dissolution of induction, is a factual error the brief specifically forbids. Police every "as X
  showed."
- **Overclaiming the conclusion.** Writing as if the argument *proves* moral realism. It does not. The
  honest line is *epistemic parity + real progress*; full realism is contested. Any sentence that says or
  implies "this settles metaethics" is a defect.
- **Skipping or softening the honest close.** The "what remains contested" module (anti-realism /
  quasi-realism can accept all this; the disputed moral analog of *test*; companions-in-guilt; moral
  disagreement) is **required**, not optional, and must crown no winner.
- **Fabrication in closing stories.** Invented quotes, dates, numbers, scenes. The highest-risk failure;
  the verify stage reads every story for a smuggled-in fake. Stay inside the attested record.
- **Importing religious or authority grounding by the back door** (e.g., over-leaning on Newton's
  conversion as the *reason* abolition was right). The view grounds morality in error-correction, not
  authority; keep the framing consistent.
- **Em dashes / directive metadiscourse / "in this module we will."** Banned by the brief and house
  style; enact the disciplines, do not announce them.

## Open questions for the curriculum stage

The brief specifically asks three of these; they are first.

1. **How much metaethics scaffolding to include.** The five objections (relativism, Hume, Moore, Ayer,
   Mackie) plus the modern debunking arguments are a lot of apparatus for a non-philosopher. *Options:*
   (a) one dense "the challenge" module that steelmans all of them in turn; (b) two modules splitting
   "morality looks like opinion" (relativism, emotivism) from "the technical objections" (is/ought,
   naturalistic fallacy, queerness/error theory); (c) introduce each objection *just-in-time* paired with
   its answer. Recommendation leans (b) — give the objections room to land at full strength before any
   reply, which the steelman discipline demands — but the curriculum stage should decide whether the
   evolutionary-debunking arguments earn inclusion at all (they sharpen the modern debate but add load).

2. **Whether the aesthetics parallel earns its own module.** The flowers argument is vivid and reinforces
   "objectivity without foundations" in a domain readers find *even more* subjective than morality — a
   strong consolidating beat. But it is a *parallel*, not a load-bearing link in the moral argument, and a
   ~10-module budget is tight. *Options:* (a) full module (item 20) late in the arc as reinforcement
   before the close; (b) a short section folded into the "objectivity without foundations" module; (c)
   cut to a closing-story-only appearance. Recommendation: keep it as its own (possibly short) module —
   it is one of the course's most memorable arguments and pays off the thesis emotionally — but mark it
   *optional/compressible* if the count runs long.

3. **How to place the honest "what remains contested" close.** It must be a real module, not a paragraph,
   and must crown no winner (realism vs. anti-realism / quasi-realism; companions-in-guilt; moral
   disagreement; the disputed moral analog of empirical *test*). *Question:* should it be the **final**
   module (end on honesty + the error-correcting method as takeaway — recommended), or split into "what
   we established" (penultimate, affirmative) and "what's still open" (final)? Recommendation: a single
   final module that does both, so the course ends on intellectual honesty without deflating the case.

4. **Module count and the slavery/open-society overlap.** Items 16–18 (moral progress, the failure of
   relativism, the open society) are tightly linked and could be 2 or 3 modules. Decide whether "the open
   society" is its own module (it carries the best Popper-life story and the political payoff) or folds
   into "moral progress."

5. **Where to put the decisive move (item 11).** It is the hinge. It needs the full induction setup
   (item 10) *immediately* before it, and the reader must feel the is/ought gap (item 3) as a real threat
   first. Confirm the ordering puts 10 and 11 back-to-back, with 3 clearly recalled at 11.

6. **How heavily to lean on World 3 (item).** It is Popper's most abstract idea and not strictly required
   for the moral argument. Recommendation: gloss it briefly where "objective knowledge" needs grounding,
   but do not build a module on it; the course's "objective" is carried by error-correction (14), not by
   World 3 ontology.

7. **Voice level and canon compatibility.** Confirm the plain-language glosses for *conjecture /
   criticism / hard to vary / reach* (above) and that the course will *not* import the formal symbols from
   the `conjecture-criticism-calculus` canon. The curriculum stage should emit this course's own
   `canon.md` fixing names (Hume/Moore/Mackie/Ayer/Popper/Deutsch ownership), the phrase caps (the brief
   bans em dashes and directive metadiscourse), and the metaphor owners (axial-tilt for hard-to-vary;
   flowers for objective beauty; ballot-box for the open society) so the blind-parallel authors do not
   blend attributions.

## Recommended shape (input to curriculum, not binding)

A ~10-module arc fitting the dependency chain and the brief's stated arc:

1. The gap between what we say and do about morality (objective vs. "just opinion"; the thesis stated).
2. The challenge, part I: relativism and emotivism (Ayer) — morality looks like opinion. *(steelman)*
3. The challenge, part II: the is/ought gap (Hume), the naturalistic fallacy (Moore), queerness and error
   theory (Mackie). *(steelman; possibly two modules)*
4. Fallibilism and the death of foundations (Popper: no justified true belief; knowledge is criticized,
   not justified).
5. The problem of induction and its dissolution (Popper): all knowledge grows by conjecture and
   criticism.
6. **The decisive move (Deutsch):** no special is/ought problem — morality is on science's footing.
7. Objectivity without foundations: good explanations, hard to vary, reach (Deutsch).
8. Moral progress is real; relativism is self-undermining (abolition as error-correction).
9. Knowledge and the good: universal explainers, the open society (Popper), optimism, evil as entrenched
   error (Deutsch).
10. The aesthetics parallel (flowers) *or* fold it; then **the honest close** (established vs. contested;
    the error-correcting method as takeaway).

This is one defensible slicing; the curriculum stage owns the final cut (see open questions 1, 2, 3, 4).
