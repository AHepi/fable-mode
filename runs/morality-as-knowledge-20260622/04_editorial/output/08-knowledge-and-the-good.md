## The hook

A government loses an election and packs up its offices. The losing prime minister gives a short speech, shakes hands with the winner, and goes home. No tanks roll. No one is shot. A few months of bad polling and a quiet Tuesday at the ballot box have done what, across most of human history, required an army, an assassination, or a generation of suffering: they have removed the people in charge and replaced them with others, and the change cost no one their life.

It is so ordinary that it is almost invisible, which is exactly why it is worth stopping on. Most societies that have ever existed could not do this. A bad king stayed a bad king until he died, or until someone killed him, and killing him usually meant killing a great many other people first. The mechanism that ends a government with a vote instead of a war is one of the strangest and most valuable things our species has built, and it is built out of the same idea this course has been tracing all along. A vote that ends a government is a society correcting one of its own mistakes without having to destroy itself to do it.

## Context & background

Recall where the argument now stands. Objective, in the course's sense, means answerable to more than opinion, able to be got wrong and improved. Popper's point was that science has no foundation either and grows by conjecture and criticism, the guess offered against a problem and the search for what is wrong with it. Deutsch took a good explanation to be one that is hard to vary, its parts locked together so none can be swapped without wrecking it, the kind module 06 made central. The thread running through all of it is error-correction. Knowledge of any kind, scientific or moral, is objective insofar as it can be exposed to criticism and put right.

This module follows that thread out of the study and into the street. If error-correction is what makes knowledge grow, then the arrangements that *permit* error-correction matter enormously, and the arrangements that *forbid* it are a kind of catastrophe. That thought has two authors, and they contributed different things to it.

The political half belongs to **Karl Popper**, the twentieth-century philosopher of science whose fallibilism underlies this whole course. The half that links the growth of knowledge to the good itself, and that pushes the idea to its boldest and most disputable edge, belongs to **David Deutsch**, the physicist and philosopher who built his moral and aesthetic views on Popper's foundation. Deutsch builds on Popper here as he did with the no-special-problem move and with hard-to-vary. The open society is Popper's. The optimism and the explicit tie from knowledge to goodness are Deutsch's. Crediting either man with the other's idea would blur the very seam this module exists to draw.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with Popper, and with a book written in flight.

In 1945 Popper published *The Open Society and Its Enemies*. Its target was the long philosophical tradition, running from Plato through Hegel to Marx, that imagines the good society as a fixed and final pattern, a blueprint that wise rulers impose and that the rest of us are then to live inside without further question. Against that, Popper set what he called the **open society**: a society whose institutions let it correct its mistakes, including the mistake of its own rulers, without violence. The deep idea is just fallibilism carried into politics. No ruler, no party, no constitution, no policy is beyond error, because nothing human is. The question that matters about a political system is therefore not the one most political philosophy had asked, *who should rule?*, the search for the wisest hands to hold permanent power. The question that matters is: *how can we arrange things so that bad rulers and bad policies can be removed before they do too much harm, and removed peacefully?*

That reframing changes everything. Once you stop looking for rulers wise enough that they deserve unchecked power, and start looking for institutions that survive bad rulers, the ballot box stops being a mere counting device and becomes something closer to an experiment. A government is a conjecture about how to run things. An election is the society's standing chance to refute it. When a party is voted out, a policy has been criticized and a correction made, and the whole thing has happened without a single death. This is conjecture and refutation in political form, the same shape of move that retires a failed scientific theory, now retiring a failed government. The closed society, by contrast, is one that entrenches its arrangements and treats them as beyond question, so that its only mechanisms for change are concealment, suppression, and eventually blood.

Now Deutsch takes the baton. He starts from a claim about what people *are*. A person, in his vocabulary, is a **universal explainer**, an entity that can create explanatory knowledge without any built-in limit on what it can come to understand. There is no problem stamped in advance as forever beyond us; whatever is permitted by the laws of physics is, given the right knowledge, achievable. That is where Deutsch locates the moral weight of a person. Persons are not valuable because they are large, or numerous, or felt to be special. They are valuable because each one is a site where unbounded knowledge-creation can happen, and knowledge-creation is the thing on which every future good depends.

From this Deutsch draws his optimism, and he states it in four words worth quoting exactly.

> Problems are inevitable. Problems are soluble.
>
> David Deutsch, *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011)

Both halves carry weight. Problems are inevitable because knowledge is never complete; every solution we reach uncovers new problems, and there is no final state of having figured everything out. Problems are soluble because there is no problem that the growth of knowledge cannot in principle reach, no barrier marked permanent in advance. Put the two together and you get a stance, not a prediction. Deutsch is not promising that things will go well. He is saying that whether they go well depends on whether we keep creating knowledge, and that nothing in the structure of reality forbids us from doing so.

This is where the good enters. For Deutsch, the good is bound up with sustaining the conditions for unbounded error-correction, and the bad is their destruction. An act, an institution, a culture is to be weighed by whether it keeps open the means of finding and fixing errors or shuts them down. A closed society does not merely make bad decisions; it disables the machinery for ever noticing they were bad. That is why Popper's open society and Deutsch's optimism are one continuous thought across two thinkers. The open society is the political form of keeping error-correction alive, and on Deutsch's view keeping error-correction alive is close to the whole of what the good asks of our institutions.

## Primary source

Popper put the political point with unusual directness, and the passage is worth reading in his own words, because the whole open society turns on the substitution it proposes.

> The question "Who should rule?" begs for an authoritarian answer such as "the best", or "the wisest", or "the people", or "the majority"... It is better to be prepared for the worst leaders, and to hope for the best. But it seems to me madness to base all our political efforts upon the faint hope that we shall be successful in obtaining excellent, or even competent, rulers.
>
> Karl Popper, *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945), restating the central argument in his later essay "The Open Society and Its Enemies Revisited"

Read what Popper is refusing. He is not offering a better answer to "who should rule?" He is rejecting the question. Every answer to it, even the democratic-sounding "the people" or "the majority", smuggles in the same hope: that if we just find the right hands, we can hand over power and stop worrying. Popper's claim is that this hope is the trap, because no hands are safe to trust with power that cannot be taken back. The sane design assumes the worst leaders will sometimes get in, and builds the means to remove them before they do too much damage. The genius of the ballot is not that it selects the wise. It is that it lets a society fire the unwise without a war. Error-correction, not the search for an infallible authority, is the thing political institutions are for.

## Analysis & interpretation

The argument so far has been one Deutsch and Popper share, and it is strong. Now comes the part that is Deutsch's alone, and that careful readers should weigh rather than swallow.

Deutsch states what he calls the Principle of Optimism, and its sharpest formulation is this: **all evils are due to insufficient knowledge.** The claim is that every evil in the world is, at bottom, a problem of missing knowledge, and therefore soluble in principle by the growth of knowledge. Famine, disease, tyranny, cruelty: name an evil, and Deutsch will say that what stands between us and its end is something we do not yet know how to do, some knowledge not yet created. There is no evil written into the nature of things as permanent.

Take the claim at its strongest first, because a great deal of it is true and bracing. Many of the horrors that earlier ages treated as fixed features of the human lot turned out to be soluble problems in exactly Deutsch's sense. Smallpox killed for millennia and was thought a scourge to be endured; it was a knowledge problem, and the knowledge was created, and now it is gone from the wild. Famine was long read as nature's verdict on overbreeding; much of it turned out to be a problem of agriculture, transport, and political arrangement, soluble and increasingly solved. Even tyranny, the oldest political evil, yields to the open society, which is itself a piece of created knowledge about how to live together. When you survey how much of what was once called fate has dissolved under the growth of knowledge, the claim that evils are problems waiting on knowledge stops sounding naive and starts sounding like a hard-won correction of an ancient pessimism. It refuses to write off any suffering as simply how things must be, and that refusal has, again and again, been vindicated.

The claim is also genuinely contestable, and the course does not present it as settled. The pressure comes at the word *all*. Some evil does not look like a shortage of knowledge at all. A torturer who understands his victim's pain perfectly well, and inflicts it precisely because he understands it, is not suffering from insufficient knowledge; he seems to have a surplus of the relevant knowledge and a will to do harm with it. Cruelty done with open eyes, malice that knows exactly what it is doing, the deliberate choice of the worse over the better: critics ask whether these really reduce to ignorance, or whether reducing them to ignorance explains away the will to evil instead of confronting it. Deutsch can reply that the torturer's culture, his upbringing, the institutions that formed and licensed him, are themselves failures of knowledge about how to raise and govern people, so that the knowledge-deficit lives one level up. That reply has force. But it also stretches "insufficient knowledge" wide enough that some critics doubt the claim still says anything falsifiable, and others feel that something real about evil, its chosenness, its embrace of harm as harm, has been dissolved rather than answered. This is Deutsch's view, argued and attractive and worth taking seriously. It is not a result the course can hand you as established. Hold the open society and the optimism firmly, which the argument earns, and hold the "all evils" claim as the bold conjecture it is, open like everything else to the criticism it invites.

## The story

In the southern winter of 1937 a thirty-five-year-old philosopher of Jewish descent boarded a ship out of a Europe that was closing around people like him. Karl Popper had grown up in Vienna; the Austria he knew was months from vanishing into Hitler's Reich, and in March 1938 the Anschluss would erase it, dissolving the country into Nazi Germany without a shot fired and without a vote cast. Popper did not wait to watch it happen from inside. He had taken a lectureship at Canterbury University College in Christchurch, New Zealand, about as far from Vienna as the inhabited world allows, and it was there, at the bottom of the Pacific, that he sat out the Second World War.

He did not sit it out idly. Through the war years in Christchurch, Popper wrote *The Open Society and Its Enemies*, the book that gave the idea this module is built on its name and its argument. He worked on it, by his own account, as his contribution to a fight he could not join with a rifle. He later called the book his "war effort." The phrase repays a moment's weight. A man who had fled a regime now had no army to join and no front to reach. What he had was an argument, and he aimed it at the deepest enemy of the open society, the old and seductive idea that the good society is a fixed pattern to be imposed by those who know best and then defended against all question. That idea, Popper argued, was the philosophical root of the very tyrannies then burning across the world.

The setting cashes out the lesson exactly. Popper was writing about institutions that let a society correct its errors without violence, and he was writing in forced exile from a country that had just demonstrated the alternative. The Anschluss was a regime entrenching its arrangements and placing them beyond question, abolishing in a stroke the machinery by which a people might have removed it peacefully. Across the same years, the open societies that still functioned were, for all their flaws, the ones that could still change their governments by counting votes rather than by counting the dead. Popper had watched a closed society devour an open one, and from twelve thousand miles away he set down, in careful prose, the case for why the capacity to remove a ruler without bloodshed is not a procedural nicety but something close to the political form of the good itself. The book appeared in 1945, the year the regimes that had taught him the lesson fell. Its argument outlived them, which is the most an open society ever asks: not to be right forever, but to stay correctable, putting its errors right as it goes.
