## The hook

Spend an evening reading what human beings have been sure was wrong, and the certainty starts to look like the problem rather than the answer.

A practice that one society treats as a sacred duty, the next treats as an atrocity, and each is as confident as the other. Eating the bodies of your dead parents has been, in some places, the highest honor a child could pay; in others, an obscenity beyond words. Marrying your cousin is unremarkable across much of the world and a scandal across the rest. Whole peoples have held that killing an unwanted infant was permissible, even responsible, and whole peoples have held it to be murder. The list runs long, and it does not get more comfortable the further down it you read. What it does not contain is a single moral question that every culture in history has answered the same way.

The reader who began the last module thinking morality might be objective now has something hard to swallow. If "slavery is wrong" is answerable to a fact beyond opinion, the way the orbit of the Earth is, then the disagreement ought to behave the way scientific disagreement behaves: temporary, resolvable, converging as people get a better look. Moral disagreement does not behave that way. It is old, deep, and shows no sign of closing. The two columns from the last module were supposed to have a fact-shaped answer waiting in the world for "slavery is wrong." Look at the human record and the cupboard starts to seem bare. Maybe the reason no one can point to the moral fact is that there is none to point at.

This module makes that case, and makes it as strongly as it can be made. No reply comes here. The reply comes much later, and it is worth nothing unless the objection it answers has first been stated in its full strength, in its own terms, with the pull it really has. So the job now is to build the other side a fortress, not a straw man.

## Context & background

There are two distinct ways to take the lesson of moral disagreement, and they are not the same view wearing two coats. Keeping them apart is the whole work of this module, because they say different things, lean on different evidence, and would have to be answered in different ways.

The first is **moral relativism**: the view that a moral claim is true only relative to a culture or a person, with no fact of the matter standing above them all. On this view "eating your dead is wrong" is not true full stop and not false full stop. It is true relative to the Greeks and false relative to the people who practiced it, and there is no further, culture-independent court in which the two verdicts could be tried against each other. Each morality is true within its own world. There is no view from nowhere, no neutral ground, from which one of them could be ranked above the other.

The second is **emotivism**, the view of the British philosopher **A. J. Ayer**, who set it out in 1936 in a short, combative book called *Language, Truth and Logic*. Emotivism says something sharper and stranger than relativism. It says moral sentences do not state facts at all, not even local ones. When you say "cruelty is wrong," you are not reporting a fact about cruelty, and you are not reporting a fact about your culture either. You are venting an attitude. You are doing in words roughly what a wince or a cheer does without them. On this view "cruelty is wrong" is closer to "cruelty, boo" than to any statement that could be true or false.

The difference is exact and it matters. Relativism keeps moral claims in the business of being true and false; it just makes the truth local, indexed to a culture the way "it is winter" is indexed to a hemisphere. Emotivism takes moral claims out of the truth business altogether. For the relativist, "slavery is wrong" was true-for-the-abolitionists and false-for-the-slaveholders, two real if local truths. For the emotivist, "slavery is wrong" was never the kind of sentence that could be true for anyone, because it never stated anything; it expressed a feeling, and feelings are not true or false. One view localizes truth. The other dissolves it. They are allies against the idea of objective morality, and enemies of each other about almost everything else.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with relativism, and start with its strongest form, because the cartoon version is easy to wave away and the cartoon version is not what a serious relativist holds.

The cartoon relativist says "who's to say" and means "anything goes," and contradicts himself in the next breath by judging the people who disagree with him. Set that figure aside. The serious relativist is making a claim about where moral facts could possibly come from, and the claim has real force. Consider what it would take for "eating your dead is wrong" to be objectively true, true independently of any human attitude or practice. There would have to be a standard of right conduct woven into the world itself, the way the laws of motion are, a standard that held whether or not any culture ever recognized it, and that the Greeks were reading correctly while the people across the river were reading wrongly. Now look at how moral standards actually arise. A child does not discover the wrongness of eating the dead by investigation, the way a child can be brought to discover that the Earth is round. The child absorbs it, from a particular family in a particular place, along with the language and the table manners and the gods. Moral conviction comes packaged with the culture that delivers it, and it comes in flatly contradictory versions to children a few hundred miles apart, each of whom grows up just as certain as the other.

The relativist asks the natural question. If moral conviction is acquired exactly the way the local customs are acquired, varies exactly the way the local customs vary, and has never, across the whole sweep of human history, converged into a single shared answer the way claims about the physical world eventually do, then what reason is there to believe it is tracking anything beyond the local custom? The simplest explanation of a morality is the culture that produced it. You do not need to posit a realm of moral facts to explain why the Greeks were horrified at eating the dead, any more than you need a realm of culinary facts to explain why they ate what they ate. The horror is fully accounted for by their upbringing. The moral fact would be doing no explanatory work; it would be a fifth wheel. And the persistence of the disagreement is not a temporary fog that better inquiry will burn off. It is what you would expect if there were nothing for the inquiry to converge on.

This is not skepticism for its own sake. It is an inference to the best explanation, and the explanation it lands on is economical and complete: moralities are internal to cultures, true within them and answerable to nothing outside them, because there is no outside for them to be answerable to. The diversity is not a curiosity at the edge of the picture. It is the central datum, and relativism is the theory that fits it without remainder.

Now Ayer, and a different and in some ways deeper cut. Ayer was not impressed by relativism's local truths; he thought the whole idea that moral sentences state facts, local or otherwise, was a confusion to begin with. His route in was a theory of meaning. A sentence states a genuine fact, on his view, only if there is some possible observation that would count for or against it; that is what it is for a sentence to have factual content. "There is a fire in the next room" qualifies, because you could go and look. Test "stealing is wrong" the same way. Imagine you have agreed on every observable fact about a particular theft: who took the money, from whom, in what circumstances, with what consequences for everyone involved. Two people can share every one of those facts and still disagree about whether the act was wrong. The disagreement does not turn on any further observation, because there is no further observation it could turn on. From which Ayer draws his conclusion: the word "wrong" is not adding any factual content to the description of the theft. It is adding something, but not a fact.

What it adds, he says, is feeling. To call the theft wrong is to express your disapproval of it, in roughly the way a tone of voice or a curled lip expresses disapproval, except in words. And here is the move that makes emotivism sharper than relativism and harder to answer. A genuine expression of feeling is not the kind of thing that can be true or false. If you wince when you stub your toe, the wince is not *true*; the question does not arise. Ayer's claim is that "cruelty is wrong" is in the same boat. It is a sophisticated wince. It can be sincere or insincere, it can move other people or fail to, but it cannot be correct or mistaken, because it never made a claim about the world that the world could answer. There is nothing there to be objective about, not because the moral facts are hidden or hard to reach, but because the sentences were never in the business of stating facts in the first place.

There is a first reaction most people have when they hear that morality is subjective, and Ayer's view turns it inside out. They want to say: but surely it really moves us, surely "cruelty is wrong" is more than a shrug, it has force, it changes what people do. Ayer grants every word of that, and turns the agreement against them. Of course it has force. That is exactly his point. Moral language carries feeling and moves people to act in a way that flat factual description does not, and the reason it does is that expressing and arousing feeling is the whole of what it is doing. The very thing that seemed to show morality was more than mere opinion, its grip, its heat, the way it stirs people, is on Ayer's account the signature of its not being a statement of fact at all. Plain factual description does not grip anyone that way because plain factual description is doing a different job. The grip is the giveaway.

So the reader who came in holding "slavery is wrong" in the uneasy third place now faces two ways of cashing out the unease, and both of them empty the third column rather than filling it. The relativist says: it was true, but only for the people who said it, and the slaveholders' contrary was just as true for them, and no further fact ranks the two. Ayer says: it was never true or false at all; it was the abolitionists evincing their horror with great power, and the slaveholders evincing theirs, and "true" was always the wrong word for what either side was doing. Neither view is the lazy "it's all relative" of a dorm-room argument. Each is a worked-out account of why the moral fact you keep reaching for is not there to be found.

## Primary source

Ayer puts the emotivist thesis in a single, much-quoted passage. He has just argued that an ethical word adds no factual content to a sentence; here is what he says it adds instead.

> The presence of an ethical symbol in a proposition adds nothing to its factual content. Thus if I say to someone, "You acted wrongly in stealing that money," I am not stating anything more than if I had simply said, "You stole that money." In adding that this action is wrong I am not making any further statement about it. I am simply evincing my moral disapproval of it. It is as if I had said, "You stole that money," in a peculiar tone of horror, or written it with the addition of some special exclamation marks.
>
> A. J. Ayer, *Language, Truth and Logic* (1936)

Read the last sentence with care, because the whole view is folded into it. The tone of horror and the exclamation marks are not decoration laid over a moral claim. They are, for Ayer, the entire moral content. Strip the tone and the marks away and what is left, "you stole that money," is a plain factual report with no ethics in it at all. The ethics lived wholly in the horror. And horror, however deep and however shared, is not true or false. A man may be horrified by what is in fact harmless and unmoved by what is in fact monstrous, but his horror is not thereby *mistaken* in the way a false belief is mistaken; it is just his horror. That is why, on this view, two people who agree on every fact about the theft can still "disagree" morally, and why their disagreement can never be settled by finding out more. They are not in fact contradicting each other. They are expressing opposed feelings, the way one person can love a piece of music another cannot stand, with no fact about the music that either is getting wrong.

## Analysis & interpretation

It would be a mistake to read these two views as making the same complaint in different words, and the mistake is worth heading off, because nearly everyone makes it. They are different complaints, and they leave the defender of objective morality with different problems.

The relativist grants that moral claims can be true. That is a real concession, and it is what separates relativism from Ayer. The relativist will happily say "slavery is wrong" is true, and mean it; he only insists that the truth is indexed, true-relative-to-a-culture, the way "it is past noon" is true relative to a place on the Earth. The pressure relativism puts on objective morality is the pressure of the diversity: across cultures there is no agreed answer and, the relativist argues, no neutral standpoint from which one local answer could be crowned correct over the others. So the question relativism forces is: is there any such standpoint, anything a moral claim is answerable to beyond the culture that holds it?

Ayer does not even reach that question, because he has cut the wire earlier. For Ayer there is no moral truth to be relative *to*. He is not saying the truth is local; he is saying the sentences never trafficked in truth. The pressure emotivism puts on objective morality is therefore not about diversity at all. Ayer's argument would go through with exactly the same force in a world where every human being agreed about every moral question, because his point was never that we disagree. His point was that moral sentences fail a test for stating facts, so they state none, so they are neither true nor false, and unanimous feeling would be no more *true* than divided feeling. The diversity is the relativist's lever. The theory of meaning is Ayer's. Confuse the two and you will try to answer Ayer by explaining the disagreement, which leaves him untouched, or try to answer the relativist by analyzing the meaning of moral words, which leaves the diversity exactly where it was.

There is a second contrast worth keeping straight, between Ayer and the relativist over the word "true." Press a relativist with a hard case, the burning of a living widow, the keeping of slaves, and he can say, with a straight face, "that was genuinely wrong, for them and by their lights and ours; truth in morals is just always local." He keeps the vocabulary of right and wrong and pays for it with the loss of any cross-cultural fact. Press Ayer with the same case and he cannot say "that was genuinely wrong" while meaning anything fact-stating by it; the most his theory lets him mean is "that, and how I shudder at it." He keeps the shudder, in its full intensity, and pays for it with the word "true." Two prices, two different bills. Both are charged to anyone who wants to go on believing that morality is objective, and this module's whole purpose is to make sure the reader feels the weight of each before any attempt is made, much later, to refuse to pay.

The relativist denies that the sentence is *objectively* true while granting that it can be true *relative to a culture or speaker*: there is no cross-cultural, standpoint-independent fact that settles it, but within a given morality it has a truth-value, true here, perhaps false elsewhere, with no neutral court above the two. The emotivist (Ayer) denies that the sentence has any truth-value at all, locally or otherwise. On his view it states no fact; it expresses the speaker's disapproval, a sophisticated equivalent of saying "torturing prisoners for amusement" in a tone of horror, and an expression of feeling is neither true nor false. So the relativist localizes truth; the emotivist removes truth-value altogether. The relativist's pressure comes from moral *diversity*; Ayer's comes from a *theory of meaning* and would stand even if everyone agreed.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Build the fortress.** Take one moral conviction you hold without hesitation. Write the strongest case a relativist could make that your conviction is true only relative to your culture, using the real fact that other cultures have held the opposite with equal confidence. Do not refute it. Then say which step in your own fortress you find it hardest to believe, and why, without yet answering it.

2. **Run Ayer's test.** Pick a moral claim and a factual claim. For the factual one, name an observation that would count for or against it. Try to do the same for the moral one. Report honestly what happens when you try, and say whether you think the difference Ayer points to is real or whether something has been smuggled into the test.

3. **The price of each view.** Relativism keeps the word "true" and gives up any cross-cultural moral fact. Emotivism keeps the full force of moral feeling and gives up the word "true." State which trade you find more painful and defend the choice, then say what someone who made the opposite trade would accuse you of giving up too cheaply.

4. **Where the grip comes from.** Ayer treats the motivating force of moral language, the way "cruelty is wrong" moves us as a flat fact never could, as evidence that it expresses feeling rather than stating a fact. Argue either that this is the right diagnosis of moral force or that moral force could come from something other than feeling. State the rival account precisely.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Keeps relativism and emotivism genuinely distinct: relativism allows local truth and is driven by moral diversity; emotivism denies truth-value altogether and is driven by a theory of meaning. A response that blurs the two into "it's all subjective" has missed the module's central distinction.
- States the chosen view in its strongest, most sympathetic form, in its own terms, before raising any doubt, and can tell steelmanning apart from setting up a weak version to knock down.
- Attributes accurately: emotivism is Ayer's specific view (moral sentences express attitude, state no fact); relativism is a position, not one author's, leaning on cross-cultural disagreement.
- Does not reach for the course's eventual reply. This module raises the objection at full strength and answers none of it; a strong response respects that and resists the urge to rebut.
- Grounds claims in the actual content of each view, the local-truth structure of relativism, Ayer's meaning test and his "tone of horror" gloss, rather than in a vague sense that morality "feels" subjective.

## The story

Some two and a half thousand years before anyone wrote the word "relativism," a Greek historian set the whole problem down in a single scene, and he set it down so cleanly that philosophers have been borrowing it ever since.

Herodotus, writing his *Histories* in the fifth century BC, is describing the reach and the strangeness of the Persian empire under its kings, and in the thirty-eighth chapter of his third book he tells of an experiment, real or imagined, that Darius the Great is said to have run at his court. Darius first called together some Greeks who were with him and asked them what price would persuade them to eat the bodies of their dead fathers. The Greeks were appalled. There was no sum, they said, that would make them do such a thing; they burned their dead, and the very suggestion was an outrage. Then, with the Greeks standing there and following the exchange through interpreters, Darius called in some men of a people Herodotus names the Callatiae, who as a matter of custom ate their dead parents, and asked them what would induce them to burn their fathers' bodies instead. The Callatiae cried out in horror and begged him not to speak of so dreadful a thing.

Set the two reactions side by side and the relativist's central datum is standing right there in the record, fully formed. Two peoples, each entirely certain, each treating its own funeral custom as the only decent way to honor the dead and the other's as an abomination. Neither is confused about the facts; each understands exactly what the other does. They simply make opposite verdicts, with equal conviction, and there is no third party in the scene with any standing to correct either one. Darius himself does not rule between them. He runs the comparison and lets it stand. What the scene shows is not that one side is right and the other benighted, but that each side's certainty is fully explained by the world it grew up in, and that the certainties point in opposite directions with nothing between them to break the tie.

Herodotus draws the conclusion himself, and he reaches for a line of poetry to do it, crediting the poet Pindar with the verdict that custom is king of all. *Nomos*, the Greek word, means custom, usage, law, the settled way a people does things, and Herodotus says it is king, sovereign over everyone, the thing that finally decides what each of us finds holy and what we find unbearable. The man who first wrote down this scene was no relativist philosopher; he was a traveler with an unusually steady eye, reporting what he saw of how widely and how deeply human practice differs and how wholly each people is gripped by its own. That datum, the bare, well-attested fact that custom runs this deep and points this many different ways, is the ground the relativist still stands on. It was ancient when Herodotus recorded it, and the centuries since have only lengthened the catalog. The slogans that morality is just culture, just opinion, just the king named custom dressed in the language of eternal truth, are slogans. The fact underneath them is not.
