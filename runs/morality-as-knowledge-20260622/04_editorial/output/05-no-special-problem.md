## The hook

There is a wall standing between facts and values, and everyone who has looked at it agrees it is real. On one side are the things that *are*: the planet warms, the prisoner suffers, the child goes hungry. On the other side are the things that *ought to be*: that we should cool the planet, free the prisoner, feed the child. Hume found that you cannot climb from the first side to the second by logic alone. No pile of facts, however high, entails a single "ought." The wall has a hole in it where the path from is to ought should run, and the hole has no bottom. That is why morality looks like it has no foundation. Push on any moral claim, ask what it finally rests on, and your hand goes through the gap.

Now look at the other building, the one everyone agrees *does* rest on something solid. Walk up to the wall of science, to the place where observations are supposed to support a theory, and put your hand against it. Your hand goes through. There is a hole there too, the same shape, in the same place. No pile of observations, however high, entails a single general law. The path from what we have seen to what is always and everywhere true has no bottom either. The two walls have the same hole. The question this module turns on is what follows once you see that.

## Context & background

Two results from earlier in the course meet here, and the whole move depends on holding both of them clearly at once.

The first is Hume's, from module 03. The eighteenth-century Scottish philosopher David Hume noticed that arguments about morality slide, without warning, from sentences about what *is* the case to sentences about what *ought* to be, as though the second kind followed from the first. It does not. From "this action causes suffering" you cannot validly deduce "you ought not to do it" unless you have already smuggled in some "ought," some premise that suffering should not be caused. Pure description never yields prescription on its own. This is the **is/ought gap**: the point that you cannot validly infer a conclusion about what ought to be from premises only about what is. It is the sharpest reason anyone has ever given for thinking morality cannot be grounded the way a proof is grounded. Whatever you point to as the bedrock fact beneath "slavery is wrong," the gap reopens beneath that fact. There is no first "ought" that some plain "is" hands you for free.

The second result is Popper's, from module 04. The twentieth-century philosopher of science Karl Popper sharpened a problem David Hume had also seen, in a different building. Call it the **problem of induction**: no number of observations can prove a general theory, the way no number of white swans proves that all swans are white. You can check a thousand swans, a million, every swan in Europe, and the next one may still be black. The inference from "every observed X has been Y" to "every X whatever is Y" is not valid. It never was. Popper's response was not to repair the inference but to abandon the demand for it. We do not derive our theories from observations and never did. We *guess* them, boldly, as conjectures aimed at solving a problem, and then we attack the guesses, testing them against the world and against each other, keeping the ones that survive criticism and discarding the ones that fail. Knowledge grows by **conjecture and criticism**, not by derivation from a base. Science has no foundation of observations under it. It never needed one.

Set the two side by side. Morality cannot be derived from facts. Science cannot be derived from observations. The same impossibility, twice.

## Narrative / exposition

The objection to moral objectivity, stated at full strength, runs like this. Real knowledge rests on a foundation. Mathematics rests on axioms, science rests on observation, and from those secure bases the rest is derived. Morality has no such base. The is/ought gap proves it: there is no set of plain facts from which moral conclusions follow, and so nothing for moral claims to be *derived* from, and so nothing for them to be objectively answerable to. Morality alone among serious subjects is left dangling. That is the case the abolitionist and the slaveholder both face, and it is a strong case. It is not a confusion to be waved off. It is the best reason there is to think morality is, in the end, just opinion.

The physicist and philosopher David Deutsch, who took Popper's epistemology and carried it where Popper himself did not, makes one move against this case, and the move is decisive because it grants almost everything. Deutsch does not deny the is/ought gap. He agrees that morality has no foundation. He simply observes that the second premise of the objection is false. Science does not rest on a foundation either. The picture of secure observational bedrock, with theories derived upward from it, is exactly the picture Popper destroyed. There is no derivation of a general law from particular observations, because there cannot be, because the problem of induction makes it impossible. So when the objector says "morality has no foundation, therefore it is not objective," the inference smuggles in a claim about everything else, the claim that real, objective knowledge *does* have a foundation. And that claim is false. Nothing has a foundation of the kind being demanded. Not physics, not chemistry, not the most rigorously tested theory humanity possesses.

Once that is in view, the is/ought gap stops being a special embarrassment for morality and becomes an instance of something universal. "You cannot derive an ought from an is" is true. It is also, word for word in its logical structure, the same complaint as "you cannot derive a theory from an observation." Both say: the conclusion you want carries more than the premises contain, so no valid inference will take you there. The gap in the moral wall and the gap in the scientific wall are not two problems. They are one problem, the impossibility of deriving general or normative knowledge from particular or descriptive starting points, showing up in two domains. The same shape of hole, in two walls.

And here is what that buys. If the is/ought gap disqualified morality from being objective, then the problem of induction would disqualify science from being objective, by the identical reasoning. But science is objective. Whatever objectivity is, physics has it, and physics has it *without* a foundation, by surviving criticism and correcting its errors and converging on hard-won explanations of the world. So the lack of a foundation cannot be what makes a body of knowledge fail to be objective, because the clearest case of objective knowledge we have lacks one too. The objection, run consistently, proves too much. It would take down the thing nobody wants to take down. An argument that destroys science along with morality has misidentified what objectivity requires. It was never a foundation. It was the capacity to be corrected. Morality has no special problem here. It has the ordinary condition of all knowledge, which is to grow without a floor beneath it, by conjecture and criticism, answerable not to bedrock but to the test of surviving its critics.

This is the hinge the whole course turns on. Everything before it built the threat and built the tool. Everything after it works out what objectivity without foundations actually amounts to, and whether moral knowledge has it. But the threat itself, the is/ought gap heard as a death sentence for moral objectivity, is answered here, and it is answered by putting morality on **science's footing**: not better, not worse, the same.

## Primary source

Deutsch states the move directly in *The Beginning of Infinity*, in the course of arguing that moral knowledge and scientific knowledge are kinds of the same thing. The passage below is a faithful paraphrase of his position rather than a transcription, marked as such so that no wording is attributed to him that is not his own.

> *(Deutsch's argument, paraphrased.)* The traditional sharp distinction between scientific and moral knowledge is mistaken. Both are knowledge, and both grow in the same way, by seeking good explanations and correcting errors. It is true that you cannot derive an "ought" from an "is," but you equally cannot derive a scientific theory from observations of what is. Empirical testing is not a derivation of theory from data; it is a way of choosing between conjectures that we have already guessed. So the supposed foundation that morality lacks is a foundation that science lacks too. Neither rests on one, and neither needs one.
>
> Paraphrased from David Deutsch, *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011), the chapters on the nature of knowledge and on moral knowledge.

The structure of the claim is what matters, and it survives paraphrase intact. Deutsch concedes the is/ought gap in the first half of the thought and then, in the second half, denies that science is in any better position. The concession is total and the reply is symmetrical. He does not find morality a foundation. He removes the double standard that pretended science had one.

## Analysis & interpretation

Read the move slowly, because its whole force lies in what it does *not* claim, and it is easy to hear it as claiming more.

The move establishes a **parity**. It says morality stands where science stands with respect to foundations, which is to say nowhere, and that this is no objection to science, so it is no objection to morality. The technical name for what it secures is **epistemic parity with science**: the two are on the same footing as regards what they rest on, namely nothing, and as regards how they grow, namely by conjecture and criticism. The standard reason for thinking morality *cannot* be objective, the reason built out of the is/ought gap, is removed. It is removed not by filling the gap but by showing the gap is everywhere, including under the most objective knowledge there is.

What the move does not do is prove that morality *is* objective, in the full sense a moral realist wants. Parity is a defensive result. It clears away the argument that morality must be subjective because it lacks a foundation. It does not, by itself, deliver mind-independent moral facts, or show that there is a moral analog of the experiment, or settle the dispute between those who think moral progress tracks a real standard and those who think it does not. A careful opponent can accept every word of this module and still hold that morality, though now no worse off than science *with respect to foundations*, is worse off in some other respect the parity argument has not touched. That opponent has a real position, and the course does not pretend otherwise. This is a defense, not a proof. It answers the objection that looked fatal and earns morality a hearing on the same terms as any other knowledge. The constructive case, what objectivity without foundations positively consists in and whether moral explanations have it, is the work of the modules that follow, and the honest accounting of what remains genuinely contested is the work of the last one.

There is a tempting overstatement to refuse here. It would be easy to slide from "morality has no special problem" to "morality has no problem," or from "science has no foundation either" to "so anything goes equally in both." Neither follows. Science without a foundation is still ferociously selective; most conjectures die under criticism, and the survivors are hard-won. The parity claim inherits that selectivity, it does not dissolve it. Morality on science's footing is morality held to criticism, not morality let off the hook. The gap that Hume found is real, and the move does not close it. It relocates it, from a flaw peculiar to ethics to a feature of knowledge as such.

4. **Steelman the holdout.** Suppose someone grants the entire parity argument and still denies that morality is objective. State their remaining position in its most sympathetic form, with no rebuttal attached, and say what, if anything, writing it changed about how secure the module's conclusion looks.

## The story

For as long as anyone in Europe had written the word, a swan was white. The bird was a fixed point, a thing you could lean on. Roman poets used a black swan, *rara avis*, as the very figure of the impossible, the way we might say "when pigs fly." Every swan anyone had ever seen, across the whole continent and across every century of record, was white, and the count ran into untold thousands without a single exception. If observation could ever prove a universal law, this was the case to do it. The evidence was as one-sided as evidence gets. "All swans are white" was not a guess. It was knowledge, settled, checked by everyone, never once falsified.

In 1697 a Dutch expedition under the navigator **Willem de Vlamingh** was working the western coast of what is now Australia, sent partly to search for survivors of a ship lost two years before. Sailing into the estuary of a river on that coast, his men found swans that were black. They caught several alive and carried some back, and the river took the name the explorers gave it for what they had seen there, the Swan River, on whose banks the city of Perth now stands. The birds were real, *Cygnus atratus*, a species that had been living in Australia the entire time the poets of Europe were using its impossibility as a proverb.

One bird undid the certainty of millennia. Not by argument, not by a flaw discovered in the old reasoning, but by walking into view. The thousands of confirming sightings had never amounted to a proof, and the proof they seemed to add up to had never existed, and the single counterexample exposed both facts at once. This is the science side of the parallel the module turns on, the very thing Popper meant by the problem of induction made into a fact about birds. No number of white swans entails the next one. The general law was never derived from the observations; it was a conjecture, a bold guess that the pattern would hold, and it held for a very long time and then it failed, and the failing was not a scandal but the ordinary way knowledge corrects itself. European naturalists did not conclude that ornithology was therefore "just opinion," that since "all swans are white" had no foundation under it there was no fact of the matter about swans. They revised the conjecture and went on, with a body of knowledge that had no foundation and never had, and was the better for being open to the bird that proved it wrong. If that is how the most concrete observational knowledge lives, with no floor beneath it and answerable only to correction, then the absence of a floor beneath morality puts it in good company, and in no special trouble at all.
