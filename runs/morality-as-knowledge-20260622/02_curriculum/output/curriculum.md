## Course metadata (for `_course.md`)

- **title:** Morality as Knowledge
- **description:** The case, built on David Deutsch's and Karl Popper's epistemology, that morality is objective: moral knowledge has no foundation and needs none, because it grows the way all knowledge does, by conjecture, criticism, and error-correction.
- **level:** undergrad

## The arc

The argument is a chain, and the modules follow it. The **challenge** is built at full strength first (2–3) so the reply is worth something; the **Popperian engine** (4) and **Deutsch's decisive move** (5, the hinge) answer it; the **constructive core** (6) says what objectivity-without-foundations is; the **payoff** (7–8) shows moral progress is real and links knowledge to the good; a **reinforcing parallel** (9, objective beauty) consolidates it; and the **honest close** (10) states precisely what is established and what remains a genuine metaethical debate. Module 5 needs module 4 immediately before it, and recalls the is/ought gap from module 3.

## Module specs

### 01 — What "Objective" Would Even Mean
- **slug:** `01-what-objective-means`
- **summary:** Almost no one really believes the Holocaust was wrong only as a matter of taste, yet almost everyone says morality is subjective, and the gap between what we say and what we do is where this course lives.
- **objectives:**
  - Distinguish "objective" (answerable to something beyond opinion, able to be got wrong and improved) from "subjective / just opinion" in the precise sense the course uses.
  - State the course's thesis: moral knowledge is objective without resting on a foundation, proof, or authority.
  - Explain why "objective" need not mean "certain" or "proven," using a case of a settled belief later corrected.
- **prereqs:** none
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** Frame the thesis and the central reframe the whole course turns on: **objective = correctable, not certain.** Use the "dial test" hook (is genocide wrong, or do you just dislike it?) and the two-columns device (objective / just opinion / and the uneasy third place "slavery is wrong" sits). Do not argue the thesis yet; set the question and the key redefinition. Owns: objective vs. subjective, the thesis, objectivity-as-correctability. Closing story: **Barry Marshall and Robin Warren** and *H. pylori*, the "settled truth" that ulcers came from stress overturned by conjecture and a self-experiment (cultured 1982; Marshall drank the culture 1984; 2005 Nobel). Points true: an objective truth with no foundation, reached by error-correction, not proof.

### 02 — Morality Looks Like Opinion
- **slug:** `02-morality-looks-like-opinion`
- **summary:** The case that morality is just feeling or culture is stronger than its slogans, and it has to be met at full strength before it can be answered.
- **objectives:**
  - State moral relativism (moral claims true only relative to a culture or speaker) at its strongest, including the real fact of moral diversity it leans on.
  - Explain Ayer's emotivism (moral sentences express attitude and state no fact) and how it differs from relativism.
  - Distinguish "morality is relative" (true-but-only-locally) from "morality states no fact at all" (emotivism).
- **prereqs:** 01
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** **Steelman, do not rebut yet.** The reader should feel the pull. Relativism's genuine evidence (deep cross-cultural moral disagreement) and emotivism's genuine insight (moral talk really does carry feeling and motivate action). Keep relativism and emotivism distinct (canon §1b: Ayer owns emotivism; relativism is a separate view). The reply comes much later (07). Owns: relativism, emotivism (Ayer). Closing story: **Herodotus on Darius and the funeral customs** (*Histories* 3.38), the Greeks who burned their dead and the Callatiae who ate theirs, each horrified by the other, and Pindar's line that "custom is king of all." The ancient, genuine root of relativism. Points true: the real force of the relativist's datum.

### 03 — The Case Against Moral Facts
- **slug:** `03-the-case-against-moral-facts`
- **summary:** Three sharp philosophical arguments, often blurred into one, say there are no moral facts to be objective about, and they are the real obstacle the course must clear.
- **objectives:**
  - State Hume's is/ought gap (no valid inference from purely descriptive premises to a normative conclusion) precisely.
  - Distinguish Moore's naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument (good is not definable as any natural property) from Hume's point about inference.
  - Explain Mackie's error theory and argument from queerness (moral claims aim at objective facts but all fail, because such facts would be metaphysically strange), and how it differs from emotivism.
- **prereqs:** 01, 02
- **est. minutes:** 30
- **notes:** **Steelman; the technical heart of the challenge.** The course's clarity depends on keeping these three distinct (the top learner confusion): Hume = an *inference* fails; Moore = a *definition* fails; Mackie = the claims aim at fact but are *all false*. Mackie agrees moral claims aim at fact (unlike Ayer); that contrast matters. Attribute precisely (canon §1b). Owns: the is/ought gap (Hume), the naturalistic fallacy (Moore), error theory / queerness (Mackie), the "no facts / no test" worry. Closing story: **Hume's *Treatise* "fell dead-born from the press"** (1739–40, Hume's own phrase about its neglect), the book containing the is/ought passage ignored in its day and now inescapable. Points true: the origin and the slow force of the most famous objection. **[verify the "dead-born" wording, well attested.]**

### 04 — Knowledge Without Foundations
- **slug:** `04-knowledge-without-foundations`
- **summary:** Popper's answer to a problem older than ethics: no knowledge rests on a foundation, not even science, and yet knowledge grows.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain fallibilism (all knowledge is conjectural and corrigible) and why it rejects knowledge as justified true belief.
  - State the problem of induction (observations never prove a general theory) and Popper's dissolution of it (we conjecture and test by attempted refutation).
  - Explain how knowledge grows by conjecture and criticism rather than by derivation from a base.
- **prereqs:** 03
- **est. minutes:** 30
- **notes:** The Popperian engine, all one idea: **there are no foundations anywhere, and knowledge grows by conjecture and criticism.** Fallibilism, anti-justificationism (the death of justified-true-belief), and the dissolution of induction. Gloss World 3 / objective knowledge lightly if at all (canon: do not build on it). This is **Popper's**, not Deutsch's (canon §1b): do not credit Deutsch with the induction dissolution. Sets up the hinge (05) immediately. Owns: fallibilism, anti-justificationism, the problem of induction and its dissolution, conjecture and criticism. Closing story: **Ignaz Semmelweis** (Vienna General Hospital, 1847), the chlorinated-lime handwashing that cut maternal mortality, rejected by offended colleagues, its discoverer committed to an asylum where he died in 1865. Points true: a correct conjecture entrenched against by a community that would not submit itself to criticism, the failure mode the epistemology diagnoses. **[verify exact mortality figures; keep general if uncertain.]**

### 05 — No Special Problem
- **slug:** `05-no-special-problem`
- **summary:** The objection that you cannot get an ought from an is turns out not to be special to morality, because you cannot get a scientific theory from observations either, and that changes everything.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain Deutsch's decisive move: since nothing is derived from a foundation (not science either), morality's lack of a foundation is the universal condition of knowledge, not a special defect.
  - Show how the is/ought gap (Hume) and the problem of induction (Popper) are the same shape of problem in two domains.
  - Assess what this move does and does not establish (epistemic parity with science, not yet full moral realism).
- **prereqs:** 04
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** **THE HINGE.** Recall the is/ought gap (03) as the threat and the induction dissolution (04) as the tool, then make Deutsch's move: "morality has no foundation" is true but unremarkable, because *neither does science*, and science is objective. This is **Deutsch's** extension of Popper (canon §1b). Begin the honesty discipline here: this secures *parity*, not yet realism (the full qualification waits for 10, but plant it). Owns: the no-special-problem move, the is/ought–induction parallel. Closing story: **the black swan**, the European certainty that "all swans are white" overturned when Dutch explorers under Willem de Vlamingh saw black swans in Western Australia in 1697, the standing emblem of how observation cannot prove a universal. Points true: the induction problem made vivid, the science side of the parallel.

### 06 — Good Explanations, Hard to Vary
- **slug:** `06-good-explanations-hard-to-vary`
- **summary:** If proof is not what makes knowledge objective, something else must be, and Deutsch's answer is the explanation that is hard to vary.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain Deutsch's criterion of a good explanation (it accounts for what it sets out to and is hard to vary while still doing so) and contrast a hard-to-vary explanation with an easily-varied one.
  - Explain reach (a good explanation solves problems beyond the one it was built for).
  - Apply the criterion to morality: a moral explanation is objective insofar as it solves real problems, survives criticism, and is hard to vary.
- **prereqs:** 05
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** The constructive core: **objectivity without foundations comes from good, hard-to-vary explanations and the capacity for error-correction.** Use Deutsch's seasons contrast (the Persephone myth, movable to any season, easy to vary and explaining nothing, versus axial tilt, locked together and unmovable) as the owned device. Then apply to morality. This is **Deutsch's** (canon §1b). Owns: good explanations, hard to vary, reach, objectivity-as-error-correction, objective moral knowledge. Closing story: **the 1919 eclipse expeditions** (Eddington and Dyson testing Einstein's general relativity against the bending of starlight, May 1919), a hard-to-vary theory making a risky, precise prediction that a vaguer theory could not, and surviving the test. Points true: hard-to-vary and reach in real science, the model for moral explanation.

### 07 — Moral Progress Is Real
- **slug:** `07-moral-progress-is-real`
- **summary:** The abolition of slavery was not a change of fashion but the correction of an error, and that single fact is very hard for relativism to survive.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain why some moral changes are corrections of errors rather than mere changes of custom, and what that presupposes.
  - Show that the reality of moral progress implies an objective standard being approached.
  - Explain why relativism cannot account for moral progress and is self-undermining.
- **prereqs:** 06
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** The payoff against the challenge of module 02. The asymmetry: that codes *changed* is trivial; that abolition was an *improvement* is the claim, and relativism cannot license "improvement." Argue it, do not assume it. **Do not import religious/authority grounding** (handle the Newton story's conversion lightly; the ground is error-correction, not authority). Owns: moral progress as error-correction, the reality of moral error, relativism self-undermines. Closing story: **John Newton**, the slave-ship captain and trader who later turned against the trade, wrote *Thoughts upon the African Slave Trade* (1788) calling his former part in it a thing his heart now shuddered at, mentored Wilberforce, and lived just long enough to see the Slave Trade Act of 1807. Points true: a person correcting a moral error under criticism and experience. **[verify Newton's exact wording before quoting; keep the conversion framing light.]**

### 08 — Knowledge and the Good
- **slug:** `08-knowledge-and-the-good`
- **summary:** Deutsch ties the good to the growth of knowledge and evil to the entrenchment of error, and Popper had already built the political form of the idea: a society that can correct itself without violence.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain Popper's open society (institutions that let a society remove bad rulers and policies without violence) as political error-correction.
  - Explain Deutsch's optimism ("problems are inevitable; problems are soluble") and his link between the good and the conditions for unbounded error-correction.
  - Assess Deutsch's stronger claim that evil is the entrenchment of error and "all evils are due to insufficient knowledge" as his contestable position, not a settled fact.
- **prereqs:** 06
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** Keep the seam clean (canon §1b): **the open society is Popper's**; the explicit link from knowledge-creation to the good, the optimism, and the "all evils" framing are **Deutsch's**, and the last is flagged as his bold, contestable claim (steelman it, then note honestly that it can be doubted). Owns: the open society (Popper), universal explainers, optimism and the good/bad in terms of knowledge (Deutsch). Closing story: **Popper writing *The Open Society and Its Enemies* in Christchurch, New Zealand**, in exile from the Anschluss, during the Second World War, calling the book his "war effort." Points true: fallibilism made political, written in flight from a regime that had destroyed exactly the institutions of error-correction. **[Te Ara / widely attested.]**

### 09 — Is Beauty Objective Too?
- **slug:** `09-is-beauty-objective-too`
- **summary:** In the one domain people are even surer is "just taste," Deutsch argues that beauty is objective, and the argument reinforces everything that came before.
- **objectives:**
  - Reconstruct Deutsch's argument that some beauty is objective (a flower's signal had to be hard to fake and hard to vary to reach pollinators across a species gap).
  - Explain how objective beauty parallels objective moral and scientific knowledge (objectivity without a foundation).
  - Evaluate the parallel honestly: what it reinforces and where the analogy has limits.
- **prereqs:** 06
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** A reinforcing parallel, not a load-bearing link. **Deutsch's** flowers argument (*The Beginning of Infinity*, "Why Are Flowers Beautiful?"): flowers and their insect pollinators coevolved a signal that had to work across a gap of shared "knowledge," and the cleanest solution is an objective standard of beauty, suggesting aesthetic criteria as objective as mathematical ones. Pays off the thesis in the domain readers find most subjective. Keep it honest (the analogy has limits; say so). Owns: objective beauty, the aesthetics parallel. Closing story: the **coevolution of flowers and pollinators** (the real natural history Deutsch's argument rests on, e.g. long-spurred orchids and long-tongued moths, the kind of match Darwin himself predicted from a flower's shape). Points true: the hard-to-fake cross-species signal, beauty as objective design. **[verify the specific orchid/moth case if named; the Darwin prediction of a long-tongued moth for *Angraecum sesquipedale* is well attested.]**

### 10 — What This Settles, and What It Doesn't
- **slug:** `10-what-this-settles`
- **summary:** The honest accounting: the case has shown morality is on the same footing as science and that moral progress is real, but whether that secures full moral realism is a question serious people still dispute.
- **objectives:**
  - State precisely what the argument establishes (epistemic parity with science; no special is/ought problem; the reality of moral progress and error).
  - State precisely what it does not settle (whether this secures mind-independent moral facts; the disputed moral analog of empirical test; the live realism vs. anti-realism debate).
  - Apply the takeaway method: hold any morality, including one's own, as a conjecture open to criticism.
- **prereqs:** 01-09
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** **Required, and crowns no winner on the metaethical question.** The affirmative summary first (what the course earned), then the honest qualification: an anti-realist or quasi-realist can accept fallibilism, error-correction, and even moral progress while denying mind-independent moral facts; the moral analog of prediction/test is itself disputed; some hold Deutsch's "objectivity" is thinner than full realism; mention the evolutionary debunking arguments (Street, Joyce) as the sharp modern objection that remains live. End on the *method*, the error-correcting stance toward one's own morality, as the durable takeaway. Owns: the established/contested ledger, the live realism debate, the method. Closing story: **Wittgenstein's Poker** (the Cambridge Moral Sciences Club, October 1946), the famous ten-minute confrontation in which Popper and Wittgenstein clashed over whether there are real philosophical problems at all, Wittgenstein brandishing a fireplace poker before leaving. Points true: these are real, unresolved questions that serious minds fight over, the right note for a course that refuses a fake verdict. **[verify the disputed details; the accounts themselves disagree, which is part of the point.]**

## Scope & integrity checks (carried from the brief and dossier)

- **Defend faithfully, but honestly:** the thesis is argued at full strength; every objection (relativism, emotivism, is/ought, naturalistic fallacy, queerness/error theory, evolutionary debunking) is steelmanned before any reply; the close crowns no winner on realism vs. anti-realism.
- **Attribute, do not blend** (the top integrity risk): the canon carries an ownership ledger. Popper owns fallibilism, the induction dissolution, the open society; Deutsch owns hard-to-vary, reach, the no-special-problem move, the moral and aesthetic objectivity extension, and the optimism; Hume/Moore/Mackie/Ayer each own one distinct objection. Never fuse them.
- **Do not overclaim:** the honest line is *epistemic parity plus real progress*, not "this proves moral realism." Any sentence implying metaethics is settled is a defect.
- **No religious/authority grounding** smuggled in (keep Newton's conversion light; the ground is error-correction).
- **No fabrication:** stories use verifiable facts only; quotations accurate or labelled paraphrase; `[verify]` specifics resolved or kept general.
- **Format:** each module ends in a fact-dense closing story (verifiable, points true, varied); no em dashes; enact the disciplines, do not recite them; no directive metadiscourse.
- **Vocabulary** compatible with the `conjecture-criticism-calculus` course's plain senses (conjecture, criticism, hard to vary, reach), but no formal symbols imported.
