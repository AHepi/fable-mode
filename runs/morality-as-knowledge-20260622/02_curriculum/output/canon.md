# Canon — Morality as Knowledge

The course's consistency contract. Binding on every authoring sub-agent and on the editorial stage.
Humanities (philosophy), undergrad. The integrity spine of this course is **attributing every claim to
the right thinker** (§1b) and **defending the thesis honestly, not as propaganda** (§0). Read §0 and
§1b before writing a word.

## 0. Non-negotiable rules (read first)

1. **Defend the thesis, at full strength.** The course argues *for* moral objectivity, in Deutsch's
   accessible register, and means it. It is not a neutral survey. The thesis: moral knowledge is
   objective, with no foundation and needing none, because it grows the way all knowledge grows, by
   conjecture, criticism, error-correction, and the search for hard-to-vary explanations.
2. **Steelman every objection before any reply.** Relativism, emotivism (Ayer), the is/ought gap
   (Hume), the naturalistic fallacy (Moore), error theory and queerness (Mackie), and the evolutionary
   debunking arguments (Street, Joyce) are each stated in their strongest, most sympathetic form
   *before* a Deutschian or Popperian answer touches them. A strawman discredits the whole course.
3. **Do not overclaim; name the live debate.** What the argument plausibly establishes: morality is
   *epistemically on a par with science* (no special is/ought problem), and moral progress and moral
   error are real. What it does **not** settle: whether that secures robust **moral realism**
   (mind-independent moral facts). An anti-realist or quasi-realist can accept all of fallibilism,
   error-correction, and moral progress and still deny mind-independent moral facts; the moral analog
   of empirical test is itself disputed. The course leads with the case and ends honestly on the open
   metaethical debate (module 10), crowning no winner there. This is a defense, not a proof, and it
   says so. Any sentence implying metaethics is settled is a defect.
4. **Attribute, never blend** (see §1b). Hume ≠ Moore ≠ Mackie ≠ Ayer ≠ Popper ≠ Deutsch. The most
   important internal seam: **Popper owns** fallibilism, anti-justificationism, the dissolution of
   induction, World 3, and the open society; **Deutsch owns** the "hard to vary" criterion, reach, the
   no-special-is/ought-problem move, the explicit extension to moral and aesthetic objectivity, and the
   beginning-of-infinity optimism. Deutsch builds *on* Popper. Never credit Popper with "hard to vary"
   or the moral move, or Deutsch with dissolving induction or the open society.
5. **No religious or authority grounding** smuggled in. The view grounds morality in error-correction,
   not in God, scripture, or any authority (it explicitly rejects those). Keep the John Newton story's
   conversion framing light: the ground of abolition is the correction of an error, not Newton's faith.
6. **No fabrication.** No invented quotes, dates, or page numbers. Deutsch, Popper, Hume, Moore, Mackie,
   and Ayer are quotable, but wording is verified or clearly-labelled paraphrase. Closing stories use
   attested detail only (the highest-fabrication-risk section). Resolve `[verify]` specifics from
   reputable sources or keep them general; never present a guess as fact.
7. **Format.** No em dashes (`—`); recompose, do not chop. No directive metadiscourse ("notice", "hold
   that", "remember", "mark the seam") and no self-narration ("in this module we will"); enact the
   disciplines, do not announce them. Any frontmatter value with a colon is wrapped in quotes (YAML).

## 1a. The central reframe (state it once, early; use throughout)

**Objective means correctable, not certain.** A claim is objective if it is answerable to something
beyond what any individual or culture happens to feel, so that it can be *got wrong* and *improved*.
Objectivity is the capacity for error-correction, not the possession of a proof or a foundation. This
redefinition (carried by Popper's fallibilism and Deutsch's good-explanations) is the move the whole
course turns on; readers arrive equating "objective" with "proven", and that must be surfaced and
replaced (module 01). Do not let "no foundation" be heard as "no objectivity": the point is that the
lack of a foundation is *universal* (science has none either), so it cannot be what disqualifies
morality.

## 1b. WHO OWNS WHICH CLAIM (the attribution ledger — do not blend)

The top failure mode is fusing distinct thinkers, especially crediting Popper with Deutsch's moves or
flattening the four metaethicists into "the anti-objectivists." Each claim below belongs to one person.

| Thinker (one-clause id on first mention) | Owns this claim, and only this | Keep distinct from |
|---|---|---|
| **David Hume** (18th-c. Scottish philosopher) | the **is/ought gap**: no valid inference from purely descriptive premises to a normative conclusion (*Treatise*, Bk III) | Moore (Hume is about *inference*, not *definition*) |
| **G. E. Moore** (early-20th-c. Cambridge philosopher) | the **naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument**: "good" cannot be *defined* as any natural property (*Principia Ethica*, 1903) | Hume (Moore is about *definition*); not a claim that ethics is mere feeling |
| **A. J. Ayer** (20th-c. British philosopher) | **emotivism**: moral sentences *express attitude* and state no fact, so are neither true nor false (*Language, Truth and Logic*, 1936) | Mackie (Ayer: moral claims state *nothing*; Mackie: they state something *false*); relativism |
| **J. L. Mackie** (20th-c. philosopher) | **error theory + argument from queerness**: moral claims *aim at* objective facts but *all are false*, because such facts would be metaphysically and epistemologically "queer" (*Ethics*, 1977) | Ayer (Mackie grants moral claims aim at fact); relativism (Mackie says they are false, not locally true) |
| **moral relativism** (a position, not one author's) | moral claims are true only *relative to* a culture or speaker; no cross-cultural fact of the matter | emotivism (relativism allows truth-value, locally); error theory |
| **Karl Popper** (20th-c. philosopher of science) | **fallibilism**; **anti-justificationism** (knowledge is not justified true belief, has no foundation, is only criticized); the **dissolution of the problem of induction** (conjecture and test by refutation); **World 3 / objective knowledge**; the **open society** as political error-correction | Deutsch (Popper does NOT own "hard to vary", reach, or the explicit moral-objectivity move) |
| **David Deutsch** (physicist and philosopher) | the **"hard to vary"** criterion of a good explanation; **reach**; the **no-special-is/ought-problem** move (morality on science's footing); the explicit extension to **objective moral and aesthetic knowledge** via good explanations; the **optimism** ("problems are inevitable; problems are soluble") and the link from the growth of knowledge to the good | Popper (these build *on* Popper but are Deutsch's); the bold "all evils are due to insufficient knowledge" is Deutsch's *contestable* claim, flagged as such |
| **Sharon Street; Richard Joyce** | the **evolutionary debunking arguments** (our moral intuitions are explained by natural selection, not by tracking moral facts), a sharp *modern objection* to steelman in module 10 | each other lightly; present as a live objection, not as the course's view |

**Popper-vs-Deutsch seam (the most-watched line):** module 04 is Popper's (induction, fallibilism);
module 05's decisive move and modules 06/09's good-explanations/beauty are Deutsch's; module 08 splits
cleanly (the open society = Popper; the optimism and the good-in-terms-of-knowledge = Deutsch). Police
every "as X showed."

## 2. Term registry (gloss in plain words at first use, then use freely)

| Term | Brief gloss to deploy at first use | Owner module |
|---|---|---|
| **objective / subjective** | objective: answerable to something beyond opinion, able to be got wrong and improved; subjective: just a matter of how someone feels | 01 |
| **moral relativism** | the view that a moral claim is true only relative to a culture or a person, with no fact of the matter across them | 02 |
| **emotivism** | Ayer's view that moral sentences express feeling ("boo" / "hurrah") rather than stating a fact | 02 |
| **the is/ought gap** | Hume's point that you cannot validly infer what *ought* to be from premises only about what *is* | 03 |
| **the naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument** | Moore's point that "good" cannot be defined as any natural property, since "this is N, but is it good?" stays an open question | 03 |
| **error theory / argument from queerness** | Mackie's view that moral claims aim at objective facts but all fail, because such facts would be too strange to exist | 03 |
| **fallibilism / critical rationalism** | the view that all knowledge is conjectural and could be wrong, and grows by finding and correcting errors | 04 |
| **justificationism / foundationalism** | the demand that knowledge rest on a secure base that justifies it; the view this course rejects | 04 |
| **justified true belief** | the traditional definition of knowledge the course rejects (knowledge has no such justification) | 04 |
| **the problem of induction** | the point that no number of observations can prove a general theory (no number of white swans proves all swans are white) | 04 |
| **conjecture and criticism** | a conjecture is a guess offered to solve a problem; criticism is the attempt to find what is wrong with it; knowledge grows by their interplay | 04 |
| **good explanation / hard to vary** | a good explanation accounts for what it sets out to and is hard to vary, its parts locked together so none can be swapped without wrecking it | 06 |
| **reach** | the tendency of a good explanation to solve problems beyond the one it was built for | 06 |
| **the open society** | Popper's name for a society whose institutions let it correct mistakes, including removing rulers, without violence | 08 |
| **universal explainer** | Deutsch's term for a person, an entity that can create explanatory knowledge without limit | 08 |
| **moral realism / anti-realism** | realism: there are mind-independent moral facts; anti-realism: there are not (the live debate the course ends on) | 10 |

**New thinkers needing a who/when/what clause on first mention:** Hume, Moore, Ayer, Mackie, Popper,
Deutsch, and (lightly, module 10) Street and Joyce.

## 6. Voice target

Deutsch's own register, undergrad: **lucid, confident, warm to the reader's intelligence, vivid with
real examples**, the voice of a writer who finds these ideas genuinely thrilling and expects the reader
to follow an argument across several steps. It gives each objection its strongest case in that
objection's own terms before answering, attributes every claim to the thinker who made it, and keeps
the case honest, it argues hard for the thesis and is equally clear about where the thesis stops. It
never sneers (least of all at Hume, Moore, Mackie, or Ayer, whose strong versions it needs), never
says "obviously," and earns each turn with an argument or a real case. It can hold, and teach the
reader to hold, a position firmly *and* an open question honestly at the same time.

**Closing stories:** each module ends in a fact-dense closing story (per `course-design.md`, "The
closing story"), real and verifiable, illustrating the module's argument and consolidating its
concepts. Assigned candidates are in the curriculum module notes (H. pylori; Herodotus on Darius;
Hume's "dead-born" Treatise; Semmelweis; the black swan; the 1919 eclipse; John Newton; Popper in
Christchurch; flower-pollinator coevolution; Wittgenstein's Poker). Verifiable detail only; the contest
over Wittgenstein's Poker's facts is itself the point of module 10's story. Vary them; no two are the
same kind of scene.

**Drift to correct in editorial:** (a) attribution-blending is the top risk, hold the §1b ledger hard,
especially the Popper/Deutsch seam; (b) keep the close (10) genuinely open on realism, the course must
not slide into "we proved it"; (c) keep the steelman *first* in every objection module (02, 03) and in
08's "all evils" beat; (d) no em dashes; (e) de-recite any "notice"/"hold that" that slips in; (f) keep
Newton's conversion light (no authority grounding).
