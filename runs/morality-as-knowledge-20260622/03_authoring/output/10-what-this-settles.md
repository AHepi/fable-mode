## The hook

A course that argued for moral objectivity could end with a flourish. It could say: there, the case is made, the skeptics are routed, morality is objective and we have proved it. That ending would be dishonest, and dishonesty is the one thing this course cannot afford, because the whole argument has been that knowledge is the thing that stays open to correction. A defense that closed itself off from criticism in its last paragraph would refute itself in the act of finishing.

So this module does two things that pull in opposite directions, and the strength of the close is holding both at once. First it states, without deflation, what nine modules of argument have actually earned. Then it states, without flinching, what they have not. The reader who can hold a firm result and a genuinely open question in the same mind, neither collapsing the open question into a false victory nor letting the open question dissolve the firm result, has learned the thing this course most wanted to teach.

## Context & background

Walk back through the argument once, fast, because what it established is precise and worth stating in its own terms before measuring what it leaves out.

The course began by repairing the question. Asked whether morality is objective, most people hear *can it be proved from a foundation*, answer no, and conclude it is mere opinion. The repair was the move the whole course turns on: objective in the course's sense means answerable to more than opinion, able to be got wrong and improved, not certain or proven (module 01). On that definition the demand for a foundation loses its grip, because nothing rests on a foundation of that kind, science least of all.

Then the challenge, given its strongest form before any reply. The relativist's claim that morality is only local, and Ayer's that it states no fact at all (module 02). Hume's gap between is and ought, Moore's point that good cannot be defined as any natural property, Mackie's error theory that moral claims aim at fact but all fail (module 03). These were not knocked down. They were taken seriously, in their own terms, as the real obstacle.

The reply ran through the epistemology. Popper's fallibilism, his dissolution of the problem of induction, his point that science has no foundation either and grows by conjecture and criticism (module 04). Then the decisive move, which is Deutsch's: morality lacks a foundation, but so does everything, so the lack is the universal condition of knowledge and not a special defect of ethics (module 05). What makes knowledge objective without a foundation is the good, hard-to-vary explanation and the capacity for error-correction (module 06). And the payoff: abolition was the correction of an error, not a change of fashion, which relativism cannot license (module 07), set inside Deutsch's larger picture of knowledge and the good and Popper's open society (module 08), reinforced by the case that even beauty can be objective (module 09).

## Narrative / exposition

Here is what that chain establishes, stated cleanly and with the confidence it earned.

Morality is **epistemically on a par with science**. This is a real result, not a consolation prize. The standard reason for thinking morality cannot be objective was that it has no foundation, no proof, no way to derive an ought from an is. That reason is answered, and answered at the root, because *no domain of knowledge has a foundation of that kind*. Science does not derive its theories from observation; it conjectures them and exposes them to criticism. The is/ought gap and the problem of induction turned out to be the same shape of hole in two different walls, and nobody concludes from the problem of induction that physics is mere opinion. The asymmetry that was supposed to disqualify morality does not exist. Morality stands where science stands, and science is the clearest case of objective knowledge we have.

Objectivity is **the capacity for error-correction**, not the possession of a proof. A moral claim is objective insofar as it is answerable to something beyond what any person or culture happens to feel, so that it can be got wrong, criticized, and improved. That capacity is real in morality. We argue about what to do, find that codes we once held were mistaken, and correct them, and the correction is not always a swap of one taste for another.

**Moral progress and moral error are real.** The abolition of slavery was the correction of an error. The people who built fortunes on owning other human beings were not merely outvoted by later fashion; they were wrong about something, the way you can be wrong about a fact, and the abolitionists were closer to right. Relativism cannot account for that, because if every code is true only relative to its own culture, then "we improved" has no content, and yet it plainly has content. The reality of moral improvement is one of the firmest things in the whole argument.

So the case is made, and made at full strength. The standard reasons for thinking morality *cannot* be objective have been met and answered. A reader who came in equating objectivity with proof, and morality with taste, should leave with both equations broken. None of that is in doubt, and none of it should be deflated to win points for modesty later. It is a result.

## Narrative / exposition (continued)

Now the other half, stated with the same precision, because the result has an exact edge and the honest thing is to find it.

Set against the result is an exact limit, and the argument never crossed it. It did not claim to have proved **moral realism**, the view that there are mind-independent moral facts, facts about right and wrong that hold regardless of what any mind thinks or feels. Realism is one thing; its denial, **anti-realism**, is another, and the whole course has shown morality to be objective in a sense that does not obviously decide between them. That is the live debate the close refuses to settle.

To see why it stays open, consider who can accept everything established above and still deny mind-independent moral facts. A **quasi-realist** can. Quasi-realism is the position, developed in the late twentieth century most fully by the philosopher Simon Blackburn, that our moral talk earns the right to all the trappings of objectivity, truth, error, progress, getting things right, even though at bottom morality expresses our attitudes rather than tracking facts that exist independently of us. The quasi-realist starts from something close to Ayer's emotivism, that moral language voices a stance rather than reporting a fact, and then argues that a sophisticated version of that view can say everything the realist says: that some moral claims are true, that we can be wrong, that we have made real progress. If that program works, then fallibilism, error-correction, and the reality of moral progress are *companions in guilt* with realism rather than evidence for it. They come along whether or not there are mind-independent moral facts, so they cannot by themselves establish that there are. A defender of the course's case has answers to give the quasi-realist, but the exchange is live and unfinished, and pretending otherwise would betray everything the course has argued about keeping claims open.

There is a second open edge, sharper for resting on the course's own analogy. The case leaned hard on the parity between moral and scientific knowledge. But science has a feature that is hard to find a clean moral analog for: the **experimental test**. A physical theory makes a risky prediction, and an experiment can kill it, the way the bending of starlight in 1919 could have killed general relativity and did not. What is the moral analog? What, exactly, tests a moral conjecture the way an experiment tests a physical one? There are candidate answers. Perhaps the test is whether a moral conjecture, lived out, solves the problems it claimed to solve, or breeds new suffering it did not foresee, or collapses under criticism it cannot answer. But whether that is genuinely a *test* in the demanding sense, something that could decisively refute a moral conjecture the way an experiment refutes a physical one, is itself disputed. The parity argument is strong, and it does real work, and it is also exactly where a careful critic presses.

And then the sharpest modern objection, which is owed its full force. **Sharon Street**, in her 2006 paper "A Darwinian Dilemma for Realist Theories of Value," and **Richard Joyce**, in his 2006 book *The Evolution of Morality*, press what are called **evolutionary debunking arguments**. The argument runs like this. Our most basic moral intuitions, that we should care for our children, keep faith with allies, recoil from gratuitous cruelty, are exactly the intuitions natural selection would have built into a social animal, because creatures wired to feel them out-reproduced creatures who were not. Selection shaped those feelings for their effect on survival and reproduction, not for their accuracy about any mind-independent moral facts. So if there were such facts, it would be a sheer coincidence that the feelings evolution handed us happened to track them, and a belief that is right only by coincidence is not knowledge. The realist, Street argues, is caught in a dilemma: either deny the evolutionary influence on our values, which is not credible, or admit it and lose any reason to think our values track moral truth. Joyce reaches a kindred conclusion, that the genealogy of our moral sense gives us reason to doubt it delivers moral *knowledge* at all.

This is a serious argument, and the honest thing is to state it at its strongest rather than to find the quick exit. There are replies in the literature, and they are not contemptible. One holds that the debunker proves too much, since our perceptual and logical faculties were also shaped by selection for survival rather than for truth, and few want to debunk arithmetic on that ground. Another holds that selection could favor creatures who track real moral facts, if tracking them is useful, the way selection favors eyes that track real light. The debunker has answers to these in turn. The point of this close is not to adjudicate the exchange. It is to report, accurately, that the exchange is *open*, that some of the best philosophers now working take the debunking arguments to be a live and unanswered threat to moral realism, and that a course defending moral objectivity has no business pretending the threat has been disposed of.

Put the ledger plainly. What the argument earned: epistemic parity with science, objectivity as error-correction, the reality of moral progress. What it did not settle: whether any of that secures mind-independent moral facts, what the moral analog of empirical test really is, and how the realist answers the evolutionary debunkers. This is a defense, not a proof, and the difference is the whole of intellectual honesty. The course crowns no winner between realism and anti-realism, because in the present state of the argument there is no winner to crown.

## Primary source

The clearest statement of why a result can be firm without resting on a foundation comes from Popper himself, writing about science, with an image that fits the honest close exactly.

> Science does not rest upon solid bedrock. The bold structure of its theories rises, as it were, above a swamp. It is like a building erected on piles. The piles are driven down from above into the swamp, but not down to any natural or "given" base; and if we stop driving the piles deeper, it is not because we have reached firm ground. We simply stop when we are satisfied that the piles are firm enough to carry the structure, at least for the time being.
>
> Karl Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (English edition, 1959)

The image is built for exactly the double thing this module is doing. The structure is real; you can build on the piles, the building stands, science is the most powerful knowledge we have. And the structure rests on no bedrock; the piles go down into swamp, and we stop driving them not because we struck firm ground but because they will hold "for the time being." That last phrase is the honest close in four words. It does not say the building will fall. It says it stands on conjecture, open to the day a pile gives and has to be driven deeper. A defense of moral objectivity that wanted a bedrock under it would be asking morality for something Popper says even physics does not have. The case for moral objectivity stands the way the building stands. That is enough to live and act on, and it is not certainty, and the difference is the lesson.

## Analysis & interpretation

There is a reading of the swamp passage that would betray the course, and it is worth naming so as to refuse it. The cynic's reading: if even science floats on a swamp, then everything is unfounded, so nothing is more objective than anything else, so morality and taste and the swamp are all one mush. That reading throws away the structure to keep the swamp. But the passage is not about the swamp; it is about the building, which stands, and carries weight, and can be improved by driving the piles deeper, none of which is true of mush. The right reading keeps both: no bedrock, *and* a real, weight-bearing, correctable structure. Holding both is the whole skill the course has been teaching, and the close is the place to show that it transfers to the course's own conclusion as readily as to science.

So apply it to the conclusion. The case for moral objectivity is a building on piles. It bears real weight: it answers the standard objections, it accounts for moral progress, it puts morality on the same standing as scientific knowledge. And it goes down into no bedrock: the realism question is open, the moral analog of test is disputed, the debunkers are unanswered. A reader tempted to want more, to want the proof that would settle realism once and for all, is wanting the bedrock, and the course's deepest claim is that there is no bedrock anywhere, not for physics and not for ethics, and that this is not a defect but the universal condition of every claim worth holding.

Which yields the takeaway, and it is a method, not a verdict. The durable thing the course hands the reader is not the sentence "morality is objective" to be filed away as settled. It is a *stance* toward moral claims, including the reader's own. Hold any morality, your own most of all, as a conjecture open to criticism: a guess offered to solve the real problem of how to live, answerable to argument and experience, able to be got wrong and improved, and never closed against the day a better criticism comes. That stance is what objectivity *is*, on the course's own definition, and it is available right now, before the realism debate is settled and whether or not it ever is. The reader who holds their deepest moral convictions that way is already doing the only thing the course ever asked: treating morality as knowledge, which is to say, as the kind of thing that can be wrong, and corrected, and made better. That mirrors, one level up, what the course just did to itself in this final module. It made its case hard, and then held the case open.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Turn the stance on yourself.** Take a moral conviction you hold firmly, one you would not want to be wrong about. Treat it, for the length of an essay, as a conjecture: state the real problem it is an answer to, the strongest criticism it faces, and what could in principle show it to be an error. Say honestly whether doing this weakened the conviction, strengthened it, or left it where it was, and what that tells you about holding a morality as knowledge.

2. **Side with a debunker.** Reconstruct Street's or Joyce's evolutionary debunking argument in its strongest form, then argue *either* that it does sink moral realism *or* that one of the replies (it proves too much; selection could favor tracking real moral facts) defeats it. Do not crown the side you happen to prefer without engaging the best version of the other.

3. **Realist, anti-realist, or neither.** The course established that morality stands on the same footing as science and that moral progress is real, and stopped short of realism. Stake out where you land: that this is enough and the realism question does not matter, that it is not enough and realism still needs proving, or that quasi-realism captures it all without mind-independent facts. Defend the placement, and say what would move you off it.

4. **Where the analogy breaks.** The case leaned on the parity between moral and scientific knowledge. Name the strongest point of disanalogy, the experimental test or another, and argue either that the parity survives it or that it does not. Be precise about what work the analogy was doing and whether it can do that work without the missing piece.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Keeps the two claims distinct: moral objectivity in the course's sense (parity, error-correction, real progress, defended) versus moral realism (mind-independent facts, left open). Treating them as the same thing is the central error to avoid.
- States any opposing view, especially quasi-realism and the debunking arguments, in its strongest and fairest form before answering it, and can tell steelmanning from knocking down a weak version.
- Does not overclaim. A strong response does not write as though the course proved realism, and does not deflate the real result either; it holds the firm result and the open question together.
- Applies the method honestly when the prompt turns it on the writer's own conviction: states the problem the conviction answers, the criticism it faces, and what could correct it, rather than defending it as beyond question.
- Stays inside what the course established, using the attributions accurately (quasi-realism is Blackburn's development; the debunking arguments are Street's and Joyce's; the parity move is Deutsch's; fallibilism and the open society are Popper's).

## The story

On the evening of 25 October 1946, the Cambridge Moral Sciences Club met in room H3 of King's College, in the rooms of Richard Braithwaite, to hear a paper. The speaker was Karl Popper, down from London, and the announced title was a question: "Are There Philosophical Problems?" In the audience sat the most famous philosopher in the world, Ludwig Wittgenstein, who chaired the club and who held, more or less, that there were not. There were puzzles, knots that language ties in itself, to be dissolved by seeing how words actually work. Popper had come to argue the opposite, that philosophical problems are real, that they are about the world and not merely about words. The two men had never met. Within minutes they were fighting, and at some point in the fight Wittgenstein had a fireplace poker in his hand.

That much is not seriously in doubt: the meeting happened, those two men were there, Bertrand Russell was in the room, the argument was fierce, and there was a poker. Almost everything past that point is contested, and the contest is the reason the episode is told here. The journalists David Edmonds and John Eidinow, who reconstructed the evening in their book *Wittgenstein's Poker*, found that the eyewitnesses, serious people who had been in the same small room, left with accounts that could not all be true. Did Wittgenstein brandish the poker as a weapon, gesturing with it at Popper to make a point, or was he merely toying with it by the fire as he often handled whatever was to hand? Did he storm out, slamming the door, or leave in the ordinary way? And the most disputed point of all, the exchange about a moral example. By Popper's own much later telling, Wittgenstein challenged him to state a single moral rule, Popper answered, "Not to threaten visiting lecturers with pokers," and Wittgenstein, in a rage, threw down the poker and left. Others who were there denied it had happened anything like that. Some thought Popper had compressed a slower argument into a punchline he wrote down decades afterward; some thought the line had been delivered after Wittgenstein was already gone.

The accounts cannot be reconciled, and no further evidence will reconcile them now, because the witnesses are dead and they disagreed while alive. There is no transcript. There is no bedrock under the question of what was said in that room for ten minutes in 1946. What there is, is a set of conjectures, some better supported than others, none of them provable, exactly the situation Popper spent his life describing.

And that is the point, sharper than any tidy verdict could be. The meeting was a fight about whether there are real, unresolved questions that honest minds can disagree over, and the meeting itself became one. Two of the century's hardest thinkers, with Russell looking on, could not settle in person what philosophy is for. The people who watched them could not settle afterward what they had seen. The questions did not close. They are open still, argued by serious people who have read everything and disagree anyway, which is not a failure of the inquiry but the ordinary condition of it. A course that had ended by declaring the moral realism debate over would have been claiming, about the hardest question it touched, a certainty that Popper and Wittgenstein could not reach about a quarrel they were both standing inside. The honest close says less and means more: the case for moral objectivity is strong, the question of what finally grounds it is open, and the right way to hold any such conviction, including this one, is the way you hold a good conjecture. Firmly enough to build on. Loosely enough to correct.
