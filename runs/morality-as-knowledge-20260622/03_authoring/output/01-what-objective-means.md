## The hook

Try a quick test on yourself. Is genocide wrong, or do you just dislike it?

Almost no one answers that the way they would answer a question about ice cream. Pressed on whether the systematic murder of millions of people is *wrong*, very few people will say it comes down to taste, the way "chocolate beats vanilla" comes down to taste, so that someone with the opposite preference is simply built differently and has made no mistake. The wrongness feels like something the killers got *wrong*, not something we happen to be against. And yet ask those same people, in a calmer moment, whether morality is objective or subjective, and most will say subjective. Morality is just opinion. Who's to say. It's all relative.

So here is a person holding two beliefs that do not fit. One: morality is just opinion. Two: the people who ran the death camps were not merely doing something the speaker would have preferred they not do, they were *wrong*, in a way that no amount of sincere disagreement on their part could fix. The first belief is the slogan. The second is what comes out under pressure, when the example is bad enough to strip the slogan away. This course lives in the gap between the two, and its wager is that the slogan is the part that should give.

## Context & background

Before the wager can even be stated, two words need pinning down, because almost every confusion in this area is really a confusion about them.

Call a claim **objective** when it is answerable to something beyond what any one person, or any one culture, happens to feel about it. An objective claim can be *got wrong*. There is a difference between thinking it is true and its being true, a difference that leaves room for everyone to be mistaken at once and to find that out later. Call a claim **subjective**, or **just opinion**, when there is no such gap: when its truth is settled entirely by how the speaker feels, so that "true for me" is the whole of the story and there is nothing further to be right or wrong about. That chocolate is the best flavor is subjective in this sense. If you love it, it is the best flavor for you, and someone who hates it has not made an error. There is no fact about the best flavor sitting out beyond the two of you, judging the dispute.

Now line up some claims in two columns. On the left, claims everyone treats as objective: the Earth goes around the Sun; water is made of hydrogen and oxygen; there were more people alive in 1980 than in 1880. On the right, claims everyone treats as just opinion: chocolate is the best flavor; that song is better than this one; a window seat beats an aisle seat. The left column is answerable to the world and can be got wrong. The right column bottoms out in preference and cannot.

Then ask where this one goes: *slavery is wrong*.

It does not sit comfortably in either column. Drop it on the right, with the ice cream, and something rebels. We do not think the abolitionists merely had different taste in social arrangements from the slaveholders, the way one diner likes the window and another the aisle. We think they were *right*, and that the people who built their fortunes on owning other human beings were not just outvoted by later fashion but were *wrong about something*, the way you can be wrong about a fact. But drop "slavery is wrong" on the left, with the orbit of the Earth, and a different worry starts up. Where is the moral fact? You can point a telescope at the Sun. What do you point at wrongness? Nobody seems able to say.

That uneasy third place, where "slavery is wrong" sits because it will not stay in either column, is the problem this whole course is built to resolve.

## Narrative / exposition

The thesis of the course can now be stated plainly, though stating it is not the same as earning it, and the earning takes the modules that follow.

The thesis is that moral knowledge is **objective**: there are moral truths, some moral claims really are better than others, and we can make genuine progress toward getting them right, not because morality rests on a foundation, a proof, or an authority, but because moral knowledge grows the way all knowledge grows, by guessing, by trying to find what is wrong with the guess, and by correcting the errors that turns up. That is the claim. Whether it survives the strongest objections to it is the work of the rest of the course, and those objections get their full, fair, undiluted hearing before any reply touches them. This module argues for none of it. It does one job: it gets the question right, and replaces one wrong idea about what an answer would even look like.

The wrong idea is this. Most people, asked whether morality is objective, hear the question as: *can it be proved?* Can you derive "slavery is wrong" from some bedrock of certainty, the way you might derive a theorem from axioms, so that anyone who denied it could be shown a flaw in their reasoning the way you would catch a slip in arithmetic? And the honest answer is no, you cannot, there is no such proof, no foundation under morality that forces the conclusion on every rational mind. From which it looks to follow that morality is not objective after all. No foundation, no proof, so just opinion.

That inference is the mistake the course exists to undo, and undoing it turns on noticing what "objective" has been quietly assumed to mean. It has been read as *certain*, *proven*, *resting on a foundation no one can doubt*. But that is not what objectivity is. **Objective means correctable, not certain.** A claim is objective when it is answerable to something beyond opinion, so that it can be got wrong and improved, and that capacity, the capacity for error-correction, is the whole of what objectivity requires. It does not require a proof. It does not require a foundation. It requires only that there be a difference between thinking you are right and being right, a gap into which error and its correction can fit.

Once objectivity is the capacity to be corrected rather than the possession of a proof, the demand for a foundation loses its grip, and for a reason that will run through every later module. The lack of a foundation is not special to morality. Nothing rests on a foundation of the kind the demand imagines, not even physics, and physics is the clearest case of objective knowledge we have. Later modules make that case in full, with the people who made it named. The point to fix now is only the shape of the question. If "objective" meant "proven from a foundation," then morality would fail the test, but so would everything else worth knowing, which is a sign that the test is wrong, not that everything is opinion.

## Primary source

The redefinition the course turns on is not a trick invented to rescue morality. It is the ordinary condition of the most objective knowledge there is, and the philosopher of science **Karl Popper** (the twentieth-century thinker whose account of how knowledge grows underlies this whole course) put it as sharply as anyone, writing about physics, with no thought of ethics at all.

> The old scientific ideal of episteme — of absolutely certain, demonstrable knowledge — has proved to be an idol. The demand for scientific objectivity makes it inevitable that every scientific statement must remain tentative for ever.
>
> Karl Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (English edition, 1959)

Read the second sentence slowly. It does not say that objectivity survives *despite* the fact that scientific statements stay forever tentative, as if certainty were the goal and tentativeness a regrettable shortfall. It says the demand for objectivity is what *makes* them tentative. To hold a claim open to correction, answerable to test and argument rather than to the authority of whoever asserts it, just is to refuse it the status of a proven, closed, certain thing. "Episteme" is the old name for exactly that proven, closed certainty, knowledge that could not possibly be otherwise. Popper calls it an idol: something people worship that was never real. The objectivity that is real is the other thing, the permanent openness to being shown wrong. A claim that could never be corrected would not be more objective than the others. It would have stepped outside the practice that makes objectivity possible at all.

## Analysis & interpretation

If objectivity were certainty, no honest person could be objective about much of anything, because certainty is not on offer, not in physics and not in ethics. So a reader who arrives equating the two has been handed a definition that makes objectivity impossible and then told that morality, alone among subjects, fails to reach it. The repair is to stop demanding the impossible thing and ask for the possible one. Can a moral claim be *answerable to more than opinion*? Can it be got *wrong*, and the error *found and fixed*? If yes, then moral knowledge is objective in the only sense any knowledge ever is, and the absence of a proof under it is no more disqualifying than the absence of a proof under chemistry.

There is a fair objection waiting here, and the course owes it an honest acknowledgment now even though the full answer comes much later. It is one thing to say objective means correctable. It is another to show that moral claims actually *are* correctable, that there is something real for "slavery is wrong" to be answerable to, some standard that the abolitionists were tracking and the slaveholders were missing. A skeptic can grant every word about objectivity and certainty and still insist that morality has no such standard, that there is nothing out there for a moral claim to be right or wrong about, and that the redefinition, however nice, changes nothing if the cupboard is bare. That objection is not a misunderstanding to be brushed off. It is the strongest thing the other side has, and the next several modules are devoted to stating it at full force, in its own best terms, before a single reply is offered. The claim of *this* module is narrower and worth being clear about. It is not that morality is objective. It is that "objective" does not mean "proven," so that the lack of a moral proof, taken by itself, settles nothing. The question is still open. What this module has done is make sure it is the *right* question, asked in words that do not rig the answer before the argument begins.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Place a claim in a column.** Take a moral claim you hold firmly (anything: that torturing children for fun is wrong, that promises should be kept). Decide whether you treat it as objective, as just opinion, or as belonging to the uneasy third place, and defend the placement. Then say what would have to be true for it to belong in one of the *other* columns instead.

2. **The slogan and the example.** The module claims most people say "morality is subjective" while acting as though some moral claims are simply correct. Argue either that this is a real inconsistency people should resolve, or that there is a coherent view on which both can be held at once. If you defend the second, state the view precisely.

3. **What objectivity costs.** Suppose you accept that *objective means correctable, not certain*. Name one belief you would have to give up to hold that consistently, in morality or anywhere else, and say whether the trade is worth it.

4. **Steelman the skeptic.** Write the strongest version of the view that morality is *not* objective, in its own terms, with no rebuttal attached. Then say what writing it changed, if anything, about how the question looks to you.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

## The story

For most of the twentieth century, everyone knew what caused stomach ulcers. The lining of the stomach is bathed in acid, and stress and spicy food and too much worry drove the acid up, and the acid burned the holes. No bacterium could live in that acid bath, every textbook agreed, and so ulcers were a disease of modern nerves, managed with antacids and bland diets and, when those failed, surgery. The knowledge was settled. It was taught, prescribed, and believed by the people who had spent their careers studying the stomach.

In Perth, in 1979, a pathologist named **Robin Warren** kept seeing curved bacteria in the stomach tissue under his microscope, sitting in the acid where nothing was supposed to survive. A young physician named **Barry Marshall** took up the puzzle with him. In 1982 they cultured the organism, later named *Helicobacter pylori*, and proposed that this bacterium, not stress and not acid alone, caused most peptic ulcers. The reception was not gratitude. The claim contradicted what the field knew, and the field had every reason to be sure, so the establishment largely brushed it aside. There was no proof that would compel assent, no demonstration that the bacterium *must* be the cause. There was a conjecture, some suggestive tissue, and a long wall of expert certainty on the other side.

So in 1984 Marshall did something a closed, proven body of knowledge would never have needed. He drank a culture of *H. pylori*. Within days he was nauseated and vomiting; an endoscopy days later showed his stomach lining inflamed and the bacteria colonizing it, exactly as the conjecture predicted, and he treated the infection and recovered. The point of the self-experiment was not to *prove* the theory beyond all doubt, since one man's stomach proves no universal law. The point was to expose the conjecture to a test that could have killed it, to put it where the world could push back. The world pushed, the theory held, evidence accumulated, and what "everyone knew" gave way. Ulcers caused by bacteria could be cured with antibiotics, and were. In 2005 Warren and Marshall were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

There was never a foundation under this. No bedrock of certainty forced the bacterial theory on anyone; the people who held the old view were not being stupid, they were holding the best-supported belief of their day, and it was wrong. What corrected it was not proof but the thing proof was never needed for: a guess offered against the consensus, a test it might have failed, an error found and fixed. The settled truth about ulcers was objective the whole time, answerable to something beyond what the textbooks felt sure of, which is exactly why it could be got wrong for decades and then be put right. That is what objective knowledge looks like in the world. Not certainty. Correction. And if a thing as concrete as a stomach can be known that way, with no foundation and no proof, the question of whether the moral life can be known that way too is at least worth asking honestly, which is where this course now begins.
