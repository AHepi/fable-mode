## Interpretation notes

**What the course is.** A faithful exposition and **defense** of the claim that **morality is
objective**, built on **David Deutsch's** epistemology and arguments and their **Popperian** roots.
The thesis the course argues, at full strength: moral knowledge is objective, there are moral truths,
and we make real progress toward them, not because morality has a foundation, a proof, or an authority
behind it, but because moral knowledge grows the way all knowledge grows, by **conjecture and
criticism**, error-correction, and the search for **good (hard-to-vary) explanations**. The user asked
for the affirmative case made well; that is the course's spine.

**The decisive argument** (Deutsch's central move, build it carefully): the is/ought gap is *not* a
special problem for morality. You cannot derive scientific theories from observations either (Hume's
problem of induction; Popper's dissolution of it). Knowledge is never *derived* from foundations at
all; it is *created* by conjecture and criticized. So "morality has no foundation" puts morality in
exactly the same position as science, not a worse one. Objectivity without foundations comes from
error-correction and good explanations, not from certainty or proof. Moral progress (the abolition of
slavery as the correction of an error, not a change of fashion) is real, and its reality implies an
objective standard being approached, which relativism cannot account for.

**Stance and integrity (the house discipline, non-negotiable).** Present Deutsch's case
*persuasively and faithfully* (the user's ask) but with intellectual honesty, not propaganda:
- **Steelman every objection before answering it.** State the strongest version of relativism, Hume's
  is/ought gap, Moore's naturalistic fallacy / open-question argument, Mackie's argument from
  queerness / error theory, Ayer's emotivism, moral disagreement, and the evolutionary debunking
  arguments (Street, Joyce), *before* giving Deutsch's and Popper's answers.
- **Attribute, do not blend.** Hume ≠ Moore ≠ Mackie ≠ Ayer ≠ Popper ≠ Deutsch. Each claim pinned to
  the person who made it. Popper owns critical rationalism, the dissolution of induction, the open
  society, objective (World 3) knowledge; Deutsch owns the "hard to vary" criterion, the reach idea,
  the beginning-of-infinity optimism, and the explicit extension to moral and aesthetic objectivity.
- **Do not overclaim; name the live debate.** Be honest about what the argument does and does not
  establish. Deutsch's case arguably shows morality is *epistemically on a par with science* (no
  special is/ought problem) and that moral progress and error are real. Whether that fully secures
  robust **moral realism** is itself contested: an anti-realist can accept fallibilism and still deny
  mind-independent moral facts, and the moral analog of empirical test/prediction is itself disputed.
  The course makes the case at full strength *and* ends honestly on what remains a live metaethical
  debate, rather than declaring the matter closed. This is a defense, not a proof, and it says so.

**Scope — in.** Why morality is widely thought to be *non*-objective (the challenge, steelmanned):
relativism, is/ought, the naturalistic fallacy, the argument from queerness, emotivism, the "no moral
facts / no test" worry. The Popperian epistemology underneath: critical rationalism, fallibilism, the
death of justificationism/foundationalism, the dissolution of induction. The decisive move (no special
is/ought problem). Good explanations, "hard to vary," reach; objectivity without foundations. Moral
problems as real problems; moral progress and the reality of moral error; the self-undermining of
relativism. Deutsch's deeper link between the **growth of knowledge** and the **good**: persons as
universal explainers, the **open society** (Popper) as political error-correction, optimism ("problems
are inevitable; problems are soluble"), evil as the entrenchment of error and the destruction of the
means of correcting it. The aesthetics parallel (objective beauty, the hard-to-vary flower signal) as
a reinforcing case. The payoff: conjecture and criticism applied to ethics; the error-correcting
stance toward one's own morality.

**Scope — out.** A full survey of normative ethics (no systematic utilitarianism vs. deontology vs.
virtue-ethics comparison; those appear only as examples of moral conjectures to be criticized). A full
metaethics textbook (the rival positions appear as steelmanned objections, not as a balanced survey
for its own sake). Religious or authority-based grounding of morality (explicitly rejected by the
view). Deutsch's physics (multiverse, quantum computation) beyond what the epistemology needs. Partisan
applied-ethics culture-war cases. The throughline is always *the case for moral objectivity via
Deutsch's epistemology*, honestly bounded.

**Rigor / kind.** `humanities` (philosophy), `undergrad`. No math. Written in Deutsch's own accessible,
vivid register (he writes for general readers), but it engages real philosophy: the arguments are
reconstructed carefully and the objections are given their strongest form. Rigor lives in the
*structure of the argument* (objection at full strength, then the answer, attributed), in accurate
attribution, and in honest qualification, not in formalism.

**Assumed background.** None technical. Every term (objective, relativism, fallibilism, justificationism,
the is/ought gap, the naturalistic fallacy, error theory, emotivism, "hard to vary", reach, the open
society) is introduced in plain words at first use. The reader need not have read Deutsch, Popper, or
any philosophy.

**Format (new pipeline rules, automatic).** Each module ends in a fact-dense **closing story** (a real,
verifiable episode that illustrates the module's argument: a case of moral progress and error-correction,
a moment in Popper's or Deutsch's life and thought, a historical reversal of a settled "truth").
Verifiable facts only. **No em dashes.** **Enact** the disciplines, do not recite them. **No directive
metadiscourse.** Density over scaffolding.

**Length.** Thorough: roughly 9–11 modules. Arc: the challenge (why morality looks un-objective) →
the epistemology (fallibilism, no foundations) → the decisive move (no special is/ought problem) →
objectivity without foundations (good explanations, hard to vary) → moral progress is real and
relativism fails → knowledge and the good (the open society, optimism, evil as entrenched error) →
the aesthetics parallel → the honest close (what is established, what remains a live debate, and the
method).

compatible where natural (conjecture and criticism, hard to vary, reach) but do not depend on it.
