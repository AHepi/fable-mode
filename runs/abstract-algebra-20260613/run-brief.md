## Interpretation notes

**What the user asked for:** a first course in abstract algebra for a reader whose math
tops out at high-school level.

**Scope (in):** the theory of *groups* as the spine — operations and their rules, the group
axioms, families of examples (modular arithmetic, symmetry groups), subgroups, cyclic groups,
order and Lagrange's theorem, homomorphisms/isomorphisms — then a closing widen-the-lens module
on rings and fields so the reader sees where groups sit in the larger landscape.

**Scope (out):** no calculus. No heavy set theory. No deep ring/field theory, no Galois theory,
no advanced topics (Sylow, group actions as machinery, classification). Those belong to a second
course.

**Assumed background (concretely, per `_config/course-design.md`):** algebra (manipulating
expressions, solving equations), functions and their notation, basic geometry/trig, exponents.
**Not** assumed: any experience reading or writing proofs, comfort with $\forall/\exists$
machinery, modular arithmetic, or set-theoretic fluency.

**Rigor:** careful and honest but explained, never assumed. Proof is *introduced gently* — the
reader has likely never seen one. Walk every step and say why in prose. State Lagrange's theorem
and make it plausible; do not demand the reader produce formal proofs.

**Level field:** `highschool`. Target ~11 modules, ~10–25 min each.

**Calls made on ambiguity:** "abstract algebra" is broad; for a first course at this level the
standard and most rewarding spine is group theory, which this course follows, with rings/fields
as a final horizon module rather than full treatments.
