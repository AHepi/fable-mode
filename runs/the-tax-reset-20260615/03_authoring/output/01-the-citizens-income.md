Ngaire works 20 hours a week at a supermarket checkout in Ōtautahi. She gets a part-time wage and a Jobseeker supplement to top it up — but every extra shift she picks up, the supplement shrinks. At some hours of work, the combined effect of tax and benefit abatement takes back more than 70 cents of every dollar she earns. It is not laziness that keeps her at 20 hours. It is arithmetic.

The Opportunity Party (TOP) — which rebranded from "The Opportunities Party" but has kept the acronym — argues that this kind of trap is not a bug in the welfare system. It is a structural feature of means-tested welfare, baked in by design. Their answer is to replace most of that system with something simpler: a Citizen's Income.

If you've worked through the Essentials course, you'll recognise this: a Citizen's Income is a near-unconditional payment to almost all adults — a floor everyone stands on, rather than a net that only catches you when you fall. This module looks at what TOP's version actually contains, why they think it's needed, and what the strongest arguments against it are.

---

## The mechanism

TOP proposes paying approximately 370 dollars a week — around 19,400 dollars a year — to almost every adult in New Zealand. The payment is near-unconditional: it doesn't shrink because you pick up extra shifts, and it doesn't require proving you're actively looking for work. Means-testing only kicks in for those earning above roughly 350,000 dollars a year, a threshold that excludes the vast majority of the population.

TOP claims the payment would be near-tax-free in the hands of recipients, which matters most for people on low incomes who currently juggle income tax and benefit abatement simultaneously.

The Citizen's Income would replace most existing welfare payments — Jobseeker Support, Student Allowance, and others — and, crucially, NZ Super, the universal pension currently paid to New Zealanders over 65. Current superannuitants would receive a transitional top-up so that no one in retirement takes an immediate cut during the changeover period.

Alongside the main payment, TOP proposes a unified Child Support Income of approximately 350 dollars a week in a child's first year, folding in Best Start payments, the Family Tax Credit, and Paid Parental Leave into one simpler stream.

The gross cost of the Citizen's Income, by TOP's own modelling, is approximately 62.8 billion dollars a year. The land value tax (covered in the next module) is the primary funding mechanism. These are TOP's figures, not independently verified projections.

---

## The problem it targets

New Zealand's current welfare system is means-tested — payments shrink as your income rises, which sounds sensible until you run the numbers. The combined withdrawal rate from tax and benefit abatement can reach extremely high levels for low-income earners, creating what economists call "abatement traps": situations where working more leaves you little or no better off in cash terms, and sometimes actively worse off.

Ngaire's situation is not unusual. The system also requires claimants to actively prove eligibility, meet stand-down periods, submit to regular checks, and navigate different agencies for different payments. This complexity imposes real costs — not just in bureaucratic time, but in the stigma that attaches to being assessed and scrutinised. People often leave money on the table because the process is daunting, or because the abatement arithmetic makes the effort unrewarding.

Beyond welfare mechanics, TOP points to NZ's cost-of-living pressures: high rents, household debt, and stagnant wages for lower-income workers. A universal floor, they argue, would give everyone a stable base regardless of employment status, and would let people take risks — starting a business, retraining, caring for a family member — that the current system actively discourages.

TOP claims the Citizen's Income would lift approximately 50,000 families out of poverty. This is TOP's own claim; it reflects their modelling of who currently falls below income thresholds and would be brought above them by the payment floor.

---

## NZ fit

New Zealand's welfare system is relatively simple compared to some countries, but it remains patchwork: a different payment for being unemployed, for being a student, for being a sole parent, for being in early childhood with a new child. Navigating between them as life circumstances change involves bureaucratic friction at every transition.

TOP argues that a single, near-unconditional floor removes most of that friction at a stroke. In a high-cost economy — where housing alone consumes a large share of household income — a simple baseline that covers basic costs has obvious appeal. The Citizen's Income as a concept also sidesteps some of the politically toxic elements of welfare reform: it doesn't require proving you "deserve" the payment, and it applies equally to a gig worker, a student, and a full-time employee.

## The critiques

Here is where the floor metaphor earns its guard. A floor everyone stands on is only as useful as its height. TOP's floor sits at approximately 370 dollars a week — a figure that is, by design, close to the current Jobseeker Support rate for a single adult.

**Adequacy for disabled people.** The most pointed objection comes from disability advocates. Supported Living Payment recipients — people with serious long-term illness or disability — can currently receive approximately 800 dollars a week when all additional payments and supports are included. TOP's Citizen's Income, at 370 dollars, would be a significant cut for this group. The policy does not spell out what disability-specific supplements would sit on top of the Citizen's Income, and critics from welfare advocates argue that collapsing a system with disability-tailored payments into a flat universal rate risks serious harm to the most vulnerable recipients.

**Replacing NZ Super is a political and financial third rail.** NZ Super is currently universal and unlinked to prior work or savings contributions: every New Zealander over 65 gets it regardless of income. The current rate is higher than 370 dollars a week for most superannuitants, and many receive it on top of other income. TOP would replace Super with the Citizen's Income and add means-testing for those over roughly 350,000 dollars. The transitional top-up is intended to protect current retirees during the changeover, but the long-run direction is that NZ Super's universal, generous character would be wound back. For many older New Zealanders — and for the politicians who represent them — this is the most difficult feature of the proposal.

**The eligibility gap.** An independent analyst at Good Ideas NZ examined TOP's published costings and found a potential discrepancy: the numbers appear to imply approximately 3.2 million Citizen's Income recipients, while the adult population is closer to 4.1 million. If correct, that means roughly 900,000 adults may not qualify — but TOP has not fully explained who falls outside the payment or why. Until that gap is accounted for, the reach and cost of the scheme remain uncertain.

**The left's concern: removing statutory entitlements.** Some welfare advocates, even those broadly sympathetic to universal payment ideas, worry that replacing individual statutory entitlements — payments people have a legal right to — with a single flat payment reduces the legal and political protections that vulnerable people currently enjoy. A means-tested entitlement is harder for a future government to cut quietly than a universal payment; paradoxically, the universality that makes the Citizen's Income politically popular might also make it easier to trim.

**The right's objection: poor targeting.** From the other direction, critics who favour tightly targeted welfare argue that paying 370 dollars a week to a household earning 100,000 dollars a year is a wasteful use of public money. The argument for universality — that it removes stigma and traps — doesn't carry much weight if you believe the priority is directing help to those who need it most at the lowest possible cost. A dollar spent on someone already comfortable is a dollar not spent on someone genuinely desperate.

An abatement trap is the situation where earning more money causes your benefit to shrink fast enough that your total income barely rises — or even falls — for each extra dollar you earn. The Citizen's Income is designed to be near-unconditional: it doesn't shrink as you work more, so there's no combined tax-and-abatement cliff to discourage you from taking more shifts.

Even with a transitional top-up for current recipients, the direction of travel is a reduction in what older New Zealanders can expect — making it politically and personally significant.

## Exercises

**1. Restate the mechanism in your own words.**

Without looking back, write two sentences: what does TOP's Citizen's Income pay, to whom, and what does it replace?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A strong answer includes: approximately 370 dollars a week, to almost all adults, near-unconditional (no work test for the vast majority), replacing Jobseeker, Student Allowance, most welfare, and NZ Super — with a transitional top-up for current superannuitants, and a separate Child Support Income for early childhood. The key word "near-unconditional" matters: the only means-testing is at roughly 350,000 dollars income.

**Common gap:** confusing "near-unconditional" with "completely unconditional." The income cutoff above 350,000 dollars is a form of means-testing; it just affects very few people.

</details>

**2. Steelman a critic.**

Choose one of the critiques — adequacy for disabled people, replacing Super, the eligibility gap, or poor targeting — and write the strongest possible version of that objection. Aim for a version a sincere, well-informed critic would actually make, not a caricature.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A strong response will do three things: (a) state the critique precisely (e.g. for disability adequacy: "Supported Living recipients currently receive approximately 800 dollars a week; a flat 370-dollar payment would halve their income, and the policy doesn't specify what supplements would compensate"); (b) identify the underlying value at stake (protecting people with the highest needs; or fiscal efficiency; or preserving legal entitlements); and (c) avoid misrepresenting TOP's position (e.g. don't omit the transitional top-up when critiquing Super).

The exercise works best if you can articulate the critique in a way that a supporter of that view would recognise as fair.

**3. What would you need to know to judge whether the floor is high enough?**

The Citizen's Income sets a floor at approximately 370 dollars a week. What information would you need — and what questions would you ask — to assess whether that level is adequate for different groups?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Good answers might include: the actual cost of housing in different NZ regions (a 370-dollar floor has very different real value in Westport versus central Auckland); what happens to people with high medical or disability costs; what the current poverty line is in NZ and how 370 dollars compares; whether 370 dollars is indexed to inflation or wages over time; what supplementary payments (if any) would sit on top for specific groups; and whether 370 dollars is intended as a minimum or as an adequate income. The "adequacy" question depends heavily on what else you're paying for — which is why the disability critique is so pointed, and why the eligibility gap matters.

</details>

---

**4. How do the left and right critiques differ at their root?**

The political left and right both have objections to the Citizen's Income, but they object to different things. What is the core disagreement?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The left's concern is about adequacy and legal protection: the worry that a flat universal payment may be too low for those with the greatest needs, and that replacing statutory entitlements with a single payment reduces the protections vulnerable people currently have. The right's concern is about targeting: that paying everyone — including those who don't need it — wastes public money that could be directed more efficiently to those in genuine hardship. Both critiques are coherent; they reflect different underlying values about how welfare should work and what it's for. A reader can hold either, or a position somewhere between, without being irrational.

</details>

## Recap

TOP's Citizen's Income is a flat near-unconditional payment of approximately 370 dollars a week to almost all adults, replacing most welfare and NZ Super, and designed to dissolve the means-testing traps that currently punish people for working more. TOP claims it would lift approximately 50,000 families out of poverty; these are TOP's own figures. The honest debate about the proposal centres not on whether a floor is a good idea in principle, but on how high it sits — and who might fall through gaps in it: disabled people currently receiving more, retirees who see Super replaced, and perhaps 900,000 adults whose eligibility the published costings don't fully explain. The next module turns to how TOP proposes to pay for it — and that is where the Land Value Tax enters the picture.
