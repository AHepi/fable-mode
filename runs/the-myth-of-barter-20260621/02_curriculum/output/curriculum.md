## Course metadata (for `_course.md`)

- **title:** The Myth of Barter
- **description:** A history-first account of where money actually came from, not from barter but from credit and debt recorded millennia before the first coin, and why the textbook origin story survives anyway.
- **level:** intro
- **kind:** humanities
- **subject:** Humanities
- **prerequisites:** none (a curious adult; no economics, anthropology, or history assumed)
- **tags:** [money, barter, debt, economic-history, anthropology, ideology-critique, history-of-money]
- **estimatedHours:** 4
- **sources** (authors/works at the dossier's confidence level; no invented page numbers):
  - Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776)
  - Carl Menger, "On the Origins of Money" (1892)
  - Caroline Humphrey, "Barter and Economic Disintegration," *Man* (1985)
  - R. A. Radford, "The Economic Organisation of a P.O.W. Camp," *Economica* (1945)
  - A. Mitchell Innes, "What is Money?" (1913) and "The Credit Theory of Money" (1914)
  - Georg Friedrich Knapp, *The State Theory of Money* (1905; Eng. trans. 1924)
  - Geoffrey Ingham, *The Nature of Money* (2004)
  - Philip Grierson, *The Origins of Money* (1977)
  - Michael Hudson, *…and forgive them their debts* (2018)
  - David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011)
  - The Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE); the biblical Jubilee (Leviticus 25)

## The arc

The throughline the brief asks for: **"here is the story you were taught" → "here is why it cannot be the history" → "here is what actually happened" → "here is what money really is, and why the myth survives."** History is the spine; the two abstract models of money are explored as objects of study in one dedicated module, after the record is in hand, never as the opening frame.

## Module specs

### 01 — The Story You Were Taught
- **slug:** `01-the-story-you-were-taught`
- **summary:** "The textbook origin of money, stated at its most convincing: barter, its frustrations, and money as the invention that fixed them."
- **objectives:**
  - State the standard barter-to-money sequence and the "double coincidence of wants" in plain words.
  - Explain why the story is intuitive and where Adam Smith and the textbooks place it.
  - Identify that the story is usually told as conjectural history, an origin reasoned out rather than dug up.
- **prereqs:** none
- **est. minutes:** 24
- **notes:** Pure steelman. Nothing debunked yet. Smith's "truck, barter, and exchange" and the butcher/baker; Menger's saleable-commodity account; the intro-econ textbook page one. **Do not strawman Smith.** Close by naming the **commodity theory of money** as "the theory this story needs," to be examined fully in module 06. Owns: barter, double coincidence of wants, the standard sequence, conjectural history.

### 02 — Why It Can't Be the History
- **slug:** `02-why-it-cant-be-the-history`
- **summary:** "No society ever ran on barter and then upgraded to money; the record shows credit and obligation came first, and barter turns up only in the margins."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain Caroline Humphrey's finding that no pure barter economy has been ethnographically described.
  - Describe what pre-money communities actually used instead (gift, reciprocity, socially tracked credit).
  - Distinguish genuine barter (between strangers, or when money collapses) from the foundational stage the myth requires.
- **prereqs:** 01
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** The empirical hammer. **Crucial nuance (anti-overclaim):** barter is real, but as a money *substitute* (Radford's POW cigarette economy, 1945; post-Soviet barter) or trade between strangers, never the primitive stage the myth needs. Teach the precise claim, not "barter never happened." Owns: the ethnographic verdict, gift/reciprocity/redistribution (glossed, compatible with companion), barter-as-substitute.

### 03 — Credit and the Ledger
- **slug:** `03-credit-and-the-ledger`
- **summary:** "Thousands of years before the first coin, Mesopotamian scribes were pricing goods and recording debts in silver and barley they never handed over."
- **objectives:**
  - Distinguish a unit of account (the measuring-stick for prices and debts) from a medium of exchange (the thing that changes hands).
  - Explain how Mesopotamian temple and palace accounting used money-of-account with no coin circulating.
  - Explain how a tally records a debt, making "money as a record of who owes whom" concrete.
- **prereqs:** 01, 02
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** The historical core and the course's hinge. **Unit of account vs. medium of exchange (concept C9) is the load-bearing distinction**; introduce it with a concrete picture and re-gloss it every time it returns (05, 06, 07). Mesopotamia ~3000 BCE; writing arose largely for accounts; the split-tally stick. When the credit-accounting lands, name the **credit/chartalist theory** as the rival to be examined in 06. Owns: unit of account, medium of exchange, money-of-account, the tally, "writing arose for accounts."

### 04 — Debt Was Law Before Coins Were Minted
- **slug:** `04-debt-was-law`
- **summary:** "A Babylonian king capped interest rates and regulated who could be seized for debt a thousand years before anyone struck a coin."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain what the Code of Hammurabi shows about debt as a governed legal institution.
  - Explain debt bondage and why it marks the credit-first past as no golden age.
  - Distinguish the evidence (the code regulates interest and debt) from the interpretation (what that implies about money).
- **prereqs:** 03
- **est. minutes:** 24
- **notes:** Hammurabi (~1750 BCE) is the **primary-source slot**. Debt as legally governed long before coinage. **Anti-romanticizing anchor:** debt bondage was real; the past was not gentle. Keep evidence/interpretation seam visible; do not quote specific clause numbers or rates unless certain. Owns: Hammurabi, debt bondage (full treatment), debt-as-governed-institution.

### 05 — Coins Are Late: Money as a State Technology
- **slug:** `05-coins-are-late`
- **summary:** "The first coins appear thousands of years after the ledgers, and they arrive through armies and taxes, not through barter."
- **objectives:**
  - State roughly when and where coins first appear and why the date overturns the myth's sequence.
  - Explain the state-war-tax account of how coinage spread (paying soldiers; taxes payable only in coin).
  - Distinguish the solid chronology from the contested causal claim that states helped call markets into being.
- **prereqs:** 03
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** The date shock (~7th c. BCE, Lydia/Ionia), landing hardest now that the reader knows money predates coins. The state/war/tax nexus (Grierson; Graeber's "military-coinage complex" — attribute as interpretation, mark the seam). This is where "money is apolitical" dies. No teleology. Owns: coinage's late date, the state/war/tax nexus, markets-partly-state-made.

### 06 — What Money Actually Is: Two Theories
- **slug:** `06-what-money-actually-is`
- **summary:** "The barter story is the origin myth one theory of money needs; a rival theory, fitting the record better, says money was never a commodity at all."
- **objectives:**
  - State the textbook functions of money and the commodity theory built on them.
  - Explain the credit/chartalist theory of money as a unit of account and measure of debt.
  - Assess what each theory explains and fails to explain, and why the choice between them is unsettled.
- **prereqs:** 02, 03, 04, 05
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** **The one models module, placed after the history** (the user's steer). The two theories are *objects of study*. **Steelman the commodity theory** (metals are durable, divisible, portable) and name it as "the theory the barter myth serves." The credit theory (Innes, Knapp, Ingham, Grierson). Fungibility and fiat as the tools to judge. **Flag the live debate** (chartalism vs. commodity theory is genuinely unsettled); honest scorecard, no winner crowned. **Don't blend the thinkers; don't turn chartalism into MMT advocacy.** Owns: functions of money, commodity theory, credit/chartalist theory, fungibility, fiat, metallism.

### 07 — The Morality of Debt
- **slug:** `07-the-morality-of-debt`
- **summary:** "Before it was a number, a debt was a bond between people, and whole societies once wiped debts clean on purpose, which is why 'you must always pay' is history, not law of nature."
- **objectives:**
  - Trace how a debt shifts from a personal relationship to an impersonal, transferable quantity.
  - Describe periodic clean-slate debt cancellations (Mesopotamian and the biblical Jubilee) and what they imply.
  - Examine the intuition that debts must always be repaid as a historical and ideological artifact rather than a fact of nature.
- **prereqs:** 06
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** The sharpest ideology-critique payoff. Relationship → quantity (same family of move as the companion's commodity fetishism; callback in spirit). Clean-slate cancellations (*amargi*/*andurarum*, the Jubilee — Hudson; **verify the *amargi* gloss, present cautiously**). Examine the reader's own "debts must be paid" feeling; do not preach. **Don't romanticize** (debt bondage from 04 is the counterweight). Owns: debt-as-relationship, relationship-to-quantity, clean-slate cancellations, the morality of debt.

### 08 — Why the Myth Survives
- **slug:** `08-why-the-myth-survives`
- **summary:** "A false story that keeps getting taught is doing work for someone; this one makes the trading individual look natural and quietly writes the state out of the history of money."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain the ideological work the barter myth performs (naturalizing the market individual; erasing the state and social obligation; making money look apolitical).
  - Explain how the myth underwrites both the commodity theory and a particular politics of debt.
  - Apply "whose interests does this story serve?" without sliding into a claim of deliberate fabrication.
- **prereqs:** 01, 02, 03, 04, 05, 06, 07
- **est. minutes:** 24
- **notes:** Ideology critique applied to a textbook page. **Guard:** function, not conspiracy — the myth's *work* is the object, not a claim that anyone plotted it; Smith did not write a cover story. Pulls the whole course together. Owns: the ideological work of the myth, the function-not-conspiracy distinction.

### 09 — How We Know, and How to Read an Origin Story
- **slug:** `09-how-we-know`
- **summary:** "The tools that overturned the myth, and the single question you can now put to any origin story: is this remembered, or is it just what some theory needs to be true?"
- **objectives:**
  - Describe how the historical claims in this course are actually known (cuneiform records, ethnography, numismatics, law codes).
  - Apply the discipline of separating evidence from interpretation to a claim of your choosing.
  - Use the diagnostic question "is this remembered, or required by a theory?" on a new origin story.
- **prereqs:** 01-08
- **est. minutes:** 24
- **notes:** The method payoff (mirrors the companion's closing method module). Should feel like recognition of what the course has been doing. Hands the reader a transferable skill. Owns: the historian's/anthropologist's method, evidence-vs-interpretation as a tool, the "remembered or required?" question.

## Scope & integrity checks (carried from the brief and dossier)

- **History-first held:** six history/ethnography modules (01, 02, 03, 04, 05, 07) to one models module (06); the models arrive after the record and are framed as tools to interpret it, never the opening frame.
- **Stance discipline:** every module steelmans before it dismantles; evidence kept visibly separate from interpretation; live debates named, not resolved (the historical-claim-vs-logical-fable status of the myth in 01/02; chartalism vs. commodity theory in 06; how far to trust Graeber throughout; the "primordial debt" over-reach, kept out or flagged).
- **Author pitfalls to avoid:** overclaiming "barter never happened" (02 teaches the precise claim); turning chartalism into MMT advocacy (06); romanticizing the pre-coinage past (04 and 07 carry the debt-bondage counterweight); teleology; flattening Smith; blending the thinkers; ideology-critique-as-conspiracy (08).
- **Pairing:** vocabulary and stance compatible with "How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural" (the gift, reciprocity, naturalization, debt) so the two read as companions in the Path; this course still stands alone and teaches its own terms from scratch.
- **Hard pipeline rules:** no em dashes anywhere in prose; length follows the idea, not a word count; every specialist term glossed in plain words at first use; quote any frontmatter value containing a colon.
