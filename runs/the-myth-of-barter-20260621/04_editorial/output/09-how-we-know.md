## The hook

You came into this course believing something, and you are leaving believing something else. That much is ordinary. People change their minds. The interesting part is *how* it happened. You were not argued out of the barter story by a cleverer story. You were shown a clay tablet, a coin, a law carved in stone, and a shelf of ethnographies. The belief did not survive the looking.

The real gift of these nine modules is not a fact about money. It is a method. You can now tell the difference between a past that is recorded and a past that is only reasoned out, and that difference travels well beyond money. There is nothing new about ledgers or jubilees left to learn here. What remains is the thing your hands have already been doing, named plainly enough to use again, with a different myth, on your own.

## Context & background

The course began, back in module 01, by asking what *kind* of story the barter myth is. It is conjectural history, an origin reasoned out from first principles ("imagine a primitive society, and ask what sensible people would do") rather than dug from any record. Nobody handed it down from memory. It is what the logic of trade seems to require, run backward from the present.

That observation came with a question. Of any origin story, one can ask: **is this remembered, or required by a theory?** A *remembered* claim rests on something that survived from the time itself, a tablet, a coin, an eyewitness account. A claim *required by a theory* rests on what some explanation needs to be true in order to hold together. The barter myth lives mostly in the second category. We believe money was born to cure the frustrations of barter not because anyone wrote it down, but because the commodity theory of money needs it to have happened that way.

The eight modules since were that question at work. Every time the course reached for a tablet or a date instead of an argument, it was sorting the remembered from the required. The tools it used are the answer to a fair challenge: if the textbook does not know how money began, why trust the alternative? The honest reply is that the alternative is not another conjecture. It is built from things that actually came down to us.

## Narrative / exposition

Four kinds of evidence did the work in this course. Together they are the working kit of a historian or an anthropologist studying money, and you already know how to read each one.

The first is **cuneiform**, the wedge-shaped script that scribes pressed into wet clay in ancient Mesopotamia. Module 03 turned on it. The decisive image was a clay tablet that is a receipt, a debt of barley or silver recorded around 3000 BCE with no coin anywhere in sight. That single object dynamites the myth's sequence, because it shows money working as a unit of account, the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in, thousands of years before money existed as a medium of exchange, the thing that physically changes hands. And clay is a gift to the historian. It does not rot. It does not melt down for its metal the way a coin does. A fire that destroys a library of paper *bakes* a library of clay tablets harder. We have hundreds of thousands of these records, and most of them, tellingly, are not poems or prayers but accounts. Writing in this corner of the world arose largely to keep track of who owed whom.

The second tool is **ethnography**, the close study of how actual living societies are organized, by observers who go and watch. Module 02 leaned its whole weight here. The barter myth makes a claim about how *people* behave when they have no money, and that is a claim you can check, because there are and were communities studied closely by people on the ground. What those observers found was not haggling over chickens. It was the running tab, the gift, reciprocity, socially tracked credit at a village where everyone knows everyone. The myth predicts barter as the natural pre-money state. Ethnography went and looked, and the prediction failed.

The third tool is **numismatics**, the study of coins and the objects that served as money, including when and where they were made and what they were made of. Module 05 ran on it. Coins can be dated, by their metal, their stamps, the rulers named on them, the hoards they turn up in. And the dating delivered a shock: the first coins appear around the seventh century BCE in Lydia and Ionia, thousands of years *after* the Mesopotamian ledgers. The thing the myth treats as the climax of money's story turns out to be a latecomer. Numismatics is what let the course say "coins are late" and mean it as a measured fact rather than a slogan.

The fourth is the **law code**, and module 04 read the oldest famous one, the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian set of laws from around 1750 BCE. A law that regulates interest, debt, and who can be seized for an unpaid debt is doing something a barter society would have no use for. You do not need rules about debt bondage if debt is not yet a governed institution. The code is evidence that debt was law a thousand years before anyone struck a coin.

These four share something. Each is a *survival*, a thing left behind by the past itself rather than reasoned out about the past from a distance. That is the whole difference between the case this course made and the case the textbook made. The textbook reasoned. This course pointed.

The same method turns its sharpest edge on the course itself. Evidence is not the same as what gets made of it. A tablet recording a debt in silver is evidence. "Therefore money is essentially a measure of debt" is **interpretation**, and a particular school's interpretation at that, the reading A. Mitchell Innes and David Graeber gave it. The tablet does not speak that sentence; a theorist does. The credit theory of money fits the record better than the commodity theory does, and the course said so. But "fits the record better" is a long way from "proven," and the argument between the two theories is genuinely unsettled. What the course offers is a method, not a new doctrine to swap in for the old one. Walk away certain that chartalism is the plain truth, and you have taken the conclusion and left the lesson.

## Primary source

For most of this course the primary sources were objects, the tablet and the coin and the stone. The last is a sentence. One sentence has been load-bearing under module 02 and is worth reading as a source in its own right. The anthropologist Caroline Humphrey, summing up the ethnographic record, put it this way:

> "No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described, let alone the emergence from it of money."
>
> Caroline Humphrey, "Barter and Economic Disintegration," *Man* (1985)

The sentence carries something precise and devastating to the myth. It says that the specific thing the standard sequence requires, a whole society running on direct goods-for-goods swaps and then graduating to money, has never once been found by the people whose job is to find such things. That is a statement about the evidence, and it is the empirical hole at the center of the myth.

What it does *not* say matters just as much. It does not say barter never happens. Module 02 was careful about exactly this: barter is real between strangers, or when an existing money system collapses, as in R. A. Radford's prison-camp economy where cigarettes circulated as money among people who already understood money. Humphrey's verdict is narrow and exact. It denies the barter *economy* as a foundational stage, not the act of bartering. There is one more limit on it. "No example has ever been described" is a claim about the record we have, which is an argument from absence. A single well-documented barter economy, if one were ever found, would dent it. That the dent has not come in the decades since is the strength of the claim, but its *shape* is still "we have looked hard and not found it," not "it is logically impossible." That distinction is the line between using the evidence and overclaiming it.

## Analysis & interpretation

The diagnostic question works on any origin story, not only this one, and that portability is the skill that lasts.

It runs in two steps. The first is the load-bearing claim about the past: under the decoration, what does the story need to have *happened* for its point to stand? The barter myth needs a real sequence, barter first, then money invented to fix it. The second step is the diagnostic itself. Is the claim a memory, attested in something that survived, or only what the theory needs to be true? The test is whether a tablet, a coin, a witness, a record of any kind came down from the time itself, or whether the only support is that the claim makes a tidy story cohere.

There is a trap, the one module 01 named. A story can be told in the full voice of history, "long ago, people did X," and still rest on nothing but what the theory requires. The "long ago" is doing rhetorical work. It dresses a deduction in the costume of a memory. The barter myth runs this move, and so do countless accounts of how nations began, why some custom exists, how a market or a technology came to be the standard. Many such stories are partly remembered and partly required, and the interesting work is sorting which parts are which.

Two cautions keep the tool from becoming a blunt instrument. "Required by a theory" is not an insult, and it is not a synonym for false. Module 01 was scrupulous about this: conjectural history is a respectable way to reason about a past that left no records, and Adam Smith was reasoning carefully, not lying. The diagnostic is not a license to dismiss a claim for being theory-driven. It only names what *kind* of support a claim has, which sets up the right next question: does the actual record, where there is one, back it up or undercut it? For the barter myth the record undercut it. For some other story it might confirm the conjecture. The answer comes from looking, not from the diagnosis alone.

The tool also has to survive being pointed at its owner. Module 08 argued that the barter myth persists because it does *ideological work*, making the trading individual look natural and writing the state out of money's history. That is a strong claim, and it is interpretation, not a tablet, which is why the course flagged it as interpretation and warned against the slide into conspiracy: the claim is about a story's *function*, not anyone's plot. The credit theory stands on the same footing. It is better supported on the present record. It is not the new gospel. If a better-documented account of money's origins arrived tomorrow, the method here would oblige a reader to follow the evidence to it, even away from everything this course concluded. A method abandoned the moment it inconveniences you was never a method. It was just a side picked in advance.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below names what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Your own origin story.** Pick an origin story you carry from outside economics: how a holiday or tradition began, why some custom or rule exists, how a piece of technology became the standard, how your family or your town tells its own beginning. Run the diagnostic on it. State the load-bearing claim about the past, then judge whether it is mostly *remembered* (resting on a record you could point to) or mostly *required by a theory* (resting on what makes the story cohere). Say how you can tell, and be honest about the parts you cannot decide.
2. **Mark the seam.** Take one claim from this course that you now believe, and split it cleanly into its evidence and its interpretation. What is the object or record, and what is the reading laid on top of it? Then name what evidence, if it turned up, would make you doubt the interpretation while leaving the evidence untouched.
3. **Defend the textbook's method.** The barter myth is conjectural history, and this course treated conjectural history as respectable when no record survives. Make the strongest case you can that reasoning out a past from first principles is a legitimate tool, then say exactly where it failed in the case of money and why.
4. **The honest scorecard.** This course argued the credit theory fits the record better than the commodity theory, while insisting the debate is unsettled. Write a few sentences a fair reader could accept from *both* camps about what is actually known versus what is still argued. Resist crowning a winner.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Names the load-bearing factual claim a story makes about the past, rather than retelling the whole story or arguing about its moral.
- Uses **remembered** versus **required by a theory** as a real test, showing the reasoning behind the verdict (what would count as a surviving record) and admitting genuine uncertainty rather than forcing a clean answer.
- Keeps **evidence** and **interpretation** visibly separate, and can say what new evidence would shift the interpretation without touching the underlying object.
- Treats "required by a theory" as a diagnosis, not a dismissal: does not use it to declare a claim false, and grants that conjectural reasoning is legitimate where no record survives.
- On money specifically, resists triumphalism: states that the credit theory is better supported on the present record *and* that the question is open, attributing contested readings (Innes, Graeber) to their authors rather than as settled fact.

## The story

There is a cliff in western Iran, above the old road from Babylon to the Persian capital, at a place now called Bisotun and long known as Behistun. High on its limestone face, far above where a passerby could reach, the Persian king Darius I had an enormous inscription carved around 520 BCE. It tells, in the king's own telling, how he came to the throne and crushed the rebellions against him. The relief shows Darius with his foot on a fallen rival and a line of bound captives before him. Below and around it run columns of wedge-shaped writing in three languages: Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian. The same boast, three times over, in three scripts.

That triple text was the gift, though no one for more than two thousand years could read a word of it. The wedge-writing of the ancient Near East, cuneiform, had gone silent. The clay tablets of Mesopotamia, the temple ledgers, the debt-records, the law of Hammurabi himself, all of it sat mute in the ground and in collections, marks no living person could turn back into language. A whole civilization's accounts were there to be read, and unreadable.

The cliff was part of how that lock was picked. In the late 1830s and into the 1840s, a British army officer named Henry Rawlinson set himself to copy the Behistun inscription, which meant getting his hands and eyes onto carvings cut deliberately out of reach. By the surviving accounts he climbed to the inscription again and again over several seasons, working from ladders and narrow ledges, and the higher and more damaged sections were reached only with ropes and, the story goes, the nerve of a local boy lowered across the rock face to take paper squeezes of the signs. How much personal danger this involved is hard to pin down precisely at this distance, but the basic difficulty is not in doubt: the text had been placed where it could be seen and not touched, and someone had to touch it.

What the copying yielded was a key. Because the Old Persian column used a simpler, partly alphabetic cuneiform, and because scholars could make headway on Old Persian from known later languages, the Behistun text gave a long passage whose meaning could be worked out and then matched, sign by sign, against the far harder Babylonian version beside it. Rawlinson published his readings, and others pressed on. Edward Hincks, an Irish clergyman, and the German-born Jules Oppert were among those who turned the opening into a working decipherment. Cracking Babylonian cuneiform was a collective labor, argued over and checked, that Rawlinson's cliff-work supplied the raw material for rather than a feat of one man alone. The scholarly world wanted proof it was real, and in 1857 four of them, working independently from a single freshly found inscription, produced translations that agreed. The script was readable again.

Set that against the textbook's barter story. The barter myth was reasoned out in a study from what trade seemed to require, and no shovel ever turned up the society it describes. The credit-first picture rests on the opposite kind of thing: hundreds of thousands of actual documents, debts in silver and barley pressed into clay around 3000 BCE, the interest ceilings and the seizures and the three-year limit on the king's stone. Every one of those is a survival, remembered in the record, not required by a theory. And every one is legible to us only because people scaled a cliff and others spent years matching sign to sign until the marks spoke. The difference between a past you can read and a past you can only assume is, in the end, work like that on the rock at Behistun. The myth asked you to imagine. The record asks only that you learn to read it, and then look.
