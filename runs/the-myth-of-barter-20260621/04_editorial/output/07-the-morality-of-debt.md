## The hook

Here is a sentence you almost certainly agree with. *You should pay back what you owe.* It barely feels like an opinion. It feels like one of the load-bearing beams of decency, somewhere near "don't lie" and "keep your promises." A person who borrows and refuses to repay has done something not just inconvenient but a little bit wrong, and most of us feel that wrongness in the body, the way we feel a broken promise.

Now set that feeling beside a fact. For thousands of years, in more than one civilization, rulers periodically stood up and cancelled debts. Not a few hardship cases. All of them, or great classes of them, at a stroke: the loans erased, the people who had been seized for unpaid debts sent home, the slate wiped clean. This was not chaos or revolution. It was policy, sometimes announced at a king's coronation, sometimes written into religious law to recur on a fixed calendar.

So we have a clash. On one side, the gut conviction that a debt is a moral absolute, that it follows you until it is paid. On the other, whole societies treating debts as things that can be, and sometimes should be, simply called off. This module is about that clash. It does not exist to talk you out of your conviction; that conviction points at something real, and we will give it its due. It exists to show you that the conviction has a history. The feeling that a debt **must always be paid** did not fall from the sky. It was built, and to see how it was built is to see that it could have been built differently.

## Context & background

Begin before the clash, with what a debt was before it became the kind of thing a king could cancel by decree.

Long before there were coins, and even before the clay ledgers you met in module 03, where Mesopotamian scribes reckoned debts in silver and barley with no coin in sight, people owed each other things. They always had. But the owing was not yet a number. A **debt** in this older sense was an obligation tangled up inside a relationship. Debt is what you owe; credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later; they are the two sides of one relation. In this older world both sides lived inside a bond between particular people, not on a tablet.

Picture the shapes it took. A neighbour helps you raise a roof, and now you owe him a day's work, or a meal, or a turn of help when his own roof sags. A family gives a daughter in marriage and receives a bride-price in return, cattle or goods that bind the two families together for a generation. A man is killed in a feud, and his kin are owed a blood-payment, a fixed compensation that, paid, can stop the killing. Early law had a word for that last kind of debt. **Wergild** was a set compensation owed for an injury or a death under early customary law, a price on a wound or a life, meant to settle a score without more blood.

Notice what all of these have in common. The debt is owed by *someone* to *someone*, and the relationship is half the point. The neighbour does not want exactly his hour back; he wants the ongoing fact that the two of you help each other. This is close to what module 02 called **the gift** and **reciprocity**, give-and-take that builds and repays a relationship rather than clearing a price. A debt of this kind is not really meant to be cleanly, finally, paid off and forgotten. To pay it off completely and walk away would be to end the relationship, which is often the last thing anyone wants. The debt is the thread that keeps two people tied.

Glossed plainly: in the beginning, a debt was a bond between people, older than money and older than coins.

## Narrative / exposition

So how did the thread become a number? How did an obligation you felt toward a particular neighbour turn into a quantity that a stranger could buy, sell, and enforce against you in a court?

The answer is the thing this whole course has been circling: money. Recall the load-bearing distinction from module 03, a **unit of account**, the measuring-stick you reckon debts in ("you owe me three shekels"), versus a **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changes hands. Once you can measure a debt in a unit of account, something quiet and enormous happens to it. It becomes a number.

Watch what the number does. First, it lets the debt be *recorded*. The neighbourly favour lived in memory and goodwill; "three shekels of silver" can be pressed into clay and kept. Second, the number lets the debt be *compared and added*. Your obligation to me and your obligation to a man you have never met can now sit in the same column, in the same unit, and be summed. Third, and this is the hinge, the number lets the debt be *transferred*. If you owe me three shekels, and I owe a third person three shekels, I can hand him your debt to settle my own. He has never met you. He does not care about your roof or your daughter or your feud. He cares about three shekels. And fourth, the number lets the debt be *enforced against a stranger*, because a recorded, transferable quantity can be taken to whatever authority stands behind it.

Here is the move, named: a debt goes from a **relationship to a quantity**. The personal obligation, embedded in a bond between particular people, hardens into an impersonal, measured, transferable, collectible sum. This is exactly what module 06 called **fungibility**, interchangeability: any one shekel of silver is as good as any other, and now any one debt of three shekels is as good as any other. The number does not know whose roof it helped raise. That forgetting is what makes it powerful, and it is also what makes it cold.

This is the same *family* of move that haunts the study of money and value more broadly: a thick human relation gets thinned into a thing you can count and trade, and once thinned, the human relation behind it drops out of view. You do not need a special term for it to feel it. A favour between friends and a line item on a balance sheet are not the same kind of obligation, even when the amount is identical.

And now resist the easy turn this story wants to take. It is tempting to read all of this as a fall: the warm web of mutual obligation, cold-stamped into hard cash and heartless ledgers, paradise lost. Do not. Module 04 is the standing counterweight. Debt was governed law long before coins (Hammurabi's code, bondage and all), and the older, more personal debts were not gentle. **Debt bondage**, being forced into servitude to work off a debt you could not pay, with yourself or your wife or your children pledged and then seized, is among the oldest features of debt we can document. The relationship that a personal debt embedded you in could be a relationship of domination. A man who owed you his labour because his harvest failed was bound to you, and the bond could be brutal. The point of this module is not that personal debt was kind and quantified debt is cruel. The point is that debt has a history, and that the feeling we attach to it is part of that history, not above it.

## Primary source

Of all the clean-slate cancellations, the most fully preserved sits in a religious text: the biblical Jubilee. Read it as a historian reads any source, as evidence of what some people, at some time, set down as law and ideal, not as a sermon and not as a record of what was always done in practice. The Book of Leviticus lays out a cycle. Every seventh year is a sabbatical; after seven sevens of years, the fiftieth year is the Jubilee, and in it debts are released, indentured people go free, and land returns to the families that lost it.

Here is the heart of it, from Leviticus 25.

> "And ye shall hallow the fiftieth year, and proclaim liberty throughout all the land unto all the inhabitants thereof: it shall be a jubile unto you; and ye shall return every man unto his possession, and ye shall return every man unto his family."
>
> Leviticus 25:10 (King James Version)

The chapter goes on to command that land sold in hardship reverts in the Jubilee, that an Israelite fallen into debt-servitude is not to be held as a permanent slave but released, and it gives a reason that cuts to the root: the land and the people belong to God, and so cannot be sold off forever. Property and debt, in this text, are not absolute. They run on a timer.

Two cautions, because this course is built on keeping evidence and interpretation apart. First, the *text* is solid; how regularly the Jubilee was actually practiced is a separate and much less certain question, and you should hold it as open. A law on the books is evidence of an ideal, not proof of a routine. Second, this is the biblical instance of a wider pattern. The historian **Michael Hudson**, who studies debt and its cancellation in the ancient Near East, has argued that Mesopotamian kings issued their own debt-remission decrees long before Leviticus was written, freeing people from debt-servitude and annulling certain debts, often on taking the throne. There is even a Sumerian word, *amargi*, often glossed as something like "freedom" or "return to the mother," that scholars connect to the cancellation of debts: freedom imagined as release from debt. Hold that last detail loosely, as a reading rather than a settled fact, but notice how startling it is even as a possibility. The word for being free might be, at its root, the word for your debts being wiped clean.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now bring the two halves of the module together, the gut conviction and the historical fact, and read them against each other.

Start by giving the conviction its full strength, because a feeling this widely shared is usually pointing at something real. An economy, even the simplest, runs on trust. If I lend you grain through the winter, I do so because I expect it back in the spring; if no one could count on repayment, no one would lend, and the grain would rot in one barn while a family starved in the next. Repaying a debt is a kind of promise-keeping, and promise-keeping is one of the things that lets human beings cooperate with people they cannot watch every minute. The feeling that debts should be honoured is not a trick played on you by the powerful. It is, in part, the felt sense of why cooperation works. Hold onto that. Any reading of this material that sneers at the intuition has lost the thread.

But now hold the intuition up to the light of the history and watch what changes. The conviction presents itself as timeless, a law of nature: a debt is a debt, owed in full and forever, full stop, no asterisk. The clean-slate cancellations are evidence that this is not how every society has seen it. The kings who wiped debts clean, the law that put property and bondage on a fifty-year timer, were not lawless. They were acting on a competing principle: that a society can be wrecked by debts as surely as by drought or war, and that when debt has bound too many people too tightly, the right thing, the *stabilizing* thing, is to release them. On that older view, the absolute is not the debt. The absolute is the community that the debt was supposed to serve, and when the two collide, the debt yields.

See what this does to the word "must". The sentence "debts **must always be paid**" sounds like a report on the nature of reality. The history reframes it as a choice among possible rules, a choice that real societies have made differently. Some held repayment sacred. Some held periodic release sacred. The feeling in your chest is real, but it is the feeling of *one* rule, the one you were raised inside, not the readout of a universal law. That is what it means to call the morality of debt a historical and ideological artifact: not that it is fake, but that it is *made*, that it has a date and a place and could have been made otherwise.

A rival reading deserves a hearing, because the seam between evidence and interpretation runs right through here. One could argue that the cancellations were not a different morality at all, but the same morality bending under pressure: debts that genuinely should have been paid, forgiven only because enforcing them had become impossible or politically dangerous. On that reading the absolute survives, and the Jubilee is the exception that proves it. The honest answer is that the evidence underdetermines the choice. What the cancellations *prove* is narrow and solid: that "all debts, always, no exceptions" has not been a human universal. What they *imply* about the deep nature of obligation is interpretation, and reasonable people read it more than one way. Keep the proof and the implication in separate hands.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer here. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below describes what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Steelman the absolute, then test it.** First, write the strongest case you can that debts genuinely *should* always be repaid, drawing on trust, promises, and cooperation. Then ask: does your strongest case actually require *no exceptions ever*, or does it leave room for a clean-slate cancellation in a crisis? Be honest about where the argument bends.
2. **Relationship to quantity, in your own life.** Describe a real obligation you feel that is *not* a clean number, a favour owed, a debt of gratitude, something between people. What would change about it if it were converted into an exact, transferable amount of money that a stranger could collect? What would be gained, and what would be lost?
3. **Read the source as a source.** Looking only at the Leviticus 25 passage above, what can you legitimately conclude, and what can you *not*? Distinguish what the text is evidence *of* (an ideal, a law on the books) from what it would take to know whether the Jubilee was actually practiced.
4. **Against romanticizing.** Module 04 gave us debt bondage; this one gives us the warmth of personal obligation. Using both, argue against the easy story that "debt used to be human and became cold." Where was the older, personal debt *not* gentle?

## Recap

A debt began as a bond between people, a favour, a bride-price, a blood-payment, older than money and older than coins. Money turned the bond into a number, and the number let the debt be recorded, compared, transferred, and enforced against a stranger who knew nothing of the relationship behind it: a move from **relationship to quantity** that made debt powerful and impersonal at once. That same impersonal debt could also enslave, as module 04's bondage reminds us, so this is contingency, not a lost Eden. And against the conviction that a debt **must always be paid**, the record sets the slate wiped clean: Mesopotamian royal debt-cancellations and the biblical Jubilee of Leviticus 25, whole societies releasing debts on purpose. The feeling that you must repay is real and points at real goods. It is also one rule among others that humans have lived by, which is the whole of what it means to call it history rather than law of nature. The next module asks why the barter myth, refuted at every turn, keeps getting taught anyway, and whose interests its survival serves.
