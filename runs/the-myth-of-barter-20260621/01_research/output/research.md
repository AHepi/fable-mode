# Research Dossier — The Myth of Barter

*Stage 01 output. Notes for the curriculum designer, not learner-facing prose.*
*Kind: humanities. Level: intro (curious adult / lower-undergrad, no prerequisites).*
*Stance: ideology critique / critical political economy, applied to a textbook story.*
*Companion to "The Naturalization of Capitalism"; reuse its disciplines, do not reuse its content.*

---

## 0. Orientation (read this before the sections)

The object of study is **the barter origin myth itself**: where it comes from, why it is told, what
it gets wrong, and what the historical record shows instead. The course is **history-first**. The
historical and ethnographic record is the spine. The two abstract economic *models* of money (the
commodity theory and the credit/chartalist theory) are **objects of study**, explored critically,
never used as the unexamined frame. The reader should leave able to see the barter story not as
remembered history but as *the origin myth the commodity theory of money requires*.

The throughline (the arc the brief asks for):

> **"Here is the story you were taught"** → **"here is why it cannot be the history"** →
> **"here is what actually happened (credit and accounting before coinage; coins as a state
> technology)"** → **"here is what money really is, the two rival theories, and why the myth
> survives."**

Four disciplines carry over **verbatim** from the companion course. They are non-negotiable:

1. **Steelman before dismantling.** For each move, give the *strongest, most intuitive* version of
   the barter story before the rebuttal. Trade and barter *do* occur; money *is* convenient; Smith
   was reasoning, not lying. The course's credibility is the steelman honestly stated.
2. **Keep evidence visibly separate from interpretation.** "Cuneiform tablets at ~3000 BCE record
   debts in silver and barley" is evidence. "Therefore money is essentially a measure of debt"
   (Innes/Graeber's reading) is interpretation. Mark the seam every time.
3. **Name the live debates, do not resolve them.** Four genuine ones run through this material
   (§5c). Flag them; pick no winner.
4. **No teleology, no romanticizing.** Money's history was not an upward march toward coins and
   markets. Pre-coinage societies were not a lost Eden; they had debt bondage, slavery, hierarchy.

A recurring structural move, as in the companion: most beats come as a **myth-claim ↔ historical
counter** pair (e.g. "money was invented to fix barter" ↔ "abstract money-of-account predates
physical money by millennia"). Build modules around the *tension*, not a lecture.

**Hard pipeline rules to honor in all downstream prose:** no em dashes; length follows the idea, not
a word count; every specialist term glossed in plain words at first use (`intro`).

**Pairing note.** This course will be grouped with "The Naturalization of Capitalism" in a Path.
Where the two overlap (the barter myth, money, debt, the gift, markets ≠ market society,
naturalization), **use the same terms and glosses** so they read as companions. But this course must
stand alone (a reader may take it first), so it teaches its own vocabulary from scratch and only
*callbacks in spirit* to the companion. The overlap is deliberately narrow: the companion treats the
barter myth in roughly one module as evidence for *embeddedness*; this course makes the myth, money,
and debt the *whole* subject, going far deeper into Mesopotamia, coinage, chartalism, and the
moralization of debt.

## 1. Scope

### In scope
- **The myth as told.** The standard sequence (barter → its inconvenience, the "double coincidence
  of wants" → money invented as a medium of exchange to fix barter → markets, banking, credit
  follow). Its sources: Adam Smith (*Wealth of Nations*, 1776, the "propensity to truck, barter, and
  exchange"; the butcher/brewer/baker), Carl Menger's account of money's spontaneous origin, and
  modern intro-economics textbooks (Samuelson and successors) that still open with it.
- **The ambiguity at its heart.** Is the barter story a *historical claim* (this is what happened) or
  a *logical fable* (imagine a moneyless world, to see why money is useful)? It is usually presented
  as conjectural history ("imagine a primitive society…"). This ambiguity is a live debate, not a
  settled point. Flag it; do not resolve it.
- **Why it cannot be the history.** The anthropological record: Caroline Humphrey's verdict that no
  example of a pure barter economy, let alone money emerging from one, has ever been described. What
  pre-money economies actually ran on: gift, reciprocity, redistribution, and above all
  **credit/debt tracked socially**.
- **Where barter does empirically occur.** Between strangers or enemies; in occasional cross-cultural
  trade; and notably *after* a money economy collapses (post-Soviet Russia; the POW-camp "cigarette
  economy", Radford 1945). Crucial nuance: these are money *substitutes* or trade *between* societies,
  not the primitive barter the myth needs.
- **What actually came first: credit and accounting before coinage.** Mesopotamia ~3000 BCE: temple
  and palace bureaucracies kept accounts in silver and barley as **units of account** with no coins
  circulating; prices, debts, rents, and wages recorded on clay. Tallies as debt records (later, the
  medieval English split-tally stick). The Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE) regulating interest, debt,
  and debt bondage. The key inversion: **writing and accounting arise largely *for* debt records;
  abstract money-of-account predates physical money.**
- **What money actually is — the two models, as objects of study.**
  (a) **Commodity theory** (metallist): money is a special commodity that emerged from barter to
  serve as a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account (the textbook "functions of
  money" list).
  (b) **Credit / state (chartalist) theory**: money is fundamentally a *unit of account and a measure
  of debt*, a creature of social and legal obligation and ultimately the state. Present both fairly,
  show what each explains and fails to explain.
- **The origins of coinage.** Coins are late (~7th c. BCE, Lydia and Ionian Greece) and emerge
  tangled with **states, war, and taxation**: coins to standardize payment to soldiers; taxes payable
  only in coin to force a population to obtain it, partly to provision armies (the
  "military-coinage(-slavery) complex" — Graeber, drawing on Grierson). Coinage is a state
  *technology*, not the natural climax of barter.
- **Debt and its moralization.** Debt as social/moral obligation older than money; the slide from
  debt-as-relationship to debt-as-quantified-and-impersonal; debt bondage and slavery as debt's dark
  side; periodic **clean-slate cancellations** (Mesopotamian *amargi*/*andurarum*, the biblical
  Jubilee and sabbatical year — Michael Hudson's work) as a recurring political response to debt
  crises. The moral charge of "one must always pay one's debts" examined critically.
- **Why the myth persists and whose interests it serves.** It makes the market individual
  (truck-and-barter man) the natural human; it erases the state and social obligation from the story
  of money; it makes money look apolitical and pre-political; it underwrites the commodity theory and
  a particular politics of debt ("debts must be paid"). Ideology critique applied to a textbook page.
- **The historian's method (closing payoff).** How we actually know this (cuneiform tablets,
  ethnography, numismatics); reading conjectural history skeptically; asking of any origin story: *is
  this remembered, or is it required by a theory?*

### Out of scope
- A technical monetary-economics course: no modern central-banking mechanics, money-supply math, or
  interest-rate theory.
- **MMT policy advocacy.** Chartalism is presented here as a *theory of money's nature and history*,
  not a modern-monetary-policy program. This is an integrity line, not just scope (§5b).
- A complete history of money to the present. Stop once the credit-first / state-coinage picture is
  established. The modern era appears only to show the myth still being taught.
- Partisan economics; left-vs-right framing. The throughline is always *the myth and the record that
  refutes it*.
- A full course in cuneiform studies, Assyriology, or numismatics. Use their findings; do not become
  them.

### Sharpening note
The brief's scope is sound. One sharpening, and it is the spine-defining decision: keep the course
**history-first**, not **theory-first**. The temptation (and the companion's lens-first habit) is to
front-load the commodity-vs-credit theories and then illustrate with history. Resist it. Lead with
the *story* and the *record*; introduce each theory only when the history has made the reader *need*
it (the commodity theory named as "the theory the myth serves" around the steelman; the credit theory
named once Mesopotamian debt-accounting is on the table). The reader should meet the models as tools
to interpret evidence already in hand, never as the frame the evidence is poured into.

## 2. Core concepts and dependency sketch

Listed in **dependency order** (each builds on those above). The grouping is the proposed *spine*;
the curriculum stage sets final module boundaries. Lettered sections roughly track the four-part arc
in §0.

### A. The story as told (state it, in its strongest form)
1. **Barter** — direct exchange of goods for goods with no money in between (I give you grain, you
   give me a pot). The thing the myth says came first.
2. **The double coincidence of wants** — for barter to work, each party must want exactly what the
   other offers, at the same time. The classic statement of barter's inconvenience, and the engine of
   the myth: money is the fix.
3. **The standard sequence** — barter → its inconvenience → money invented as a medium of exchange →
   markets, banking, credit follow. State it cleanly and *sympathetically*: it is intuitive, tidy,
   and in every textbook.
4. **Its pedigree** — Adam Smith (the "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange"; the butcher and
   baker), Menger (money emerges spontaneously as the most saleable commodity), and modern intro-econ
   textbooks. Establishes the story is canonical, not a strawman.

> *Dependency:* A1→A2→A3→A4. This whole section is the **steelman**. Nothing is debunked yet. The
> reader must believe the story is reasonable before it is taken apart.

### B. Why it cannot be the history (the ethnographic record)
5. **Conjectural / "philosophical" history** — an origin story reasoned out from first principles
   ("imagine a primitive society…") rather than reconstructed from evidence. The crucial diagnostic
   category: is the barter story this, or a claim about the actual past? **(Live debate — see §5c.)**
6. **The anthropological verdict** — Caroline Humphrey: no example of a pure barter economy, let alone
   money emerging from one, has ever been described by ethnographers. The empirical hole at the center
   of the myth.
7. **What pre-money economies actually did** — gift, reciprocity, redistribution, and credit/debt
   tracked socially. Within a community, people did not haggle good-for-good; they kept running tabs
   of who owed whom, settled by custom and relationship.
8. **Where barter really occurs** — between strangers or enemies (no ongoing relationship to track a
   tab on), in occasional cross-cultural trade, and *after a money economy collapses*. The Radford
   POW-camp cigarette economy and post-Soviet barter are **money substitutes**, not primitive barter
   — flag this nuance hard; it is the most-misused example.

> *Dependency:* B needs A (you can only refute a story you have stated). B6 is the empirical hammer;
> B7 supplies the positive alternative (credit was already there); B8 disarms the "but barter does
> happen!" objection without overclaiming "barter never happened" (§5b).

### C. What came first: credit and accounting before coinage (the historical core)
9. **Unit of account vs. medium of exchange** — the load-bearing distinction of the whole course. A
   *unit of account* is the measuring-stick you price and reckon debts in ("you owe me 3 shekels of
   silver"); a *medium of exchange* is the thing that physically changes hands. The myth assumes the
   medium came first; the record shows the unit of account came first, with no coin circulating.
10. **Mesopotamian temple/palace accounting (~3000 BCE)** — large bureaucracies recorded prices,
    debts, rents, and wages on clay in silver and barley as units of account, centuries to millennia
    before coins existed. The single most important historical fact in the course.
11. **Writing arose largely for accounts** — the earliest cuneiform is overwhelmingly administrative:
    lists, receipts, debts. Record-keeping, not poetry, drove early writing. Abstract
    money-of-account predates physical money.
12. **The tally / debt record** — a physical record of a debt (the medieval English split-tally
    stick: notched, split in two, each party keeps a half). Money as a *record of who owes whom*, made
    concrete. A bridge object between "ledger" and "thing that circulates."
13. **The Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE)** — a law code regulating interest rates, debt, and debt
    bondage. Evidence that debt was a central, legally governed social institution long before coinage.
    (Primary-source candidate for the humanities "primary source" slot.)

> *Dependency:* C is the spine's payoff for "what actually happened." C9 must come first; everything
> else in C is evidence for it. C9→C10→C11 establish account-before-coin; C12 makes it tangible; C13
> shows debt was governed law, not informal favor. This is the densest, most evidence-heavy stretch.

### D. The origins of coinage (a state technology, not barter's climax)
14. **Coinage is late and sudden** — the first coins appear ~7th c. BCE in Lydia and the Ionian Greek
    world, thousands of years *after* the accounting in C. Coins are not the origin of money; they are
    a late chapter.
15. **The state/war/tax nexus** — coins standardize payment to soldiers; taxes payable only in coin
    force a population to obtain coin (by selling goods or labor), which seeds markets partly to
    provision armies. Grierson and Graeber's reading: the "military-coinage(-slavery) complex." Coinage
    is a technology of states at war.
16. **Markets partly created by states** — a key inversion of the myth: rather than markets arising
    naturally from barter and then money lubricating them, *states* using coin and tax helped *call
    markets into being*. (Interpretation, but well grounded; mark the seam.)

> *Dependency:* D needs C (so the reader already knows money predates coins; D shows what coins *added*
> and *why*). D14 is the chronological shock; D15–D16 supply the causal story. This section is where
> "money is apolitical" dies.

### E. What money is — the two models, examined (objects of study)
17. **The functions-of-money list** — the textbook account: money is a medium of exchange, a store of
    value, and a unit of account. Worth stating plainly because the commodity theory leans on it and
    the credit theory reorders it (unit of account first).
18. **The commodity theory of money (metallism)** — money is a commodity that emerged from barter to
    ease exchange; its value rests on the value of its material (or on its saleability). **This is the
    theory the barter myth serves**: the myth is the commodity theory's required origin story. Naming
    that link is the course's central analytical move. **Steelman it** (precious metals are durable,
    divisible, portable, scarce; this is not stupid).
19. **The credit / state (chartalist) theory** — money is fundamentally a *unit of account and a
    measure of debt*, a creature of social and legal obligation, ultimately underwritten by the state
    (especially its power to impose and collect taxes/fines). Money is a *relationship and a record*,
    not a thing. (Innes; Knapp; Ingham; Grierson on money as abstract measure rooted in customary law
    and wergild compensation.)
20. **Fungibility** — interchangeability: one shekel of silver, or one dollar, is as good as any
    other. Needed to explain how a tracked personal debt becomes impersonal, transferable money.
21. **Fiat money** — money that is money by declaration and acceptance, not by the value of its
    material (a banknote, a bank balance). Useful contrast: the commodity theory struggles to explain
    why valueless tokens work; the credit theory takes it as the normal case.
22. **What each model explains and fails to explain** — commodity theory: good on why metals were
    chosen, weak on account-before-coin and on fiat. Credit theory: good on Mesopotamia, debt, and
    fiat, weaker / contested on universality (the "primordial debt" overreach, §5c). **The
    chartalism-vs-commodity question is genuinely unsettled (§5c).**

> *Dependency:* E needs B–D as evidence to weigh. E17 sets the textbook frame; E18 names the myth's
> theory and steelmans it; E19 gives the rival; E20–E21 are the tools (fungibility, fiat) that let the
> reader judge; E22 is the honest scorecard and the live debate. Note the history-first discipline:
> the models arrive *here*, after the record, as interpretive tools — not at the front.

### F. Debt and its moralization
23. **Debt as social relationship** — before it was a number, a debt was an obligation embedded in a
    relationship (a favor, a bride-price, a blood-payment). Older than money; older than coinage.
24. **From relationship to quantity** — money lets a debt be measured, recorded, transferred, and
    enforced against a stranger; the personal obligation hardens into an impersonal, quantified,
    collectible sum. (Connects to fungibility, C20, and to commodity fetishism in the companion course
    — same family of move.)
25. **Debt bondage and slavery** — debt's dark side: failure to pay could cost freedom (self, wife,
    children pledged or seized). A standing feature of pre-coinage and ancient societies. **Anti-
    romanticizing anchor:** the credit-first past was not gentle.
26. **Clean-slate cancellations** — periodic debt amnesties as a recurring political response to debt
    crises: Mesopotamian *amargi* / *andurarum* (royal "freedom" / debt-remission decrees), the
    biblical sabbatical year and Jubilee (Michael Hudson's work). Evidence that "debts must always be
    paid" is not a timeless law but a historically contested norm.
27. **The morality of debt examined** — the intuition that repaying a debt is a moral absolute, held
    up as itself a historical and ideological artifact, not a fact of nature. The course's sharpest
    application of ideology critique to a feeling the reader holds.

> *Dependency:* F draws on C (debt-accounting), D (coinage made debt enforceable at scale), and E
> (the credit theory makes debt central). F23→F24 is the key arc (relationship → quantity); F25
> guards against nostalgia; F26→F27 is the ideological payoff. **Open question: is F its own module,
> or split across the history and the close?** (§7.)

### G. Why the myth persists, and the historian's method (the close)
28. **The ideological work the myth does** — it naturalizes the self-interested market individual
    ("truck-and-barter man" as the default human), erases the state and social obligation from the
    origin of money, makes money look apolitical and pre-political, and underwrites both the commodity
    theory and a politics of debt ("debts must be paid"). Ideology critique applied to a textbook page.
    Whose interests does the story serve?
29. **The historian's / anthropologist's method** — how we actually know (cuneiform tablets,
    ethnography, numismatics, legal codes); the discipline of separating evidence from interpretation;
    reading any origin story by asking: *is this remembered, or is it required by a theory?* The
    transferable skill the reader leaves with.

> *Dependency:* G gathers and names what the whole course has been *doing*. G28 is the
> "whose-interests" capstone; G29 is the method payoff (mirrors the companion's section H — the reader
> realizes they already learned the method). Should feel like recognition, not new material.

## 3. Prerequisite analysis (no-background reader)

**Assume essentially nothing.** This is `intro`. The reader is a curious adult who may never have
heard of chartalism, Hammurabi, Innes, or Graeber, and has no economics, anthropology, or ancient-
history background. Calibration (`course-design.md` §2 `intro`): unpack every term a curious adult
would look up, on first use, in plain words; claims made plausible by example and picture; no
formalism. The "double coincidence of wants" is explained in plain words and a concrete example, not
formalized.

### Can be assumed (general literacy / lived experience)
- Everyday use of money: buying things, paying with cash or card, the idea of price, the idea of a
  loan and of owing someone.
- The *feeling* that money obviously replaced barter — this is the reader's starting intuition and the
  course's raw material. Use it; the reader almost certainly believes the myth. Do not assume they
  already doubt it.
- A rough sense that history happened (there were ancient empires, the Middle Ages) with no precise
  dates or scholarship.
- Comfort following an argument and a narrative; willingness to have an intuition challenged.

### Must be taught from scratch — every specialist term glossed at first use
Each is an **orphan-term risk** (`course-design.md` §3): if used unglossed, it is a defect. The
plain-words gloss is the curriculum/authoring stage's job; collected here so none is missed. At
`intro`, callbacks **re-gloss** (a few words restating the term), they do not just re-name it.

| Term | One-line plain gloss to deploy at first use |
|---|---|
| **barter** | swapping goods directly for goods, with no money in between |
| **double coincidence of wants** | barter only works if each side happens to want exactly what the other has, at the same time |
| **medium of exchange** | the thing that physically changes hands in a deal (the coin, the note) |
| **unit of account** | the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in (you owe me "3 shekels"), even if no coin moves |
| **store of value** | something you can hold onto now and spend later without it spoiling |
| **commodity money** | money that is also a useful good (cattle, grain, metal), valued for its material |
| **commodity theory of money** | the idea that money is a special commodity that grew out of barter to ease trade |
| **fiat money** | money that counts as money by declaration and acceptance, not for its material (a banknote) |
| **credit theory / chartalism** | the rival idea that money is basically a measure of debt, backed by social and state obligation |
| **fungible** | interchangeable: any one dollar (or shekel of silver) is as good as any other |
| **credit** | the trust that lets you receive now and pay later; the other side of a debt |
| **debt** | what you owe; an obligation to pay or repay, whether in money, goods, or service |
| **tally** | a physical record of a debt (a notched stick split in two, one half each) |
| **debt bondage** | being forced into servitude to work off a debt you cannot pay |
| **jubilee** | a periodic wiping-clean of debts (a biblical example; Mesopotamia had its own) |
| **conjectural history** | an origin story reasoned out from first principles ("imagine a primitive society…"), not dug up from evidence |
| **ethnography** | the close study of how actual living societies are organized, by observers among them |
| **numismatics** | the study of coins and money objects, and what they reveal |
| **cuneiform** | the wedge-shaped script pressed into clay in ancient Mesopotamia |
| **shekel** | a Mesopotamian unit of weight/value (a measure, long before it was a coin) |

| **metallism** | the view that money's value rests on the precious metal it is made of |

### Named figures and works — introduce with one identifying clause, never assume known
Each gets a one-clause "who/when/what" the first time: **Adam Smith** (*Wealth of Nations*, 1776),
**Carl Menger** (19th-c. economist, money's spontaneous origin), **Paul Samuelson** (mid-20th-c.
textbook economist, as a stand-in for the modern textbook), **Caroline Humphrey** (anthropologist,
the no-barter-economy verdict), **R. A. Radford** (the POW-camp cigarette-economy account, 1945),
**A. Mitchell Innes** (1913–14 essays, the credit theory), **Georg Friedrich Knapp** (*The State
Theory of Money*), **Geoffrey Ingham** (*The Nature of Money*), **Philip Grierson** (money as
abstract measure; coinage and the state), **Michael Hudson** (debt cancellations, the ancient Near
East), **Hammurabi** (Babylonian king, ~1750 BCE, the law code), and **David Graeber** (*Debt: The
First 5,000 Years*, 2011) — the last attributed carefully and noted as a contested popular synthesis
(§5c). Do not assume the reader knows any of them.

### No silent gaps check
Every concept needed to reach the payoff (G29's method; G28's "why it persists") is introduced in
A–F as "must teach." The unit-of-account vs. medium-of-exchange hinge (C9) is the only conceptually
demanding idea and is explicitly taught with a concrete picture before it is leaned on. The only
"assumed" items are general adult literacy and everyday money experience, listed above. No beat in
the spine relies on un-taught machinery.

## 4. Hooks & examples (concrete, checkable entry points)

Vivid, specific, anchored. The curriculum can assign these to modules. Each is a *door into a
concept*. Rough dates only; specifics needing confirmation are marked `[verify]`.

- **Open your intro-economics textbook to page one.** Almost any will tell the barter story before it
  defines money. A concrete, checkable anchor that the myth is *taught now*, not a dusty error. → A3,
  A4, and the closing "why it persists" (G28). **[anchor: that textbooks open this way is well
  documented; Smith and Menger are the named pedigree.]**
- **Smith's butcher, brewer, and baker.** *Wealth of Nations*'s picture of self-interested traders
  trucking and bartering their way to a division of labor. The myth's most elegant statement. → A4.
  (Steelman door: Smith is reasoning, not lying — §5b.)
- **The double coincidence of wants, made concrete.** You have shoes and want bread; the baker wants
  not shoes but a chicken; the chicken-keeper wants shoes but already has bread… The everyday
  frustration the myth says money solved. State it *sympathetically* before dismantling. → A2.
- **"No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described."** Caroline Humphrey's
  verdict. The empirical hole, in one line. → B6. **[anchor: Humphrey's claim is real and frequently
  cited; quote only if the exact wording can be confirmed — otherwise paraphrase.]**
- **The POW-camp cigarette economy.** R. A. Radford's 1945 account of cigarettes circulating as money
  in a prison camp. The *trap*: it looks like barter-to-money emerging spontaneously, but it is a
  *money substitute* among people who already knew money — exactly **not** the primitive barter the
  myth needs. A perfect teaching nuance. → B8. **[anchor: Radford 1945, well established.]**
- **Post-Soviet barter.** When the ruble economy seized up in the 1990s, firms and people fell back on
  barter. Again: barter *after* money, not before it. → B8. **[anchor: well documented; keep general.]**
- **A clay tablet that is a receipt.** A ~3000 BCE Mesopotamian tablet recording a debt of barley or
  silver: no coin in sight, yet money-of-account is plainly at work. The image that overturns the
  whole sequence. → C10, C11. **[anchor: temple/palace accounting in silver and barley is solid; do
  not invent a specific tablet's contents or museum number.]**
- **The split-tally stick.** A notched stick snapped in two, each party keeping a half that must match
  to settle the debt; used in medieval England for centuries `[verify exact span]`. Money as a
  *record of who owes whom*, you can hold in your hand. → C12.
- **Hammurabi caps the interest rate.** A ~1750 BCE law code setting interest limits and rules on debt
  and debt bondage. Debt as governed law, a thousand years before coins. → C13. (Primary-source slot.)
  **[anchor: the code regulates debt/interest/bondage — solid; do not quote specific clause numbers or
  rates unless verified.]**
- **The first coins, in Lydia.** ~7th c. BCE, in Lydia and the Greek world: electrum lumps stamped by
  authority. The shock is the *date*: thousands of years after the tablets. Coins are a late chapter,
  not the beginning. → D14. **[anchor: ~7th c. BCE Lydia/Ionia is solid; keep the date rough.]**
- **Coins to pay soldiers; taxes to be paid in coin.** A state mints coin to pay its army, then
  demands taxes payable only in that coin, forcing everyone to obtain it, seeding markets to provision
  the troops. The "how coinage actually spread" story. → D15, D16. (Grierson/Graeber reading — mark as
  interpretation.)
- **"Surely one must pay one's debts."** The reader's own moral intuition, stated flat. Then the
  jubilee: whole societies periodically *cancelling* debts on purpose. The collision is the hook for
  the morality-of-debt module. → F26, F27.
- **The word that means "freedom" is a debt-cancellation word.** Sumerian *amargi*, often glossed as
  "freedom" / "return to mother", is bound up with the cancellation of debts. Freedom as
  *release from debt*. A startling door into debt's depth in the social order. → F26. **[verify the
  *amargi* etymology/gloss before stating it as fact; present cautiously.]**
- **"Is this remembered, or is it required by a theory?"** The single question the course hands the
  reader for any origin story. The method, in one line. → G29. (Good *closing* hook; could also seed
  the opening as a promise.)

## 5. Common pitfalls & misconceptions

### (a) Learner misconceptions the course must dismantle
For each: the misconception, the **steelman** (its strongest, most intuitive form), and the
**historical/critical counter**.

1. **"First there was barter; money was invented to fix it."**
   - *Steelman:* clean, logical, intuitive, and in every textbook; barter *is* clumsy, and money *does*
     fix the double-coincidence problem.
   - *Counter:* no ethnographic record of a barter economy that later "upgraded" to money; within
     communities, credit and socially tracked debt came first, and money-of-account predates coins by
     millennia. (Humphrey; Innes; the Mesopotamian record.) **Live debate over the *status* of the
     story — see (c).**

2. **"Money is basically the precious metal it's made of."**
   - *Steelman:* metals are durable, divisible, portable, scarce; for most of history money *was*
     metal, and the metal had real uses.
   - *Counter:* the earliest money was a *unit of account* (debts in silver and barley) with no coin
     circulating; and fiat money works fine with no precious metal at all. The material is one
     implementation, not the essence. (The commodity-vs-credit debate, E.) **Unsettled — see (c).**

3. **"Coins are where money begins."**
   - *Steelman:* coins are the most visible, iconic money; "money" and "coins" feel synonymous.
   - *Counter:* coins (~7th c. BCE) are thousands of years *later* than money-of-account and debt
     records; they are a late, state-driven technology, not money's origin. (D14.) Distinguish *money*
     from *coinage* explicitly and early.

4. **"Barter never happened anywhere."** (The *over-correction* — a misconception the course can
   accidentally *create*.)
   - *Steelman of the correct view:* the myth's *sequence* (barter society → money) has no support.
   - *Counter to the over-claim:* barter genuinely occurs — between strangers/enemies, in cross-
     cultural trade, and when a money system collapses. The claim is "barter was never the foundational
     stage the myth needs," **not** "barter is fictional." Teach the precise claim. (B8.) (This is both
     a learner trap and an author trap — see (b).)

5. **"Debts must always be repaid; that's just basic morality."**
   - *Steelman:* trust, promises, and repayment are real moral goods; an economy needs them.
   - *Counter:* whole societies have periodically and deliberately *cancelled* debts (jubilees,
     *amargi*); "debts must always be paid" is a historically contested norm, not a law of nature, and
     it serves particular interests. (F26, F27.) Examine the feeling; do not preach against it.

6. **"The state just shows up later to regulate money that markets already made."**
   - *Steelman:* money clearly circulates in markets, and the state does regulate it.
   - *Counter:* states were entangled in money from early on — coinage spread through soldiers and
     taxes; states helped *call markets into being*. Money is not pre-political. (D15, D16, E19.)
     (Interpretation-heavy; mark the seam.)

### (b) Author-integrity pitfalls (non-negotiable given the stance)
These protect the course's credibility. Several are named explicitly in the brief.

- **Do not overclaim "barter never happened."** The defensible claim is narrow and precise: no
  *barter-economy-to-money* sequence in the record; barter *does* occur in specific contexts. Stating
  the strong, false version hands critics an easy win and is simply wrong. (Ties to (a)4.)
- **Do not turn chartalism into MMT policy advocacy.** The credit/state theory is presented as a
  theory of money's *nature and history*. Modern Monetary Theory is a contemporary *policy* program
  that draws on it; conflating them politicizes the course and exceeds scope. Keep them separate.
- **Do not romanticize pre-coinage societies.** They had debt bondage, slavery, hierarchy, and
  coercion (F25 exists precisely to prevent this). "Credit came first" is not "the past was kinder."
  The point is contingency, not nostalgia.
- **No teleology.** Money's history is not an upward march toward coins, markets, and capitalism. Do
  not narrate "money was heading toward its modern form." Coinage was contingent; so is everything
  after it.
- **Do not flatten Adam Smith into a fool or a liar.** He was reasoning carefully from the evidence and
  tools he had, and he is partly *right* (trade and division of labor are real). The myth is an error
  of *conjectural method*, not stupidity or bad faith. Strawmanning Smith discredits the course.
- **Do not blend the thinkers.** Innes, Knapp, Ingham, Grierson, Hudson, and Graeber are distinct, and
  they disagree on specifics. Attribute claims; do not fuse them into one "chartalist" voice. Graeber
  especially must be attributed *as Graeber's reading*, not as settled consensus (§5c).
- **Keep evidence and interpretation visibly separate.** Dates, tablets, codes, ethnographic reports =
  evidence. "Money is essentially debt"; "coinage spread *because* of war"; "the myth serves the rich"
  = interpretation. Mark every seam. A course about a theory-driven origin story must not smuggle in
  its own.
- **No fabricated quotes, page numbers, or precise dates.** Use rough dates ("~3000 BCE", "~7th c.
  BCE"). Mark uncertain specifics `[verify]`. Do not invent a tablet's contents, a clause number, an
  interest rate, or an etymology.
- **Do not let ideology critique become a conspiracy story.** "Whose interests does the myth serve?"
  (G28) is an analysis of *what a story makes seem natural*, not a claim that anyone consciously
  fabricated it. Smith did not invent a cover story; the myth's *function* is the object, not a plot.

### (c) Genuine live debates to acknowledge, not resolve
The course gains credibility by naming these as open. Flag each; pick no winner.

- **Historical claim or logical fable?** Economists often defend the barter story as a *pedagogical
  device* about the *logic* of money's usefulness, not a claim about the actual past, so "it's
  ethnographically false" misses the point. Critics reply that it is routinely *presented* as history
  ("imagine a primitive society…") and read that way by students. This ambiguity is itself a live
  debate and a teachable one. Present both readings; do not declare the story simply "debunked."
- **Chartalism vs. the commodity theory.** Genuinely unsettled. Each explains some evidence and
  strains on other evidence (E22). Do not crown the credit theory the winner just because it fits
  Mesopotamia better; note what it strains on (e.g. the universality question, below) and what the
  commodity theory still captures (why metals were chosen, cross-border trade money).
- **How far to trust Graeber's *Debt*.** Influential, and it synthesizes real scholarship (Innes,
  Humphrey, Hudson, Grierson). But it is a popular work, and specialists contest specific claims,
  datings, and the strength of some sweeping arguments. Use it as a *gateway and synthesis*, attribute
  its readings *to Graeber*, and lean on the underlying primary scholarship where possible. Do not
  treat *Debt* as settled history.
- **The "primordial debt" thesis.** The strong claim that society as such rests on an original,
  founding debt (to gods, ancestors, the community) and that money/tax descend from it. Provocative and
  influential; criticized as over-reaching and unfalsifiable. If used at all, present as a contested
  interpretation, clearly flagged, not as the course's settled frame. (Risk: it can tip the course
  into exactly the kind of theory-required origin story it is teaching the reader to distrust.)

## 6. Sources

Real, well-established scholarship. One-line note on contribution + contestation. Where I am unsure
of a specific (exact date, edition, wording, attribution), I mark **[verify]** and keep the claim at
the level I am confident in. **No fabricated page numbers, quotes, or precise dates beyond what is
flagged as solid.**

### The myth and its pedigree (primary sources for "the story as told")
- **Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776).** The "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange"; the
  butcher/brewer/baker; barter's inconvenience motivating money. Primary source for A. *Note:* Smith is
  more careful and qualified than the caricature — avoid strawmanning (§5b). *Solid* as the canonical
  statement.
- **Carl Menger, "On the Origins of Money" (1892)** and *Principles of Economics* (1871). Money as the
  spontaneous emergence of the most saleable commodity as a medium of exchange — the sharpest
  commodity-theory account of the myth. Primary source for A4 and E18. *Solid* as the canonical
  commodity-theory origin story; *[verify]* exact 1892 essay title/translation before citing precisely.
- **Paul Samuelson, *Economics* (textbook, many editions from 1948).** Stand-in for the modern intro
  textbook that still opens with barter. *[verify]* the exact wording/edition before quoting; the
  *general* fact that textbooks open this way is solid and widely noted.

### Why it cannot be the history (anthropology)
- **Caroline Humphrey, "Barter and Economic Disintegration," *Man* (1985).** The verdict that no pure
  barter economy has been ethnographically described; what economies do instead. Source for B6, B8.
  *Solid and frequently cited;* `[verify]` the exact wording before quoting.
- **Marcel Mauss, *The Gift* (1925)** and the wider economic-anthropology tradition (e.g. Malinowski on
  the Trobriand *kula*). The gift and reciprocity as the real organizing forms of pre-money exchange.
  Background for B7. *Canonical.* (Overlaps the companion course — keep the gloss compatible.)
- **R. A. Radford, "The Economic Organisation of a P.O.W. Camp," *Economica* (1945).** Cigarettes as
  money in a prison camp. Source for the B8 nuance: a money *substitute*, not primitive barter. *Solid
  and well known.*

### Credit and accounting before coinage (the historical core)
- **A. Mitchell Innes, "What is Money?" (1913) and "The Credit Theory of Money" (1914),** *Banking Law
  Journal*. The foundational modern statement that money is credit/debt, not a commodity; debt records
  predate coin. Primary source for C and E19. *Solid* as a primary text; an outsider work, long
  neglected, later revived — note its history.
- **Assyriological / ancient-Near-East scholarship on temple and palace accounting** (e.g. the work
  collected around Hudson and the ISLET/ancient-economies group; general Assyriology). Mesopotamian
  accounts in silver and barley as units of account ~3000 BCE; debts on clay. Source for C9–C11.
  *General picture solid;* `[verify]` any specific tablet, figure, or museum reference before using.
- **On early writing as administrative/accounting in origin** (e.g. Denise Schmandt-Besserat on tokens
  and the origins of cuneiform; standard Assyriology). Source for C11. *[verify]* the
  Schmandt-Besserat token thesis specifically — it is influential but *contested* in its strong form;
  the broad claim that early cuneiform is overwhelmingly administrative is solid.
- **The Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE).** Babylonian law code regulating interest, debt, and debt
  bondage. Primary source for C13 (humanities primary-source slot). *Solid* that it governs debt and
  bondage; *[verify]* before quoting specific clause numbers, rates, or wording.
- **The medieval English split-tally stick** (Exchequer tallies). Money/debt as a physical record.
  Source for C12. *General fact solid;* `[verify]` the exact span of use and the date of their
  abolition/burning before stating it.

### Coinage and the state
- **Philip Grierson, *The Origins of Money* (1977)** and related numismatic work. Money as an abstract
  measure originating in customary law and wergild compensation; coinage and the state. Source for D,
  E19, and the wergild point. *Solid and authoritative* (Grierson is a leading numismatist) — a good
  ballast against over-reliance on Graeber.
- **On the first coins (~7th c. BCE, Lydia/Ionia).** Standard numismatic history. Source for D14.
  *Solid;* keep dates rough.
- **The state/war/tax account of coinage's spread** (Grierson; Graeber's "military-coinage(-slavery)
  complex"). Source for D15, D16. *The chronology (coins + soldiers + taxes) is solid; the strong
  causal/“complex” framing is interpretation* — attribute and mark the seam (§5c).

### What money is — the models
- **Georg Friedrich Knapp, *The State Theory of Money* (*Staatliche Theorie des Geldes*, 1905; Eng.
  trans. 1924).** The classic statement that money is a creature of the state (chartalism; "charta" =
  token/ticket). Source for E19. *Solid* as the foundational chartalist text.
- **Geoffrey Ingham, *The Nature of Money* (2004).** A modern sociological synthesis: money as a
  social relation and unit of account, with a careful account of the commodity-vs-credit debate.
  Source for E18, E19, E22. *Well regarded;* good for the balanced "what each explains" scorecard.
- **(Background) the commodity theory / metallism** as represented by Menger (above) and the standard
  textbook "functions of money." Source for E17, E18. *Solid* as the position to be steelmanned.

### Debt and its moralization
- **David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011).** The synthesis that ties the whole picture
  together: myth of barter, credit before coinage, coinage and the state, debt's moralization,
  jubilees, "primordial debt." Source across B, D, F. *Influential and synthesizes real scholarship,
  but contested in specifics and a popular work* — attribute its *readings* to Graeber, lean on the
  primary scholarship it draws on (Innes, Humphrey, Hudson, Grierson), and flag the contestation
  (§5c). This is the most-load-bearing-yet-most-contested source; handle with the most care.
- **Michael Hudson, on debt cancellations in the ancient Near East** (e.g. *…and forgive them their
  debts*, 2018, and his scholarly work on *amargi*/*andurarum* and the Jubilee). Source for F26.
  *Solid on the existence of royal debt-remission decrees;* `[verify]` specific etymologies (esp.
  *amargi*) and any precise dating before stating as fact; some of Hudson's broader framing is
  interpretation.
- **The biblical sabbatical year and Jubilee** (Leviticus 25; Deuteronomy 15). Primary-source anchor
  for periodic debt release. Source for F26. *Solid* as texts; treat their historical *practice* (how
  often actually observed) as uncertain `[verify]`.

### Method / background (for the author)
- **The economic-anthropology substantivist tradition** (Polanyi and successors) framing money and
  exchange as embedded in social relations — shared ground with the companion course; keep vocabulary
  compatible. *Solid* as a tradition. (Do not import the companion's full apparatus; this course is
  narrower and deeper on money specifically.)

## 7. Open questions for the curriculum stage

1. **Module count.** Recommend **9 modules** (within the brief's 8–10), with a clear path to **10**
   if debt-moralization is split out (Q4) and to **8** if E and F are each compressed. The four-part
   arc (story → why-not → what-happened → what-money-is/why-myth-survives) maps cleanly. See the
   proposed shape below.

2. **How many modules does the history get vs. the models?** This is the brief's central steer and the
   key decision. Recommendation: **the history dominates** — roughly **5–6 history/ethnography modules
   (A, B, C, D, plus debt-history in F)** to **1–2 models modules (E)**. The two theories (commodity
   vs. credit) should occupy *one* dedicated module (E), arriving *after* the historical core, framed
   as "tools to interpret what we have just seen," with the commodity theory explicitly named as "the
   theory the myth requires." Do **not** open with the models or give them equal billing with the
   record; that would invert the history-first steer.

3. **Where does coinage / the state sit?** Recommendation: **its own module (D), placed after the
   credit-and-accounting core (C) and before the models (E).** Reasons: the *date shock* (coins are
   ~7th c. BCE, millennia after the tablets) is most powerful once the reader already knows money
   predates coins; and the state/war/tax story is where "money is apolitical" dies, which sets up both
   the credit theory (E19) and the "why the myth persists" close (G28). Folding coinage into the
   models module would bury its strongest beat.

4. **Is debt-moralization its own module?** Recommendation: **yes, its own module (F)** — it carries
   the course's sharpest ideology-critique payoff (the reader's own "debts must be paid" intuition,
   the jubilees, *amargi*) and its key anti-romanticizing anchor (debt bondage). It depends on the
   credit theory (E) being in hand, so place it *after* the models module. If count is tight, the
   relationship→quantity history (F23–F25) could fold into C/D and only the moralization payoff
   (F26–F27) ride into the closing module — but that weakens a first-rate beat. Prefer keeping F whole.

5. **Where does "why the myth persists" (G28) and the historian's method (G29) live?** Recommendation:
   the **final module**, as the close/payoff (mirrors the companion's section H). It should feel like
   recognition of what the course has been doing, not new material. Decide whether G28 (ideology
   critique: whose interests) and G29 (method: "remembered or required?") share one module or split;
   one module is likely enough if the prior modules have done their work.

6. **How to honor the history-first steer structurally.** Beyond module *count* (Q2), the curriculum
   should ensure each models concept (E) is *triggered by* a piece of evidence already taught, not
   introduced cold. Suggested triggers: name the commodity theory at the end of the steelman (A) as
   "the theory this story serves", then fully treat it in E; name the credit theory the moment
   Mesopotamian debt-accounting lands (C), then fully treat it in E. The reader meets each model as an
   answer to a question the history has raised.

7. **The unit-of-account vs. medium-of-exchange hinge (C9).** This is the one demanding idea and the
   pivot of the whole course. Curriculum should decide whether it gets explicit early billing (a clear
   "two different jobs money does" passage with a concrete picture) and then spirals back in D, E, F.
   Recommendation: yes — introduce it carefully in the history-core module and reuse the exact gloss
   each time it returns (re-gloss, per the `intro` dial).

8. **How much Graeber to lean on.** *Debt* is the most convenient synthesis and the most contested
   source (§5c, §6). Curriculum should decide a policy: use Graeber as a *gateway/narrative spine* but
   anchor load-bearing claims in the primary scholarship (Innes, Humphrey, Grierson, Hudson, Knapp)
   and attribute Graeber's *readings* as his. Avoid building any module's core claim on Graeber alone.

9. **The POW-camp / post-Soviet nuance — how prominent?** It is the cleanest way to teach "barter
   occurs, but as a money *substitute*, not money's origin" and to inoculate against the "barter never
   happened" over-correction (§5a/4, §5b). Recommendation: give it a clear beat within the
   "why-it-can't-be-the-history" module (B); it is too useful a distinction to relegate to a footnote,
   but does not need its own module.

### Proposed module shape (a starting point for curriculum, not a decision)
1. **The story you were taught** — barter, the double coincidence of wants, the standard sequence;
   Smith and Menger; the textbook page. *Stated sympathetically (the steelman). Name the commodity
   theory at the close as "the theory this story needs."* (A)
2. **Why it can't be the history** — conjectural history vs. evidence; Humphrey's verdict; what
   pre-money economies actually did (gift, reciprocity, credit); where barter really occurs (POW camp,
   post-Soviet) as money *substitutes*. (B)
3. **What came first: credit and the ledger** — unit of account vs. medium of exchange; Mesopotamian
   temple/palace accounting ~3000 BCE; writing arose for accounts; the tally stick. (C9–C12)
4. **Debt was law before coins were minted** — the Code of Hammurabi; interest, debt, and debt bondage
   as governed institutions. *(Could merge with module 3; kept separate to give Hammurabi the
   primary-source slot.)* (C13)
5. **Coins are late: money as a state technology** — the ~7th-c.-BCE date shock; coins, soldiers,
   taxes; markets partly called into being by states. (D)
6. **What money actually is: two theories** — functions of money; the commodity theory (steelmanned,
   named as the myth's required theory); the credit/chartalist theory; fungibility and fiat; what each
   explains and fails to explain; the unsettled debate. *(The models module — one, after the
   history.)* (E)
7. **The morality of debt** — debt as relationship → as quantity; debt bondage (no nostalgia); the
   clean-slate cancellations and the Jubilee; "must debts always be paid?" examined. (F)
8. **Why the myth survives** — the ideological work it does (the market individual naturalized, the
   state erased, money made apolitical, "debts must be paid" underwritten); whose interests it serves.
   (G28)
9. **How we know, and how to read an origin story** — cuneiform, ethnography, numismatics, law codes;
   evidence vs. interpretation; "is this remembered, or required by a theory?" The method the reader
   keeps. (G29)

*(That sketch is 9. Merge 3/4 to reach 8; split 8/9 differently, or split F's history from its
moralization, to flex toward 10. The load-bearing, protect-at-all-costs beats: the steelman (1), the
ethnographic refutation + the barter-as-substitute nuance (2), the account-before-coin core (3), the
coinage date shock + state nexus (5), the two models with the commodity-theory-serves-the-myth link
(6), and the closing method (9).)*
