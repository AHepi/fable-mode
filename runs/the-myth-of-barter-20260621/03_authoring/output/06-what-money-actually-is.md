## The hook

For five modules you have been gathering evidence. Now you get to do something with it.

You started with the story everyone is taught: people bartered, barter was clumsy, money was invented to fix it (module 01). Then the record refused to cooperate. No society has ever been found running on barter and then upgrading to money (module 02). Thousands of years before the first coin, Mesopotamian scribes were already pricing goods and recording debts in silver and barley they never handed over (module 03). Debt was governed law, with a Babylonian king capping interest, a full millennium before anyone struck a coin (module 04). And coins, when they finally arrive, come stamped to pay soldiers and collected back as taxes, a state technology, not barter's happy ending (module 05).

So here is a fair question. If the barter story is wrong about where money came from, what *is* money? Not "what does a coin do," but the deeper thing: what kind of object is money, underneath all its forms?

There are two serious answers. They have been arguing with each other for over a century, and the argument is not settled. This module is where you finally meet both, lay them side by side, and judge them with the evidence you now hold. One of them, you will see, is the very theory the barter story was built to serve.

## Context & background

A warning before the two theories, because this module is the one place in the course where the abstractions come out, and abstractions are where careful thinking goes to die.

You will meet two rival accounts of what money is. The temptation will be to pick a team, especially since the whole course so far has been knocking down the story that team one needs. Resist it. Both theories are *objects of study* here, not candidates for your loyalty. The honest position at the end of this module is not "the credit theory won." It is "here is what each one explains, here is what each one strains to explain, and here is why scholars still disagree." A course that spent five modules teaching you to distrust a too-tidy story would be cheating if it handed you another one.

Think of the two theories as **two maps of the same country**. A road map and a topographic map of the same stretch of land are both true and both useful, yet they show different things and hide different things. The road map shows you how to drive from one town to the next and says nothing about the mountains. The topographic map shows every ridge and river and leaves the roads out. Ask which map is "correct" and you have asked a confused question. Ask which map you need *for this journey* and you can answer. Two maps of the same country: that is how to hold the commodity theory and the credit theory in your head at once, without pretending one of them is the territory itself.

One more piece of ground to lay first. Before either theory, there is a plain textbook list of what money *does*, and both theories have to account for it.

Open that intro-economics textbook again, a page or two past the barter story, and you will find **the functions of money**: the textbook list of the jobs money does, usually given as three. Money is a **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changes hands in a deal so the deal can happen (you hand over a coin, you walk away with bread). Money is a **store of value**, something you can hold onto now and spend later without it spoiling in the meantime (you can keep a coin in a drawer for a year; you cannot keep a fish). And money is a **unit of account**, the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in, the thing prices are quoted in even when no coin is moving ("you owe me three shekels").

That last one should ring a bell. The split between **unit of account** (the measuring-stick for debts) and **medium of exchange** (the thing that changes hands) was the hinge of module 03, the distinction that let a clay tablet record a debt in silver with no silver coin anywhere in sight. Keep both jobs clearly in mind. The two theories disagree, more than anything else, about *which job comes first*.

## Narrative / exposition

### The first map: money is a special commodity

Start with the theory the barter story was made for, and steelman it, because it is far stronger than the previous five modules might have led you to expect.

Call it **the commodity theory of money**, sometimes called **metallism**: the view that money is fundamentally a special kind of commodity, a useful good that grew out of barter to make trading easier, and whose value rests on the material it is made of. On this account, money is a *thing*. A particular thing, chosen out of all the other tradeable things because it does the medium-of-exchange job best.

You met the seed of this in module 01, where it was named as "the theory this story needs," and then set aside. Here is the full plant. The reasoning runs straight out of the barter problem. If direct swapping fails because you cannot always find someone who wants exactly what you have (the double coincidence of wants, from module 01: barter only works when each side wants what the other has, at the same moment), then the fix is to trade through some good that *everyone* will accept. And once you are choosing such a good, some candidates are obviously better than others.

This is where the commodity theory earns real respect. Why did metals win, across so many different societies, so often? Not by accident. A lump of silver is **durable**: it does not rot, rust away, or die in the night, so it can store value across time. It is **divisible**: you can cut it into smaller pieces and melt them back together, so it can settle a debt of any size. It is **portable**: a little of it carries a lot of value, so you can put your wealth in a pouch instead of a barn. It is **scarce**: there is not so much of it lying around that it becomes worthless. Cattle store value but cannot make change. Grain is divisible but rots. Salt dissolves in the rain. Metal does none of these foolish things. The commodity theory looks at the long history of silver and gold and says: of course money settled on metal, because metal is simply the best commodity for the job. That is not a stupid argument. It is a good one, and any honest account has to grant it.

The commodity theory also has a clean answer for **fungibility**, a word worth glossing now because both theories lean on it. To be **fungible** is to be interchangeable: any one shekel of silver, or any one dollar, is as good as any other, so debts and prices can be reckoned in standard units that nobody needs to inspect individually. A bushel of *this particular* wheat differs from a bushel of *that* wheat; an ounce of pure silver is an ounce of pure silver, full stop. The commodity theory says money is fungible because the *stuff* is uniform. Silver is silver wherever you find it.

So the first map is drawn. Money is a commodity. It emerged from barter as the most convenient medium of exchange. Its value comes from its material. The barter story is its origin chapter, and a tidy one. Carl Menger, the nineteenth-century Austrian economist you met in module 01, gave this view its most careful form: money is the good that emerges, with no inventor, as the *most saleable* commodity, the one traders drift toward because everyone else will take it too.

Hold that map. Now watch a second map of the same country get drawn, by people looking at the same money and seeing something else entirely.

### The second map: money is a measure of debt

The rival theory starts not from the marketplace but from the ledger. You have already seen the ledger. Module 03's clay tablet, the receipt that recorded a debt in silver with no coin in sight, is this theory's home ground.

Call it **the credit theory of money**, also known as **chartalism**: the view that money is fundamentally a *unit of account and a measure of debt*, backed by social and state obligation, rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. On this account money is not a thing at all. It is a *relationship and a record*: a way of measuring who owes what to whom, and a promise that the owing will be honored.

Notice immediately what this theory does with the functions of money. It reorders them. The commodity theory puts the medium of exchange first (money is the thing that changes hands) and treats the unit of account as something the thing happens to do on the side. The credit theory flips this. The **unit of account** comes first, it says, the measuring-stick for debts, and a *medium of exchange* is just one optional way to settle a debt once you have a way to measure it. This is exactly the hinge from module 03, now doing theoretical work. Mesopotamia had money-of-account, prices and debts reckoned in a standard measure, for millennia before it had a coin to hand across a counter. On the credit theory that is not a curiosity. It is the normal shape of money, and circulating coin is the late and optional part.

Where the commodity theory explains fungibility by the uniformity of the metal, the credit theory explains it by the uniformity of the *measure*. A debt of three shekels is interchangeable with any other debt of three shekels not because both are made of the same stuff but because both are three units on the same scale, like two distances both measured as three miles. The shekel, recall from module 03, was a unit of weight and value long before it was ever a coin.

This is the moment to be careful in the way the whole course has tried to be careful: **do not blend the thinkers.** "The credit theory" is a family, not one voice, and its members disagree.

**A. Mitchell Innes**, an early-twentieth-century writer on money, made the boldest version of the case in two essays in 1913 and 1914. His claim was flat and startling: money is credit and nothing but credit. A coin, a banknote, an entry in a ledger, all of them are simply records of a debt, an acknowledgment that the issuer owes the holder something. The metal in a coin, on this view, is almost beside the point.

**Georg Friedrich Knapp**, a German economist, made a different and more specifically *state*-centered argument in his 1905 book, usually translated as *The State Theory of Money*. This is where the word "chartalism" comes from, from the Latin *charta*, a token or ticket. Knapp's point was that what makes a thing money is not its material but the state's declaration that this is what it will accept and demand, above all in payment of taxes. Money is a creature of law and authority. Note the difference from Innes: Innes builds money out of *private* debts and credits between any two people; Knapp builds it out of the *state's* power to name what counts. Related, but not the same.

**Philip Grierson**, a leading numismatist (a numismatist studies coins and money objects and what they reveal), came at it from yet another angle. Studying early medieval law, he argued that the very idea of a universal money-measure may have grown out of systems of *compensation*: the detailed schedules in early law codes setting how much was owed for a particular injury or death. To have such a schedule, a society needs an abstract measure of value before it needs a coin. Grierson is not simply restating Innes or Knapp; he is locating the *origin of the measure* in customary law.

**Geoffrey Ingham**, a present-day sociologist of money, has done the work of pulling these strands into a careful modern synthesis, treating money as a social relation, while keeping honest about what the theory does and does not settle.

Four thinkers, four emphases: pure credit (Innes), the state (Knapp), customary law (Grierson), social relation (Ingham). They share a starting move, money as measure and obligation rather than commodity, and they diverge after it. Attribute the claim to the person who made it, and you will not go wrong.

One last tool this map needs, and the commodity theory's most uncomfortable case. **Fiat money** is money that counts as money by declaration and acceptance, not for the value of its material: a paper banknote, a number in a bank account. The note in your pocket is not worth anything as paper. It is money because it is issued, accepted, and demanded back in taxes. The commodity theory has to work hard to explain why a worthless token functions as money. The credit theory does not blink: of course it works, because money was never about the material in the first place. Fiat is not a strange modern exception on this view. It is money showing its true nature.

There is a stronger and more sweeping version of the credit theory that you should know about and then set firmly aside. Some writers push the idea all the way to a claim that human society as such rests on an original, founding "primordial debt" to gods or ancestors, from which money and taxation descend. It is a provocative idea, associated with parts of David Graeber's work and others before him. It is also widely judged an over-reach, the kind of grand claim that cannot really be tested. Flag it as a contested extension, not as part of the solid credit theory, and move on. Notice the danger: a theory that explains *everything* by an unprovable founding debt starts to look like exactly the kind of theory-driven origin story this course has taught you to distrust.

A second guard, named in module 05 and worth repeating. The credit theory, and chartalism in particular, has a modern descendant in a school of *policy* argument about government spending and money. That is a separate thing. Here, chartalism is a theory about what money *is* and where it came from, a claim about its nature and history. It is not a program for what a government should do today. Keep the historical theory and the modern policy debate in separate drawers.

## Primary source

Here is the credit theory in its author's own voice. A. Mitchell Innes, writing in 1913, wants you to see that the dollar is not a thing you can hold.

> "Shillings and pence are merely the [terms] in which debts and credits are expressed... The eye has never seen, nor the hand touched a dollar. All that we can touch or see is a promise to pay or satisfy a debt due for an amount called a dollar."
>
> A. Mitchell Innes, "What is Money?", *Banking Law Journal* (1913)

Read the central line slowly: *the eye has never seen, nor the hand touched a dollar.* Innes's claim is that what you call a dollar is not the coin or the note in your hand. The coin is a *token* of a dollar. The dollar itself is an abstract amount, a quantity on a scale, and what the coin represents is a promise, a debt that someone owes you and will satisfy. This is the credit theory compressed into one sharp image: money is a measure and a promise, and the physical token is only its receipt.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now do the thing this whole module exists for. Lay the two maps side by side and keep an honest scorecard. Crown no winner. The aim is to see clearly what each one shows and what each one hides.

Start with **what the commodity theory explains well**, because the previous modules may have left it looking weaker than it is.

It explains, better than its rival, *why metals were chosen.* When money does take a physical, circulating form, it overwhelmingly lands on metal, and the commodity theory's reasons (durable, divisible, portable, scarce) are simply correct. The credit theory can say money need not be metal, which is true, but it has less to say about why, when a society did want a thing to hand across borders, silver kept winning. Which points to the commodity theory's other strength: **trade between strangers and across borders.** A foreign merchant who shares none of your laws, your ruler, or your social bonds will still take silver, because silver is worth something as silver. Here the medium-of-exchange job really does come first, and a debt-and-trust account strains, because there is no shared web of trust or law to anchor the debt. The commodity theory's home ground is exactly the place the credit theory is weakest: the deal between people who have no relationship at all.

Now **what the commodity theory strains to explain**, using the evidence you have gathered.

It strains against the chronology. Its origin story is barter giving rise to a circulating commodity-medium, yet the record shows the **unit of account came first** (module 03), with debts reckoned in silver that never moved. A theory whose whole picture is "the medium of exchange came first" has to explain away the millennia in which the measuring-stick existed and the medium did not. It strains against **fiat money**, too. If money's value rests on its material, a worthless paper note should not be money, and yet it plainly is. And it strains against the history of coinage you saw in module 05: coins arrive late and arrive through soldiers and taxes, not as barter's natural climax.

Turn the scorecard over. **What the credit theory explains well.**

It fits the Mesopotamian record almost without effort. Debts in silver, recorded with no coin circulating (module 03), are puzzling for a commodity theory and obvious for a credit theory: of course the measure and the debt came first, because measure and debt are what money *is*. It handles **fiat money** with no strain, as you saw. And it sits naturally with module 04's finding that debt was a governed legal institution before coins existed, and with module 05's finding that coins spread through the state's demand for taxes. The credit theory expected the state to be there at the root of money. The commodity theory expected the state to show up late, merely stamping a metal that markets had already settled on.

And finally, in fairness, **what the credit theory strains to explain.**

It is weaker on cross-border, stranger-to-stranger trade, as already noted: where there is no shared law and no ongoing relationship, "money is a measure of social and state obligation" has less to grip. It can struggle to say why metal specifically, so often, when on its own account the material should hardly matter. And its boldest extensions, the "primordial debt" idea above, over-reach into claims that cannot be tested, which is its own kind of failure.

So the scorecard does not name a champion. It names a genuine, unsettled disagreement, and that is the honest finding. The commodity theory is strong exactly where money meets the stranger and the border; the credit theory is strong exactly where money meets the ledger, the law, and the state. Each map shows real roads. Each map leaves some roads off. Reasonable scholars, looking at the same evidence, still come down differently, and a careful reader should be able to say *why each one is tempting* rather than rushing to pick.

But notice the one thing this module *has* settled, because it is the analytical payoff and it does not depend on choosing a theory. The barter story is not a neutral piece of history that happens to sit near the commodity theory. It is **the commodity theory's required origin myth.** If money is essentially a commodity that grew out of barter to ease exchange, then there *must* have been a barter stage for it to grow out of, and the story of clever traders converging on silver is not optional decoration; it is load-bearing. The theory needs the story to be true. That is why the barter myth is told with such confidence despite an empty space where its evidence should be (module 02). It is not remembered. It is required, by a theory of what money is. Whether that theory is right is unsettled. That the story serves it is not.

The two theories disagree about the *order* of money's functions. State the disagreement: which function does each theory put first, and why does that ordering matter?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The commodity theory puts the **medium of exchange** first: money is the thing that changes hands, a commodity chosen out of barter because everyone will accept it, and the unit-of-account job is something that thing does on the side. The ordering matters because it decides what counts as the *normal* case. If the medium comes first, money-of-account with no coin (Mesopotamia) looks like an oddity; if the unit of account comes first, circulating coin looks like the late, optional part, and the Mesopotamian record looks like money behaving normally.

## Recap

You now hold both maps of the same country. The **functions of money** are the textbook list of its jobs: medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account. The **commodity theory** (metallism) says money is fundamentally a special commodity that grew out of barter, valued for its material, with the medium of exchange first; it explains beautifully why metals were chosen and how strangers trade across borders, and it strains against the unit-of-account-before-coin record and against fiat money. The **credit theory** (chartalism) says money is fundamentally a unit of account and a measure of debt, backed by social and state obligation, with the measuring-stick first; here Innes, Knapp, Grierson, and Ingham each say something distinct, and the theory fits Mesopotamia, governed debt, and fiat money while straining on metal and the stranger. The scorecard names no champion, because the disagreement is real and unsettled. But one thing is settled: the barter story is the commodity theory's *required* origin myth, needed by the theory rather than dug from the ground. The next module takes the credit theory in hand and turns to debt itself, and to the feeling, which you almost certainly hold, that a debt must always be paid.
