## The hook

The story you were taught (module 01) makes a promise it has never kept. Recall the shape of it: first **barter**, swapping goods directly for goods with no money in between; then the friction of the **double coincidence of wants**, the need for each side to want exactly what the other has at the same moment; then money, invented to end the friction; then markets and credit, in that order. It is clean, it is intuitive, and the last module made the case for it as strongly as it can be made. The story even has a place in the world where it says it happened: long ago, in the moneyless village, the chicken-keeper and the shoemaker.

So go and look. That is the move this module makes, and it is the simplest move in the whole course. The barter story is a claim about the past. It says there was once a stage of human life that ran on direct swaps and then climbed out of it by inventing money. If that stage was real, it left a world behind, and people who study the actual lives of actual societies should be able to point to it. Anthropologists have been looking, in the field and in the archives, for well over a century. The question of this module is not whether the story is elegant. The last module granted that it is. The question is whether, when you go looking for the barter village, you find it.

You do not.

## Context & background

To see why that is, you need to know who has been looking, and how.

The people who study how living and recently-living societies actually run their economies do **ethnography**: the close study of how real societies are organized, carried out by observers who live among them, watch what people do, ask them, and write it down. It is unglamorous, patient work. An ethnographer does not deduce from an armchair what a society "must" do. She goes and finds out what it does. Over the last hundred-odd years, ethnographers have described the workings of an enormous range of communities, including many with little or no money in the textbook sense, across every inhabited continent.

This matters because of what kind of claim the barter story is. Last module ended on the distinction the whole course turns on. There is a difference between a thing that is *remembered or recorded*, attested in some evidence you can point to, and a thing that is *required by a theory*, believed because the reasoning seems to demand it. The barter story, told as **conjectural history**, an origin reasoned out from first principles rather than dug up from evidence, lives mostly in the second category. It was deduced, not observed. Ethnography is exactly the kind of evidence that could move it into the first category, if the evidence were there. A field that has catalogued so many moneyless and near-moneyless societies is precisely where the barter economy should turn up, if it ever existed.

Keep one distinction firmly in hand as you read, because the course's honesty depends on it. There is the **evidence**, what ethnographers actually found in the field. And there is the **interpretation**, what that evidence implies about money. This module will mark the seam between them every time. The finding that no one has described a barter economy is evidence. The conclusion that money therefore did not come from barter is a reading of that evidence. Strong, but a reading. Watch for the line between the two.

## Narrative / exposition

Here is what the ethnographers found instead. Not a moneyless world of strangers haggling good-for-good, but something both more ordinary and more interesting: people who already knew each other, keeping track of who owed whom.

Picture a small village where everyone knows everyone. A woman needs a basket; her neighbour makes baskets; she takes one. No one weighs out a chicken against it on the spot. No one matches up wants in a single frozen instant. The basket-maker knows that the woman keeps bees, and that honey will come around in season, and that her own roof will need rethatching before winter, and that this woman's brother is good with a roof. Everyone in the village is carrying, in their head and in the village's collective memory, a rough running tab of favours given and owed. Nothing is settled today because nothing needs to be. The relationships are long, the memories are good, and the accounts are kept not on clay or paper but in the dense web of who-did-what-for-whom that binds the village together. This is the image to hold for the whole module: **the running tab at a village where everyone knows everyone.** People did not barter. They ran tabs.

Notice that this is the exact opposite of the barter story's predicament. The barter story's whole engine, the double coincidence of wants, only bites if every exchange must close on the spot, fully, then and there. You give me the basket, I must hand you something you want for it right now, or there is no deal. But the running tab dissolves that problem before money ever appears. If I can take the basket now and let the debt stand, then I never need to be holding, at this instant, the precise thing you happen to want. I can settle later, in honey, in labour, in a turn of help when you need it. The "problem money was invented to solve" is not a problem these communities had. They had already solved it, with memory and obligation, long before any coin.

Anthropologists have names for the forms this took. **The gift** and **reciprocity** are give-and-take that build and repay a relationship rather than clearing a price: you give, and the giving creates a bond and an expectation, repaid in time and not always in kind. **Redistribution** is a center, a temple, a chief, a big household, gathering goods in and handing them back out, so the flow runs through the middle rather than directly between two traders. And underneath the gift and redistribution alike runs the thing that matters most for this course: **credit and debt**, tracked socially. Credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later; debt is what you then owe; they are the two sides of one relationship. The running tab is credit and debt, kept by a community instead of a bank.

So the record does not just fail to show the barter stage. It shows something standing where the barter stage was supposed to be, doing the job the barter stage was supposed to fail at. Within communities, credit came first. That is the positive finding, and it is the spine of everything this course builds next.

## Primary source

The sharpest single statement of the negative finding comes from **Caroline Humphrey**, an anthropologist who set out to examine the barter story against the ethnographic record and reported the verdict in a 1985 essay. Read her words exactly, because the precision is the point. She does not say barter is fictional. She says something narrower and harder to wriggle out of.

> "No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described, let alone the emergence from it of money; all available ethnography suggests that there never has been such a thing."
>
> (Caroline Humphrey, "Barter and Economic Disintegration," *Man*, 1985)

Sit with the structure of that sentence. The claim is not "no one ever bartered." The claim is that no *barter economy* has been described, no whole society running on direct swaps as its basic way of getting things, and, the crucial second clause, no case of money *emerging from* such an economy. The very sequence the textbook teaches, barter society becoming a money society, is the thing the record does not contain. Humphrey is not denying that goods get swapped. She is denying that the swapping was ever the foundation a society stood on, or the soil that money grew out of.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now mark the seam, carefully, because this is exactly where a careless reader, or a careless author, ruins the argument.

The evidence is Humphrey's: no barter economy in the ethnographic record, and no money emerging from one. The interpretation, the reading this course will defend, is that the standard sequence is therefore not history. The barter-to-money story is not something humanity remembers or that the record preserves. It is something a theory needed to be true. (Whether the people who told it ever meant it as literal history, or only as a fable about the logic of money, is the live argument module 01 raised and module 06 will return to. Hold it lightly here. Either way, the *sequence* has no foothold in the record.)

But here is the trap, and it is set for you personally. Having heard that the barter economy is a phantom, it is tempting to swing to the far wall and announce: barter never happened, barter is a myth, no one ever swapped a goat for a pot. That is false, and stating it would hand every critic of this course an easy and deserved win. Barter is real. It happens. The precise claim is narrow, and the whole credibility of the argument rides on keeping it narrow: there is no record of a *barter-economy-to-money sequence*; there is no foundational barter stage that societies climbed out of. Barter as such is alive and well. It simply does not appear where the myth puts it.

Where does it appear? In two places, and both are revealing.

The first is **between strangers**, between people with no ongoing relationship and so no running tab to keep. The village tab works because the people are bound to each other and will meet again. Strip that away, two groups who meet once, who may be rivals or enemies, who share no web of obligation, and there is nothing to track a debt on. So they swap on the spot, good for good, settled in the moment, exactly because they do not trust each other to settle later. Barter, in the record, is overwhelmingly a thing that happens at the *edges* between communities, not the *inside* of one. It is what you do with people you cannot run a tab with.

The second place is more striking, because it looks for all the world like the myth coming true, and it is the cleanest test of whether you have understood the claim. Barter turns up when a **money economy collapses.** The economist and former prisoner of war **R. A. Radford** wrote in 1945 a famous account of a wartime POW camp where, with Red Cross rations arriving and no official currency, cigarettes became money. Prices were quoted in cigarettes; a cigarette bought a coffee or a portion of jam; the camp grew a small economy on a tobacco standard. It is a beautiful little case, and it is constantly misused. People point to it and say: look, money emerging spontaneously from trade, just as the story says. But look again through the distinction this course has built. These were not people climbing out of a primitive barter stage. They were modern adults who had grown up with money, dropped into a place with no coin, who promptly *reinvented* something to do money's job. The cigarette was a money *substitute*, reached for by people who already knew exactly what money was for. The same shape appears in **post-Soviet Russia** in the 1990s, when the ruble economy seized up and firms and households fell back on barter to keep functioning. That is barter *after* money, in money's absence, not barter *before* money on the road to inventing it.

This is the beat that inoculates you against the over-correction. The POW camp and post-Soviet barter are not the barter stage the myth needs. They are the opposite case. They are what happens when a society that *has* money loses access to it, and they show barter as a fallback, a substitute, a thing people reach for when the normal machinery fails, never the ground floor of economic life. The myth needs barter at the *bottom*, as the thing that came before everything. The record finds it at the *edges* and in the *wreckage*, never at the bottom.

So the honest summary is not "barter is a lie." It is sharper than that and harder to dodge. Barter is real, but it is a money substitute or a stranger's transaction, and the one thing it has never been shown to be is the foundational stage from which money grew. The myth did not get a detail wrong. It put the one form of exchange that happens *between* and *after* communities at the *origin* of community economic life, where the record shows something else entirely standing: the running tab, kept on credit, among people who knew each other.

## Check yourself

Pause before reading on. Caroline Humphrey's verdict is often summarized as "barter never happened." That summary is wrong. In your own words: what does her finding actually claim, and how is that different from "barter never happened"?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Humphrey's finding is that no *barter economy*, a whole society running on direct goods-for-goods swaps as its basic system, has ever been described by ethnographers, and that no case of money *emerging from* such an economy has been described either. The claim is about a missing *stage* and a missing *sequence*, not about whether swapping ever occurs. "Barter never happened" is a much stronger and false claim: barter does happen, between strangers and when a money economy collapses. The precise finding leaves room for real barter while still denying the myth its foundational barter-to-money story.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer here. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **State the precise claim without overclaiming.** A friend says, "I read that barter is a complete myth and never existed." In a short paragraph, correct them *without* swinging to the opposite error. Say exactly what the record shows about barter and exactly what it does not, and explain why the narrow version is the defensible one.
2. **Why credit and not barter?** Using the running tab in a village where everyone knows everyone, explain why a small, tightly-bound community would naturally keep track of debts rather than settle every exchange on the spot. What does an ongoing relationship let people do that strangers cannot?
3. **The hardest test case.** Take the POW-camp cigarette economy (or post-Soviet barter) and argue *both* sides: first the case that it looks like the barter story coming true, then the case that it is actually the opposite. Which reading do you find better supported, and what specific feature of the situation settles it for you?
4. **Evidence and interpretation.** Identify one sentence in this module that is *evidence* and one that is *interpretation*, and explain how you can tell them apart. Why does it matter for a course like this one to keep marking that line?

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- States the precise claim accurately: no barter *economy* and no barter-to-money *sequence* in the record, while granting that barter genuinely occurs between strangers and when money collapses. Does not slide into "barter never happened."
- Uses the key terms correctly and keeps them distinct: **ethnography**, **the gift / reciprocity**, **redistribution**, **credit / debt**, **barter as a money substitute**. Re-glosses rather than just naming.
- Attributes the finding to Caroline Humphrey as Humphrey's, and treats the POW-camp case as R. A. Radford's account, rather than fusing everything into one anonymous "anthropologists say."
- Keeps evidence (what ethnographers found; Humphrey's verdict; Radford's camp) visibly separate from interpretation (that the myth's sequence is therefore not history), and can point to the seam.
- On the POW-camp prompt, recognizes that the decisive feature is that the participants already knew money, so what they built was a substitute, not a first invention.

## Recap

The barter story makes a claim about the past, so this module went and checked it against the past. Through **ethnography**, the close study of how real societies actually run, anthropologists have described a vast range of communities, and **Caroline Humphrey** put the verdict bluntly: no barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described, let alone money emerging from one. What the record shows instead is the running tab at a village where everyone knows everyone: not strangers haggling good-for-good, but neighbours tracking who owes whom, settled later by relationship and custom. That is **credit and debt**, kept socially, and within communities it was already there before any money. Barter is real, but it lives at the edges (between strangers) and in the wreckage (when a money economy collapses, as in R. A. Radford's POW camp or post-Soviet Russia), as a money *substitute*, never the foundational stage the myth needs. The myth did not get a detail wrong; it put exchange-between-strangers at the origin of life-within-a-community. That leaves a large and obvious question. If credit and obligation were already there at the start, what did the keeping of those running tabs actually look like, written down, before there were any coins? The next module opens the ledger.
