## The hook

Picture the end of a long evening at a bar where the bartender knows you. You have had four drinks. You have not paid for a single one of them, and yet at no point tonight were you drinking on credit in any way that felt like borrowing. You did not sign anything. No coin left your pocket. When you finally settle up, the bartender does not weigh out what you owe in beer or in peanuts. She reads a number off a slip or a screen, in dollars, and you hand over the dollars.

Stop on that for a second, because something quietly strange has happened, and the strangeness is the whole of this module. All night, your tab was reckoned in dollars. Every drink had a price in dollars the instant it was poured. Your debt grew in dollars, drink by drink. But for most of the evening, no dollar moved at all. The dollars were doing their work as a measuring-stick long before any dollar bill changed hands. The thing that counted the debt and the thing that finally paid it were not the same kind of thing, and they did not arrive at the same time.

That gap, between the money you reckon in and the money you hand over, is the most important idea in this course. The barter myth never sees it. Once you can see it, the myth's whole sequence falls apart, and the falling-apart happens not in a thought experiment but in the dirt of southern Iraq, five thousand years ago.

## Context & background

Two modules of groundwork stand behind this one. In module 01 you met the story at its strongest: **the barter myth**, the standard sequence in which people first swapped goods directly for goods, money was then invented to fix the clumsiness of that swapping, and markets and credit grew up afterward on top of money. In module 02 you saw why that sequence cannot be the history. No society has ever been found running on barter and then upgrading to money. What communities actually ran on, the record shows, was **credit and debt tracked socially**: debt is what you owe, credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later, and the two are simply the two sides of one relationship. People kept a running tab of who owed whom, settled by custom and relationship rather than by haggling out a price every time.

This module asks the next question. If credit came first, what did that credit get reckoned in? You can owe someone, but you have to owe them *some amount of something*. What was the something, before there were coins to be the something?

The answer comes from **Mesopotamia**, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, roughly where Iraq is now, in the centuries around 3000 BCE. This is the world of the first cities, the first large temples and palaces, and the first writing. It is also, for our purposes, the world of the first surviving account books. And here is the fact the whole module turns on. The temples and palaces of Mesopotamia were running detailed accounts, pricing goods and recording debts, in silver and in barley, more than two thousand years before anyone anywhere struck a coin. There were no coins in this world. There was a great deal of money.

To hold that sentence steady, without it sounding like a paradox or a trick, you need one distinction. It is the distinction the bar tab was quietly built on. We can finally name it.

## Narrative / exposition

Go back to the tab. Two different jobs were being done by "the dollar," and they are worth pulling apart and looking at one at a time, because the myth jams them together and that is its central mistake.

The first job is measuring. All night, the dollar was the yardstick that said how much each drink was worth and how much, in total, you owed. It answered the question "how much?" It did this whether or not a single dollar was in the room. A debt of "twelve dollars" is a perfectly definite thing even while it sits unpaid; the dollar here is a unit you count in, the way a foot is a unit you measure length in. You can say a table is six feet long without ever laying a physical foot against it. A measuring-stick used this way, the standard you price things and reckon debts in, is what economists call a **unit of account**: the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in (you owe me "twelve dollars"), even when no coin moves.

The second job is changing hands. At the very end, you handed over actual dollar bills, and *those* dollars did something the measuring-stick dollars never had to do. They physically moved, from you to the bartender, and that movement settled the debt. A thing used this way, the object that physically passes between two people to complete a deal, is what economists call a **medium of exchange**: the thing that physically changes hands in a deal, the coin, the note, the bill.

Two jobs, then. A unit of account answers "how much do you owe?" A medium of exchange answers "what do you actually hand over?" In a modern economy the same word, "dollar," does both jobs, which is exactly why they are so easy to confuse. But notice they came apart on the tab, in time and in substance. The measuring went on for hours; the handing-over took a moment at the end. The myth's whole sequence assumes the handing-over thing came first, that money began as some good people passed around, a medium of exchange, and only later got used to keep score. The Mesopotamian record shows the opposite order. The measuring-stick came first. The score was being kept for two thousand years before there was a coin to hand over.

Look at what the scribes were actually doing. A temple in a Sumerian city ran something like a vast estate. It took in barley at harvest, held herds, employed weavers and brewers and field hands, rented out land, and lent out grain and silver. All of this had to be tracked, and it was tracked on clay. Scribes recorded what was owed and what was paid, what went into the storehouse and what came out, the wages of a workgroup, the rent on a field, the interest on a loan. To track all that, you need a common measure, a single yardstick to put a bushel of barley, a day's labor, and a sheep onto the same page so the books can balance. Mesopotamian scribes used two: barley, by volume, and silver, by weight, measured in a unit called the **shekel** (which here is a weight, a measure, long before it was ever a coin). A debt might be written as "so many shekels of silver" or "so much barley." The silver and barley were the units of account. They were the dollars on the tab.

And here is the part that should stop you. Much of that silver never moved. A debt could be incurred, recorded, carried for a season, and settled, all in silver, without a lump of silver ever changing hands, because it could be cleared against other debts, or paid in barley or labor at a customary rate, or simply rolled forward on the books. The silver was doing the measuring-stick job, the unit of account, while rarely doing the changing-hands job. Prices, debts, rents, and wages, all reckoned in a standard measure with no coin circulating: that is **money-of-account**, money that exists as a way of keeping score without any minted thing passing from hand to hand. This is money. It is just not the kind of money the myth had taught you to picture.

There is a second clue sitting right alongside the account books, and it is almost too neat. We know all this because the Mesopotamians wrote it down, and when you ask what the earliest writing was *for*, the answer is not prayers or poems or the deeds of kings. The earliest cuneiform, the wedge-shaped marks pressed into wet clay, is overwhelmingly administrative: lists, receipts, inventories, ration records, debts. Writing arose largely for accounts. The first thing humans seem to have wanted a durable record of was not their gods or their stories but who owed what to whom. Stories and hymns in writing come later. The ledger comes first. If money were really born as a handy good people passed around, this is a strange thing to find at the dawn of literacy. It is exactly what you would expect to find if money were born as a way of keeping track of debts.

## Primary source

This module's evidence is not a single quotable line but a *type* of object, so the honest thing is to describe that type faithfully and read it, rather than to invent the contents of one particular tablet. (The general picture, debts and prices reckoned in silver and barley on clay, is solid; the contents of any specific tablet are not something to make up.)

Picture, then, a small clay tablet of a kind found by the many thousands across Mesopotamian sites. It is a private loan record. Pressed into it, in cuneiform, is roughly this shape of information: a quantity of silver or barley, named as the amount owed; the name of the person who owes it; the name of the person it is owed to; often a rate of interest; sometimes a date or a term by which it must be repaid; and the names of witnesses. Some such tablets were sealed inside an outer clay envelope, itself inscribed with a copy of the terms, so that the inner tablet could not be altered without breaking the seal. A few were shaped so they could be destroyed when the debt was paid, the physical record cancelled along with the obligation.

Now read it. Notice what is and is not on this object. There is a precise quantity of value, in silver or barley. There are the two parties and the relationship between them, one owes, one is owed. There is, on many, an interest rate and a due date. What is *not* there is any coin. The silver named on the tablet need never have been seen by the borrower; it is the measure the debt is counted in, not necessarily a thing that was handed over. The tablet is not money in the sense of something you spend. It is money in a deeper sense: it is the record of a debt, the thing that says who owes whom and how much, made durable in clay. Hold that object in your mind. It is the image that overturns the whole sequence: money-of-account made visible, a debt in silver with no coin in sight.

A reader who wants a named voice for this reading can have one, with the seam marked. **A. Mitchell Innes**, an early-twentieth-century British writer on money whose 1913 and 1914 essays are the foundational modern statement of this view, argued that credit and debt are older than coined money, and that money is at bottom a matter of credit, an obligation recorded and transferable, rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. That last step is *interpretation*, and an interpretation we will weigh carefully later, not a fact the tablet hands us. The tablet is the evidence: debts reckoned in silver, on clay, before coins. What it *means* about the nature of money is the argument the rest of the course takes up.

The friend has collapsed the two jobs back together. Silver was serving as the **unit of account**, the measuring-stick the debt was reckoned in ("so many shekels of silver"), but it was very often not the **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changed hands. Debts in silver were frequently settled some other way, by clearing them against other debts, by paying in barley or labor at a customary rate, or by rolling them forward, with no silver moving at all. So silver as a measure of value came first and did most of the work; silver as a thing you hand over was the lesser, occasional job. The myth needs money to begin as a thing people pass around. The record shows it beginning as a standard for keeping score. Naming the debt in silver is not the same as paying it in silver, and the gap between the two is the whole point.

## Narrative / exposition (continued)

There is one more object to put in your hands, because so far the money-of-account can feel abstract, a number on a clerk's tablet. It need not be. A debt can be a physical thing you carry without being a coin you spend, and the clearest example is the **tally**: a physical record of a debt, classically a notched stick split in two, with one half kept by each party.

Here is how it works, and it is worth picturing exactly. You lend me ten units of something. We take a stick and cut notches across it to mark the amount, a system of notch-sizes standing for the sums. Then we split the stick lengthwise, straight down through the notches, into two halves. You keep one half, the longer piece. I keep the other. The notches now run across both halves identically, because they were one cut. When the debt is settled, the two halves are brought back together, and they must match, notch for notch and grain for grain, or the record is false. No one can quietly add a notch, because the wood's own grain would betray the join. (This is not only ancient. The English Exchequer kept its accounts on split tally sticks for centuries.)

Look at what the tally is. It is not the thing owed. It is not a medium of exchange you spend on bread. It is the *record* of who owes whom, made tangible, splittable, carryable, verifiable. The debt is the reality; the stick is its receipt, held in the hand. And the half you hold is genuinely worth something, because it is a claim, a proof that someone owes you. People could and did pass such claims along to third parties, which is the first hint of how a tracked personal debt might one day harden into impersonal, transferable money. We will come back to that hardening. For now the tally does one job: it makes the module's whole thesis something you could hold. Money here is not a coin. It is a record of who owes whom.

## Analysis & interpretation

Step back and weigh what we have, and keep the seam between evidence and interpretation in view, because this is exactly the kind of place a course about a theory-driven story could smuggle in a theory-driven story of its own.

Here is the **evidence**, and it is firm. Mesopotamian temples and palaces, around 3000 BCE, kept detailed accounts of debts, prices, rents, and wages, reckoned in silver and barley, more than two thousand years before coinage. Much of the silver was a measure, not a thing that moved. The earliest writing is overwhelmingly administrative, made for accounts. Debts were recorded on clay, with parties, amounts, often interest and a term. Tallies record debts as physical, verifiable claims. None of that is contested. It is what the dirt has actually produced.

Here is the **interpretation**, which is a further step and must be marked as one. From this evidence, Innes, and later the anthropologist David Graeber in his 2011 book *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (a sweeping and influential synthesis, and one specialists contest on specifics, so its readings travel under its own name), draw a large conclusion: that money is *essentially* credit, a measure of debt and an obligation, rather than a commodity that emerged from barter. On this reading the unit of account is what money fundamentally *is*, and the coin is a late and secondary thing. That conclusion fits the Mesopotamian record strikingly well. But notice that fitting this evidence well is not the same as being proven. The evidence shows that money-of-account preceded coinage. Whether it shows that money is *in its nature* debt rather than a commodity is a separate claim, an argument about the essence of a thing, and arguments about essences are not settled by a single body of evidence, however good.

So plant a flag and leave it planted. There is a rival theory of what money basically is, built around exactly this reading: the **credit theory of money**, also called **chartalism**, which holds that money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt, backed by social and ultimately state obligation, rather than a commodity that grew out of barter. It is the direct competitor to the commodity theory you met at the close of module 01, the theory the barter myth needs, which says money is essentially a special tradeable good. The Mesopotamian ledgers are the credit theory's home ground. We will put the two theories side by side, steelman each, and weigh what each explains and fails to explain, in module 06. Do not let this module talk you into crowning a winner. It has done its job if you can see that the unit of account came first in the record, and that this fact is what gives the credit theory something to stand on.

One guard before we close, carried from module 02 and owed to honesty. None of this makes early Mesopotamia a gentle place, a lost age of trust before cold cash. These were stratified societies with temples and palaces commanding labor, and the same loan tablets that prove debt was reckoned in silver also record interest, terms, and the machinery of obligation that could, when a debt went bad, cost a person their freedom. That darker side of debt is the subject of the next module. The point here is not that the past was kinder. The point is only, and it is enough, that money did not begin the way you were told.

## Recap

Money did not begin as a thing people passed around. It began as a way of keeping score. The hinge that lets you see this is the difference between a **unit of account**, the measuring-stick you price things and reckon debts in ("you owe me twelve shekels"), and a **medium of exchange**, the thing that physically changes hands to settle a deal. The bar tab pulls them apart: the dollar measures all night, but the dollar bill moves only at the end. Mesopotamia pulls them apart by two thousand years. Around 3000 BCE, temple and palace scribes were pricing goods and recording debts in silver and barley, **money-of-account** with no coin circulating, and the earliest writing we have was made largely to keep exactly these accounts. The **tally**, a notched stick split in two whose halves must match, makes the same truth something you can hold: money as a record of who owes whom. All of that is evidence, and it is firm. The further claim that money is therefore *essentially* debt is interpretation, the home ground of a rival to the commodity theory called the **credit theory of money**, or **chartalism**, which we will weigh in module 06. The next module stays in this world and asks a harder question. If debt was already this central, this early, how was it governed, and what happened to people who could not pay?
