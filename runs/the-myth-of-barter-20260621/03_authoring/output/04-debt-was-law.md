## The hook

Imagine you wanted to settle, once and for all, that a thing was serious. Not a private understanding, not a custom that bends with the weather, but a fixed and public rule that everyone could be held to. You might do what a Babylonian king is said to have done nearly four thousand years ago. You would have your laws carved into a tall pillar of black stone, set up where people passed, so that the rules stood there in the open, the same for the strong and the weak, outlasting the king who made them.

Now ask what you would bother to carve. You would not waste good stone on trivia. You would inscribe the things a society cannot afford to leave vague. And when we read what one such stone actually says, a large part of it turns out to be about debt. Who may charge what for a loan. What happens when a debtor cannot pay. How long a person handed over against a debt may be held before they must go free. Debt, in other words, was not a soft favour between neighbours in the ancient world. It was hard law, written in stone, governed in detail, a thousand years before the first coin was ever struck.

## Context & background

The last module left you somewhere most people never expect to stand. Long before coins, the cities of Mesopotamia, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates in what is now Iraq, were already running on **debt** and **credit**: debt is what you owe, credit is the trust that lets you receive now and pay later, the two sides of one relationship. Scribes priced goods and recorded who owed whom in standard measures of silver and barley, with no coin circulating at all. That is the picture from module 03: a clay tablet that is really a receipt, money working as a measuring-stick before it was ever a thing in the hand.

So debt came first. The obvious next question, and the one this module answers, is: how seriously was it taken? You might guess that without coins, without banks, without a money economy as we know it, debt was a loose and neighbourly thing, a running tab settled by goodwill. That guess is wrong, and the evidence that it is wrong is one of the most famous documents to survive from the ancient world.

That document is **the Code of Hammurabi**, a Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE that regulates interest, debt, and bondage. Its author, **Hammurabi**, was a Babylonian king of that era who unified much of Mesopotamia under his rule. The code was inscribed on stone, and a great stele bearing it survives, carved with provision after provision in the wedge-shaped script the Mesopotamians pressed into clay and stone, the script called cuneiform. It is not the first law code from the region, and it was not the only one. But it is full, it is detailed, and it survives, which makes it a window into how an early state thought about the obligations between its people.

Here is the one term you need before we open it, because it is the dark heart of the whole module. **Debt bondage** is being forced into servitude to work off a debt you cannot pay. Not slavery from birth, not capture in war, but bondage that begins with an ordinary loan and an ordinary failure to repay it. A man borrows against the harvest, the harvest fails, and the lender takes what the man has left to give: his labour, or worse, the labour of his wife, his son, his daughter. Hold that definition. It is the thing the code is forced to manage, and it is the reason this module will not let you romanticize the world that credit built.

## Narrative / exposition

What does it mean to say debt was "governed"? It means a society had decided that the relationship between a lender and a borrower was too consequential to be left to the two parties alone, and stepped in to set the terms.

Consider what governing debt actually requires. First, a rule about the price of a loan, the **interest**, the extra a borrower repays on top of what was lent. If there is no limit, a desperate borrower will agree to almost anything, and a lender can write a loan that can never be repaid. So a state that wants debt not to swallow its people sets a ceiling. The Code of Hammurabi does this. It distinguishes loans of silver from loans of grain and caps what may be charged on each. We will not put a number on the ceiling here, because the exact rates and their reading are a matter for specialists and the translations vary. What matters for our purpose is the bare fact, and the bare fact is striking enough: nearly four thousand years ago, a government was already in the business of telling lenders how much they could charge.

Second, governing debt means rules about what a creditor may seize, and from whom, and for how long. This is where the code turns to debt bondage, and where its provisions become, by turns, harsh and humane in the same breath. The code takes for granted that a person can be handed over against a debt. That is the harshness, and we should not look away from it: a defaulting debtor's dependants could be given up to work off what was owed. But the code also limits the practice. It sets bounds on how long such a person may be held, and on what may and may not be done to them. The state, in other words, accepts that debt bondage exists and then tries to fence it in.

That double move, accept the institution, then cage it, tells you something precise about the ancient world. Debt bondage was common enough, and dangerous enough, that a king found it worth carving limits on it into stone. You do not legislate against a thing that never happens. The very existence of these provisions is evidence that loans went bad, that people lost their freedom over them, and that the loss was a standing feature of life, not a rare catastrophe.

There is one more thing the existence of this code quietly establishes, and it is the spine of the whole course. None of this needed coins. The cap on interest, the rules on seizure, the limit on bondage: all of it governs debts reckoned in silver and grain, in a world with no coined money at all. Debt was a fully developed legal institution, complete with a price control and a labour regime, while money was still a number in a ledger rather than a disc in a purse. Coins, when they finally arrive in module 05, will be late to a story that was already well under way.

## Primary source

Here is a provision of the Code of Hammurabi on debt servitude. It is one of the code's most frequently translated and discussed clauses, and it concerns exactly the limit we have been circling: how long a member of a debtor's household may be held before the debt is treated as discharged and the person set free. The wording below follows the sense of the standard translations of this provision; where translators differ on a phrase, I have kept to what they agree on, and I have not attached a clause number, since numbering varies between editions.

> If a man has a debt against him and sells or hands over his wife, his son, or his daughter for silver, or gives them into service to work off the debt, they shall work in the house of their buyer or holder for three years; in the fourth year their freedom shall be restored.
>
> Source: paraphrase of a provision of the Code of Hammurabi (~1750 BCE) on debt servitude.

Read it slowly, because there is a great deal packed into a few lines.

Notice first what it simply assumes. A man's debt can reach past the man himself and fall on his wife and children. They can be handed over, "for silver" or into service, as a way of paying what he owes. The provision does not argue for this or apologize for it. It treats it as an ordinary fact of the world, the background against which the rule is written. That single assumption is the anti-romantic core of this module, and we will come back to it.

Notice next what the provision actually does, against that background. It sets a limit: three years of service, and then, in the fourth year, freedom. The debt is treated as worked off; the person is released. This is not nothing. It is a state stepping between a creditor and the people in his power and declaring that the claim has an end. A debt may take your labour, the rule says, but it may not take your life. There is a line, and here is where it falls.

So the same short passage shows you both faces of debt-as-law at once. The harsh face: people were pledged and seized for the debts of their household. The restraining face: the law put a fence around how far that could go. Hold both. Neither one alone is the truth of the ancient world; the truth is the tension between them, carved into the same stone.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now for the move this whole course is built on: separating what the evidence shows from what we choose to make of it. They are not the same, and a careful reader keeps them apart.

Start with the evidence, stated as plainly as it can be. A Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE regulates interest on loans, governs the seizure of people for debt, and limits how long debt bondage may last. That is solid. It rests on a surviving document that scholars have read for well over a century. We can point to it. It is, in the language of module 01, **remembered**, attested in a record we can hold up, not merely reasoned out from what seems likely.

Now the interpretation, which is a separate act. What does that evidence *imply* about money? Here we are no longer reading the stone; we are arguing about what it means. One reading, and it is the reading that fits this course's spine, runs like this: if debt was already a central, legally governed institution before coined money existed, then money cannot have begun as a handy commodity that emerged from barter to grease trade. The thing being governed, debt, was there first and mattered enormously, while coins were nowhere in sight. On this view, the code is one more piece of evidence that money is, at bottom, a matter of obligation and account rather than of metal in the hand. That reading has a name we have already met in passing, the **credit theory** of money, the idea that money is basically a unit of account and a measure of debt rather than a special tradeable commodity. We will weigh it properly against its rival in module 06. For now, mark it clearly as an *interpretation*, a way of reading the evidence, not a fact the stone states.

Why insist on the seam so hard? Because the opposite temptation, blending evidence and interpretation, is exactly the error this course exists to expose. The barter myth does it: it takes a piece of armchair reasoning and dresses it up as remembered history. We do not get to do the same in the other direction. The code regulating debt is a fact. "Therefore money is essentially debt" is a claim, a good one, a contested one, but a claim. Keep them in separate hands.

There is a second interpretive trap here, and it is the one this module was built to spring shut. It is the temptation to read "credit came first" as "the past was kinder." It is an easy slide. We have spent two modules dismantling a story in which money and markets are the heroes, and it would feel tidy if the world before coins turned out to be gentler, more human, more bound by trust than by cash. Resist that completely. The same record that shows debt coming first shows debt bondage coming with it. People were pledged and seized for what they owed. A bad harvest could cost a family its freedom. The code's three-year limit is not evidence of a soft world; it is evidence of a world harsh enough that such a limit was worth carving in stone. The point of this history was never that the past was better. The point is that it was *different*, that money and debt did not arrive in the order or the spirit the myth claims. Contingency, not nostalgia. The credit-first past was not an Eden, and module 07, when it returns to debt's morality, will lean on exactly this counterweight.

1. **Read the limit two ways.** The provision caps debt servitude at three years. One reader calls this evidence of a relatively humane state protecting the vulnerable; another calls it evidence of how brutal debt bondage must have been, since it needed limiting at all. 3. **Against nostalgia.** This module insists that "credit came first" does not mean "the past was kinder." Using the primary source, make the case for that insistence in your own words. What in the provision itself blocks the romantic reading?

## Recap

You came into this module knowing that debt came before coins; you leave knowing that debt came before coins *and was governed law*. The Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian law code from around 1750 BCE, caps interest, rules on the seizure of debtors and their families, and limits **debt bondage**, the forced servitude that began with a loan a person could not repay, all of it in a world with no coined money at all. Two disciplines came with that. First, keep the seam visible: that the code regulates debt is evidence; that this proves money is essentially debt is the credit theory's interpretation, to be weighed in module 06. Second, and this is the guard the rest of the course depends on, do not mistake "credit came first" for "the past was kinder." The same stone that records the priority of debt records the bondage that came with it. The next module turns to the thing that has been conspicuously absent all along: coins, who finally arrive, late and from an unexpected direction.
