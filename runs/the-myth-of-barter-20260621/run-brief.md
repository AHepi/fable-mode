## Interpretation notes

**What the topic is.** The course dismantles the standard origin story of money — *"first people
bartered; barter was clumsy (the double coincidence of wants); so money was invented to fix it; then
markets, banking, and credit followed"* — and replaces it with the historical record, which runs the
other way: **credit, debt, and social obligation predate coined money by millennia.** The object of
study is the myth itself: where it comes from, why it is told, what it gets wrong, and what actually
happened instead.

**Same vein as "How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural."** Humanities, intro level, written from
ideology critique / critical political economy. The disciplines carry over verbatim: **steelman before
denaturalizing** (state the barter story's strongest, most intuitive form before dismantling it),
**keep evidence visibly separate from interpretation**, **name the live scholarly debates rather than
resolving them**, **no teleology** (money's history was not an upward march toward coins/markets), **no
romanticizing** pre-coinage societies (they had debt bondage, slavery, hierarchy), and **don't blend
the thinkers**. Voice: the same essayistic narrator — sharp, concrete, talking to one intelligent
reader who has never questioned this. **No em dashes** (now a hard pipeline rule); length follows the
idea, not a word count (also now pipeline policy).

**History is the throughline; models are explored, not assumed (the user's key steer).** The spine is
the historical and ethnographic record: Mesopotamian temple/palace credit and accounting, tallies,
the Code of Hammurabi and recorded debts, the actual ethnography of exchange, the origins of coinage
in states/war/taxation, debt bondage and periodic debt cancellations (jubilees). The abstract
**models** — the *commodity theory of money* (money as a special commodity that emerged from barter to
ease exchange) versus the *credit / state ("chartalist") theory* (money as a unit of account for
measuring debt, backed by the state's power to tax) — are treated as **objects of study**: explored
critically, their assumptions exposed, never used as the unexamined frame. The course teaches the
reader to see the barter story *as a model doing ideological work*, not as remembered history.

**Scope — in.** The barter origin story as told by Adam Smith (*Wealth of Nations*) and repeated in
modern intro-economics textbooks; the "double coincidence of wants" argument and why it is a logical
fable, not a historical claim; the anthropological record (Caroline Humphrey: no example of a pure
barter economy has ever been described; where barter *does* occur — between strangers/enemies, or when
an existing money system collapses); credit and accounting *before* coinage (Mesopotamia ~3000 BCE,
temple and palace ledgers, silver and barley as units of account without circulating as coin, tallies,
Hammurabi's debt provisions); what money actually is (the two models above, with A. Mitchell Innes's
credit theory, Geoffrey Ingham, Philip Grierson on money as an abstract measure); the origins of
**coinage** (~7th c. BCE Lydia and the Greek world; the state/war/soldier/tax nexus — coins to pay
troops and taxes to provision them; markets partly *created* by states); debt and its **moralization**
(the language of debt as moral obligation; debt bondage; periodic clean-slate cancellations / biblical
Jubilee and Mesopotamian *amargi*); why the myth **persists** and whose interests its persistence
serves (it naturalizes the market individual and hides the state and social obligation behind money);
the historian's method as the closing payoff.

**Scope — out.** Not a technical monetary economics course (no modern central-banking mechanics,
money-supply math, or MMT policy advocacy — chartalism is presented as a *theory of money's nature/
history*, not a policy program). Not a complete history of money to the present (stop once the credit-
first / state-coinage picture is established; the modern era appears only to show the myth still being
taught). Not partisan economics. The throughline is always *the myth and the record that refutes it*.

**Rigor / kind.** `humanities`, no math (the "double coincidence of wants" is explained in plain words
and a concrete example, not formalized). Standard is historical and conceptual rigor: claims anchored
to places, rough dates, and named scholarship; every specialist term glossed in plain words at first
use; rival readings noted.

**Assumed background.** None. A reader who has never heard of chartalism, Hammurabi, or Graeber can
follow. Specialist terms (barter, double coincidence of wants, commodity money, fiat money, unit of
account, medium of exchange, credit/chartalism, fungible, tally, debt bondage, jubilee) are each
introduced in plain words at first use, through a concrete picture.

**Pairing.** Will be grouped with "How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural" in a **Path** afterward.
Keep vocabulary and stance compatible: where the two overlap (the barter myth, money, debt, the gift,
markets ≠ market society, naturalization), use the same terms and glosses so the two courses read as
companions. This course can *callback in spirit* but must stand alone (a reader may take it first).

**Length.** Thorough as the companion: roughly 8–10 modules, a clear arc from "here is the story you
were taught" → "here is why it cannot be true" → "here is what actually happened" → "here is what money
really is, and why the myth survives."
