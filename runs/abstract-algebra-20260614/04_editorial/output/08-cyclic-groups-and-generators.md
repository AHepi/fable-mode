Pick a single hour on the clock — say, the 3 — and start adding it to itself. Three, six, nine, twelve, three again. Five numbers and you're back where you began, having visited a quarter of the dial each step. Now try the 1 instead. One, two, three, all the way around: you touch every hour before you loop. Same clock, same operation, but one starting element sweeps the whole face and the other only manages four stops.

A group can sometimes be *grown from a seed* — pick one element, keep applying it, and watch how much of the group it fills. Some seeds fill everything. Some fill a corner. This chapter is about telling them apart.

In the last module you learned to spot subgroups: smaller groups hiding inside a bigger one. Now we'll build them, not just find them. The tool is repetition.

Take any element of a group and apply the operation to it over and over. In a group written with $+$ (like $\mathbb{Z}_n$), "applying it again" means *adding it to the running total*: $a$, then $a+a$, then $a+a+a$, and so on. In a group written multiplicatively, it means combining: $a$, $a * a$, $a * a * a$. Either way you're stacking the same element onto itself, step after step. We'll write that pile-up as a **power**, $a^k$, meaning "combine $a$ with itself $k$ times" — even when the operation is addition. (A bit of a pun, but it reads cleanly and it's standard.)

Here is the thing that makes a *finite* group interesting: the powers can't all be different. There are only so many elements to land on, so sooner or later a power has to repeat. And the first time you land back on the identity — the element that does nothing — you've closed a loop. The length of that loop is a number worth naming. It tells you how long the element takes to "come home."

When one element's loop reaches *every* element of the group, that element is a generator, and the group is as simple as a group can be: a single circle of repeats. Let's make all of this precise.

## Definition (Order of an element)

Let $G$ be a group with identity $e$, and let $a \in G$. For a positive integer $k$, write $a^k$ for the result of combining $a$ with itself $k$ times (and $a^0 = e$).

The **order of $a$**, written $|a|$, is the *smallest* positive integer $k$ such that $a^k = e$. If no such $k$ exists, $a$ has **infinite order**.

In a group written additively, $a^k$ means $\underbrace{a + a + \cdots + a}_{k \text{ times}}$, and the condition $a^k = e$ reads $k \cdot a = 0$.

## Worked example

## Example (Order of an element in $\mathbb{Z}_6$)

**Problem.** Find the order of $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}_6$ (the group $\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}$ under addition mod $6$, with identity $0$).

**Solution.** Compute the powers of $2$ — that is, the repeated sums — until we reach the identity $0$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2^1 &= 2, \\
2^2 &= 2 + 2 = 4, \\
2^3 &= 2 + 2 + 2 = 6 = 0 \pmod 6.
\end{aligned}
$$

The first positive power that equals the identity is $2^3$, so $|2| = 3$.

Notice that $2$ never visited $1$, $3$, or $5$ — it only touched $\{0, 2, 4\}$. Three of the six elements, looping every three steps. That set of visited elements is no accident; it's exactly the subgroup $2$ builds, and naming it is the next idea.

## Definition (Cyclic subgroup generated by an element)

Let $G$ be a group and $a \in G$. The **cyclic subgroup generated by $a$**, written $\langle a \rangle$, is the set of all powers of $a$:

$$
\langle a \rangle = \{\, a^k : k \in \mathbb{Z} \,\}.
$$

For an element of finite order $n = |a|$, this set is exactly

$$
\langle a \rangle = \{\, e, a, a^2, \ldots, a^{n-1} \,\},
$$

and it has $n$ elements. In particular, **the order of the element equals the size of the subgroup it generates**: $|a| = |\langle a \rangle|$.

That $\langle a \rangle$ really *is* a subgroup — closed, containing the identity, closed under inverses — follows from the subgroup test of the previous module: combining two powers gives a power ($a^i * a^j = a^{i+j}$), the identity is $a^0$, and the inverse of $a^k$ is another power that completes the loop.

## Worked example

## Example (The subgroup generated by $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}_6$)

**Problem.** List the elements of $\langle 2 \rangle$ in $\mathbb{Z}_6$.

**Solution.** We already computed the powers of $2$: they cycle through $2, 4, 0$ and then repeat. Collecting the distinct values,

$$
\langle 2 \rangle = \{\, 0, 2, 4 \,\}.
$$

This is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}_6$ with $3$ elements — and $3 = |2|$, exactly as the definition promises. One element, applied repeatedly, *built* a smaller group inside the larger one.

## Definition (Cyclic group and generator)

A group $G$ is **cyclic** if there exists an element $g \in G$ such that $\langle g \rangle = G$ — that is, every element of $G$ is some power of $g$. Such an element $g$ is called a **generator** of $G$.

Equivalently, a finite group $G$ is cyclic precisely when it contains an element whose order equals the size of the whole group: $|g| = |G|$.

## Which groups are cyclic

Two of our running examples are cyclic, and seeing why ties the module together.

**$\mathbb{Z}_n$ is cyclic.** Take $g = 1$. Its powers are $1, 1+1 = 2, 1+1+1 = 3, \ldots$ — you just count, and counting eventually names every element before wrapping back to $0$. So $\langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_n$, and $1$ is a generator. (It needn't be the *only* one; in $\mathbb{Z}_6$, the element $5$ generates too, running the same circle backwards.)

**The rotations of the triangle are cyclic.** Back in the dihedral module, the triangle had three rotations: do nothing ($e$), rotate by $120^\circ$ (call it $r$), and rotate by $240^\circ$ (which is $r^2$). Apply $r$ a third time and you're back to $e$, so $r^3 = e$ and $|r| = 3$. The three rotations $\{e, r, r^2\}$ form a cyclic subgroup of the full symmetry group — one rotation, repeated, generates all of them. The *flips* are what kept that group from being cyclic overall, but the rotations alone are a clean little circle.

The picture to keep: a cyclic group is a single loop. Walk it step by step from a generator and you visit every element exactly once before returning home.

## Exercises

**1. (Mechanical)** Find the order of $4$ in $\mathbb{Z}_8$.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

Add $4$ to itself, reducing mod $8$, until you hit the identity $0$: $4^1 = 4$, then $4^2 = 4 + 4 = 8 = 0$. The first power equal to $0$ is $4^2$, so $|4| = 2$.

</details>

**2. (Mechanical)** List the elements of the cyclic subgroup $\langle 3 \rangle$ in $\mathbb{Z}_9$, and state its size.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

Take repeated sums of $3$ mod $9$: $3^1 = 3$, $3^2 = 6$, $3^3 = 9 = 0$, and then it repeats. So $\langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6\}$, a subgroup with $3$ elements. As a check, the size of the subgroup equals the order of the element: $|3| = 3$.

</details>

**3. (Conceptual)** In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, the element $2$ has order $3$ and so does *not* generate the whole group. Explain in one or two sentences why $2$ fails to be a generator, and name an element of $\mathbb{Z}_6$ that *does* generate it.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

A generator's powers must reach *every* element. But $\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4\}$ — the even elements only — so $2$ can never produce $1$, $3$, or $5$, and it isn't a generator. The element $1$ generates $\mathbb{Z}_6$ (counting $1,2,3,4,5,0$ hits everything); so does $5$. The tempting mistake is to assume any nonzero element generates — order matters: only an element of order $6$ can generate a group of size $6$.

</details>

**4. (Conceptual)** True or false: every cyclic group is abelian (every two elements commute). Justify your answer using the powers of a generator.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

True. If $G$ is cyclic with generator $g$, then any two elements are powers of $g$, say $a = g^i$ and $b = g^j$. Then $a * b = g^i * g^j = g^{i+j} = g^{j+i} = g^j * g^i = b * a$, because integer addition in the exponents commutes. So all elements commute. (This is why the *full* triangle group can't be cyclic — it has non-commuting symmetries, as you saw in the dihedral module — even though its rotations alone form a cyclic subgroup.)

</details>

**5. (Conceptual)** Recall $|r| = 3$ for the $120^\circ$ rotation $r$ of the triangle. What is the order of $r^2$ (the $240^\circ$ rotation)? Compute its powers to decide.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

Apply $r^2$ repeatedly: $(r^2)^1 = r^2$, then $(r^2)^2 = r^4 = r^3 \cdot r = e \cdot r = r$, then $(r^2)^3 = r^6 = (r^3)^2 = e$. The first power equal to the identity is the third, so $|r^2| = 3$. So $r^2$ is *also* a generator of the rotation subgroup $\{e, r, r^2\}$ — running the same circle in the opposite direction, just as $5$ does in $\mathbb{Z}_6$.

</details>

**6. (Stretch)** In $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, find the order of $8$ without listing all twelve elements. (Hint: you're looking for the smallest positive $k$ with $8k$ a multiple of $12$.)

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

The order of $8$ is the smallest positive $k$ with $8k \equiv 0 \pmod{12}$, i.e. the smallest $k$ making $8k$ a multiple of $12$. Test multiples: $8 \cdot 1 = 8$ (no), $8 \cdot 2 = 16$ (no), $8 \cdot 3 = 24 = 12 \cdot 2$ (yes). So $|8| = 3$, and $\langle 8 \rangle = \{0, 8, 4\}$ — the multiples of $4$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$. The shortcut: $|a|$ in $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is $n$ divided by the largest factor $a$ and $n$ share — here $12 / \gcd(8,12) = 12/4 = 3$. The common trap is guessing the order is just $a$ itself; it depends on how $a$ and $n$ share factors, not on $a$ alone.

</details>

## Recap

We learned to grow a group from a single seed. The **order** of an element counts how many steps it takes to loop back to the identity; the powers of that element fill out a **cyclic subgroup** $\langle a \rangle$ whose size equals that order. When one element's loop is long enough to reach everything, it is a **generator** and the group is **cyclic** — a single circle of repeats, $\mathbb{Z}_n$ and the triangle's rotations being our two clean examples. Notice the quiet pattern threading these exercises: the size of $\langle a \rangle$ always *divided* the size of the whole group. That is no coincidence, and the next module — Lagrange's theorem — explains exactly why.
