## The hook

Hand someone a group of size $12$ and ask which subgroups it could possibly hold. They might guess that anything goes — a subgroup of size $5$, maybe, or $7$, or $11$. It feels like the kind of question where you'd have to roll up your sleeves and hunt.

You don't. A subgroup of size $5$ inside a group of size $12$ is impossible, and you can know that before you've drawn a single Cayley table. The reason is almost embarrassingly simple: $5$ does not divide $12$. That one observation — a fact about division you've owned since grade school — turns out to police every subgroup of every finite group there is. This module is about why counting gets the last word.

## Intuition

Here is the picture to carry with you. Take a finite group $G$ and a subgroup $H$ living inside it. The subgroup $H$ has some size; call the leftover space the rest of $G$. The claim is that $H$ cuts $G$ into slices, and — this is the whole trick — **every slice has exactly the same size as $H$.**

Think of $G$ as a chocolate bar and $H$ as one square. If the subgroup slices the bar into equal pieces, and each piece is the size of $H$, then the total — the size of $G$ — is just *the size of one piece times the number of pieces*. The size of the whole is a whole-number multiple of the size of $H$. Said the other way around: the size of $H$ divides the size of $G$, with nothing left over.

So why are the slices equal? Take any element $g$ of the group and "shift" all of $H$ by it — combine $g$ with each member of $H$ in turn. You get a new batch of elements, the same count as $H$ (shifting can't merge two distinct elements into one, because in a group you can always undo a shift). These shifted copies of $H$ are the slices. They tile the whole group, they never overlap partway, and they all carry the same head-count as $H$ itself. Mathematicians call each shifted copy a **coset** of $H$ — we'll use the name, but lightly; the only thing we need from it is *the slices are all the same size as $H$.*

Equal slices that tile the whole: the total has to be a multiple of one slice. That is the entire idea. The theorem below just writes it down.

## Theorem (Lagrange's Theorem)

For a finite group $G$ with a subgroup $H$, write $|G|$ for the number of elements in $G$ and $|H|$ for the number of elements in $H$.

**Theorem (Lagrange).** If $H$ is a subgroup of a finite group $G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

In symbols, $|H| \mid |G|$ — there is a whole number $k$ with $|G| = k \cdot |H|$. (That number $k$ counts the slices: how many cosets of $H$ it takes to fill $G$.)

Notice what the theorem does *not* say. It does not promise that a subgroup of every dividing size exists — only that any subgroup you *do* find must have a size that divides $|G|$. The dividing is a necessary toll, not a guarantee.

## Worked example

**Problem.** The group $\mathbb{Z}_6$ (the integers $\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}$ under addition mod $6$) has $|\mathbb{Z}_6| = 6$. Which sizes are even *allowed* for a subgroup, and what is the subgroup generated by the element $2$?

**Solution.** By Lagrange's theorem, the size of any subgroup must divide $6$. The divisors of $6$ are $1$, $2$, $3$, and $6$, so those are the only sizes a subgroup could have. A subgroup of size $4$ or $5$ is ruled out before we look.

Now generate from $2$, using the cyclic-subgroup idea from the last module — keep adding $2$ to itself mod $6$:

$$
2,\quad 2+2 = 4,\quad 4+2 = 6 \equiv 0,\quad 0+2 = 2.
$$

The element $2$ produces $\{0, 2, 4\}$ and then cycles. That set is a subgroup of size $3$ — and sure enough, $3$ divides $6$, with $|G| = 2 \cdot |H|$. The two slices are $\{0,2,4\}$ itself and its shift-by-one $\{1,3,5\}$: two equal halves tiling all of $\mathbb{Z}_6$.

No. By Lagrange's theorem, a subgroup's size must divide the size of the group, and $5$ does not divide $12$ (the divisors of $12$ are $1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12$). So no subgroup of order $5$ can exist — without checking a single element.

## An element's order divides the group's order

Back in the last module you met the **order of an element**: start with an element $a$, keep applying it, and the order of $a$ is how many distinct elements you get before it loops back to the identity. Those powers of $a$ form a cyclic subgroup, and its size *is* the order of $a$.

Feed that into Lagrange. The cyclic subgroup generated by $a$ is a subgroup, so its size divides $|G|$ — which means **the order of any element divides the order of the group.** In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, an element can have order $1$, $2$, $3$, or $6$, but never order $4$ or $5$. The element controls a slice, and the slice has to fit.

## Check yourself

A group $G$ has $|G| = 10$. Someone claims it contains an element of order $4$. Without knowing anything else about $G$, can you say whether they're right?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

They're wrong. An element's order divides the order of the group, and $4$ does not divide $10$ (the divisors of $10$ are $1, 2, 5, 10$). So $G$ can have elements of order $1$, $2$, $5$, or $10$ — but never order $4$.

</details>

## Groups of prime order

Here is the cleanest payoff. Suppose $|G|$ is a prime number $p$ — say $p = 7$. A prime's only divisors are $1$ and itself, so by Lagrange the only sizes a subgroup can have are $1$ and $p$. The size-$1$ subgroup is just the identity sitting alone; the size-$p$ subgroup is the whole of $G$. There is nothing in between.

A group of prime order has **no nontrivial subgroups** — no proper subgroup bigger than the lone identity. It is, in the structural sense, indivisible. (And a small bonus: pick any element other than the identity, and the subgroup it generates can't have size $1$, so it must have size $p$ — meaning that single element generates the entire group. Every group of prime order is cyclic.)

## Check yourself

How many subgroups does a group of order $7$ have, and what are their sizes?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Exactly two: the trivial subgroup containing only the identity (size $1$) and the whole group (size $7$). Since $7$ is prime, its only divisors are $1$ and $7$, so Lagrange leaves no room for any subgroup in between.

</details>

## Exercises

**1.** List all the sizes that a subgroup of a group of order $20$ is *allowed* to have.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

A subgroup's size must divide $20$. The divisors of $20$ are $1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20$, so those are the only allowed sizes. (Lagrange tells us which sizes are *possible*; it does not promise a subgroup of each size actually exists — only that no other size can occur.)

</details>

**2.** Could a group of order $12$ contain a subgroup of order $5$? Explain why or why not.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

No. Lagrange's theorem says $|H|$ must divide $|G| = 12$, and $5$ does not divide $12$ — there is no whole number $k$ with $12 = 5k$. The tempting mistake is to reason "$5 < 12$, so a subgroup of size $5$ fits inside." Size alone isn't the test; *divisibility* is. The equal-slices picture explains why: five-element slices can never tile a twelve-element group evenly, because $12$ is not a multiple of $5$.

</details>

**3.** An element $a$ of a group $G$ has order $6$. What is the smallest the group $G$ could possibly be?

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

The order of an element divides the order of the group, so $6$ must divide $|G|$. The smallest positive multiple of $6$ is $6$ itself, so $G$ could be as small as $6$ elements — and that happens exactly when $a$ generates all of $G$ (the group is cyclic of order $6$, like $\mathbb{Z}_6$). Any smaller group can't house an element of order $6$, since its order wouldn't be divisible by $6$.

</details>

**4.** A group $G$ has order $15$. List every order an element of $G$ could have.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

An element's order divides $|G| = 15$, and the divisors of $15$ are $1, 3, 5, 15$. So every element has order $1$, $3$, $5$, or $15$ — nothing else is possible. (Order $1$ belongs only to the identity; the rest are open.)

</details>

**5.** *(Conceptual.)* Explain in your own words why the "equal slices" picture forces $|H|$ to divide $|G|$. Where does the equal-sizes fact do the work?

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

The subgroup $H$ shifts to cover $G$ in cosets — slices that tile $G$ with no gaps and no overlaps. The load-bearing fact is that every slice has exactly the same size as $H$ (shifting by a group element can't merge or lose any element, because every shift can be undone). If $G$ is built from $k$ slices each of size $|H|$, then $|G| = k \cdot |H|$ — a whole-number multiple of $|H|$. "Is a whole-number multiple of" is exactly what "is divisible by" means, so $|H|$ divides $|G|$. Drop the equal-sizes fact and the argument collapses: unequal slices wouldn't force any divisibility at all.

</details>

**6.** *(Conceptual.)* A friend says: "Lagrange's theorem guarantees that a group of order $12$ has a subgroup of order $6$." Are they right? What does the theorem actually claim?

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

They're overreaching. Lagrange runs in one direction only: *if* a subgroup exists, *then* its size divides $|G|$. It rules sizes out; it does not conjure subgroups into being. So $6$ dividing $12$ means a subgroup of order $6$ is *permitted* — not that one is *promised*. (Many groups of order $12$ do happen to have one, but that's a separate fact Lagrange alone can't deliver. The converse of Lagrange is false in general.)

</details>

## Recap

Lagrange's theorem says the size of a subgroup always divides the size of the group, and the reason is pure counting: a subgroup slices its group into equal-sized cosets, so the whole is a clean multiple of the part. From that one fact we got two free consequences — every element's order divides the group's order, and a group of prime order has no subgroups except the trivial ones. We used divisibility here as a filter that rules sizes out; in the next module we turn to maps between groups, and ask when two groups that look different are secretly the very same structure.
