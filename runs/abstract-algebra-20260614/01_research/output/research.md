# Research Dossier — Abstract Algebra (highschool background)

## Scope

**In:** Group theory as the spine, presented concretely. Operations and their rules; the group
axioms; modular arithmetic and symmetry groups as the two workhorse example families; subgroups;
cyclic groups; order and Lagrange's theorem; homomorphisms and isomorphisms; a closing horizon
module on rings and fields.

**Out:** calculus; set-theoretic machinery; deep ring/field theory; Galois theory; group actions,
Sylow theorems, classification — all second-course material.

## Core concepts and dependency sketch

- **Operation / binary operation** → the atom. Everything is "a set + a way to combine two
  elements." Needs: closure, associativity, identity, inverses. (depends on: functions, algebra)
- **Modular arithmetic ($\mathbb{Z}_n$, "clock arithmetic")** → the friendliest concrete example
  of a finite operation with identity and inverses. (depends on: arithmetic, division-with-remainder)
- **Group** → a set with one operation satisfying the four axioms. (depends on: operation)
- **Examples of groups** → $\mathbb{Z}$ under $+$, $\mathbb{Z}_n$ under $+$, nonzero rationals under
  $\times$, symmetries of shapes. (depends on: group, modular arithmetic)
- **Symmetry / dihedral group** ($D_3$, symmetries of a triangle) → the first *non-commutative*
  example; introduces composition of motions and the Cayley table. (depends on: group, geometry)
- **Subgroup** → a subset that is itself a group. (depends on: group, examples)
- **Cyclic group / generator** → a group generated by repeatedly applying one element. (depends on:
  subgroup, order of an element)
- **Order** (of an element, of a group) → counting. (depends on: cyclic)
- **Lagrange's theorem** → $|H|$ divides $|G|$; made plausible via equal-sized cosets. (depends on:
  subgroup, order)
- **Homomorphism / isomorphism** → structure-preserving maps; when two groups are "the same."
  (depends on: group, functions)
- **Ring, field** → two operations; integers as a ring, rationals/reals as a field. (depends on:
  group — a ring is "a group under $+$, with a multiplication bolted on")

A clean ordering falls right out of this graph: operation → modular arithmetic → group → examples →
non-commutative example → subgroup → cyclic → order/Lagrange → homomorphism → rings/fields. A short
motivating opener (symmetry/structure) precedes all of it.

## Prerequisite analysis (highschool reader)

**Can assume known:** manipulating algebraic expressions; solving linear/quadratic equations;
function notation $f(x)$, composition informally; coordinates and basic geometry (triangles,
rotations, reflections); exponents.

**Must teach from scratch:**
- **Division with remainder / modular arithmetic** — likely unseen formally. Teach via clocks.
- **The very idea of an abstract "operation"** divorced from numbers.
- **Reading a definition** and **what a proof is** — the reader has no proof experience. Introduce
  the *idea* of justifying a claim gently; never assume proof fluency.
- **Set notation** beyond the basics — keep it light; introduce $\in$, $\{\dots\}$ as needed.
- **Composition of functions/motions** as an operation — make concrete with physical symmetries.

## Hooks & examples worth using

- A clock: $9 + 5 = 2$ on a 12-hour clock — the gateway to $\mathbb{Z}_n$.
- The symmetries of an equilateral triangle you can act out with a paper triangle: 3 rotations + 3
  flips, composing them like dance moves — gateway to $D_3$ and non-commutativity.
- "$1, i, -1, -i$ under multiplication" — a small, clean cyclic group of order 4.
- Rubik's cube / wallpaper patterns / molecule symmetry as motivation for "symmetry is a group."
- Integer addition as the most familiar infinite group; nonzero rationals under $\times$ for a
  multiplicative one.

## Common pitfalls / misconceptions

- Thinking a "group" must be made of numbers. (It's any set + operation satisfying the axioms.)
- Assuming every operation is commutative — the triangle flips break this; surface it early.
- Confusing the *identity* (does nothing) with *inverses* (undo each other).
- Believing $a \cdot b = a \cdot c \Rightarrow b = c$ is obvious — it follows from inverses; show why.
- Modular arithmetic: thinking $\mathbb{Z}_n$ "runs out" — it wraps; the operation stays closed.
- Reading Lagrange's theorem as "subgroups can be any size" — its content is that they can't.

## Sources

Standard first-course treatments (Fraleigh, *A First Course in Abstract Algebra*; Pinter, *A Book of
Abstract Algebra* — especially good for this audience; Gallian, *Contemporary Abstract Algebra*).
These are well-established; no `[verify]` flags. Pinter's concrete, motivation-first approach is the
closest match to the assumed background and the house voice.

## Open questions for the curriculum stage

- How much modular arithmetic before defining "group"? (Recommendation: one dedicated module, used
  immediately as the first finite example.)
- Introduce permutations abstractly or only via the triangle? (Recommendation: only via symmetry, to
  stay concrete; full $S_n$ is second-course.)
- Cosets: name them, or only use the "equal-sized slices" picture for Lagrange? (Recommendation: use
  the picture, name cosets lightly, do not formalize quotient groups.)
