Pick a rotation. Not all of them — just one. Rotate an equilateral triangle by $120^\circ$ and do it again: you're at $240^\circ$. Do it once more: you're back where you started. Three moves, back home. That one rotation, applied to itself over and over, visited every rotation in the group before it returned.

That is the question this module answers: when can a single element, combined with itself repeatedly, produce every element of a group? Such an element is called a **generator**, and a group with one is called **cyclic**. The $n$-hour clock $\mathbb{Z}_n$ (from module 02) is the model example — and we will see exactly why.

---

## One element spinning out a group

Recall the group operation $*$ from modules 03 and 04: a way to combine two elements and land back in the group (closure, associativity, identity $e$, inverses). In module 07 we met **subgroups** — subsets of a group that are themselves groups under the same operation.

Here is the seed idea: take any element $a$ in a group $G$ and ask what you get by combining $a$ with itself repeatedly.

Define the **powers** of $a$ as follows:

$$
a^1 = a, \quad a^2 = a * a, \quad a^3 = a * a * a, \quad \text{and so on.}
$$

Also set $a^0 = e$ (the identity — the do-nothing element) and $a^{-1}$ for the inverse of $a$ (module 04), with $a^{-2} = a^{-1} * a^{-1}$, and so on.

The collection of all powers of $a$ — positive, zero, and negative — forms a subgroup of $G$. We call it the **cyclic subgroup generated by $a$**, written $\langle a \rangle$.

---

## Definition (Order of an element)

Let $G$ be a group with identity $e$, and let $a \in G$. The **order of $a$**, written $|a|$, is the smallest positive integer $n$ such that

$$
a^n = e.
$$

If no such $n$ exists, we say $a$ has **infinite order**.

*Bridge.* In words: start at the identity, apply $a$ once to get $a^1$, apply it again to get $a^2$, and keep going. The order is the number of steps before you cycle back to $e$. The key phrase is "smallest positive integer" — we want the first time the clock completes a lap, not the second or third.

---

## Definition (Cyclic group; generator)

A group $G$ is **cyclic** if there exists an element $g \in G$ such that every element of $G$ equals some power $g^k$ (for an integer $k$). Such an element $g$ is called a **generator** of $G$.

The cyclic subgroup generated by $a$ is

$$
\langle a \rangle = \{\, a^k : k \in \mathbb{Z} \,\}.
$$

*Bridge.* A cyclic group is one where a single element's repeated self-combination sweeps through everything. The generator $g$ is the seed; the group is the full grown plant.

## The clock as a cyclic group

$\mathbb{Z}_5$ (the 5-hour clock from module 02, integers $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ under addition mod 5) is cyclic. The generator is $1$.

Starting from $0$ (the identity under $+$), add $1$ over and over:

| Step | Computation | Result |
|------|-------------|--------|
| $1^1 = 1$ | $0 + 1$ | $1$ |
| $1^2 = 1 + 1$ | $1 + 1$ | $2$ |
| $1^3 = 1 + 1 + 1$ | $2 + 1$ | $3$ |
| $1^4 = 1 + 1 + 1 + 1$ | $3 + 1$ | $4$ |
| $1^5 = 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1$ | $4 + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$ | $0 = e$ |

At step 5 we hit $0$ — the identity. Every element $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ appeared. The cycle closed after exactly 5 steps: the order of $1$ in $\mathbb{Z}_5$ is $5$, and $\langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_5$.

**Picture.** Imagine a clock face with marks at $0, 1, 2, 3, 4$. Starting at $0$, each $+1$ moves the hand one notch clockwise. After five notches, the hand is back at $0$. It swept every mark along the way.

This is what "cyclic" means geometrically: the group is one closed loop.

## Example: a cyclic subgroup inside $\mathbb{Z}_6$

$\mathbb{Z}_6$ (the 6-hour clock, integers $\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}$ under addition mod 6) is the full group. What subgroup does the element $2$ generate?

| Step | Computation | Result |
|------|-------------|--------|
| $2^1$ | $2$ | $2$ |
| $2^2$ | $2 + 2$ | $4$ |
| $2^3$ | $2 + 2 + 2 = 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$ | $0 = e$ |

The cycle closes after 3 steps. So $|2| = 3$ and

$$
\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4\}.
$$

This is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}_6$ — the even elements — with 3 members, not 6. The generator $2$ did not spin out all of $\mathbb{Z}_6$; it generated a proper cyclic subgroup inside it. (Module 07 identified $\{0, 2, 4\}$ as a subgroup by the closure test; here we see how it arises from a single seed.)

## Check yourself

What is the order of the element $3$ in $\mathbb{Z}_6$?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Compute successive additions of $3$ mod 6:

$3^1 = 3$, $3^2 = 3 + 3 = 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$.

The cycle closes after 2 steps. So $|3| = 2$ and $\langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3\}$.

A tempting wrong answer: saying the order is $6$ because $\mathbb{Z}_6$ has $6$ elements. The order of an element is how many steps it takes *that element* to return to $e$ — not the size of the group.

</details>

## Rotations of the triangle: a cyclic subgroup of $D_3$

In module 06 we built $D_3$, the symmetries of an equilateral triangle (rotations $e, r, r^2$ and reflections $s, rs, r^2s$). The rotation $r$ by $120^\circ$ generates the rotations:

| Step | Result |
|------|--------|
| $r^1$ | $r$ (rotate $120^\circ$) |
| $r^2$ | $r^2$ (rotate $240^\circ$) |
| $r^3$ | $e$ (full $360^\circ$ — back home) |

So $|r| = 3$ and $\langle r \rangle = \{e, r, r^2\}$ — a cyclic subgroup of $D_3$ with 3 elements. The full group $D_3$ is *not* cyclic (no single element generates all six symmetries — the reflections do not arise from powers of a rotation), but it contains a cyclic subgroup.

## Exercises

**1.** Compute the order of each element in $\mathbb{Z}_5 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ under addition mod 5.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

$|0| = 1$ (it is the identity; $0^1 = 0 = e$ immediately).

For any nonzero element $a \in \{1,2,3,4\}$, add it to itself until you reach $0$:

- $|1| = 5$: $1, 2, 3, 4, 0$.
- $|2| = 5$: $2, 4, 1, 3, 0$.
- $|3| = 5$: $3, 1, 4, 2, 0$.
- $|4| = 5$: $4, 3, 2, 1, 0$.

Every nonzero element of $\mathbb{Z}_5$ has order $5$, which is the size of the group. Each one is a generator — a natural consequence of $5$ being prime (module 09 will illuminate why).

</details>

---

**2.** In $\mathbb{Z}_8 = \{0,1,2,3,4,5,6,7\}$, find $\langle 4 \rangle$ and state the order of $4$.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

Add $4$ to itself mod 8:

$4^1 = 4$, $4^2 = 8 \equiv 0 \pmod{8}$.

The cycle closes after 2 steps. So $|4| = 2$ and $\langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4\}$.

A subgroup of size 2 inside a group of size 8 — entirely legal; its size (2) divides the group size (8).

</details>

---

**3.** In $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, list the elements of $\langle 4 \rangle$ and determine whether $4$ is a generator of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

Add $4$ repeatedly mod 12:

$4, 8, 12 \equiv 0 \pmod{12}$.

So $\langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4, 8\}$, a subgroup of size 3. Since $\langle 4 \rangle \neq \mathbb{Z}_{12}$, the element $4$ is **not** a generator of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$.

</details>

---

**4.** Give an element of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ that **is** a generator. Verify it.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

$1$ is a generator: $1, 2, 3, \ldots, 11, 0$ — twelve steps, every element appears.

$7$ is also a generator. Check: $7, 14 \equiv 2, 9, 4, 11, 6, 1, 8, 3, 10, 5, 0$ — all 12 elements appear before returning to $0$. (You can check without listing all twelve: if adding 7 over and over never revisits a number until it reaches $0$, it generates the whole clock. This happens precisely when $\gcd(7, 12) = 1$, which is true since $7$ is odd and not divisible by $3$. Module 09 explains the counting behind this.)

</details>

---

**5.** True or false: every subgroup of a cyclic group is itself cyclic.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

**True.** If $G = \langle g \rangle$ is cyclic, then any subgroup $H \leq G$ consists entirely of powers of $g$. The smallest positive power of $g$ that lands in $H$ generates all of $H$. (This is the statement rather than a full proof — the argument uses an idea from Lagrange's theorem in module 09. For now, check it against the examples: every subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ we computed above was itself of the form $\langle a \rangle$.)

</details>

---

**6.** (Conceptual) Can a finite group have an element of infinite order? Explain.

<details><summary>Show solution</summary>

No. If $G$ is finite with $|G| = n$, then the powers $e, a, a^2, \ldots, a^n$ are $n+1$ elements drawn from a set of size $n$. By the pigeonhole principle (if you have more pigeons than holes, two pigeons share a hole), two of these powers must be equal: $a^i = a^j$ for some $0 \leq i < j \leq n$. Multiplying both sides by $a^{-i}$ gives $a^{j-i} = e$, so $a$ returns to $e$ after at most $n$ steps. Every element of a finite group has finite order.

</details>

## Recap

This module introduced three tightly linked ideas. The **order** of an element $a$ in a group is the number of steps before its repeated self-combination returns to the identity: $|a|$ is the smallest positive $k$ with $a^k = e$. The **cyclic subgroup** $\langle a \rangle$ is the set of all powers of $a$; it is always a subgroup. A group is **cyclic** if some element generates the whole group — $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is the canonical example, with $1$ as a generator under addition.

Module 09 picks up the question of *size*: Lagrange's theorem will show that $|\langle a \rangle|$ — the size of any cyclic subgroup — must divide $|G|$, the size of the whole group. The order of an element and the order of a group are about to collide, and the result is one of the cleanest facts in all of algebra.
