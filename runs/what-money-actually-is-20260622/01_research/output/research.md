## 0. Orientation (read this before the sections)

The object of study is **the question the intro course deliberately left open**: not "is the barter myth false" (settled there) but "what *is* money, fundamentally — a commodity that the market threw up, or a unit of account and a measure of debt instituted by law and the state?" The intro course refused to crown a winner. This course does the harder thing: it walks the reader *into* the unsettlement at full strength, builds the sharpest objection the intro course ducked, and still refuses the tidy verdict, because the field refuses it.

**The defining commitment, held in tension: SHARPER and MORE QUALIFIED.** These pull against each other and both must hold.

- **Sharper** means engaging the *strongest* form of each argument, not the convenient one. The flagship example, the beat the intro course skipped: the Mesopotamian standard was denominated in **silver**, and silver is a commodity. So "unit of account before coins" does **not**, by itself, refute the commodity theory. The metallist can say the market simply chose its most saleable good as the measuring-stick. The course must build the decisive reply — that the silver/barley ratios, the prices, the rents, the interest rates were **administered** by temple, palace, and law (Hammurabi), the *shekel was a unit of weight*, and the temple/palace were the great creditors, so this is an *instituted* standard, not a market spontaneously converging on Menger's most-saleable good. And then, having built that reply, it must honestly weigh whether the instituted-standard point *settles* the silver question or only *shifts* it (the strong metallist rejoinder: silver was *chosen* as the standard precisely for its commodity properties, and an administered ratio still rides on a commodity's value). Present both. Crown no winner.

- **More qualified** means: the **steelman comes first**, in its strongest honest form, *before* any criticism, for every position — the course's credibility is the strongest version of the view it is about to question. It means stating precisely **what the evidence can and cannot bear** (the Mesopotamian record shifts the metallist case but does not kill it; the coinage chronology is solid but the causal "complex" is interpretation; Hudson's clean-slate decrees are attested but their frequency and reach are contested). It means flagging where a famous synthesis **over-reaches** (Graeber's primordial debt; the strong military-coinage-slavery causation; the strong Hudson jubilee thesis) and saying *as whose claim*.

**Enact the disciplines; do not recite them.** The intro course was allowed to write "mark the seam." This course is **not** (a hard pipeline rule now). The evidence/interpretation distinction must live in the *structure of the argument* — evidence stated as evidence, the reading named as someone's reading, the rejoinder given room — never in a recited slogan. No "notice", "hold that", "remember"; no "mark the seam." The curriculum and authoring stages enforce this; this dossier supplies the material *structured* so the seam falls out of the argument's shape.

**Keep the thinkers distinct.** This is the integrity spine of an undergrad treatment. Menger ≠ Mises (spontaneous-emergence narrative ≠ the regression theorem that grounds it in subjective-value praxeology). Innes ≠ Knapp ≠ Keynes ≠ Lerner ≠ Grierson ≠ Ingham (credit theory ≠ state theory ≠ money-of-account-as-primary ≠ "creature of the state" ≠ wergild-origin of abstract measure ≠ sociological synthesis). Graeber ≠ Hudson, and both ≠ the cautious consensus they draw on. Schumpeter's **"monetary vs. real analysis"** is the meta-distinction that *organizes* the whole quarrel — name it as his and use it as the course's analytic backbone. Attribute the bold readings (Graeber, Hudson, the primordial-debt theorists) **as theirs**.

**Chartalism is not MMT.** Chartalism / the state theory is a theory of money's *nature and history* (Knapp 1905; Lerner; Keynes's interest in Knapp). **Modern Monetary Theory** is a *present-day policy program* (deficits, the job guarantee, "taxes don't fund spending") that *draws on* chartalism. Name the relationship in one fenced passage, then keep them in **separate drawers**. **No policy advocacy** anywhere. This is an integrity line, not just scope.

**No fabrication.** Rough dates ("~1750 BCE", "~7th c. BCE", "1913/1914"). No invented quotes, clause numbers, interest rates, etymologies, museum accession numbers, or precise figures. Primary-source quotations must be accurate or **clearly labelled paraphrase**. Everything I am not confident is real is marked **[verify]**. Undergrad rigor does not relax this; it tightens it.

**The arc the brief fixes** (theory at strength → the instituted-standard problem → coinage → debt → the over-reaches → modern money → settled-vs-open):

> *Recall the open question* → *the commodity/metallist theory at full strength* → *the credit and state theories, distinguished* → *the instituted-standard problem and the Mesopotamian ambiguity (the sharpest beat)* → *coinage and the state/war/tax nexus* → *debt, interest, clean slates* → *the over-reach (primordial debt)* → *modern money (credit money, fiat, chartalism-not-MMT)* → *what is settled vs. open, and the method.*

**Hard pipeline rules for all downstream prose:** no em dashes; no directive metadiscourse; length follows the idea; each module ends in a fact-dense **closing story** (real, verifiable, points true, varied); enact, don't recite.

## 1. Scope

### In scope
- **The commodity / metallist theory at full strength.** Carl Menger's account of money as the spontaneous emergence of the most **saleable** (most marketable) commodity as a medium of exchange (*Principles*, 1871; "On the Origins of Money", 1892). Ludwig von Mises's **regression theorem** (*The Theory of Money and Credit*, 1912): the purchasing power of money today is explained by its purchasing power yesterday, traced back regressively to the point where the money-stuff was valued purely as a non-monetary commodity — Mises's answer to the circularity in "money is valued because it's accepted, accepted because it's valued." The genuine force of **"why metal"** (durable, divisible, portable, recognizable/hard to fake, relatively scarce, homogeneous). And the metallist's strongest empirical ground: **stranger / cross-border trade**, where no shared sovereign, no shared law, and no ongoing relationship anchors a debt, so the credit theory is at its weakest and a thing with intrinsic acceptability does real work. Steelman all of this hard. It is not a strawman.
- **The credit and state theories, properly distinguished (kept un-blended).** **A. Mitchell Innes** — money is credit; a coin or note is a transferable IOU, and the creditor/debtor relation is primary ("What is Money?", 1913; "The Credit Theory of Money", 1914). **Georg Friedrich Knapp** — the **state theory** ("chartalism", from *charta*, a token/ticket): money is what the state declares acceptable at its pay-offices, the validity is *proclamatory*, not metallic (*The State Theory of Money*, 1905; Eng. 1924). **J. M. Keynes** — "**money of account**" as the *primary* concept (the thing in which debts and prices are *expressed*) in *A Treatise on Money* (1930); his engagement with Knapp and his interest in ancient/Babylonian standards. **Abba Lerner** — "money is a creature of the state" / acceptance for taxes drives a money's value ("Money as a Creature of the State", 1947 `[verify exact title/venue]`). **Philip Grierson** — the *abstract measure of value* could only originate where a society already had a fixed scale of equivalences, which he locates in the **compensation tariffs / wergild** of early law codes (a list of what is owed for each injury), not in the higgling of a market. **Geoffrey Ingham** — the sociological synthesis: money as a *social relation*, a claim, money-of-account as a social and political institution (*The Nature of Money*, 2004). **Joseph Schumpeter** — the meta-distinction between **"monetary analysis"** (money and credit are essential to the structure, the claim theory) and **"real analysis"** (money is a veil over an essentially barter economy, the commodity theory) — the cleanest frame for the whole dispute (*History of Economic Analysis*, 1954). Each claim stays attributed to its author.
- **The instituted-standard argument and the genuine ambiguity of the Mesopotamian evidence (the sharpest beat).** Build it as a two-move structure: (a) the *flagship objection the intro course ducked* — the standard was **silver**, a commodity, so unit-of-account-before-coins does not refute metallism on its own; (b) the *decisive development* — the silver/barley equivalences, prices, rents, and interest were **administered** by temple, palace, and law code (Hammurabi); the **shekel was a unit of weight**, not a coin; temple and palace were the **great creditors**. **Karl Polanyi**'s argument that money's "uses" (means of payment, standard of value, store of wealth, medium of exchange) are **separable** and need *not* be united in a single all-purpose commodity (so "money" in archaic society is special-purpose, not the textbook all-in-one). **Michael Hudson** and the *ancient economies* scholarship on administered prices and palace/temple credit. Then the **honest qualification**: a strong metallist replies that silver was *chosen* as the standard *because* of its commodity properties, and that an administered ratio still rides on a commodity's underlying value — so the evidence **shifts** the debate rather than **settling** it. Both sides on the page. No winner.
- **Coinage and the state / war / tax (/ slavery) nexus.** Grierson's numismatics: the first coins ~7th c. BCE in **Lydia and Ionian Greece**, electrum (a gold-silver alloy), the chronology solid. Against this, **Graeber's** bolder causal arc — the **"military-coinage(-slavery) complex"**: coin to pay roving soldiers who won't extend credit, taxes payable in coin to force a population to feed the army, war producing the slaves and the bullion. Separate the **solid fact** (coins are late and state-stamped; soldiers and taxes are entangled with their spread) from the **interpretation** (the strong causal "complex"), and note the **critics** who find the strong causation over-drawn or regionally uneven.
- **Debt, interest, and clean-slate cancellations at rigor.** **Michael Hudson's** case that royal "clean slate" decrees — Sumerian/Akkadian *misharum* ("justice/equity") and *andurarum* ("a restoration/return", debt and bond release), and the biblical **Jubilee** (Leviticus 25) and sabbatical-year release (Deuteronomy 15) — were **recurrent** and **central** to ancient political economy, a standing royal tool against debt peonage. And the historians who think he **over-generalizes** from limited or unevenly distributed evidence (how regular, how enforced, how widely these decrees actually operated is contested). The moral economy of debt: interest-bearing debt is very old (attested in the Mesopotamian record), and so is the political problem of debt bondage.
- **The "primordial debt" thesis and its critics.** The strong claim — society itself rests on a founding, originary debt (to gods, ancestors, the cosmos, the community) from which tax, sacrifice, and money descend — associated with the French **primordial-debt** theorists (Michel Aglietta and André Orléan; Bruno Théret) and taken up by **Graeber**. Present it as a *genuine but contested* position, **widely judged an untestable over-reach** (it explains everything and so risks explaining nothing, and it reintroduces exactly the theory-required origin story the intro course taught the reader to distrust). Flag it clearly as the boldest claim in the course; do not let it become the frame.
- **Modern money, carefully bounded.** Endogenous / bank-created **credit money**: the now-mainstream "**loans create deposits**" account — when a bank lends, it credits the borrower's account into existence; the money supply is created *endogenously* by lending, not multiplied up from a fixed base of reserves. The **Bank of England's 2014** *Money creation in the modern economy* paper, which stated this plainly and described the textbook **money-multiplier** story as misleading. **Fiat money** (money by declaration and acceptance, not metal backing). And the **clearly fenced** distinction: chartalism = a theory of money's *nature*; **MMT** = a present-day *policy* program drawing on it. Name the relationship, then keep them apart. No advocacy.
- **What is genuinely settled vs. genuinely open**, and the *method* for weighing any monetary-origin or monetary-nature claim. The closing payoff: what the evidence can bear, what it cannot, and how to read the next confident story about "what money really is."

### Out of scope
- **Re-teaching the barter myth or the basic vocabulary from scratch.** Recall each intro term in a clause on first reuse (unit of account, medium of exchange, money-of-account, commodity theory/metallism, credit theory/chartalism, fiat, fungibility, coinage-is-late), then build past it. The intro course is the prerequisite; this is its advanced sequel. (See §3.)
- **Technical monetary economics.** No IS-LM, no formal money-demand functions, no interest-rate models, no money-multiplier *mechanics presented as settled* (the multiplier appears only as the textbook story the Bank of England reversed).
- **MMT policy advocacy.** The deficit/job-guarantee program is *named only to fence it off*. No claim about what fiscal policy *should* do.
- **A complete history of money to the present.** Stop once the modern credit-money / fiat picture is established as the payoff. No survey of paper money, the gold standard's full history, Bretton Woods, or crypto (crypto may earn one bounded mention as a live test of the commodity-vs-credit question `[verify scope with curriculum]`, but is not a module).
- **Partisan or left-vs-right framing.** The throughline is the *adjudication*, not a politics.
- A full course in Assyriology or numismatics; use their findings, do not become them.

### Sharpening note (the spine-defining decision)
The intro course was deliberately **history-first** (lead with the record, bring the theories in as tools). This course **inverts that emphasis without abandoning the discipline**: it is **theory-and-evidence interleaved**, because the *question* is now explicitly "which theory does the evidence support, and how far." The reader already has the history (from the intro course); what they lack is the two theories *at full strength* and the *adjudication*. So lead each theory at its strongest (Menger/Mises; then Innes/Knapp/Keynes), *then* bring the hardest evidence (the instituted-standard problem) to bear on both, *then* widen to coinage, debt, the over-reaches, and modern money. The reader should never meet a theory as a slogan; they meet it as the best case its best advocate ever made, and then watch it meet the evidence. The recommended sequencing question (theory-first vs. history-first, and where the instituted-standard problem sits) is the curriculum stage's biggest call — see §7.

## 2. Core concepts and dependency sketch

Listed in **dependency order** (each builds on those above). The grouping is the proposed *spine*; the curriculum stage sets final module boundaries. The whole course is organized by **Schumpeter's monetary-vs-real distinction** (B0 below): "real analysis" = money is a veil over barter (the commodity theory's home); "monetary analysis" = money/credit is constitutive (the credit theory's home). Naming this once gives the reader a frame to sort every later argument into.

### A. The question, recalled and sharpened
1. **The open question** — recall (one paragraph, not a re-teach) that the intro course killed the barter *sequence* but left the *nature* question open: is money fundamentally a **commodity** (the metallist/real view) or a **unit of account and measure of debt** (the credit/chartalist/monetary view)? Recall the load-bearing intro vocabulary in a clause each. State the course's contract: steelman both, adjudicate at the evidence, crown no winner where the field has not.
2. **Schumpeter's frame: monetary vs. real analysis** — the meta-distinction (his) that sorts the whole dispute. *Real analysis*: money is technically convenient but theoretically a veil; strip it and you see a barter economy underneath (commodity theory's natural home). *Monetary analysis*: money and credit are part of the essential structure, not a veil (credit/state theory's home). This is the course's sorting tool; introduce it early and spiral it back.

> *Dependency:* A1 recalls; A2 supplies the analytic backbone. Everything after sorts into one column or the other (and the best beats refuse to sit cleanly in either).

### B. The commodity / metallist theory at full strength
3. **Saleability and spontaneous emergence (Menger)** — money as the most *saleable* / marketable commodity, selected by countless individuals each trying to trade their goods for something more widely accepted, converging without design on a single medium. A market-evolutionary story, not a state story. Steelman: it is elegant, requires no planner, and matches the intuition that some goods (salt, cattle, metal) really are easier to pass on.
4. **The regression theorem (Mises)** — the deeper grounding: money's value today rests on its value yesterday, regressing back to a first day when the money-stuff had **non-monetary commodity value**, which breaks the circularity ("accepted because valued, valued because accepted"). Distinct from Menger: Menger narrates the *emergence*; Mises gives the *value-theoretic proof* that money must originate in a commodity. **Keep them separate.**
5. **"Why metal"** — the real, non-stupid case for precious metal: durable, divisible without loss, portable (high value-to-weight), recognizable and hard to counterfeit, relatively scarce/stable in supply, homogeneous. The commodity theory's strongest concrete ground.
6. **Stranger / cross-border trade** — the metallist's best empirical territory and the **credit theory's weakest**: where there is no shared sovereign, no shared law, and no ongoing relationship to anchor and enforce a debt, a thing with intrinsic acceptability does work that an IOU cannot. The course must concede this is real. It is the standing objection the credit theory must answer.

> *Dependency:* B3→B4 (emergence then its value-theoretic ground); B5 and B6 are the empirical pillars. This whole section is the **steelman**; nothing is criticized yet.

### C. The credit and state theories, distinguished
7. **The credit theory (Innes)** — money *is* credit: a coin or note is a transferable acknowledgment of debt; what circulates is obligations, and "paying" is the cancellation of a debt against a credit. The primary thing is the creditor/debtor relation, not a commodity.
8. **The state theory / chartalism (Knapp)** — money is what the **state** names as valid at its own pay-offices; validity is *proclamatory* (by *charta*, the token), not metallic. The state's choice of what it will *accept* (especially for taxes/fees) is what makes a money. **Distinct from Innes:** Innes centers private credit relations; Knapp centers the state's naming power.
9. **Money of account as primary (Keynes)** — in *A Treatise on Money*, the **money of account** (the unit in which debts and prices are *expressed*) is the **primary** concept; the actual money-things are secondary, things that *answer to* the description the money of account provides. Keynes's reading of Knapp; his interest in Babylonian standards. **Distinct again:** this is a claim about *conceptual priority* (account before thing), not the same as Knapp's claim about *state power*.
10. **"Creature of the state" / tax-drives-money (Lerner)** — the sharper modern formulation: a state can give an otherwise worthless token value by requiring it in payment of taxes, creating a demand for it. The cleanest bridge from chartalism toward (but **not into**) MMT.
11. **The abstract measure from customary law / wergild (Grierson)** — the *unit of account* could only arise where a society already had a **fixed scale of equivalences**, and Grierson locates that scale in the **compensation tariffs (wergild)** of early law codes (a tabulated price for each injury and death), not in market haggling. A non-state, non-market *origin* for the measure: law and custom.
12. **The sociological synthesis (Ingham)** — money as a **social relation** and a politically instituted unit of account; an attempt to hold the credit and state insights together without collapsing into either, and against the commodity theory's "real-analysis" framing.

> *Dependency:* C7→C8→C9→C10 are four *distinct* claims often mushed together; the module's whole job is to keep them apart. C11 (Grierson) supplies the deep-origin story for the unit of account; C12 (Ingham) is the modern synthesis. All of C is the credit/monetary column's steelman.

### D. The instituted-standard problem and the Mesopotamian ambiguity (the sharpest beat)
13. **The flagship objection (the silver problem)** — the move the intro course ducked: the Mesopotamian standard was denominated in **silver**, a commodity. So "unit of account before coins" does **not** by itself refute metallism; a metallist reads it as the market choosing its most saleable good as the measuring-stick (Menger), exactly as predicted. State this objection at full strength. It is the hinge.
14. **The decisive development (the administered standard)** — the reply: the silver/barley **equivalences, prices, rents, and interest rates were administered** by temple, palace, and **law code** (Hammurabi); the **shekel was a unit of weight**, not a coined object; and the temple/palace were the **great creditors** of the society. This is an *instituted* standard set by authority, not a market spontaneously converging on a saleable good.
15. **Separable money-uses (Polanyi)** — the supporting argument: money's uses (means of payment, standard of value, store of wealth, exchange) are **logically separable** and historically were carried by *different* things for *different* purposes; the all-in-one commodity-money of the textbook is not the archaic norm. This weakens the metallist assumption that one saleable commodity naturally unites all the functions.
16. **The honest qualification (shift, not settle)** — the strong metallist rejoinder: silver was *chosen* as the standard *because* of its commodity properties (durable, divisible, scarce), and an administered ratio still rides on a real commodity's value, so the temple did not conjure the measure from nothing. Therefore the instituted-standard evidence **shifts** the burden and the plausibility toward the credit/state side **without settling** the question. Present both; this is the course's model of "sharper *and* more qualified." **No winner.**

> *Dependency:* D needs B (the metallist case) and C (the credit case) both in hand, because it is where they *collide on one body of evidence*. D13 raises the objection, D14 answers it, D15 reinforces, D16 re-opens it honestly. This is the densest, most evidence-heavy, most carefully balanced module in the course.

### E. Coinage and the state / war / tax nexus
17. **The chronology (Grierson, numismatics)** — first coins ~7th c. BCE, Lydia and Ionian Greece, electrum; stamped by authority. Solid, dateable fact. Coins are *late* (recall from intro) and are a *state* technology.
18. **The military-coinage(-slavery) complex (Graeber) — as interpretation** — the bolder causal arc: coin solves the problem of paying roving armies who will not extend credit; taxes-in-coin force the population to obtain coin (by feeding the soldiers), seeding markets; war supplies bullion and slaves. State the *solid* core (coin + soldiers + taxes are entangled) separately from the *strong causal "complex"* (which is Graeber's reading), and note the **critics** (the causation is over-drawn, regionally uneven, and reads a single mechanism into varied histories).

> *Dependency:* E needs D (the reader knows the unit of account predates coin and may be instituted). E17 is solid evidence; E18 is the strong interpretation, attributed and qualified. The structure itself carries the evidence/interpretation seam.

### F. Debt, interest, and the clean slate
19. **Interest and debt at rigor** — interest-bearing debt is very old (attested in the Mesopotamian record); debt as a *measured, enforceable* obligation predates coin. The moral economy: debt as both economic instrument and political danger (debt bondage, the loss of land and freedom).
20. **Clean-slate cancellations (Hudson) — steelman then qualify** — royal *misharum* / *andurarum* decrees and the biblical Jubilee (Leviticus 25) / sabbatical release (Deuteronomy 15) as **recurrent** royal tools wiping debt and freeing bond-servants to prevent the collapse of the free population into peonage. **Steelman Hudson's case** (the decrees are real and attested; the institution mattered). Then **qualify**: how *regular*, how *enforced*, and how *widely* they operated is contested; Hudson is read by some specialists as **over-generalizing** from limited evidence. Solid fact vs. strong thesis, kept distinct.

> *Dependency:* F draws on D (administered standard, temple as creditor) and C (the credit theory makes debt primary). F19 is the rigor; F20 is the contested-thesis beat.

### G. The over-reach
21. **The primordial-debt thesis (Aglietta/Orléan/Théret; Graeber) — flagged hard** — the strong claim that society rests on an originary debt (to gods, ancestors, the cosmos) from which money and tax descend. Present as a **genuine but contested** position, **widely judged untestable / over-reaching** — it explains everything and risks explaining nothing, and it smuggles back exactly the theory-required origin story the intro course dismantled. The course's clearest case of "a famous synthesis over-reaching," named *as* its authors' claim, used to teach the reader where the line between argument and unfalsifiable story falls.

> *Dependency:* G needs F (debt) and the whole method so far. It is the course's sharpest exercise in *qualification*: taking a bold, attractive, influential thesis and showing exactly why the evidence cannot bear it, without sneering.

### H. Modern money, bounded
22. **Endogenous / credit money ("loans create deposits")** — the mainstream modern account: banks create money by lending (crediting an account into existence), so the money supply is created *within* the economy by credit, not multiplied up from a fixed reserve base. The **Bank of England 2014** paper as the institutional statement that reversed the textbook **money-multiplier** story. This is the credit theory's modern vindication on *one* front — but stated as *how modern money works*, not as proof the credit theory wins the *origin* debate.
23. **Fiat money, and the commodity theory's modern strain** — money by declaration and acceptance, with no metal behind it; the metallist must explain (via Mises's regression to a former commodity link, now historically severed) why valueless tokens hold value, while the chartalist takes fiat as the normal case (tax-acceptance, state naming). The contrast, fairly stated.
24. **Chartalism is not MMT (the fenced distinction)** — chartalism = a theory of money's *nature/history*; **MMT** = a present-day *policy* program (deficits, job guarantee, "taxes don't fund spending") that *draws on* chartalism. Name the relationship in one passage; keep them in separate drawers; **no policy advocacy**. This is an integrity beat as much as a content beat.

> *Dependency:* H needs C (credit/state theory) and B (commodity theory) to be the payoff that connects ancient debate to the money in the reader's account. H24 is a fence, not an argument.

### I. Settled vs. open, and the method (the close)
25. **What is settled vs. open** — an honest ledger: *settled* (the barter-sequence is dead; coins are late and state-stamped; money-of-account is ancient; banks create credit money endogenously). *Open* (metallism vs. chartalism on money's *nature*; whether the instituted-standard point settles or shifts the silver question; the causation behind coinage's spread; how far to trust Hudson on jubilees; the primordial-debt over-reach; how much "money of account first" Keynes vs. the modern chartalists actually claim).
26. **The method** — how to weigh any monetary-origin or monetary-nature claim: separate evidence from interpretation; ask what a theory *needs to be true* and whether the record supplies it; steelman before judging; tolerate an honest "unsettled." The transferable skill, enacted across the course and named at the end. (Mirror, at one level up, the intro course's "remembered, or required by a theory?" — but here the diagnostic is "which *theory* does this evidence actually bear on, and how far?")

> *Dependency:* I gathers what the course has been doing. Should feel like recognition, not new material.

## 6. Common pitfalls & misconceptions

### (a) Learner misconceptions the course must address
For each: the misconception, the **steelman** (its strongest form), and the **critical counter** — kept distinct, no winner crowned where the field crowns none.

1. **"The intro course already showed money is really credit/debt, so chartalism wins."**
   - *Steelman of the worry:* the Mesopotamian ledgers, the credit-before-coin record, do fit the credit theory beautifully.
   - *Counter:* the record was denominated in **silver, a commodity**, and a metallist reads it as the market choosing its most saleable good. "Account before coin" shifts the case; it does not settle it. The whole point of this course is that the intro reader **over-learned** the credit theory. (→ D, the central correction.)

2. **"Menger's barter story was debunked, so the commodity theory is dead."**
   - *Steelman of the error:* the intro course did bury the barter *sequence*.
   - *Counter:* the commodity *theory of money's nature* is not the same as the barter *origin story*. Mises's regression theorem and the "why metal" / stranger-trade arguments stand independently of any claim that a barter economy historically preceded money. Do not let the dead myth take the live theory down with it. (→ B.)

3. **"Innes, Knapp, Keynes, and Lerner are all just 'the chartalists'."**
   - *Steelman:* they share an anti-metallist instinct and are often cited together.
   - *Counter:* credit theory (Innes, private debt primary) ≠ state theory (Knapp, state naming primary) ≠ money-of-account-as-primary (Keynes, conceptual priority) ≠ tax-drives-value (Lerner). They disagree about *what* makes money money. Blending them is the single most common undergrad error here. (→ C; integrity pitfall (b) below.)

4. **"Chartalism means MMT / money-printing has no limits."**
   - *Steelman:* MMT genuinely draws on chartalism, so the association is not invented.
   - *Counter:* chartalism is a claim about money's *nature*; MMT is a *policy* program. One can hold the state theory of money and reject every MMT policy, or vice versa. The course names the link once and fences it. (→ H24; integrity pitfall (b).)

5. **"The instituted-standard point proves the chartalists right."**
   - *Steelman:* an administered standard set by temple/palace really does cut against a pure market-emergence story.
   - *Counter:* silver was plausibly *chosen* for its commodity properties, and an administered ratio still prices *something* with real-world value. The evidence **shifts** the debate; it does not end it. Teaching this as a knockout would betray the course's defining discipline. (→ D16.)

6. **"Coins spread *because* of the military-coinage-slavery complex."**
   - *Steelman:* coin, soldiers, taxes, war, and slavery genuinely co-occur in the record.
   - *Counter:* co-occurrence is not the strong causal "complex"; that is **Graeber's interpretation**, contested as over-drawn and regionally uneven. Solid chronology, contested causation. (→ E18.)

7. **"Hudson proved the ancients ran on regular debt jubilees."**
   - *Steelman:* the *misharum*/*andurarum* decrees and the biblical Jubilee are real and attested.
   - *Counter:* how *regular*, *enforced*, and *widespread* they were is contested; Hudson is read by some as over-generalizing. Attested institution, contested scope. (→ F20.)

8. **"There must be a true answer to what money fundamentally is; we just haven't found it."**
   - *Steelman:* the question feels like it has a fact of the matter.
   - *Counter:* part of what an undergrad course must teach is that some of this is **genuinely, productively unsettled**, and that "it depends what you weight as essential" is a defensible final position. The course models tolerating that. (→ I.)

### (b) Author-integrity pitfalls (non-negotiable)
These protect credibility and are mostly named in the brief.

- **Steelman first, every time.** The strongest honest version of Menger/Mises *before* the credit-theory reply; the strongest version of Innes/Knapp/Keynes/Lerner *before* the metallist rejoinder; the strongest Hudson *before* the qualification. A view criticized before it is fairly stated is a strawman and discredits the course.
- **Do not blend the thinkers.** Menger ≠ Mises; Innes ≠ Knapp ≠ Keynes ≠ Lerner ≠ Grierson ≠ Ingham; Graeber ≠ Hudson. Each claim attributed; never fused into one "chartalist" voice. (This is the integrity spine of the course; (a)3 is its learner-facing twin.)
- **Attribute the bold readings as theirs.** Graeber's military-coinage complex and his use of primordial debt; Hudson's strong jubilee thesis; the primordial-debt theorists' originary debt. Named *as their claims*, set beside the cautious consensus, never presented as settled.
- **Do not turn chartalism into MMT advocacy.** Name the relationship, fence it, no policy stance. (Carried verbatim from the intro course's integrity line, now load-bearing because the modern-money module invites the slip.)
- **Keep evidence and interpretation separate in the *structure*, not by reciting "the seam."** Evidence stated as evidence (a weighed-silver loan tablet, a ~7th-c. coin, the BoE paper); readings named as readings (Graeber's, Hudson's, the chartalists'); rejoinders given room. The new pipeline rule forbids the slogan; the *shape* of each argument must carry it. (This is the course's hardest craft demand.)
- **Crown no winner where the field has not.** Metallism vs. chartalism stays open; the instituted-standard point *shifts not settles*; coinage causation, Hudson's scope, and the primordial-debt thesis stay flagged. The reader must be able to leave holding both theories.
- **No fabrication.** Rough dates; no invented clause numbers, interest rates, etymologies, accession numbers, figures, or quotations. Primary quotations accurate or labelled paraphrase. Mark uncertain specifics `[verify]`. Undergrad rigor tightens this, it does not relax it.
- **Do not let "shifts the debate" become mush.** Qualified is not vague. State *precisely* what shifts (the plausibility and the burden move toward the credit/state side) and *precisely* what does not (silver's commodity properties and the metallist's stranger-trade ground survive). Precision is the form qualification takes.

### (c) Genuine live debates to acknowledge, not resolve
Name each; pick no winner. (These are the course's spine of unsettlement.)

- **Metallism vs. chartalism on money's *nature*.** Genuinely unsettled. Each explains some of the evidence and strains on the rest. The course's central open question.
- **Does the instituted-standard point settle or only shift the silver question?** The sharpest live debate, and the course's signature beat. Build both readings; refuse the verdict.
- **The causation behind coinage's spread.** Solid chronology (~7th c. BCE, Lydia/Ionia, state-stamped) vs. the strong "military-coinage-slavery complex" (Graeber's reading), with critics. Fact vs. interpretation.
- **How far to trust Hudson on clean slates.** Attested decrees vs. contested frequency/scope/enforcement. Steelman vs. qualification.
- **The primordial-debt thesis.** A genuine but contested, widely-judged-untestable over-reach (Aglietta/Orléan/Théret; Graeber). Flagged hard; not the frame.
- **How much "money of account first" Keynes vs. the modern chartalists actually claim.** Keynes's *conceptual-priority* claim in the *Treatise* is narrower than some modern chartalist readings make it; do not retroject the strong modern thesis onto Keynes. A scholarship-attribution debate worth naming.

## 8. Open questions for the curriculum stage

1. **Module count.** Recommend **10 modules** (within the brief's 9–11), with a clean path to **11** if the commodity theory's "why metal + stranger trade" splits from "Menger + Mises" (two strong modules instead of one), and to **9** if coinage (E) folds part-way into the instituted-standard module (D). The proposed shape (below) is 10. The protect-at-all-costs beat is **D** (the instituted-standard problem); never compress it to save a module.

2. **Theory-first or history-first, and where does the instituted-standard problem sit?** *This is the central sequencing decision and the brief flags it.* The intro course was history-first; this sequel should be **theory-at-strength-then-evidence**, because the *question* is now "which theory does the evidence bear on." Recommendation: build the commodity theory at full strength (B), then the credit/state theories distinguished (C), **then** collide them on the instituted-standard problem (D) as the course's hinge — D sits at the **center**, roughly module 5 of 10, after both theories are armed and before coinage/debt/modern money widen out. Do **not** open with the evidence (the reader has it from intro) and do **not** bury D late (its power is that it tests both freshly-built theories at once). The whole front half exists to load D; the back half spends it.
