## The hook

Killing the barter sequence felt like killing the commodity theory along with it, and the previous module argued it did no such thing. That promise now has to be cashed. A myth about where money came from died; a claim about what money *is* walked out of the wreckage, and what follows gives that claim its strongest possible form, with nothing held back and nothing yet criticized.

That ordering is the discipline the whole course runs on. A theory you mean to question gets its best advocate first, stated in its own terms, before a single objection touches it. The commodity theory has had advocates as good as economics produces, and two of them built a case far sharper than "money is gold because gold is shiny." One showed how money could arise from trade with no king and no plan behind it. The other showed why money's value cannot be a trick of pure convention, why it has to bottom out in something that was once an ordinary good. Take both at full strength, add the genuine, unsilly reasons metal kept winning, and the commodity theory stops being the easy target the barter myth made it look like. It becomes the thing the credit theory will have to answer.

## Context & background

Recall the ground the intro course cleared. The textbook story the intro course buried ran barter first, then money to fix it, and no society has been found that worked that way. Coins came thousands of years after the ledgers, and the commodity theory, or metallism, the view of money as a special commodity grown out of trade, seemed to go down with the barter sequence it was tangled up in.

It does not go down, and seeing why comes first. The barter *sequence* is a claim about history: that a barter economy existed and then invented money. The commodity *theory* is a claim about money's nature: that money, whenever and however it arose, is at bottom a tradeable good, valued for what it is before it is valued as money. The first claim is dead. The second is independent of it. A commodity theorist can grant every word of the intro course about the absence of any barter age and still hold that money, once it exists, is a commodity by its essential character. The historical origin story and the theory of the nature are two different things, and the death of the first leaves the second standing. The second is the one with a case worth making.

It sorts cleanly into Schumpeter's frame from the last module, the open question of commodity or claim laid out as real analysis against monetary analysis. The commodity theory is the home of real analysis: money as a veil over an essentially barter economy, a convenient tool laid on top of exchange rather than woven into its structure. Every argument that follows sits in that column, and each one is taken at the most it can bear.

## Narrative / exposition

The first advocate is **Carl Menger**, the nineteenth-century Austrian economist the intro course met as the man who first made the case for money as a market product. His question was the one any market account of money has to answer: how does a whole society come to accept one good as money, when no individual trader can decide that for everyone, and there is no king issuing a decree?

His answer turns on a single property of goods he called **saleability**, or marketability: how readily a good can be sold on without loss, how easily you can pass it along again near the price you took it at. Goods differ enormously in this. A cow is worth a great deal but is hard to sell in a hurry without taking a beating on the price; a rare painting may be precious and nearly unsellable on a given afternoon. Other goods move easily, find buyers anywhere, hold their price as they change hands. Saleability is a spectrum, and every trader feels it whether they have a word for it or not.

Now run the consequence. A trader who wants sandals but holds only grain faces the old problem of finding someone who wants grain and sells sandals at the same moment. The trader who sees further makes a move: trade the grain not for what is wanted but for something *more saleable* than grain, something that can be passed on again more easily, and use that to get the sandals. Each trader, acting only for private convenience, reaches for a good more widely accepted than their own. The more a good is taken for this reason, the more accepted it becomes, and the more accepted it becomes, the more reason the next trader has to take it. The process feeds itself. Out of countless private decisions, with no one intending it and no authority imposing it, the society converges on one or a few most saleable goods as the things everyone will take. That convergence *is* the emergence of money. The most saleable commodity becomes the medium of exchange because each person, seeking only their own advantage, finds it the safest thing to hold.

That is an account of money's *origin* as a spontaneous order, a thing built by many hands and intended by none. It is elegant, it needs no planner, and it matches a real intuition: some goods genuinely are easier to pass on than others, and a trader really would rather hold the easy one. What Menger's story does *not* do, on its own, is explain why the money-good has the value it has. And there is a circle waiting exactly there.

The circle is this. Why do you accept money? Because it has value, it will buy things. Why does it have value? Because people accept it. Accepted because valued, valued because accepted. State it baldly and money's value looks like a confidence trick that holds up only because everyone expects everyone else to keep playing along, a value resting on nothing but itself. If that were the whole story, the commodity theory would have no firmer ground than the convention it was supposed to beat.

The second advocate breaks the circle. **Ludwig von Mises**, an early-twentieth-century Austrian economist, gave the commodity theory its value-theoretic spine in 1912 with what is called the **regression theorem**: the argument that money's value today traces back, day by day, to a time when the money-stuff was valued as an ordinary commodity. Take the question "why does money have the purchasing power it has today?" and Mises answers: because of the purchasing power it had yesterday. People form today's expectations of what money will buy from their memory of what it bought yesterday. But that only pushes the question back a day, so ask it of yesterday, and the answer is the day before, and so on backward. The chain cannot regress forever on nothing. It must terminate, and Mises says it terminates at the first day the money-stuff was demanded not as money at all but as a plain commodity, wanted for its own sake, the way silver was wanted for ornament or use before anyone reckoned prices in it. At that first point the good had a value that owed nothing to its monetary role, because it had no monetary role yet. Everything after is the chain of yesterdays building on that non-monetary footing.

The circle breaks because the regress has a floor. Money is not valued because it is accepted and accepted because it is valued, spinning on nothing. It is valued today because of yesterday, and the ladder of yesterdays rests at the bottom on a real commodity demand that came before money existed. Hold the two advocates apart, because the temptation is to fuse them into one "Austrian" story and the fusion loses the point. Menger narrates the *emergence*: how trade throws up a money with no planner. Mises proves a different thing, that money's *value* must originate in a former commodity value, on pain of an infinite regress that explains nothing. Menger answers "how did it arise?"; Mises answers "what keeps its value from being pure convention?". The emergence story and the value proof are separate achievements, and a reader who collapses them into a single claim has thrown away half of the commodity theory's strength.

Both arguments lean on a fact they do not by themselves explain: across most of recorded history, the good the market kept converging on, and the good standing at the bottom of so many regress chains, was metal. So the case has to face the concrete question. Why metal?

The answer is not stupid, and it is worth stating with care because the credit theory's later attack only lands if this case is taken seriously first. Precious metal has a cluster of physical properties that suit it to the job almost uncannily. It is **durable**: it does not rot, rust away, or spoil, so a store of it survives a season or a generation. It is **divisible** without loss: cut a bar of silver in half and you have two pieces worth, together, what the bar was worth, which is not true of a cow or a cloak. It is **portable**, carrying high value in low weight and bulk, so a fortune can move on a single animal or in a single purse. It is **recognizable and hard to fake**, with a color, weight, and feel that an experienced hand can test, so a stranger can be reasonably sure of what they are taking. It is **scarce** and slow to mine, so its supply does not balloon overnight and wreck its worth. And it is **homogeneous**: one ounce of fine silver is interchangeable with any other, fungible in the intro course's sense, any unit as good as any other, so a quantity of it is a clean measure. Durable, divisible, portable, recognizable, scarce, homogeneous. Lay those properties beside the demands of trade and metal looks less like an arbitrary winner than like the good a market searching for its most saleable commodity would be expected to find. That is the commodity theory's strongest concrete ground, and it is real.

## Primary source

Menger set out the saleability argument in "On the Origins of Money" (1892). The passage below gives the sense of his central move, that goods differ in how readily they can be sold on and that trade pushes everyone toward the more saleable good, rather than reproducing his exact wording across the essay. It is a labelled paraphrase, not a transcription.

> The various commodities differ greatly in the readiness with which they can be sold: some can be disposed of at any time, at prices corresponding to the general economic situation, while others can be sold only with difficulty and at a loss. Each man, bringing his own less saleable wares to market, learns that he reaches his end more surely and economically by first exchanging them for goods of greater saleableness, even when he has no direct use for those goods. So, with progressive economic development, we may everywhere observe the phenomenon of a certain number of goods being singled out, by the trading public itself, as preferred media of exchange.
>
> Carl Menger, "On the Origins of Money" (1892), on saleability, paraphrased

Read what the paraphrase carries. Nothing in it appeals to a state, a law, or a decree. The selection is done "by the trading public itself," by private traders each reaching their own end "more surely and economically," and the engine is the plain difference in how readily goods can be sold. This is real analysis in its purest form, money as a thing thrown up by exchange, and it is the commodity theory stated without a strawman in sight. The credit theory, when it arrives, will have to answer *this*, not a cartoon of it.

## Analysis & interpretation

What makes this version of the commodity theory strong is precisely that it survives the death of the barter myth, and reading the two arguments closely shows why. Neither Menger's emergence story nor Mises's regression theorem requires that a barter economy ever historically existed as a stage that money later replaced. Menger needs only that traders, *whenever* they trade, face goods of unequal saleability and reach for the easier ones; that is a claim about the logic of exchange, not a claim about a vanished barter age. Mises needs only that the money-stuff once had a non-monetary use, which is a claim about silver and gold being wanted for ornament and craft, amply true, and not a claim that society ran on direct goods-for-goods swaps before coins. The intro course killed a historical sequence. It left both of these arguments untouched, because neither depends on that sequence being real.

This is where a careful reader has to resist an easy verdict in either direction. It would be too quick to say the commodity theory is refuted, because, as just argued, its strongest form does not ride on the dead barter story. It would be equally too quick to say the theory is therefore vindicated, because everything offered here is a steelman and not yet an adjudication. Two things in particular sit waiting, and honesty names them now even though the course will not press them until later. The saleability story explains convergence on a medium of exchange, but the intro course's hardest lesson was that the *unit of account*, the measuring-stick debts and prices are reckoned in, is a different thing from the *medium of exchange*, the object that changes hands, and Menger's market may explain the second without touching the first. And the "why metal" case explains why metal would make a good money, but the silver standard of the early ledgers was a standard set by something, and whether that something was a market or an authority is the fight the whole course is built around. Neither point is an objection yet. They are debts the commodity theory has incurred, marked here and paid out later, with the theory standing at its full height in the meantime.

## The story

The case for metal was not made in lecture halls. It was made every day, in the open, by men with a black stone and a balance.

For most of the long history in which precious metal served as money, the metal did not arrive stamped, counted, and guaranteed. It arrived as bullion: lumps, bars, broken jewelry, dust, scrap, of uncertain purity and unknown weight, offered by a stranger whose word counted for nothing. To take it as payment, a merchant had to answer two questions on the spot. Is this really the metal it looks like, and how much of it is there? Two ancient tools answered them, and between them they are the "why metal" case made flesh.

The first was the **touchstone**, a piece of fine-grained dark stone, the kind a jeweller still keeps. Rub a piece of gold across it and it leaves a streak. The color and character of that streak, read by a practiced eye, betray how pure the gold is, because a streak of high-karat gold differs visibly from one cut with copper or silver. Sharpen the test by comparing the unknown streak against streaks made by needles of known, graded alloy laid down beside it, and a skilled assayer could judge fineness closely, with nothing but stone and daylight. The very word for unsparing judgment, the touchstone of a claim, comes from this homely object. It worked because metal is **recognizable and hard to fake**: it carries its identity in properties an experienced hand can test, so a man could take silver or gold from someone he had never met and would never see again, and be reasonably sure of what he held.

The second tool was the **balance**. Because the standard was weight and not count, money among strangers had to be weighed, and merchants across the ancient and medieval world carried small, precise scales and sets of standard weights for exactly this. You poured out the metal, balanced it against the weights, and read off the amount. This worked because metal is **divisible** without loss and **homogeneous**: you could cut a piece to bring it to the weight you needed, and any portion of fine silver was as good as any other portion of the same weight, so the scale gave a clean and honest measure. And the whole transaction was possible at all only because metal is **durable** and **portable**, surviving the journey to the marketplace intact and carrying real value in a weight a man could lift.

Watch the touchstone and the balance work together and the commodity theory's concrete claim stops being abstract. A trader who wanted a good more saleable than his own, in Menger's sense, had a good reason to want metal, because metal could be verified by anyone, anywhere, by stone and scale, with no king's stamp and no shared law required. The physical properties were not a list in a textbook; they were the daily difference between a payment a stranger would accept and one he would not. The argument over money's nature is still wide open, and later modules will press hard on what the touchstone and the balance cannot settle. But the case for why metal, of all things, kept being chosen, was tested and weighed in the marketplace for thousands of years before anyone wrote it down as a theory.
