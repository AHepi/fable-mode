## Interpretation notes

**What this course is.** A **follow-up** to the intro course "The Myth of Barter," pitched one level
up (`undergrad`). The predecessor cleared away the false origin story and deliberately left the deeper
question open, refusing to crown a winner between the commodity and credit theories. This course picks
up exactly that unresolved question, *what money fundamentally is*, and adjudicates it at full
scholarly rigor. It is **sharper** (it confronts the strongest form of each argument, not the easy
version) and **more qualified** (it states precisely what the evidence can and cannot bear, names every
live debate, and still crowns no winner where the field has not).

**The defining commitment: sharper AND more qualified.** These pull in opposite directions and the
course must hold both. *Sharper* means engaging the hardest objections head-on. The flagship example,
the one the intro course ducked: because the Mesopotamian standard was denominated in **silver**, and
silver is itself a commodity, "money-of-account before coins" does **not** by itself refute the
commodity theory. The course must build the decisive move, that the silver/barley ratios, prices, and
interest rates were **instituted** by temple, palace, and law (Hammurabi), an *administered* standard
rather than a market spontaneously converging on the most-saleable good (Menger), and then honestly
weigh whether that *settles* the question or only *shifts* it. *More qualified* means: steelman each
position at full strength before any criticism; mark the evidence/interpretation seam by **enacting**
it in the argument (never by reciting "mark the seam"); flag where a famous synthesis over-reaches
(Graeber's "primordial debt", the strong "military-coinage complex" causation, the strong Hudson
jubilee thesis); and refuse the tidy verdict the reader will want.

**Scope — in.** The commodity/metallist theory at full strength (Menger's spontaneous emergence;
Mises's regression theorem; the real force of "why metal" and of stranger/cross-border trade, the
credit theory's weakest ground). The credit and state theories properly distinguished (Innes; Knapp's
state theory / the word "chartalism"; Keynes's "money of account" in the *Treatise on Money*; Lerner;
Grierson on the abstract measure from customary compensation/wergild; Ingham's synthesis; Schumpeter's
monetary-vs-real analysis). The instituted-standard argument and the genuine ambiguity of the
Mesopotamian evidence (administered ratios, the shekel as weight, the temple as great creditor;
Polanyi's separable "money uses"; Hudson), built so the reader sees both the chartalist reading and the
metallist rejoinder. Coinage and the state/war/tax(/slavery) nexus (Grierson's numismatics vs.
Graeber's bolder causal arc, and critics; solid chronology vs. interpretation). Debt, interest, and
clean-slate cancellations at rigor (Hudson's case and its historian critics; the moral economy of
debt). The "primordial debt" thesis and its critics. Modern money as the payoff, **carefully bounded**:
endogenous/bank-created credit money ("loans create deposits"; the Bank of England 2014 account), fiat,
and a clearly fenced note that chartalism (a theory of money's nature) is **not** Modern Monetary
Theory (a present-day policy program), kept in separate drawers with no policy advocacy. A closing
module on what is genuinely settled vs. genuinely open, and how to weigh any monetary-origin claim.

**Scope — out.** Re-teaching the barter myth or the basic vocabulary from scratch (recall briefly,
build past). Technical monetary economics (no IS-LM, money-multiplier mechanics presented as settled,
or interest-rate models). MMT *policy* advocacy (the deficits/jobs-guarantee program is named only to
fence it off). A complete history of money to the present. Partisan framing.

**Rigor / kind.** `humanities`, `undergrad`. No math. Rigor lives in: engaging primary scholarship
directly and accurately; giving each theory its strongest steelman; distinguishing solid evidence from
contested interpretation in the *structure of the argument*; and qualifying claims precisely. The
reader is expected to hold two live theories at once and to tolerate an honest "unsettled."

**Assumed background.** The "Myth of Barter" vocabulary is known; recall each term in a clause on first
reuse but do not re-derive it. New specialist terms (regression theorem, chartalism done properly,
endogenous money, money-of-account in Keynes's sense, wergild, "monetary vs real analysis") are
introduced and glossed, but at undergrad pace, not intro hand-holding.

**Format (new pipeline rules, automatic).** Each module ends in a fact-dense **closing story** (a real,
verifiable episode that illustrates the module's argument and consolidates its concepts), verifiable
facts only, points true, varied across the course. **No em dashes.** **Enact** the disciplines, do not
recite them (no "mark the seam"); **no directive metadiscourse** ("notice", "hold that", "remember").
Confidence, not comfort; density over scaffolding.

**Length.** Thorough, intermediate: roughly 9–11 modules. Arc: recall the open question → the two
theories at full strength → the instituted-standard problem and the Mesopotamian ambiguity → coinage
and the state → debt, interest, jubilees → the over-reaches (primordial debt) → modern money (credit
money, fiat, chartalism-not-MMT) → what is settled vs. open + method.

**Pairing.** Sequel to "The Myth of Barter"; will be added to the Path "Economics as History, Not
Nature" (or a successor) as the advanced step. Keep vocabulary and stance compatible; this course
stands alone for a prepared reader but explicitly builds past the intro one.
