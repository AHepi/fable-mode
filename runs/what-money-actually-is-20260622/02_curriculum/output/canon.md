# Canon — What Money Actually Is

The course's consistency contract. Binding on every authoring sub-agent and on the editorial stage.
Humanities, undergrad. Sequel to "The Myth of Barter"; reuse that course's glosses for shared terms
verbatim. The integrity spine of this course is **keeping the thinkers distinct** (§1b) and **enacting
disciplines rather than reciting them** (§0). Read §0 and §1b before writing a word.

## 0. Non-negotiable rules (read first)

1. **Steelman first, every time.** State the strongest honest version of a position *before* any
   criticism: Menger/Mises before the credit reply; Innes/Knapp/Keynes/Lerner before the metallist
   rejoinder; Hudson before the qualification. A view criticized before it is fairly stated is a
   strawman and discredits the course.
2. **Enact the evidence/interpretation distinction; never recite it.** This is a hard pipeline rule.
   Do **not** write "mark the seam", "notice", "hold that", "remember", "keep in view", or any
   directive metadiscourse. The distinction lives in the *structure* of each argument: evidence in
   its own beat (the tablet, the coin, the decree, the BoE paper), each reading in a **named,
   attributed** beat ("Graeber reads this as…", "Hudson's case is…", "the metallist replies…"), the
   rejoinder/qualification a third beat. The seam *is* the section shape.
3. **Crown no winner where the field has not.** Metallism vs. chartalism on money's *nature* stays
   open; the instituted-standard point **shifts, not settles**; coinage causation, Hudson's scope, and
   the primordial-debt thesis stay flagged. The reader must leave able to hold both theories.
4. **Qualified is not vague.** State *precisely* what shifts (plausibility and burden move toward the
   credit/state side) and *precisely* what does not (silver's commodity properties and the metallist's
   stranger-trade ground survive). Precision is the form qualification takes.
5. **Chartalism is not MMT.** Chartalism / the state theory is a theory of money's *nature and
   history*. MMT is a present-day *policy* program that draws on it. Name the relationship once
   (module 9) and fence it. **No policy advocacy anywhere.** Integrity line, not just scope.
6. **No fabrication.** Rough dates ("~1750 BCE", "~7th c. BCE", "1913/1914"). No invented quotes,
   clause numbers, interest rates, etymologies, museum accession numbers, or precise figures. Primary
   quotations accurate or **clearly labelled paraphrase**. Resolve `[verify]` specifics from
   well-established knowledge or keep them general; never present a guess as fact.
7. **No em dashes** (`—`) in prose; recompose, do not chop. **No directive metadiscourse** (rule 2).
   Any frontmatter value containing a colon is wrapped in quotes (YAML safety).
8. **Recall, never re-teach.** The intro vocabulary (unit of account vs. medium of exchange,
   money-of-account, commodity theory/metallism, credit theory/chartalism, fiat, coinage-is-late) is
   recalled in a clause on first reuse, never re-derived. The course must read as the advanced sequel
   from page one.

## 1a. The Schumpeter frame (the course's analytic backbone)

Introduce once (module 1) and use throughout as the sorting tool. **Monetary analysis** (Schumpeter):
money and credit are part of the essential structure of the economy, not a veil; the credit/state
theory's home. **Real analysis**: money is technically convenient but theoretically a veil over an
essentially barter economy; the commodity theory's home. Every later argument can be sorted into one
column, and the best beats refuse to sit cleanly in either. This distinction is **Schumpeter's**;
attribute it.

## 1b. WHO OWNS WHICH CLAIM (the integrity registry — do not blend)

The top failure mode of an undergrad money course is fusing distinct thinkers into one "chartalist"
voice. Each claim below belongs to one person and is stated as *their* claim. Never attribute one's
claim to another; never blur them into a single position.

| Thinker (one-clause id on first mention) | Owns this claim, and only this | Keep distinct from |
|---|---|---|
| **Carl Menger** (19th-c. Austrian economist) | money emerges, with no planner, as the most **saleable** commodity | Mises (Menger narrates emergence; he does not give the value-theoretic proof) |
| **Ludwig von Mises** (early-20th-c. Austrian economist) | the **regression theorem**: money's value traces back day by day to a former non-monetary commodity value | Menger (Mises grounds the value; he does not just narrate emergence) |
| **A. Mitchell Innes** (early-20th-c. writer on money) | money **is credit**: a coin/note is a transferable IOU; the creditor-debtor relation is primary | Knapp (Innes centers *private* credit, not the state's naming power) |
| **Georg Friedrich Knapp** (German economist) | the **state theory / chartalism**: money is valid because the **state** names it acceptable at its pay-offices (proclamatory, by *charta*) | Innes (Knapp centers the *state*, not private debt); Lerner (Knapp = naming; Lerner = tax-demand) |
| **J. M. Keynes** (economist) | **money of account** is the *primary* concept (the unit debts/prices are expressed in); money-things are secondary (*Treatise on Money*, 1930) | Knapp (Keynes's claim is *conceptual priority*, not state power); the modern chartalists (do not retroject their strong thesis onto Keynes) |
| **Abba Lerner** (20th-c. economist) | **tax-acceptance gives money value**: the state can make a token valuable by demanding it in taxes ("creature of the state") | Knapp (Lerner = the *tax-demand* mechanism, sharper than naming); MMT (Lerner is the bridge *toward*, not into, the policy program) |
| **Philip Grierson** (numismatist) | the abstract **unit of account** originates in the **wergild / compensation tariffs** of early law codes, not in market haggling | Knapp/Innes (Grierson's origin is *customary law*, neither state-naming nor private credit) |
| **Geoffrey Ingham** (present-day sociologist of money) | the **sociological synthesis**: money as a **social relation** and a politically instituted unit of account | the others (Ingham holds credit + state insights together; he is not any single one of them) |
| **Karl Polanyi** (economic historian) | money's **uses are separable** (payment, standard, store, exchange) and need not unite in one all-purpose commodity | the chartalists (Polanyi's point is about *function-separation*, used to weaken the metallist's all-in-one assumption) |
| **Joseph Schumpeter** | the **monetary-vs-real-analysis** meta-distinction (claim theory vs. commodity theory) | everyone (his is the *frame*, not a position within it) |
| **David Graeber** (anthropologist, *Debt*, 2011) | the **military-coinage(-slavery) complex** (a *causal* reading of coinage's spread); a *user* of the primordial-debt thesis | Grierson (Graeber's complex is interpretation; Grierson's chronology is the fact); the primordial-debt theorists (Graeber borrows, did not originate it) |
| **Michael Hudson** (historian of ancient debt) | the strong **clean-slate thesis**: royal *misharum*/*andurarum* decrees and the Jubilee were **recurrent and central** | the attested *fact* of such decrees (which is solid); Hudson's *scope* claim is the contested part |
| **Aglietta & Orléan; Bruno Théret** (French theorists) | the **primordial-debt** origin thesis (society rests on a founding debt to gods/ancestors) | Graeber (he *uses* it); present as *their* claim, flagged as over-reaching |
| **Bank of England** (Monetary Analysis, 2014) | the statement that **"loans create deposits"** and the money-multiplier story is misleading | a *description of how modern money works*, not a verdict on the *origin* debate |

**Bold readings are attributed as theirs:** Graeber's military-coinage complex and his use of primordial
debt; Hudson's strong recurrence/centrality thesis; the primordial-debt theorists' originary debt.
Never present any of these as settled consensus. Set each beside the cautious view it departs from.

## 2. Term registry (new undergrad terms; gloss briefly, then use)

Intro terms are recalled in a clause (rule 0.8), not re-glossed at length. New terms get a brief
undergrad gloss on first use, then free use.

| New term | Brief gloss to deploy at first use | Owner module |
|---|---|---|
| **saleability / marketability** (Menger's sense) | how readily a good can be sold on without loss, Menger's engine of money's emergence | 02 |
| **the regression theorem** (Mises) | the argument that money's value today traces back, day by day, to a time when the money-stuff was valued as an ordinary commodity | 02 |
| **monetary vs. real analysis** (Schumpeter) | money as part of the essential structure versus money as a veil over an essentially barter economy | 01 |
| **money of account** (Keynes's primary-concept sense) | the unit debts and prices are *expressed in*; for Keynes the primary thing, the money-things secondary | 04 |
| **chartalism / state theory** (Knapp, done properly) | money valid because the state names it acceptable at its pay-offices, not because of its metal | 04 |
| **proclamatory / *charta*** | valid by token or declaration of authority rather than by material | 04 |
| **compensation tariff / wergild** (Grierson) | a fixed legal scale of what is owed for each injury or death, Grierson's candidate source of the abstract unit of account | 04 |
| **administered / instituted standard** | an equivalence (e.g. silver-to-barley) set by authority or law, not by a market converging on a price | 05 |
| **the shekel as a unit of weight** | a measured weight of silver, not a coin; the standard was weighed, not minted | 05 |
| **separable money-uses** (Polanyi) | payment, standard, store, and exchange can be done by different things and need not unite in one money | 05 |
| **endogenous / credit money; "loans create deposits"** | money created within the economy when banks lend, rather than multiplied from a fixed reserve base | 09 |
| **the money multiplier** (the reversed textbook story) | the older textbook claim that banks lend a multiple of fixed reserves; the BoE 2014 paper called it misleading | 09 |
| **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT)** | a present-day *policy* program drawing on chartalism, named only to fence it off from the *theory* of money | 09 |
| **primordial debt** (Aglietta/Orléan/Théret; used by Graeber) | the strong, contested claim that society rests on an original debt to gods/ancestors from which money and tax descend | 08 |

**New thinkers needing a who/when/what clause on first mention** (intro introduced Menger, Innes,
Knapp, Ingham, Grierson, Hudson, Graeber; this course adds): **Mises**, **Keynes** (on money),
**Lerner**, **Schumpeter**, **Polanyi** (here owning a specific argument), **Aglietta & Orléan /
Théret**, and the **Bank of England** 2014 paper.

## 6. Voice target

The intro course's essayistic narrator, **leveled up to undergrad**: still concrete and confident, but
it expects a reader who can hold two live theories at once and tolerate an honest "unsettled." It
gives each position its strongest case in that position's own terms before any criticism, attributes
every claim to the thinker who made it, and keeps evidence and interpretation apart by the *shape* of
the argument rather than by announcing it. It never sneers (least of all at Mises, Menger, Graeber, or
Hudson, whose strong versions it needs), never says "obviously," and earns each turn with a date, a
text, or an artifact. It tolerates, and teaches the reader to tolerate, a genuine open question.

**Closing stories:** each module ends in a fact-dense closing story (per `course-design.md`, "The
closing story") that illustrates the module's argument through a real, verifiable episode and
consolidates its concepts by instantiation. Verifiable facts only (this course's load is heavy with
contested specifics, so the fabrication risk is high). Vary them; the assigned candidates are in the
curriculum module notes (Schumpeter's last book; the touchstone; cowries in the slave trade; Innes the
outsider; the tablet read twice; the Artemision electrum; Solon's seisachtheia; the Vedic debt-texts;
the BoE 2014 paper; Keynes on ancient standards). Yap stones are the intro course's and are excluded.

**Drift to correct in editorial:** (a) thinker-blending is the top risk, hold the §1b registry hard;
(b) keep the hinge module (05) genuinely balanced, the metallist rejoinder must land; (c) keep the
modern-money module (09) clear of MMT policy; (d) de-recite any "seam"/"notice"/"hold that" that slips
in; (e) no em dashes; (f) keep the steelman *first* in every contested module.
