## Course metadata (for `_course.md`)

- **title:** How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural
- **description:** A historical, critical account of how a recent and deliberately built economic order came to feel like the natural condition of human life — and a method for seeing it as contingent again.
- **level:** intro
- **kind:** humanities
- **subject:** Humanities
- **prerequisites:** none (a curious adult; no economics, history, or theory assumed)
- **tags:** [capitalism, ideology-critique, economic-history, political-economy, historical-sociology, critical-theory]
- **estimatedHours:** 5
- **sources** (representative — authors/works at the confidence level the dossier fixed; no invented page numbers):
  - Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944)
  - Karl Marx, *Capital*, vol. 1 (1867) — esp. Part 8, "So-Called Primitive Accumulation," and Part 1 on commodity fetishism

## The arc

Three movements, the dossier's throughline: **"it feels natural" → "here is how it was made" → "here is how to see it as contingent."**

- **Movement I — The eyes (1).** Hand the reader the lens before any history, so the history reads as *evidence of contingency*, not trivia.
- **Movement II — Compared to what? (2–3).** Establish that capitalism differs *in kind* from earlier economic life, so "it was made" has a baseline to be measured against.
- **Movement III — How it was made (4–9).** The historical machinery: dispossession (enclosure, slavery), the invention of "the market"/"the economy," the science that made it law, and the remaking of the person (time, the acquisitive self, the gendered hidden subsidy).
- **Movement IV — How it holds, and how to doubt it (10–11).** The production of consent (fetishism, hegemony, discipline), the deliberate modern manufacture of "no alternative," and the denaturalizing method handed back to the reader.

## Module specs

### 01 — The Lens: How a Made Thing Comes to Seem Natural
- **slug:** `01-the-lens`
- **summary:** The tools the whole course runs on — naturalization, contingency, ideology critique, and the trick of making the familiar strange.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain what "naturalization" means and give an everyday example of it at work.
  - Distinguish a *contingent* arrangement (it happened this way but could have gone otherwise) from a *necessary* one.
  - Apply "making-strange" to one familiar economic scene (a job interview, a price tag) and say what it reveals.
- **prereqs:** none
- **est. minutes:** 26
- **notes:** Lens-first, but anchored entirely in vivid hooks ("the market demands…", "it's just human nature," the price tag) so the abstractions arrive through concrete language. Introduces *reification* lightly as a name for the experience; module 10 gives it full content. Owns: naturalization, contingency vs. necessity, ideology (critique sense), estrangement.

### 02 — Compared to What? The Embedded Past
- **slug:** `02-the-embedded-past`
- **summary:** Before capitalism, "the economy" was not a separate thing — producing and exchanging were woven into kinship, status, and obligation.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain what it means for economic life to be *embedded* in social relations.
  - Contrast a gift (which builds a bond) with a market transaction (which clears a price).
  - Argue why "markets have always existed" does not establish that *capitalism* is natural.
- **prereqs:** 01
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** The crucial early correction: *markets ≠ market society*. Steelman "trade is ancient" honestly, then draw the distinction. Mauss's gift, Polanyi's embeddedness, Sahlins's reciprocity (present the "original affluent society" as contested interpretation, and do NOT romanticize the past — embedded economies had their own hierarchy and violence). Owns: embeddedness, the gift / reciprocity, markets-vs-market-society.

### 03 — The Myth of Barter
- **slug:** `03-the-myth-of-barter`
- **summary:** The textbook story that money was invented to fix barter has no historical support — and unpacking why teaches the lens in miniature.
- **objectives:**
  - State the standard "barter → money → markets" origin story and the evidence problem with it.
  - Explain how debt and social obligation can precede coined money.
  - Identify the naturalizing work the barter myth does (making market exchange look like the human default).
- **prereqs:** 01, 02
- **est. minutes:** 24
- **notes:** Graeber's *Debt*; the older Innes/Humphrey point. **Flag the live debate honestly**: the critique is strong but contested; the textbook story may be a logical fable, not a historical claim — present the critique as powerful, not as closed. The debt hook (the moral absolute of "paying what you owe") doubles as a lens example. Owns: the barter myth, debt-before-coinage, the moralization of debt.

### 04 — Enclosure: The Making of the Worker
- **slug:** `04-enclosure-and-the-worker`
- **summary:** Capitalism needed people with nothing to sell but their labour — and that class was produced, by fence and by law, out of the commons.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain what the commons were and why they were a *governed* system, not a free-for-all.
  - Trace how enclosure converted common land into private property and dispossessed customary users.
  - Explain Marx's "so-called primitive accumulation" and why the phrase is sarcastic.
- **prereqs:** 01, 02
- **est. minutes:** 30
- **notes:** The violence behind the calm. Correct the "tragedy of the commons" misread (Hardin described unmanaged access; real commons were governed — Ostrom). **Flag the live debate** on enclosure's degree/coercion: efficiency gains *and* dispossession are both real; naturalization keeps only the first. "Dull compulsion of economic relations": once alternatives are gone, hunger does what the whip did, and it looks like free choice. Owns: the commons, enclosure, primitive accumulation, the "free" labourer, dull compulsion.

### 05 — The Polite Origin Story Hides Slavery
- **slug:** `05-colony-and-plantation`
- **summary:** Capitalism's origin tale of thrift and enterprise omits the plantation and the slave ship, which were integral to accumulation, not external to it.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain why Atlantic slavery and colonial extraction were integral to capitalist accumulation rather than a pre-capitalist holdover.
  - Distinguish the widely-accepted "integral, not external" claim from the contested strong claim that slave profits *financed* industrialization.
  - Identify how the "peaceful thrift" origin story naturalizes capitalism by erasing coercion.
- **prereqs:** 01, 04
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** Williams's *Capitalism and Slavery*; "second slavery" (the *expansion* of slavery inside 19th-c. industrial capitalism). **Steelman/contest carefully**: accept the integral claim; flag the strong financing claim as debated. Guards against a Eurocentric, England-only account that would itself naturalize. Owns: colonial/Atlantic accumulation, the plantation, second slavery, the erased-coercion move.

### 06 — Inventing "the Economy" and "the Market"
- **slug:** `06-inventing-the-market`
- **summary:** The self-regulating market did not grow on its own — land, labour, and money were turned into commodities, and laissez-faire was built and enforced by the state.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain Polanyi's "fictitious commodities" (land, labour, money) and why treating them as ordinary goods is a founding fiction.
  - Explain the claim that "laissez-faire was planned" and how it overturns "market natural, state intrusive."
  - Distinguish a self-regulating market as a *project* from a self-regulating market as a *spontaneous fact*.
- **prereqs:** 01, 04
- **est. minutes:** 30
- **notes:** Polanyi keystone. Fictitious commodities = reification applied to land/labour/money (callback to 01's reification, re-glossed). "Laissez-faire was planned" is the course's single most powerful denaturalizing claim — give it weight. Double movement optional/brief. Owns: the self-regulating market, fictitious commodities, "laissez-faire was planned."

### 07 — The Science That Made It Law
- **slug:** `07-the-science-that-made-it-law`
- **summary:** A young discipline recast a recent, made order as a system of natural laws — and even the word "economy" only lately came to mean a thing you can measure.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain how political economy recast historically specific arrangements as natural laws (Smith's "invisible hand," Ricardo's "iron law of wages").
  - Explain how the marginalist turn made economics about timeless individual preference rather than class and history.
  - Trace how "*the* economy" became a single measurable national object only in the 20th century.
- **prereqs:** 01, 06
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** Keep tightly tied to the *naturalization function*, not a "great books" detour. **Don't strawman Smith** (he was more qualified and moral than the caricature — *Theory of Moral Sentiments*). Mark the "marginalism depoliticized economics" reading as interpretation. The word-"economy" anchor (oikonomia → national accounts, ~1930s–40s; Mitchell) may carry more weight for a beginner than marginalist detail — lead with it. Owns: political economy, marginalism, "the economy" as a recent object.

### 08 — Clocks, Bells, and the Made Self
- **slug:** `08-clocks-bells-and-the-self`
- **summary:** Industrial capitalism remade time and the person — turning hours into something you spend and owe, and the acquisitive individual into "human nature."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain how clock-time and work-discipline replaced task-orientation, and how punctuality and idleness became moral categories.
  - Explain "possessive individualism" — the picture of a person as essentially an owner, including self-owner.
  - Evaluate the "it's just human nature" / acquisitiveness claim, steelmanning it before showing the *form* is historically produced.
- **prereqs:** 01, 04
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** Thompson's factory clock/bell (sensory, vivid). Macpherson on possessive individualism (his Locke reading is contestable — present the *concept* as influential, the textual reading as disputed). The acquisitiveness pair must be steelmanned hardest — it is the reader's deepest intuition; cash out the counter via callback to 02's gift/reciprocity. Owns: work-discipline & time, possessive individualism, the "human nature" pair.

### 09 — The Hidden Subsidy
- **slug:** `09-the-hidden-subsidy`
- **summary:** The "free" worker is sustained by unpaid, largely women's labour — care and household work made invisible by being coded as nature.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain "social reproduction" and why unpaid household and care work is a hidden subsidy to the wage economy.
  - Explain how coding reproductive labour as "women's nature" is itself an act of naturalization.
  - Assess Federici's reading of the witch hunts as part of primitive accumulation, distinguishing the interpretation from the contested causal claim.
- **prereqs:** 01, 04
- **est. minutes:** 28
- **notes:** Adds the dimension the Marx/Polanyi spine under-weights. Federici's *Caliban and the Witch*. **Flag the contestation** clearly (historians dispute causation/chronology) — present as a major reframing interpretation, not settled fact. Owns: social reproduction, the gendered "hidden subsidy," the witch-hunt reframing.

### 10 — How It Comes to Feel Natural
- **slug:** `10-how-it-feels-natural`
- **summary:** Three mechanisms turn a made order into common sense — relations between people appearing as relations between things, consent won in culture, and a self that polices itself.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain commodity fetishism — how the people and labour behind a thing vanish behind its price.
  - Explain hegemony — how an order secures *consent* by becoming everyone's "common sense."
  - Explain how discipline produces self-regulating subjects, and distinguish Foucault's account from the "ideology" concept.
- **prereqs:** 01, 06
- **est. minutes:** 30
- **notes:** The conceptual capstone for "how it *feels* natural and holds." Fetishism (Marx) anchored by the price tag (the concrete version), reification (Lukács) as its generalization (full content of 01's preview — re-gloss), hegemony (Gramsci), discipline (Foucault). **Don't blend the thinkers** — note that Foucault is skeptical of the very "ideology" concept Gramsci/Marx use; attribute carefully. One big idea: the machinery of naturalized consent, at three sites (the shelf, common sense, the self). Owns: commodity fetishism, reification (full), hegemony, discipline.

### 11 — There Is No Alternative — and How to Doubt It
- **slug:** `11-there-is-no-alternative`
- **summary:** The most "natural" common sense — that no alternative is even thinkable — was deliberately manufactured in living memory; here is how it was built, and how to see through it.
- **objectives:**
  - Explain neoliberalism as a constructed, organized project rather than a spontaneous return to natural markets.
  - Explain "capitalist realism" and why "there is no alternative" is an achievement of naturalization, not evidence of necessity.
  - Apply the denaturalizing toolkit (historicize, compare, estrange, ask who benefits) to a "natural" claim of your own choosing.
- **prereqs:** 01, 10
- **est. minutes:** 32
- **notes:** Climax + payoff. The Mont Pèlerin Society (1947) as the vivid proof that "natural" common sense was *manufactured, recently, on purpose* (Mirowski, Slobodian — neoliberalism *encases* markets from democracy, the 20th-c. echo of "laissez-faire was planned"; callback to 06). Fisher's capitalist realism; Thatcher's TINA. Then the **method** as the close: the denaturalizing tools the reader has been using all along, now named (historicize / compare / estrange / cui bono). **Honestly place the "what instead?" question**: denaturalizing gives eyes, not a blueprint — and the demand for a ready-made alternative is itself part of how foreclosure works. End on the method, not a manifesto. Owns: neoliberalism-as-project, capitalist realism, TINA, the denaturalizing method.

## Scope & integrity checks (carried from the brief and dossier)

- **Stance discipline:** every module steelmans the naturalizing claim before dismantling it, and keeps evidence visibly separate from interpretation. This is a critical course, not a polemic.
- **Live debates named, not resolved:** barter critique (03), enclosure's coercion (04), the Williams financing thesis (05), the marginalism reading (07), Federici's causation (09). Each flagged in-module.
- **Author pitfalls to avoid:** polemic-not-argument; teleology/Whig history (no "history was heading here" — contingency runs *both* directions); "capitalism" as a monolith or single dated event (speak of processes); romanticizing the pre-capitalist past; blending thinkers who disagree; anachronism.
- **Out of scope, stated honestly in 11:** no worked-out replacement system; no partisan politics; not a general economics of capitalism.
