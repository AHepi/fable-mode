# Canon — How Capitalism Came to Seem Natural

The course's consistency contract. Binding on every authoring sub-agent and on the editorial stage.
This is a **humanities** course: no math, no Definition/Theorem blocks. "One name per object" here
means one *term*, one *spelling*, one *gloss*, and one *attribution* for each recurring concept and
thinker — so the eleven blind-parallel authors don't drift in vocabulary, framing, or who-said-what.

## 0. Non-negotiable stance rules (read first)

These bind every module. They are what makes this a critical analysis rather than a polemic.

1. **Steelman, then denaturalize.** Whenever a module touches a naturalizing claim ("markets are
   human nature," "barter came first," "enclosure was efficient," "there is no alternative"), state
   its *strongest* version fairly before answering it. The argument's credibility is the honest
   steelman.
2. **Evidence vs. interpretation, kept visibly separate.** "Enclosure happened in these places over
   these centuries" is evidence. "Enclosure was the founding violence of capitalism" is Marx's
   interpretation, and contested. Mark the seam in the prose ("the record shows… / the critical
   reading of it is…"). The course teaches a *method*, not a creed.
3. **Name live debates; don't resolve them.** Where historians genuinely disagree (see §2 ledger's
   "contested" rows), say so in-module. Hiding contested ground commits the sin the course diagnoses.
4. **No teleology.** Never narrate "feudalism → capitalism" (or capitalism → its end) as an
   inevitable march. Contingency runs *both* directions. Banned move: "history was heading toward…".
5. **"Capitalism" is a process, not an event or a monolith.** No "capitalism began in [year]." Speak
   of processes, tendencies, uneven and plural development.
6. **Don't romanticize the pre-capitalist past.** "Different in kind" ≠ "better." Embedded economies
   had hierarchy, serfdom, scarcity, patriarchy, violence. The point is *contingency*, not nostalgia.
7. **Don't blend the thinkers.** Marx, Polanyi, Mauss, Foucault, Gramsci, Federici disagree with one
   another. Attribute each claim to its tradition; note frictions (esp. Foucault vs. the "ideology"
   concept; Polanyi is *not* a Marxist). See §1.

**Thinker attributions — one identifying clause each, on first mention in any module** (never assume
the reader knows them, including Marx):
- **Karl Marx** — 19th-century German critic of political economy (*Capital*, 1867).
- **Karl Polanyi** — Hungarian economic historian writing in the 1940s (*The Great Transformation*, 1944).
- **Marcel Mauss** — French anthropologist of the 1920s (*The Gift*).
- **Marshall Sahlins** — 20th-century American anthropologist (*Stone Age Economics*).
- **David Graeber** — American anthropologist (*Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, 2011).
- **E. P. Thompson** — English social historian (the 1967 essay on time and work-discipline).
- **C. B. Macpherson** — Canadian political theorist (1962).
- **Silvia Federici** — Italian-American feminist scholar (*Caliban and the Witch*, 2004).
- **Antonio Gramsci** — Italian Marxist imprisoned by Mussolini; wrote the *Prison Notebooks* (1929–35).
- **Michel Foucault** — French historian-philosopher of power (*Discipline and Punish*, 1975).
- **Eric Williams** — Trinidadian historian and statesman (*Capitalism and Slavery*, 1944).
- **Adam Smith** — 18th-century Scottish moral philosopher (*The Wealth of Nations*, 1776); more qualified than his caricature.
- **Philip Mirowski / Quinn Slobodian** — present-day historians of neoliberalism.
- **Mark Fisher** — British writer and critic (*Capitalist Realism*, 2009).

**Quotation rule (integrity):** quote only well-established, verifiable phrases, attributed to
author + work + year — **no invented page numbers, no invented wording**. Safe, famous anchors:
Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange"; Marx's "so-called primitive accumulation" and
"dull compulsion of economic relations"; Thatcher's "there is no alternative" and "no such thing as
society"; Polanyi's "laissez-faire was planned." For the "easier to imagine the end of the world than
the end of capitalism" line, attribute as Fisher popularized it (he credits Jameson/Žižek) — do not
pin a fabricated source. If unsure of exact wording, paraphrase and mark it as paraphrase rather than
quote. The humanities "Primary source" block must use a genuine, accurately-rendered passage.

## 5. Voice target

One narrator across all eleven: a **sharp, widely-read critic talking to one intelligent friend who
has never questioned this before.** Warm but not soft; confident, never hectoring. The register is
*essayistic* — concrete nouns, real history, the occasional dry turn — closer to a very good long-form
magazine essay than a lecture or a manifesto. It respects the reader enough to steelman the other
side and to admit what scholars still fight about. It never sneers, never chants, never says
"obviously"; it earns each turn with an example or a date. Pitch every abstraction through something
the reader can see (a fence, a bell, a price tag) before naming it.

**Drift to correct in editorial:** (a) keep any module from sliding into *sermon* — if a paragraph is
all assertion and adjectives with no evidence, it has drifted; pull it back to the example. (b) Keep
the **history modules (04–09) from going dry/textbookish** and the **theory modules (01, 10) from
going abstract** — level both toward the same concrete, essayistic storyteller. (c) De-soothe and
de-adverb per house style (no *gently/quietly/simply*; confidence, not comfort).
