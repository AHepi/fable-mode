# Research Dossier — The Naturalization of Capitalism

*Stage 01 output. Notes for the curriculum designer, not learner-facing prose.*
*Kind: humanities. Level: intro (curious adult / lower-undergrad, no prerequisites).*
*Stance: ideology critique / critical political economy — naturalness is the explanandum.*

---

## 0. Orientation (read this before the sections)

The object of study is **naturalization itself**, not capitalism's economics. Every module must earn
its place by showing one mechanism by which a contingent, recently-built order came to *feel* like the
natural backdrop of human life — or by handing the reader a tool to see through it. The throughline:
**"it feels natural" → "here is how it was made" → "here is how to see it as contingent."**

Two disciplines run through the whole dossier and the eventual course:

1. **Steelman before dismantling.** For each naturalizing claim ("markets are human nature," "barter
   came first," "enclosure was efficient"), give the *strongest* version, then the historical/critical
   rebuttal. The argument's credibility is the strongest version honestly stated.
2. **Distinguish evidence from interpretation.** "Enclosure happened on these dates in these places"
   is evidence. "Enclosure was the original violence that made capitalism" is an interpretation
   (Marx's, contested). Keep the two visibly separate so the course teaches a *method*, not a creed.

A recurring structural move for the course: most concepts come as a **naturalizing claim ↔ denaturalizing
counter** pair. The dossier flags these pairs explicitly so the curriculum can build modules around the
*tension*, not around a lecture.

## 1. Scope

### In scope
- What "naturalization," reification, "second nature," and ideology critique *mean* — the conceptual
  toolkit, taught first so the reader has a lens.
- Pre-capitalist economic life as **embedded** in social relations: that it differed *in kind*, not
  just in degree (substantivist anthropology; the gift; the barter myth).
- The **transition**: enclosure of the commons, "primitive/original accumulation," proletarianization,
  the manufacture of the "free" labourer.
- The **invention of "the economy"** and "the self-regulating market" as autonomous spheres
  (Polanyi; fictitious commodities; "laissez-faire was planned").
- The rise of **political economy as a science** that recast a historical order as natural law
  (Smith → Ricardo → marginalism).
- **Work-discipline and the remaking of time and the self** (Thompson; clocks, bells, the moralizing
  of punctuality and idleness).
- **Possessive individualism** and "human nature" arguments (Macpherson; "man is naturally
  acquisitive / given to truck, barter, and exchange").
- **Commodity fetishism / reification** as the mechanism by which social relations appear as
  relations between things (Marx, Lukács).
- **Hegemony and common sense** (Gramsci) and **discipline / governmentality** (Foucault) as the
  machinery of consent and naturalness.
- **Social reproduction and the body** (Federici): the gendered layer — the witch hunts, the enclosure
  of women's labour, the household as the hidden subsidy.
- The **colonial/slavery dimension** of accumulation (Williams; "second slavery"; the plantation) —
  see Open Questions re: placement.
- The **deliberate 20th-c reconstruction** of market common sense: neoliberalism as a *constructed
  project* (Mont Pèlerin; Mirowski, Slobodian; "there is no alternative").
- **Capitalist realism** (Fisher): the foreclosure of imaginable alternatives.
- **Tools for denaturalizing**: historicizing, comparison, estrangement — the method handed to the reader.

### Out of scope
- General economics of capitalism — business cycles, finance mechanics, growth theory, monetary
  policy. (Money/debt appear only as *naturalization* topics, not as macroeconomics.)
- A survey of socialist programs or a "what to do instead" manifesto.
- Present-day partisan / electoral politics.
- A complete chronological history of capitalism. The course is thematic, organized by *mechanism of
  naturalization*, anchored to history but not a textbook narrative of it.

### Sharpening note
The brief's scope is sound. One sharpening: keep the **lens-first** discipline. The reader needs the
concepts of *naturalization* and *ideology critique* before any history lands, or the history reads as
"interesting facts" rather than as evidence of contingency. Module 1 is a lens, not a chronology start.

## 2. Core concepts and dependency sketch

Listed in **dependency order** (each builds on those above). The grouping below is the proposed *spine*;
the curriculum stage decides final module boundaries.

### A. The lens (taught first — gives the reader the eyes)
1. **Naturalization** — the social process by which a made, historically specific arrangement comes to
   seem like an unchangeable fact of nature ("that's just how things are"). The course's central object.
2. **Contingency vs. necessity** — the distinction the whole course turns on: *was it inevitable, or did
   it happen this way and could have happened otherwise?* Naturalization is the erasure of contingency.
3. **Ideology critique** — analyzing ideas not just for truth but for the social interests and
   arrangements they make seem normal. (Gloss: "ideology" here means *taken-for-granted common sense
   that serves an order*, not "a political program you sign up for.")
4. **Reification / "second nature"** — when human-made relations harden into things that seem to have a
   life of their own, confronting us as external facts. (Lukács's term; "second nature" = the social
   world experienced as if it were first nature.)
5. **Estrangement / making-strange** — the *method* counterpart: deliberately treating the familiar as
   if alien (an anthropologist visiting a stock exchange) to see its strangeness. Introduced early as a
   tool; returned to at the end as the payoff.

> *Dependency:* A1→A2→A3; A4 depends on A1+A3; A5 is the method shadow of A1 and pays off in section G.

### B. Capitalism is recent and specific (the "compared to what?" move)
6. **Embeddedness** — in pre-capitalist societies, economic activity was *embedded* in social relations
   (kinship, religion, status, obligation); there was no separate "economy." (Polanyi.) Plain gloss:
   producing and exchanging were *part of* social life, not a distinct machine called "the economy."
7. **The substantivist insight** — economic life takes different *forms* (reciprocity, redistribution,
   householding) across societies; the self-interested market exchanger is one historically local form,
   not the human default. (Polanyi; Sahlins, *Stone Age Economics* — reciprocity, the "original affluent
   society" [interpretation, contested]).
8. **The gift** — exchange organized to create and sustain social bonds rather than to clear a price
   (Mauss, *The Gift*: gifts are obligatory, create reciprocal ties, are never "free"). Contrast class
   with the impersonal market transaction.
9. **The myth of barter** — the textbook origin story (barter → invented money to fix barter → markets)
   has no ethnographic support; credit/debt and social obligation appear to precede coined money
   (Graeber, *Debt*; the critique restates an older anthropological point — Caroline Humphrey, A. Mitchell
   Innes). **[contested: status of the critique is itself debated — see Pitfalls.]**

> *Dependency:* B6 needs the lens (A). B7,B8,B9 are evidence *for* B6 — that capitalism's "economic man"
> is local, not universal. This section establishes the comparative baseline the rest needs.

### C. The transition: how the natural order was *made* (the violence behind the calm)
10. **The commons** — land and resources held and used in common under customary rules (open fields,
    grazing, gleaning, wood-gathering). Not "no-man's-land"; a *governed* system. (Counters the "tragedy
    of the commons" misread — Hardin described unmanaged access, not actual commons; see Ostrom.)
11. **Enclosure** — the legal/physical conversion of common and customary land into exclusive private
    property (English enclosure, ~16th–19th c.; the Parliamentary enclosures peaking later 18th–early
    19th c.). Dispossessed customary users; created people with nothing to sell but their labour.
12. **Primitive / original accumulation** — Marx's name for the historical, often violent process that
    created the *preconditions* for capitalism (concentrated property + a propertyless class), exposing
    that capital did *not* arise from peaceful thrift. "So-called primitive accumulation" — Marx is being
    sarcastic about the polite origin story. Plain gloss: the starter capital and the "free" workers were
    *produced*, not saved up by the virtuous.
13. **Proletarianization / the "free" labourer** — the manufacture of a class free in two senses: free
    of feudal bonds *and* "freed" from any means of subsistence, hence *compelled* to sell labour.
    Marx's "dull compulsion of economic relations": once the alternatives are gone, no whip is
    needed — hunger does the work, and that compulsion *looks like* free choice.

> *Dependency:* C builds directly on B (you can only see the transition as a transition once you know
> what it transitioned *from*). C10→C11→C12→C13 is a tight causal chain and could be one or two modules.

### D. Inventing "the economy" and "the market" (contingency becomes architecture)
14. **The self-regulating market** — the idea of a market that coordinates society on its own, needing
    no external governance, treated as natural. Polanyi: this is a *project*, not a spontaneous order.
15. **Fictitious commodities** — land, labour, and money were turned into things-bought-and-sold though
    none was *produced for sale* (land = nature; labour = human life; money = a token of trust). Treating
    them as ordinary commodities is the founding fiction of the market society. (Polanyi.)
16. **"Laissez-faire was planned"** — the free market did not emerge by leaving things alone; it was
    *built and enforced* by deliberate state action (Poor Law reform, repeal of protections, legal
    construction of contract and property). Polanyi's paradox: it took massive state intervention to
    create the "non-interfering" market. (Counters "market natural / state intrusive.")
17. **The double movement** — society spontaneously protects itself (labour law, regulation) against the
    disembedding market; the "free market" and its counter-reactions arrive together. (Polanyi.)

> *Dependency:* D needs C (the human material exists) and the lens (D15 *is* reification applied to
> land/labour/money). D16 is the single most powerful denaturalizing claim in the course — flag for
> emphasis. D17 is optional depth.

### E. The science that made it law (naturalization in the language of theory)
18. **Political economy as naturalizing science** — how a young discipline recast a historical, made
    order as a system of natural laws (Smith's division of labour and the "invisible hand"; Ricardo's
    laws of rent/wages; the "iron law of wages"). **Steelman:** these thinkers were doing serious
    analysis of a new reality. **Counter:** their categories (rational self-interest, the market as
    nature) universalized the local and present.
19. **The marginalist turn (~1870s)** — Jevons, Menger, Walras shifted economics from value-as-social
    (labour, classes, distribution) to value-as-subjective-preference and equilibrium, modeled on
    physics. Effect: economics became the study of timeless allocation by rational agents — capitalism's
    specific institutions vanish into "the economy as such." Naturalization via mathematization.
    **[some specifics — exact framing of motives — mark interpretation, not fact.]**
20. **"Economy" the word** — before roughly 1800 "economy" meant *household/estate management* (Gk.
    *oikonomia*) or prudent thrift; "*the* economy" as a single national object you can measure and
    manage is a 20th-c. arrival (consolidated with national accounts/GDP, ~1930s–40s; Timothy Mitchell,
    *Rule of Experts*). A concrete, teachable proof that "the economy" is invented, not eternal.

> *Dependency:* E needs D (there must be a "market" to theorize). E20 is a self-contained, vivid hook
> module and could open the science section. E18→E19 is a history-of-thought arc.

### F. Remaking the person: discipline, time, the acquisitive self
21. **Work-discipline and time** — the imposition of clock-time, factory bells, and the moral coding of
    punctuality/idleness; time becomes a thing you *spend* and *owe*. E.P. Thompson, "Time, Work-Discipline,
    and Industrial Capitalism" (1967). Task-orientation gives way to timed labour; "time is money"
    becomes intuitive. Concrete, sensory, excellent teaching material.
22. **Possessive individualism** — the political theory (read back into "human nature") that a person is
    essentially an owner of themselves and their capacities, owing nothing to society for them; freedom =
    freedom to contract and accumulate. C.B. Macpherson, *The Political Theory of Possessive
    Individualism* (Hobbes, Locke). Plain gloss: the idea that you *are* what you own, including owning
    yourself, presented as a fact about humans rather than a chosen picture.
23. **"It's just human nature" / acquisitiveness** — Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange,"
    the assumption of the naturally self-interested, accumulating individual. **Steelman:** humans do
    trade, want, compete. **Counter:** the *form* (impersonal, maximizing, market-mediated) is
    historically produced (loops back to B7, the gift, reciprocity). The strongest naturalizing claim;
    must be steelmanned hardest.
24. **Social reproduction and the body (the gendered layer)** — capitalism's "free" worker is sustained
    by unpaid reproductive labour (childbirth, care, the household), made invisible and coded "natural"
    (women's nature). Federici, *Caliban and the Witch*: the witch hunts and the enclosure of women's
    bodies/labour as part of primitive accumulation — the disciplining of reproduction alongside the
    disciplining of work. **[Federici's causal claims are contested by some historians; present as a
    powerful interpretation, flag the dispute.]**

> *Dependency:* F needs C+D. F21 and F24 both deepen "the worker was *made*." F22+F23 are the "human
> nature" pair and connect back to B. F24 adds the dimension B–E mostly omit; flag for placement.

### G. Producing consent, and the modern foreclosure
25. **Commodity fetishism** — under capitalism the social relations between people (who made this, under
    what conditions) appear as relations between *things* (prices, goods on a shelf); the labour and power
    behind a commodity are hidden in its price tag. Marx, *Capital* vol. 1. The master concept of the
    course: it *names the experience* of naturalization at the point of sale.
26. **Reification (extended)** — Lukács generalizes fetishism: the whole social world comes to be
    experienced as a collection of fixed things obeying laws, and people as objects within it. (Already
    introduced as A4; here it gets its full content.)
27. **Hegemony and common sense** — Gramsci: a ruling order secures *consent*, not just coercion, when
    its worldview becomes the shared "common sense" of the ruled, the unquestioned frame of ordinary
    talk. Naturalization as a *political achievement* maintained in everyday culture, schools, media.
28. **Discipline / governmentality** — Foucault: power works not only by repression but by *producing*
    self-regulating subjects (the timetable, the exam, the norm; "the conduct of conduct"). How
    individuals come to police themselves into the productive, calculable person the order needs.
29. **Neoliberalism as a constructed project** — the 20th-c. *deliberate* rebuilding of market common
    sense: the Mont Pèlerin Society (founded 1947), Hayek, Friedman, the Chicago school; an organized,
    funded, decades-long campaign to remake institutions and intuitions. (Mirowski, *Never Let a Serious
    Crisis Go to Waste*; Slobodian, *Globalists* — neoliberalism as *encasing* markets from democracy, via
    law and supranational institutions, not as "leaving alone.") The sharpest demonstration of the whole
    thesis: the most "natural" common sense was *manufactured*, and recently, on purpose.
30. **"There is no alternative" / capitalist realism** — Fisher, *Capitalist Realism* (2009): the
    pervasive sense that capitalism is the only viable system, that even imagining an outside is
    impossible ("easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism"). The endpoint of
    naturalization: not argued for, simply unthinkable-otherwise. Thatcher's "there is no alternative"
    (TINA) as the slogan.

> *Dependency:* G needs the lens (A) and most of B–F as its evidence. G25 is the conceptual capstone
> for "how it *feels* natural"; G27/G28 explain *how consent is held*; G29/G30 are the modern, on-purpose
> version. Strong candidate for the course's climactic stretch.

### H. The payoff: tools for denaturalizing
31. **Historicizing** — ask of any "natural" feature: *when did this begin, and what came before?*
    (Every prior module is a worked example.)
32. **Comparison / the ethnographic move** — ask: *do all human societies do this?* If not, it isn't
    nature. (Cashes in section B.)
33. **Estrangement** — make the familiar strange on purpose (A5's payoff): describe a job interview, a
    mortgage, a Black Friday as a Martian would.
34. **Reading for contingency** — spotting the naturalizing move in live language ("the market
    *demands*," "you can't fight economics," "it's just human nature") and asking *who benefits from this
    seeming inevitable?* The reader leaves with a method, not a doctrine.

> *Dependency:* H gathers and names tools the course has been *using*. It should feel like recognition,
> not new material — the reader realizes they already learned the method.

## 3. Prerequisite analysis (no-background reader)

**Assume essentially nothing technical.** This is `intro`. The reader is a curious adult who may never
have read Marx, Polanyi, or any social theory. Calibration rule from `course-design.md` §2: unpack every
term a curious adult would have to look up, on first use, in plain words; claims made plausible by
example and picture, no formal apparatus.

### Can be assumed (general literacy / lived experience)
- Everyday experience of working for wages, buying things, paying rent/bills, debt as a familiar idea.
- A rough sense that history happened — there were kings, feudalism, an Industrial Revolution — without
  any precise dates or theory.
- Comfort following an argument and a narrative; willingness to have intuitions challenged.
- The *feeling* that capitalism is "just how things are" — this is the reader's starting intuition and
  the course's raw material. Use it; don't assume the reader already doubts it.

### Must be taught from scratch — every specialist term glossed at first use
Each of these is an **orphan-term risk** (§3 of course-design): if used unglossed, it's a defect.
The plain-words gloss is the curriculum/authoring stage's job; flagged here so none is missed.

### Named thinkers — introduce with one identifying clause, never assume known
Marx, Polanyi, Mauss, Sahlins, Graeber, E.P. Thompson, Macpherson, Lukács, Gramsci, Foucault, Federici,
Mirowski, Slobodian, Fisher, Smith, Ricardo. Each gets a one-clause "who and when" the first time
(e.g. "Karl Polanyi, a Hungarian economic historian writing in the 1940s…"). Do **not** assume the
reader knows who any of them are, including Marx.

### No silent gaps check
Every concept needed to reach the payoff (section H's method) is introduced in A–G as "must teach."
Nothing in the spine relies on un-taught machinery. The only "assumed" items are general adult

## 4. Hooks & examples (concrete, checkable entry points)

Vivid, specific, anchored. The curriculum can assign these to modules. Each is a *door into a concept*.

- **"There is no alternative" (TINA).** Margaret Thatcher's slogan (UK, late 1970s–80s). Door into
  capitalist realism and the foreclosure of imagined alternatives. → concept 30.
- **"It's just human nature."** The everyday rebuttal the reader has probably *made* or heard. Door into
  possessive individualism and the acquisitiveness debate. → concepts 22, 23.
- **The barter origin myth in econ textbooks.** Open almost any intro economics textbook: "before money,
  people bartered…" — then note no society organized this way has been found. Door into the myth of
  barter and embeddedness. → concept 9. **[anchor: the claim that textbooks repeat this is well
  documented; the *strength* of the anthropological refutation is itself contested — see Pitfalls.]**
- **A common field, before and after enclosure.** A specific English open-field village: villagers
  grazing, gleaning, gathering wood by custom — then a fence, a deed, a hedge, and the same villagers
  with nowhere to graze. Door into the commons, enclosure, dispossession. → concepts 10, 11.
  (Anchor to English enclosure broadly; for a named episode, see Sources/[verify].)
- **The factory clock and bell.** The shift from working by the task and the daylight to working by the
  clock; the factory bell; fines for lateness; the moralizing of "idleness." Thompson's material. Door
  into work-discipline and time. → concept 21. **[anchor: Thompson 1967, well established.]**
- **The word "economy" before ~1800.** It meant managing a household or estate (and "economy" = thrift),
  not a national thing you measure. "*The* economy" as a measurable national object is a 20th-c. arrival.
  Door into the invention of "the economy." → concept 20.
- **Debt and the moralization of "paying what you owe."** The intuition that a debt *must* be repaid as a
  moral absolute; Graeber's contrast with historical debt cancellations (jubilees) and the older
  entanglement of debt with social obligation and violence. Door into the barter/money/debt cluster and
  the moral coloring of economic "facts." → concept 9 (and a hook for the lens, A3).
- **The price tag.** A coffee on a shelf with a price and no trace of who grew, picked, shipped, or
  roasted it. The everyday face of commodity fetishism. Door into concept 25 — the master concept made
  ordinary.
- **The Mont Pèlerin Society.** A founding meeting in Switzerland in 1947 of economists and intellectuals
  organizing, on purpose, to rebuild market common sense over decades. The vivid proof that "natural"
  common sense was *manufactured*. Door into neoliberalism-as-project. → concept 29.
- **The witch hunts as economic history.** The European witch persecutions (roughly 16th–17th c.) read
  by Federici as bound up with the disciplining of women's reproductive labour during the same era as
  enclosure. A startling reframe; door into social reproduction and the gendered layer. → concept 24.
  **[present as interpretation; Federici's causal claims are contested.]**
- **"The market demands…" / "you can't fight economics."** Live phrases from news and politics that treat
  the market as a force of nature with wishes. Door into the lens (ideology critique) and the
  denaturalizing method. → concepts 1, 3, 34. (A good *opening* hook for the whole course.)
- **The job interview / the mortgage, seen by a Martian.** Estrangement exercise: describe selling 40
  years of your waking hours, or borrowing to own a place to sleep, as an alien anthropologist would.
  Door into estrangement as method. → concepts 5, 33.

## 5. Common pitfalls & misconceptions

### (a) Learner misconceptions the course must dismantle
For each: the misconception, the **steelman** (why it's intuitive / its strongest form), and the
**denaturalizing counter**.

1. **"Capitalism = just trade and markets, which have always existed."**
   - *Steelman:* trade, markets, money, and merchants *are* ancient and near-universal.
   - *Counter:* capitalism is not "markets exist" but a *society organized around* market dependence —
     where land, labour, and money are commodities and most people must sell their labour to live.
     Markets-in-society ≠ market society. (Polanyi; the embeddedness material, B.) Distinguish *markets*
     from *capitalism* explicitly and early — this is the single most important correction.

2. **"Markets are natural; the state is an outside intrusion on them."**
   - *Steelman:* heavy regulation can distort markets; the image of the free market vs. the meddling
     state is coherent.
   - *Counter:* the "free market" was *built and enforced* by the state — property law, contract,
     enclosure acts, Poor Law reform. "Laissez-faire was planned." Markets and states co-arise.
     (Polanyi, D16.)

3. **"Barter came first; money was invented to fix barter's problems."**
   - *Steelman:* it's a clean, logical story and it's in every textbook.
   - *Counter:* no ethnographic record of a barter economy later "upgrading" to money; credit, debt, and
     social obligation appear to precede coinage. (Graeber, B9.) **Live debate — see (c).**

4. **"Enclosure was a neutral efficiency improvement."**
   - *Steelman:* enclosed, consolidated farms did raise agricultural productivity, and this is part of
     the standard economic-history account.
   - *Counter:* it was also large-scale dispossession of customary users, achieved partly by force and
     law, and it *produced* the propertyless class capitalism needed. Efficiency and dispossession are
     both true; naturalization keeps only the first. (Marx; agrarian historians.) **Degree of coercion
     is a live debate — see (c).**

5. **"Human nature is acquisitive / competitive / self-interested, so capitalism just fits us."**
   - *Steelman:* humans clearly do want, trade, compete, and accumulate.
   - *Counter:* the *form* those drives take (impersonal, maximizing, market-mediated, treating self-
     interest as the master motive) is historically produced; other societies channel the same drives
     through gift, reciprocity, status, obligation. The acquisitive market-individual is one local form,
     read back onto all humanity. (B7, B8, F22, F23.) **Steelman this one hardest — it's the reader's
     deepest intuition.**

6. **"There's no alternative; this is just how a modern economy has to work."**
   - *Steelman:* every actually-tried alternative at scale has serious problems; the feeling is honest.
   - *Counter:* "unthinkable" is the *achievement* of naturalization, not evidence of necessity; the
     course's whole point is that the present order was once itself unthinkable and then was built.
     (Fisher, G30.) Note: the course denaturalizes — it does *not* owe the reader a worked-out
     replacement (that's out of scope), and should say so honestly rather than gesture at one.

### (b) Intellectual-integrity pitfalls for the *author* to avoid
These protect the course's credibility. They are non-negotiable given the stance.

- **Polemic instead of argument.** "Against capitalist ideology" = *denaturalize*, not *propagandize*.
  Show the contingency; don't assert villainy. Every strong claim earns its keep with evidence or is
  marked as interpretation.
- **Teleology / Whig history.** Do not narrate feudalism → capitalism as an inevitable upward march
  (the very naturalizing move the course attacks, just with a different destination). No "history was
  heading here." Keep contingency *both* directions: capitalism wasn't fated, and neither is its end.
- **Flattening real scholarly disputes.** Where historians genuinely disagree (see (c)), say so. A
  course that hides its own contested ground commits the sin it diagnoses.
- **"Capitalism" as a monolith or a single dated event.** It's plural, uneven, and emerged over
  centuries in different forms in different places. Avoid "capitalism began in [year]." Speak of
  *processes* and *tendencies*.
- **Romanticizing the pre-capitalist past.** Embedded economies also had serfdom, hierarchy, scarcity,
  patriarchy, violence. "Different in kind" ≠ "better" or "a lost Eden." The point is *contingency*,
  not nostalgia. Sahlins's "original affluent society" especially must be presented as a provocative,
  contested interpretation, not a fact.
- **Confusing the map and the thinkers.** Marx, Polanyi, Foucault, Gramsci, Federici disagree with each
  other; don't blend them into one voice. Attribute claims to specific traditions and note where they
  pull apart (e.g. Polanyi is not a Marxist; Foucault resists the ideology concept Gramsci relies on).
- **Anachronism.** Don't read present-day market intuitions back into actors who didn't have them
  (that's the error being studied). E.g. don't assume medieval peasants "really" wanted to maximize.

### (c) Genuine live debates to *acknowledge*, not paper over
The course gains credibility by naming these as open. Each is something to flag, not resolve.

- **The degree and coercion of enclosure.** How violent / dispossessing vs. how gradual/legal/locally
  variable? Marxist and "agrarian-improvement" historians read it differently. Don't pick a winner;
  show the range.
- **The Brenner debate (transition to capitalism).** Robert Brenner's argument that the transition was
  driven by changes in *agrarian class/property relations* (esp. in England), against demographic,
  commercialization (Pirenne/Sweezy "trade-led"), and World-Systems (Wallerstein) accounts. The
  "how/why capitalism" question is genuinely unsettled. Treat as: scholars dispute the *mechanism*; all
  agree it was a *transition*, not a natural state. **[anchor: Brenner debate, 1970s–80s, well documented.]**
- **The status of the barter critique.** Graeber's "myth of barter" rests on real anthropology, but some
  economists/historians push back (barter *does* appear in some contexts; the textbook story is a
  *pedagogical fiction* about logic, not a historical claim, they argue). Present the critique as strong
  but contested. Don't overclaim "barter never happened."
- **Whether Polanyi overstates "disembedding."** Critics argue all economies are *always* embedded in
  *some* institutions (markets included), so "disembedding" is too strong; the market society re-embeds
  in new institutions rather than floating free. A useful nuance: naturalization may be *re-embedding in
  institutions that hide themselves* rather than pure disembedding.
- **Federici's causal claims** about the witch hunts and primitive accumulation. Powerful and
  influential; contested by historians of witchcraft on causation and chronology. Present as a major
  interpretation that reframes the question, flag the dispute.
- **Weber vs. Marx on causation** (if the Protestant-ethic thread is used). Did a religious ethic help
  *produce* capitalism (Weber) or is that idealist over-reach (the materialist critique)? The brief says
  handle Weber *critically*; if included, present as a contested causal story, not settled fact.

## 6. Sources

Real, well-established scholarship. One-line note on contribution + contestation. Where I'm unsure of a
specific (exact date, edition, attribution), I mark **[verify]** and keep the claim at the level I'm
confident in. **No fabricated page numbers, quotes, or precise dates beyond what's flagged as solid.**

### Foundational / the lens
- **Karl Marx, *Capital*, vol. 1 (1867).** Commodity fetishism (Part 1); "so-called primitive
  accumulation" (Part 8); the "dull compulsion of economic relations"; the making of the "free" worker.
  The course's analytic backbone for C, D15, G25. *Contested:* the labour theory of value and the
  inevitability of crisis are not needed by this course and should be left aside; use the *historical
  and ideological* arguments, not the economic-prediction apparatus.
- **Georg Lukács, *History and Class Consciousness* (1923),** esp. "Reification and the Consciousness of
  the Proletariat." Generalizes fetishism into reification / second nature. Source for A4, concept 26.
  *Contested:* later partly repudiated by Lukács himself; dense — use the core idea, not the Hegelian
  scaffolding.
- **Antonio Gramsci, *Prison Notebooks* (written 1929–35).** Hegemony, common sense, consent vs.
  coercion. Source for concept 27. *Note:* fragmentary, written under censorship; concepts are
  reconstructed by editors — attribute carefully.
- **Michel Foucault, *Discipline and Punish* (1975)** and lectures on **governmentality** (*The Birth of
  Biopolitics*, lectures 1978–79). Discipline, the production of subjects, "conduct of conduct," and a
  notable analysis of *neoliberalism* as a mode of governing. Source for concept 28 and a bridge to 29.
  *Contested:* Foucault is skeptical of the "ideology" concept Gramsci/Marx use — note the tension
  rather than blending them.

### The embedded past / anthropology
- **Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944).** The keystone for D (and much of B): embeddedness,
  fictitious commodities, the self-regulating market as project, "laissez-faire was planned," the double
  movement. *Contested:* "disembedding" overstated (see 5c); some economic history dated/superseded —
  but the *framework* remains canonical and pedagogically gold.
- **Marcel Mauss, *The Gift* (Essai sur le don, 1925).** The gift as obligatory, bond-creating exchange;
  "total social fact." Source for B8. Canonical; foundational anthropology.
- **Marshall Sahlins, *Stone Age Economics* (1972).** Substantivist economics; reciprocity; the "original
  affluent society" thesis. Source for B7. *Contested:* the "affluent society" claim is provocative and
  much-debated — present as interpretation.
- **David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011).** The myth of barter; debt and credit before
  coinage; the moralization of debt. Source for B9 and the debt hook. *Contested:* a popular synthesis,
  not without critics; the barter critique restates older points (Innes 1913; Caroline Humphrey's
  ethnographic claim that "no example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described")
  and some specifics are disputed. Strong but flag.
- **Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (1990)** and the critique of **Garrett Hardin, "The Tragedy of
  the Commons" (1968).** Useful for C10: real commons were *governed*, not free-for-alls; Hardin
  described unmanaged open access. Corrects a widespread misconception. *Solid.*

### Transition / enclosure / agrarian history
- **The "Brenner debate"** (Robert Brenner, "Agrarian Class Structure and Economic Development in
  Pre-Industrial Europe," 1976, and the responses collected in *The Brenner Debate*, eds. Aston & Philpin,
  1985). Source for 5c. *Live debate* — present as such.
- **E.P. Thompson, *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963)** and **"Time, Work-Discipline, and
  Industrial Capitalism," *Past & Present* (1967).** The remaking of time, work-discipline, the lived
  experience of class formation. Source for concept 21 and the factory-clock hook. *Solid and canonical.*
- General agrarian / enclosure history (for the commons-and-enclosure narrative): the broad facts of
  English open-field agriculture and Parliamentary enclosure are well established. **[verify]** a specific
  named village/episode and exact dates before using them as anchors; the *general* process is solid.
- **Marx, *Capital* vol. 1, Part 8** (listed above) is also the primary-source text for primitive
  accumulation in the humanities "primary source" slot.

### The person / human nature / the gendered layer
- **C.B. Macpherson, *The Political Theory of Possessive Individualism: Hobbes to Locke* (1962).** Source
  for concept 22. *Contested:* his readings of Hobbes/Locke are disputed by intellectual historians;
  present the *concept* as influential, the specific Locke reading as contestable.
- **Silvia Federici, *Caliban and the Witch* (2004).** Social reproduction; witch hunts and the
  disciplining of women's labour as part of primitive accumulation. Source for concept 24. *Contested:*
  causation and chronology disputed by witchcraft historians — major interpretation, flag the dispute.
- **(Optional) Max Weber, *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905).** The ethic /
  rationalization thread. *Brief says: handle critically.* If used, present as a contested causal story
  and pair with its materialist critics. *Contested.*

### Political economy as science
- **Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776)** — division of labour, "invisible hand," "propensity to
  truck, barter, and exchange." Primary source for E18 and concept 23. (Note: Smith is more
  qualified/moral than the caricature; *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* shows it — avoid strawmanning him.)
- **David Ricardo, *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817)** — rent, the "iron law of
  wages." E18. *Solid* as a landmark; specifics light.
- **The marginalist revolution (~1871–1874): Jevons, Menger, Walras.** The subjective-value /
  equilibrium turn. Source for concept 19. *[verify]* exact framing of the "naturalization" reading —
  the *historical fact* of the turn is solid; the *interpretation* that it depoliticized economics is
  mine/standard-critical and should be flagged as interpretation.
- **Timothy Mitchell, *Rule of Experts* (2002)** (and related work) — "the economy" as a 20th-c. object
  consolidated with national accounting. Source for concept 20. *Solid* for the claim that "*the*
  economy" is a recent object; *[verify]* exact dates of GDP/national-accounts consolidation (broadly
  1930s–40s, Kuznets/Stone era).

### The colonial / slavery dimension
- **Eric Williams, *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944).** Atlantic slavery and the slave trade as integral to
  (not incidental to) capitalist accumulation; the "Williams thesis." *Contested:* the strong economic
  version (slave profits *financed* the Industrial Revolution) is debated; the *integral, not external*
  point is now widely accepted. Source for the colonial dimension of C.
- **"Second slavery"** (Dale Tomich and others) — the *expansion* of slavery *within* 19th-c. industrial
  capitalism (cotton, sugar), against the idea that slavery was a pre-capitalist holdover capitalism
  outgrew. *[verify]* attribution specifics; the concept is real and useful. Relevant to whether the
  colonial dimension deserves its own module (Open Questions).
- **(Adjacent, for the author)** the "New History of Capitalism" / slavery-and-capitalism literature
  (e.g. Sven Beckert, *Empire of Cotton*, 2014) — useful background that accumulation was global and
  coerced from the start. *Contested* in its quantitative claims; use for framing.

### The modern reconstruction
- **Philip Mirowski, *Never Let a Serious Crisis Go to Waste* (2013)** and **Mirowski & Plehwe (eds.),
  *The Road from Mont Pèlerin* (2009).** Neoliberalism as an organized, constructed "thought collective."
  Source for concept 29. *Solid* on the constructed-project claim; polemical in tone — use the documented
  history, attribute the framing.
- **Quinn Slobodian, *Globalists: The End of Empire and the Birth of Neoliberalism* (2018).** Neoliberalism
  as *encasing* markets from democracy via law and supranational institutions — sharpens "laissez-faire
  was planned" for the 20th c. Source for concept 29. *Well regarded.*
- **Mont Pèlerin Society** — founded 1947 (Hayek convened it). *Solid* factual anchor.
- **Mark Fisher, *Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative?* (2009).** The foreclosure of imaginable
  alternatives; "easier to imagine the end of the world…" (Fisher attributes the sentiment to Jameson/
  Žižek — *[verify]* exact attribution before quoting). Source for concept 30. *Solid* as the framing
  text; a popular essay, not academic monograph — present as such.
- **Margaret Thatcher, "there is no alternative" (TINA)** — UK political slogan, late 1970s–80s. *Solid*
  factual anchor for the hook.

### General method / background (for the author)
- The **substantivist–formalist debate** in economic anthropology (Polanyi vs. neoclassical
  formalists) — frames B and the "is the market natural to humans" question. *Solid* as a real debate.
- **Berger & Luckmann, *The Social Construction of Reality* (1966)** — optional general scaffolding for
  "naturalization / reification" as a social process; pairs with Lukács in plainer language. *Optional.*

## 7. Open questions for the curriculum stage

1. **Module count.** Recommend **10 modules** (within the brief's 9–11). The spine (A–H) maps cleanly:
   1 lens, 1–2 embedded-past, 1–2 transition, 1–2 invention-of-the-economy/science, 1–2 person, 1–2
   consent-and-foreclosure, 1 method. See proposed shape below. 11 if the colonial dimension gets its own
   module; 9 if the science section folds into D.

2. **Does the colonial / slavery dimension get its own module?** Strong case **yes** (→ 11 modules):
   it's central to *accumulation* (Williams, second slavery) and to denaturalizing the "peaceful thrift"
   origin story; omitting it risks an England-only, Eurocentric account that itself naturalizes. Strong
   case for **folding into the transition module** (→ keep 10): length discipline, and the throughline is
   *naturalization* not *a full history of accumulation*. **Recommendation:** give it its own module if
   count allows — it's too important to be a subsection, and "the polite origin story hides slavery" is a
   first-rate denaturalizing beat. Flagged for the curriculum's call.

3. **Where does the gendered / social-reproduction layer slot?** Two options: (a) its own module in the
   "remaking the person" section (F), foregrounding Federici; (b) woven through the transition (enclosure
   of the commons *and* of women's labour together). **Recommendation:** its own module — it deepens
   "the worker was made" and adds the dimension the Marx/Polanyi spine under-weights; flag the Federici
   contestation in-module.

4. **Lens-first vs. history-first opening.** Recommend **lens-first** (open with naturalization,
   contingency, ideology critique, estrangement) so history reads as *evidence* not trivia. Risk: an
   abstract opening for an `intro` reader. Mitigation: anchor the lens module entirely in the vivid hooks
   ("the market demands…," "it's just human nature," the price tag) so the *concepts* arrive through
   concrete language. The curriculum should decide how much lens to front-load vs. let emerge.

5. **How much history of economic thought (E)?** The Smith→Ricardo→marginalism arc is rich but risks
   becoming a "great books" detour that loses the `intro` reader. **Recommendation:** keep it tightly
   tied to the *naturalization* function (how theory recast the made as natural-law); the word-"economy"
   hook (concept 20) may carry more weight for a beginner than the marginalist turn. Consider making 20
   the spine of the module and 18–19 supporting, rather than the reverse.

6. **Weber / Protestant ethic — in or out?** The brief says "handled critically." It's optional depth; it
   risks adding a contested causal sub-debate that doesn't directly serve *naturalization*. **Recommendation:**
   mention briefly (as one naturalizing story among others — capitalism as the fruit of virtue) rather
   than a full module; don't let it eat scope.

7. **Where do fetishism (25) and reification (26) live relative to the lens (A4)?** Reification is
   introduced *as a tool* early (A4) but gets full content late (G26), with fetishism (25) as its
   concrete anchor. Confirm this split works pedagogically — or introduce fetishism earlier as the
   intuitive version and reification later as the generalization. Either way, avoid teaching the abstract
   (reification) before the concrete (fetishism / the price tag).

8. **What to cut if length is tight?** Lowest-priority-for-the-throughline candidates: the marginalist
   detail (19, keep the *fact*, cut the depth), the double movement (17, optional), Weber (6 above).
   *Protect:* the lens (A), embeddedness + barter (B), enclosure + primitive accumulation (C),
   fictitious commodities + "laissez-faire was planned" (D), fetishism (25), neoliberalism-as-project
   (29), capitalist realism (30), and the method (H). Those are the load-bearing beats.

9. **The "what instead?" question.** The reader *will* ask it. The course's honest answer (per scope) is:
   "denaturalizing is not the same as prescribing; this course gives you eyes, not a blueprint." Decide
   *where* to say this explicitly (likely the final module) so it doesn't read as evasion. This is itself
   a teachable point: the demand for a ready alternative is part of how foreclosure (capitalist realism)
   works.
