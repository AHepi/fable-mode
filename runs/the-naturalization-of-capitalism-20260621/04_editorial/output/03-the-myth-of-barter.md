## The hook

Almost every economics textbook ever printed tells the same little fairy tale on page one, and almost none of them mention that it never happened.

Open the first chapter and you will find some version of it. *Long ago, before money, people bartered.* A man with a surplus of arrowheads wants a sack of grain; he must find a farmer who happens to want arrowheads, and who happens to have grain to spare, at the same time, in the same place. This is clumsy, economists call the problem the "double coincidence of wants," and so, the story continues, people invented money to grease the wheels. First barter, then money to fix barter, then markets, then us. Tidy, intuitive, and taught to roughly everyone who ever took an economics class.

It is also, as far as the historical and ethnographic record can tell, a story with no documented case behind it. No one has found the village of arrowhead-traders waiting to invent the coin. The tale survives not because the evidence supports it but because it *feels* like it must be true. That feeling, not the facts, is what this module is about. The barter myth is the lens from module 01 in miniature: a contingent arrangement wearing the mask of the necessary. Pull the mask off here, on a small checkable example, and the whole method comes into focus.

## Context & background

Two tools from earlier, glossed quickly. From module 01: **naturalization**: the move that makes a recent, made arrangement look like an unchangeable fact of nature. From module 02: economic life used to be **embedded**, woven into kinship, status, and obligation, and goods often moved through the **gift**, an exchange that builds bonds rather than clearing prices. Module 02's line matters most here: markets are ancient, but a *market society* organized around impersonal buying and selling is not. Hold those, and the barter story starts to look strange in a useful way.

Now meet the story's most distinguished author, because the point is not to mock a careless textbook writer but to take the strongest version seriously. The barter origin tale is usually traced to **Adam Smith**, the 18th-century Scottish moral philosopher whose *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) is the founding text of modern economics, a careful, humane thinker, far more qualified than the caricature of him as a cheerleader for greed. He needed a story for where money came from and reasoned his way to one from his armchair. The barter story was *reasoned*, not *observed*: it is what exchange would have to look like *if* it began with isolated individuals swapping goods. Whether it ever did is the question the rest of this module tests.

## Narrative / exposition

Here is the steelman, because a lens that only works on weak arguments is a toy. The barter story is not stupid. Imagine two strangers, each owning goods and wanting what the other has, and yes, direct swap is awkward, money would help, the story writes itself. The logic is so clean it can feel less like a hypothesis than a deduction: *of course* it went that way; how else? That sense of inevitability is precisely the thing to be suspicious of. It has the texture of a naturalized claim, an argument that has stopped feeling like an argument.

So put the question to the record. If barter economies were the universal first stage of human exchange, the world's anthropologists, who have spent two centuries living among societies of every kind, many with no coined money at all, should have found some. They have looked. Caroline Humphrey put the finding about as flatly as a scholar can: no example of a pure barter economy has ever been described, let alone one from which money then emerged. Societies without coins are everywhere in the record; societies that ran on the spot-swap of goods between strangers, waiting for someone to invent money, are not there.

What the record shows *instead* reverses the order. In societies before or without coined money, people did not mostly swap goods on the spot; they kept track of who owed what. A neighbor's pig went to a wedding feast and was remembered; a debt was incurred and carried, sometimes for years, settled later in kind or in service or in marriage or not at all. Goods moved on **credit**, on the strength of an ongoing relationship and a running tally of obligation, long before they moved for cash. Fix this in plain words: **debt and obligation appear to come *before* coined money, not after it.** Credit is not a late upgrade markets invented; it looks, if anything, like the older thing, and impersonal cash exchange like the latecomer.

The fullest recent telling is by **David Graeber**, the American anthropologist whose *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) gathered the case for a wide readership, though Graeber was restating a point older than himself, running back at least to the credit theorists of the early twentieth century. The most-taught origin story in economics rests on a stage of history no one has located.

Now (this is where the lens earns its keep) the barter myth carries a passenger. Smuggled inside "first there was barter" is a much bigger claim: that the natural, default way for humans to deal with each other is *the impersonal swap*, two self-interested parties, no prior bond, the relationship beginning and ending with the transaction. Set the myth beside the embedded economies of module 02, where exchange built bonds rather than clearing prices, and the trick shows: the barter story takes one historically specific person, the stranger who trades to win, and installs him at the dawn of time, so *our* way of exchanging looks like the thing all the others were clumsy attempts at. That is the naturalizing work. The story is not really about the origin of money; it makes one arrangement of human life pass for the bedrock under all the others.

There is a second passenger, quieter, because you have felt it. From this same soil grows the conviction that **a debt must be repaid**, not as a practical matter but as a moral absolute, a law older than any particular law. It feels less like a rule than like gravity. But if obligation came before coined money, then "what you owe" was once a flexible, social, negotiable thing, forgiven, deferred, settled in a dozen non-cash ways, sometimes wiped clean. The iron sense that a number on a ledger *must* be paid is not the natural state of debt. It is itself a naturalized arrangement, the moralizing of an economic fact, worth remembering the next time the word "must" arrives wearing the robes of nature.

## Primary source

Here is the story in its most famous form, from the man himself. Read it as the anthropologist from module 01 would, not to cheer or boo, but as a specimen.

> "This division of labour … is the necessary, though very slow and gradual, consequence of a certain propensity in human nature … the propensity to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another."
>
> — Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), Book I, Chapter II

Look at where Smith locates the source: not in a custom, not in an institution, but in *human nature*, a "propensity" we are born with, like hunger or speech. A specific way of dealing with one another is declared a feature of the species, *what people are like*. He wasn't lying; he was reasoning without an ethnographic record he could not have had. But the costume from module 01 sits right here in the founding text of the discipline: a claim about *one human arrangement* wearing the clothes of a claim about *human nature as such*. Two and a half centuries of textbooks inherited it and forgot it was a costume.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now the discipline this course insists on: keep evidence and interpretation visibly apart, especially here, because this is a live debate.

The **evidence** is strong and fairly settled: Smith's 1776 passage, the absence of any documented barter-to-money economy, the rich attestation of credit in societies without coinage. These are checkable claims.

The **interpretation** is where honest scholars still disagree. The strong reading (Graeber's, broadly) is that the textbook story is not just unproven but an ideological folk tale, one that naturalizes the impersonal market by writing it into the origin of the species. This course finds it persuasive, but it is contested, and the best counter deserves stating fairly. Some economists reply that the barter story was never meant as *history* at all. It is a *logical fable*: a thought experiment about *why money is convenient*, not a claim about a real past. Read that way, the absence of a real barter village is no more damning than the absence of a real "state of nature" is damning to a philosopher who uses one to reason about government.

That counter has real force, and it should soften the critique without dissolving it. Two things are true at once. Barter genuinely *does* occur, between strangers or enemies with no relationship to run a tally on, or in the wreckage of a collapsed currency. And the textbook *does*, again and again, present the fable as if it were history, letting readers carry away a picture of the impersonal market as the human default. The defensible position is not the slogan "barter never happened." It is more careful: *barter is real but marginal; it was never the universal first stage the story needs; and the story's staying power comes less from evidence than from how natural it makes our own way of exchanging feel.* Hold the critique as powerful and the debate as open. That is not a hedge but the method. A course that flattened a genuine dispute to win a point would be naturalizing its own reading.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer here. The rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Hold the line on a live debate.** State, as fairly as you can, the "it's only a logical fable" defense of the barter story. Then say precisely how much of the critique it rescues and how much it leaves standing, and what you would need to know to settle the rest. (The goal is not to declare a winner but to map the disagreement honestly.)
2. **Read the costume.** Take Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange" passage. In one paragraph, separate what Smith *observed* from what he *assumed*, and name the exact move where a claim about one human arrangement gets re-dressed as a claim about human nature.
3. **The moral weight of "must."** "A debt must be repaid" feels like a law of nature. Drawing on the idea that obligation predates coined money, argue for or against the claim that this feeling is itself a *naturalized* arrangement rather than a moral bedrock, and ground your answer in something concrete (a historical debt cancellation, a personal IOU between friends, a payday loan).
4. **Run the method.** Find another origin story told as if obvious, such as "the family has always been…", "trade between nations naturally…", "people have always wanted to own land." Apply this module's move: what does the story install at the beginning, and whom does it make look like the human default?

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Stakes a clear, arguable claim, not a summary of the module, and not the slogan "barter never happened" (the module explicitly rejects overclaiming).
- Quotes or reads the **specific** words and details, Smith's actual phrasing, a real case, rather than gesturing at "the textbooks" in the abstract.
- Keeps **evidence separate from interpretation**: distinguishes the well-supported finding (no documented pure-barter-to-money economy) from the contested reading (the story is ideological folk tale).
- **Steelmans the other side**: gives the "logical fable" defense its honest due before answering it, and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.
- Uses *naturalization* and the *embedded / gift* distinction from module 02 precisely, and resists romanticizing the past: "credit came first" is a claim about contingency, not about a kinder lost world.

## Recap

The most-taught origin story in economics (barter, then money to fix it, then markets) rests on a stage of history no one has located: the record shows credit and obligation running *ahead* of coined money, not behind it. We took the strong version seriously, traced it to Smith, and kept the live debate open: the critique is powerful, but the story may be a logical fable rather than a false history, and barter is real if marginal. What the myth reliably does, fable or not, is naturalize. It installs the impersonal market exchanger at the dawn of time, and lends the cold authority of nature to the feeling that a debt simply *must* be paid. Next the course turns from "compared to what?" to the harder question: *how was the new arrangement actually made?* The answer begins not with a clever invention but with a fence.
