## Interpretation notes

**What the topic is.** The course traces the *historical process* by which capitalism — a
specific, recently-invented way of organizing production, work, land, money, and time — came to be
experienced as the natural, inevitable, common-sense backdrop of human life ("there is no
alternative," "it's just human nature," "markets are how things have always worked"). The object of
study is not capitalism's economics but its **naturalization**: the social, political, legal, and
cultural work that turned a contingent arrangement into a perceived fact of nature.

**The stance the user asked for.** "Thorough analysis separate from / against capitalist ideology."
So the course is written from the tradition of **ideology critique and critical political economy** —
it treats the naturalness of capitalism as the thing to be *explained and denaturalized*, not
assumed. Intellectual sources to draw on: Marx (commodity fetishism, primitive accumulation,
"the dull compulsion of economic relations"); Polanyi (the "great transformation," fictitious
commodities, the disembedding of the economy, "laissez-faire was planned"); Weber and the
Protestant-ethic / rationalization thread (handled critically); E.P. Thompson (time, work-discipline,
the making of the working class); the anthropology of markets and money (Mauss, Sahlins, Graeber —
"the myth of barter," debt); historians of enclosure and the commons; Foucault / Gramsci (discipline,
hegemony, common sense); Federici (enclosure of the body, social reproduction); Macpherson
(possessive individualism); Mirowski / Slobodian on neoliberalism's deliberate construction; Fisher
(capitalist realism). Naturalization is achieved through many channels — political economy as a
"science," law and property, education, the disappearance of alternatives from memory, language
itself — and the course should show that machinery.

**Stance discipline (important).** "Against capitalist ideology" means *denaturalizing*, not
*propagandizing*. The course's credibility depends on doing real intellectual history: steelman the
naturalizing arguments before dismantling them, cite the actual historical record, distinguish
evidence from interpretation, and acknowledge genuine scholarly disputes (e.g. how coercive
enclosure was, the barter debate's status, whether "feudalism→capitalism" is too tidy). It is a
critical course, not a polemic. The "thoroughness" the user wants *is* the argument: contingency is
demonstrated, not asserted.

**Scope — in.** Pre-capitalist economic life and how it differed; the transition (enclosure,
primitive accumulation, the commons, proletarianization); the invention of "the economy" and "the
market" as autonomous spheres; the rise of political economy as the science that naturalized them;
work-discipline and the remaking of time and the self; possessive individualism and "human nature"
arguments; money / debt / barter myths; law and property as naturalizing machinery; the 20th-century
*deliberate* reconstruction of market common sense (neoliberalism); "capitalist realism" and the
foreclosure of alternatives; tools for denaturalizing / seeing the contingency.

**Scope — out.** Not a general history of capitalism's economics (no detailed treatment of business
cycles, finance mechanics, growth theory). Not a survey of socialist programs or a "what to do
instead" manifesto. Not present-day partisan politics. The throughline is always *naturalization*,
not capitalism-in-general; every module earns its place by showing a piece of how the contingent
came to look natural — or how to see through it.

**Rigor / kind.** `humanities`. No math. The standard is historical and conceptual rigor: claims
anchored to periods, places, and named scholarship; key terms defined; rival readings noted. Use the
humanities module shape (argument + evidence + texts / cases), not the STEM definition/theorem shape.

**Assumed background.** None technical. A reader who has never read Marx or Polanyi should be able to
follow. Every specialist term (commodity, enclosure, fictitious commodity, hegemony, reification,
proletarianization) is introduced in plain words at first use. Bridges carry the reader from everyday
intuition into each concept.

**Length.** Thorough as requested: roughly 9–11 modules, a coherent arc from "it feels natural" →
"here is how it was made" → "here is how to see it as contingent."
