## The hook

Almost every economics textbook ever printed tells the same little fairy tale on page one, and almost none of them mention that it never happened.

Open the first chapter and you will find some version of it. *Long ago, before money, people bartered.* A man with a surplus of arrowheads wants a sack of grain; he must find a farmer who happens to want arrowheads, and who happens to have grain to spare, at the same time, in the same place. This is clumsy — economists call the problem the "double coincidence of wants" — and so, the story continues, people invented money to grease the wheels. First barter, then money to fix barter, then markets, then us. It is tidy, it is intuitive, and it has been taught to roughly everyone who ever took an economics class.

It is also, as far as the historical and ethnographic record can tell, a story with no documented case behind it. No one has found the village of arrowhead-traders waiting to invent the coin. The tale survives not because the evidence supports it but because it *feels* like it must be true — and that feeling, not the facts, is what this module is about. The barter myth is the lens from module 01 in miniature: a made arrangement dressed up as the human default, a contingent thing wearing the mask of the necessary. Pull the mask off here, on a small and checkable example, and the whole method comes into focus.

## Context & background

First, a quick re-stocking of two tools from earlier, because we are about to lean on both.

From module 01: **naturalization** is the move that makes a recent, made arrangement look like an unchangeable fact of nature — a choice dressed as a truth. From module 02: economic life used not to be a separate thing called "the economy." It was **embedded** — woven into kinship, status, and obligation — and the way people moved goods around was often the **gift**, an exchange that builds and repays a social bond rather than clearing a price on the spot. Module 02 drew the line that matters most for what follows: markets are ancient, but a *market society* — one organized around impersonal buying and selling — is not. Hold those two, and the barter story starts to look strange in a useful way.

Now meet the story's most distinguished author, because the point is not to mock a careless textbook writer but to take the strongest version seriously. The barter origin tale is usually traced to **Adam Smith**, the 18th-century Scottish moral philosopher whose *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) is the founding text of modern economics. Smith was no fool — a careful, humane thinker, far more qualified than the caricature of him as a cheerleader for greed. He needed a story for where money came from, and reasoned his way to one that made sense from his armchair. That is worth naming plainly: the barter story was *reasoned*, not *observed*. It is what exchange would have to have looked like *if* it began with isolated individuals swapping goods. Whether it ever did is a separate question — and the one the rest of this module puts to the test.

## Narrative / exposition

Here is the steelman, stated as strongly as it deserves, because a lens that only works on weak arguments is a toy. The barter story is not stupid. It has a real logic. If you imagine two strangers, each owning some goods and wanting some of what the other has, then yes — direct swap is awkward, money would help, and the story writes itself. The logic is so clean that it can feel less like a hypothesis than like a deduction: *of course* it went that way; how else could it have gone? That sense of inevitability is precisely the thing to be suspicious of. It is the texture of a naturalized claim — an argument that has stopped feeling like an argument.

So put the question to the record. If barter economies were the universal first stage of human exchange, the world's anthropologists — who have spent two centuries living among societies of every kind, including many with no coined money at all — should have found some. They have looked. What they report is the opposite of the textbook. The anthropologist Caroline Humphrey put the finding about as flatly as a scholar can: no example of a pure barter economy has ever been described, let alone one from which money then emerged. Societies without coins are everywhere in the record. Societies that ran on the spot-swap of goods between strangers, waiting for someone to invent money, are not there.

What the record shows *instead* is the surprising part, and it reverses the order of the story. In societies before or without coined money, people did not mostly stand around swapping goods on the spot. They kept track of who owed what. A neighbor's pig went to a wedding feast and was remembered; a debt was incurred and carried, sometimes for years, settled later in kind or in service or in marriage or not at all. Goods moved on **credit** — on the strength of an ongoing relationship and a running tally of obligation — long before they moved for cash. This is the claim worth fixing in plain words: **debt and obligation appear to come *before* coined money, not after it.** Credit is not a sophisticated upgrade that markets eventually invented. It looks, if anything, like the older thing, and impersonal cash exchange like the latecomer.

The fullest recent telling of this is by **David Graeber**, an American anthropologist whose *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) gathered the case for a wide readership. But Graeber was restating a point older than himself — it runs back at least to the credit theorists of the early twentieth century, and Humphrey's ethnographic verdict had already laid the empirical floor. The argument has a long pedigree because the gap it names is so large: the most-taught origin story in economics rests on a stage of history no one has located.

Now — and this is where the lens earns its keep — notice that the barter myth carries a passenger. Smuggled inside "first there was barter" is a much bigger claim: that the natural, original, default way for humans to deal with each other is *the impersonal swap* — two self-interested parties, no prior bond, each out to get the better of the trade, the relationship beginning and ending with the transaction. The myth makes the market exchanger look like the human starting point, the bedrock onto which everything else was built. But module 02 already showed that figure to be a local form, not a universal one. Set the myth beside the embedded economies of module 02, where exchange built bonds rather than clearing prices, and you can see the trick: the barter story takes one historically specific kind of person — the stranger who trades to win — and installs him at the dawn of time, so that *our* way of exchanging looks like the thing all the others were clumsy attempts at. That is the naturalizing work. The story is not really about the origin of money. It is about making one arrangement of human life feel like the floor under all the others.

There is a second passenger, quieter, because you have felt it. From this same soil grows the conviction that **a debt must be repaid** — not as a practical matter but as a moral absolute, a law older than any particular law. It feels less like a rule than like gravity. But if obligation came before coined money, then "what you owe" was once a flexible, social, negotiable thing — forgiven, deferred, settled in a dozen non-cash ways, sometimes wiped clean entirely. The iron sense that a number on a ledger is a sacred quantity that *must* be paid is not the natural state of debt. It is itself a naturalized arrangement — the moralizing of an economic fact — worth remembering the next time the word "must" arrives wearing the robes of nature.

## Primary source

Here is the story in its most famous form, from the man himself. Read it as the anthropologist from module 01 would — not to cheer or boo, but as a specimen.

> "This division of labour … is the necessary, though very slow and gradual, consequence of a certain propensity in human nature … the propensity to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another."
>
> — Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), Book I, Chapter II

Look at where Smith locates the source: not in a custom, not in an institution, not in a particular society, but in *human nature* — a "propensity" we are born with, like hunger or speech. This is the move, performed by a serious thinker in good faith. A specific way of dealing with one another — truck, barter, exchange, the impersonal swap — is declared a feature of the species, present from the start, simply *what people are like*. Everything else in the economy then follows from this seed as a plant follows from a kernel.

Smith was reasoning, not reporting; he had no ethnographic record and could not have had one. The point of reading him is not that he lied — he didn't — but that the costume from module 01 is right here in the founding text of the discipline: a claim about *one human arrangement* wearing the clothes of a claim about *human nature as such*. Two and a half centuries of textbooks inherited the costume and forgot it was one.

## Analysis & interpretation

Now the discipline this course insists on: keep evidence and interpretation visibly apart, especially here, because this is a live debate and pretending otherwise would commit the very sin the course diagnoses.

The **evidence** is strong and fairly settled. Smith did write the propensity-to-truck passage in 1776. Anthropologists have not documented a pure barter economy that then gave rise to money; credit and obligation are richly attested in societies without coinage. These are checkable claims, and the barter critique rests on solid ground.

The **interpretation** is where honest scholars still disagree, and you should know exactly where the line runs. The strong reading — Graeber's, broadly — is that the textbook story is not just unproven but a kind of ideological folk tale, one that quietly naturalizes the impersonal market by writing it into the origin of the species. That is a powerful reading, and this course finds it persuasive. But it is contested, and the best counter is worth stating fairly. Some economists reply that the barter story was never meant as *history* at all. It is a *logical fable* — a thought experiment about *why money is convenient*, not a claim about a real past. Read that way, "first there was barter" means "money solves a coordination problem you can see by imagining its absence," and the absence of a real barter village is no more damning than the absence of a real "state of nature" is damning to a philosopher who uses one to reason about government.

That counter has real force, and it should soften the strongest version of the critique without dissolving it. Two things can be true at once. Barter genuinely *does* occur — between strangers or enemies who lack a relationship to run a tally on, or in the wreckage of an economy where the money has collapsed and people improvise. And the textbook *does*, again and again, present the fable as if it were history — "long ago, people bartered" — and lets readers carry away a picture of the impersonal market as the human default. The defensible position, then, is not the slogan "barter never happened." It is more careful and more interesting: *barter is real but marginal; it was never the universal first stage the story needs; and the story's staying power comes less from evidence than from how natural it makes our own way of exchanging feel.* Hold the critique as powerful and the debate as open. That is not a hedge but the method — and a course that flattened a genuine dispute to win a point would be naturalizing its own reading.

Drawing on the idea that obligation predates coined money, argue for or against the claim that this feeling is itself a *naturalized* arrangement rather than a moral bedrock — and ground your answer in something concrete (a historical debt cancellation, a personal IOU between friends, a payday loan).
4. **Run the method.** Find another origin story told as if obvious — "the family has always been…", "trade between nations naturally…", "people have always wanted to own land." ## Recap

The most-taught origin story in economics — barter, then money to fix it, then markets — turns out to rest on a stage of history no one has located: the record shows credit and obligation running *ahead* of coined money, not behind it. We took the strong version of the story seriously, traced it to Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange," and kept the live debate open: the critique is powerful, but the story may be a logical fable rather than a false history, and barter is real if marginal. What the myth reliably does, fable or not, is naturalize — it installs the impersonal market exchanger at the dawn of time, making our own way of dealing with each other look like the human default, and it lends the cold authority of nature to the feeling that a debt simply *must* be paid. We have now seen, twice over, that capitalism's economic life differs in kind from what came before. Next the course turns from "compared to what?" to the harder question — *how was the new arrangement actually made?* — and the answer begins not with a clever invention but with a fence.
