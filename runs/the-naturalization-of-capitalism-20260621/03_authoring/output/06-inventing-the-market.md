## The hook

Two pictures of where the free market came from, and you have almost certainly been handed the wrong one.

In the first, the market is a garden that grew wild. Left alone by kings and councils and meddling officials, people just *traded* — the way water finds its level — and out of millions of small bargains a vast self-running system grew up on its own, the way a forest grows where no one planted it. The state, here, is the gardener who keeps wanting to prune and fence and intervene, and the wisdom of the free market lies in telling him to put the shears down. Market: spontaneous. State: intrusion.

In the second, the market is a machine someone built. Every part of it — that land can be bought, that a person's working hours can be rented by the hour, that money is a thing you can lend at interest rather than a token of who owes whom — every part was made, by specific people, through specific laws, often against fierce resistance, and is *maintained* by constant effort the way any machine is. The state, here, is not the gardener standing outside the garden. It is the engineer who built the engine and keeps it running, and who had to clear the ground first, by force, to lay the foundation.

This module is about why the second picture is the true one, and how completely the first has fooled us. Built, not grown. And the man who saw it most clearly wrote it down in the middle of the Second World War.

## Context & background

The man is **Karl Polanyi**, a Hungarian economic historian writing in the 1940s, whose one indispensable book, *The Great Transformation* (1944), is the keystone of everything that follows. A word of caution before we lean on him, because this course does not let its sources off easy. Polanyi is *not* a Marxist — he disagreed with Marx on plenty, and his quarrel was as much with socialist orthodoxy as with the free-market kind. Some of his specific economic history has dated since 1944. But his central diagnosis has held up better than almost anything written about capitalism in the last century, and it is the cleanest tool we have for taking apart the garden picture.

To use the tool you need one thing already in hand from earlier in the course. Back in module 04, we watched the commons get fenced — common land turned into private property, and a whole class of people turned, by enclosure and law, into the **"free" labourer**: "free" of feudal bonds and of any land to live on, and therefore compelled to sell the only thing left, their working time. Hold onto that, because Polanyi is going to tell you what happened *next*. The dispossession made the human material; this module is about the machine that material was fed into, and about the astonishing claim that someone had to *design* the machine and pass laws to keep it fed.

One more piece of vocabulary to set down, because the whole module turns on it. The thing Polanyi is anatomizing is the **self-regulating market** — the idea of a market that coordinates society on its own, needing no governance, running like a thermostat that no one has to touch. Not a marketplace, not trade, not a fair on a Tuesday. Those are ancient, and module 02 already made the distinction we need: *markets are ancient; a market society is not*. A self-regulating market is the far stranger thing — a whole society organized so that the market is the master institution, the one everything else has to bend around. Polanyi's claim, the one this module exists to deliver, is that such a thing has never once arisen on its own, and could not.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the foundation, because it is the part that sounds most like common sense and is in fact the founding fiction.

A market needs things to sell. An ordinary commodity is something produced in order to be sold — a chair, a loaf, a barrel of nails. The word does real work here: a **commodity** is something made *for the market*, and the genius of a market economy is that it can coordinate the production of millions of such things without anyone in charge, by the signal of price. So far the garden picture holds. If everything traded were a chair or a loaf, you could almost believe the system grew itself.

But a market society needs to buy and sell three things that are not commodities at all, because none of them was produced to be sold. These are Polanyi's **fictitious commodities**, and naming them is the hinge of his whole argument.

**Land** is the first. Land is just another word for nature — the earth, with its rivers and its soil and the weather over it. Nobody manufactured it for sale. It existed before there were markets and would exist if every market vanished tomorrow. Yet a market society must treat land as real estate: a thing with a price, bought, sold, mortgaged, and parcelled like crates of nails.

**Labour** is the second, and the strangest of all. "Labour" is just another word for human beings going about the activity of living — a person's hours, energy, attention, the use of their own body. No one is born in order to be hired. Your working time is not a product that rolled off a line; it is your life, the only one you get, sliced into rentable hours. Yet a market society must treat labour as a commodity with a wage-price, bought and sold like the loaf.

**Money** is the third. Money, in origin, is a token of trust and obligation — a way of keeping track of who owes what to whom, as module 03 found when it took apart the barter myth and discovered debt running underneath the coins. Nobody produces money the way a baker produces bread; a society conjures it into being. Yet a market society must treat money, too, as a commodity to be bought and sold and lent at a price called interest.

Here is the move you already have a name for. To treat land, labour, and money as ordinary goods made for sale is to take three things that are nothing of the kind — nature, human life, and social trust — and force them to *appear* as commodities, with prices, behaving like objects on a shelf. That is **reification**, the idea module 01 slipped into your back pocket: human relations, and even the living earth, hardening into things that seem to have a life of their own, as fixed as the weather. The labour market makes your own hours look like a substance with a market rate, set by forces as impersonal as the tides. Polanyi's "fictitious commodities" is reification with the receipts — the precise points where the market society pretends that what was never a commodity is one, and gets a whole civilization to believe it.

Call it fiction, though, and you might hear an accusation: that the whole thing is fake, a con to be swept away by anyone clear-eyed enough to see through it. That is not the claim, and the careful reader holds the line here. The fictions are *real in their effects* — wage labour, real estate, and the bond market are as solid as anything in your life; they organize the planet. Polanyi's word is exact: a fiction in the legal sense, a thing we agree to treat *as if* it were what it is not, because the treating-as-if does enormous work. The point is not that the market society is unreal. The point is that it rests on a decision, not on the nature of things — and a decision could have been, and was, made differently.

Which brings us to the engine, and to the single most important sentence in this course.

If land, labour, and money are not commodities by nature, then somebody has to *make* them into commodities and *force* them to behave like commodities — and somebody did. The fences of module 04 turned land into property and people into wage-seekers; that was step one, and it took acts of Parliament and, often, soldiers. But the harder work was keeping the machine running once it was built, and that work was done by the state, deliberately, at enormous scale, in the very decades that the free market was supposedly being *left alone*. Polanyi's name for the result is the one to carry out of this module:

**"Laissez-faire was planned."**

Sit with the paradox in it, because the paradox is the whole point. *Laissez-faire* is French for "let it be" — the doctrine that the market works best when government keeps its hands off. So "laissez-faire was planned" sounds like a contradiction: how can leaving-alone be planned? Polanyi's answer is that the "leaving alone" never happened. Creating a society in which the market runs the show took relentless, coordinated state action — writing the property law that lets land be sold, building the law of contract that makes a wage agreement enforceable, passing the enclosure acts that cleared the commons, and, most tellingly, *reforming the laws that had protected people from the market* so that they would have no choice but to enter it.

The English Poor Law reform of 1834 is Polanyi's sharpest example, so make it concrete. The old system had given the poor a floor — relief that meant a labourer did not absolutely have to take any wage on offer to avoid starvation. To make a true labour market, that floor had to go: only a person facing real hunger will reliably sell their hours at whatever price the market sets. So the law was rewritten, on purpose, by a powerful and centralized state, to make poverty frightening enough that no one could refuse the market. That is not the state stepping back from the market. That is the state stepping in, hard, to *manufacture* the market — using the kind of coercion module 04 called the **dull compulsion of economic relations**, where once the alternatives are gone, hunger does the work the whip used to, and it all looks like free choice.

So the garden picture has it exactly backwards. The "non-interfering" market was itself one of the most ambitious interventions any state has ever undertaken — it took a mountain of planning, legislation, and force to build a system that could then be described as self-running. Markets and states did not arrive as enemies, one natural and one intrusive. They arrived together, the second building the first. This is the course's single most powerful answer to the deepest piece of common sense it confronts — *markets are natural, the state is an intrusion on them.* No: the market was built, not grown, and the builder was the state.

(Polanyi has a companion idea, the **double movement** — the observation that as the disembedding market tears at the fabric of ordinary life, society spontaneously throws up protections against it, so that the "free market" and the regulations that resist it actually arrive as a pair. We will not develop it here; for this module, hold the simpler, sharper fact: the market itself was planned.)

## Primary source

Polanyi states the claim more plainly than any paraphrase, in a single sentence that has become the most-quoted line in the book:

> "Laissez-faire was planned; planning was not."
>
> — Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944)

The second half is the twist of the knife. The free market — the thing presented as spontaneous, as what happens when no one plans — was the planned thing, built by design. What was *not* planned, what arose spontaneously and from below, was society's reaction against it: the labour laws, the protections, the resistance, all the messy unscripted defending of ordinary life. The picture is inverted top to bottom. The order we call natural was engineered, and the disorder we call interference was the closest thing to a natural, unplanned response.

A note on integrity, in the spirit of this course. That sentence is genuine and famous, from Polanyi's chapter on the rise of the market economy; I am confident of the wording but I have not pinned it to a page number, and I will not invent one. Take the rest of Polanyi's case — the Poor Law, the fictitious commodities — as accurately rendered argument rather than direct quotation. When this course quotes, it quotes for real; when it paraphrases, it says so.

## Analysis & interpretation

Keep the seam between evidence and interpretation visible, because here it matters a great deal.

The **evidence** is hard and checkable: the English Poor Law was reformed in 1834 to cut the old relief; enclosure proceeded by thousands of specific acts of Parliament; property and contract law was built and extended across the same period; the modern state grew, it did not shrink, during the rise of "free" markets. These things demonstrably happened. Reasonable historians do not dispute the bare record.

The **interpretation** is Polanyi's framing of what the record means: that all of it adds up to the deliberate manufacture of a self-regulating market, and that "laissez-faire was planned" is the law underneath the facts. That is a powerful reading — this module argues it is the right one — but it is a reading, and it has serious critics worth taking on their strongest ground.

The sharpest objection is to Polanyi's idea of *disembedding* — his claim that the market society tore the economy loose from the social relations that had always held it (the kinship and obligation of module 02's embedded past) and set it floating free as an autonomous sphere. Critics answer: no economy ever floats free. Every economy, the market included, is **embedded** in *some* set of institutions — and the market society sits in a dense thicket of them: property and contract law, the courts, the central bank, the whole apparatus Polanyi himself describes. So "disembedding" is too strong. The market did not escape society into a state of nature; it was *re-embedded* in new institutions rather than freed from all of them.

Pressed honestly, that objection does not weaken Polanyi's main point — it sharpens it. If the market society is held up by an enormous structure of law, it is even *less* self-running than the garden picture claims, not more. The criticism cuts against the loose word "disembedding," not against "laissez-faire was planned." And it gives us a more precise way to say what naturalization is. The market society's institutions did not disappear; they became invisible — so woven into the floor of ordinary life, so much the unquestioned background, that we stopped seeing them as institutions at all and began experiencing them as nature. Naturalization, on this reading, is not the economy escaping into thin air. It is *re-embedding in institutions that have learned to hide themselves* — a machine so well built and so familiar that we mistake it for a garden.

1. **Pick a fiction and test it.** Choose one of the three fictitious commodities — land, labour, or money — and describe a real situation from your own world where treating it as an ordinary good produces something that feels strange once you look (a layoff described as "cutting costs," a forest priced for sale, a payday loan). Say what the commodity-fiction makes visible and what it hides.
2. **Steelman the garden.** State the strongest honest version of the view that the free market is a spontaneous order the state only disturbs — its best evidence and its real appeal. Then say exactly where Polanyi's record meets it, and where you think the disagreement actually lies.
3. **Weigh the criticism.** The critics say "disembedding" is too strong — every economy, the market included, is embedded in *some* institutions. Does that objection wound Polanyi's "laissez-faire was planned," leave it standing, or strengthen it? Argue your answer using the difference between *no institutions* and *institutions that hide themselves*.
4. **Run the lens.** Take the claim "you have to work for a living — that's just reality." Make it strange the way module 01's anthropologist would, then use this module to separate what is genuinely necessary in it from what was built by law and could have been built otherwise.

## Recap

You now hold the course's hardest blow against its central misconception. The self-regulating market — a whole society run by the market — is not a garden that grew wild but a machine someone built and maintains: **built, not grown**. Its foundation is three **fictitious commodities**, land, labour, and money, none of them made for sale, all of them forced to *appear* as goods on a shelf — reification with the receipts. And the machine was kept running by the state, not in spite of it: **"laissez-faire was planned,"** while the resistance to it was the unplanned thing. The deepest piece of common sense this course confronts — that markets are natural and the state is an intrusion — turns out to have the arrow pointing backwards. Next we follow the market into the lecture hall, where a young science took this freshly built order and rewrote it as a system of natural laws.
