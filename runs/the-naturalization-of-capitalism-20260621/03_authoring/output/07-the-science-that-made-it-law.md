## The hook

Open a newspaper to the business page and you will find the word doing the strangest work in modern English. "The economy slowed last quarter." "The economy needs confidence." "What's good for the economy." There it sits — a single thing with a temperature and a mood, something experts measure and politicians tend like a sick relative. Of course there is *the* economy. What else would you call it?

Hand that sentence to a literate person from the year 1700, though, and they would not know what you meant. They knew the word *economy*; they used it daily. It meant running a household well — managing your estate and stores without waste. "An economical man" was a careful one. What they could not have had was *the* economy: a single national object, separate from politics and family and morality, that goes up and down and that a country can be said to have. That thing is not much older than the photograph. And once you notice that the most ordinary word on the business page is a recent invention, a question opens under the whole page: what *else* that now reads as a permanent fact about the world was, not long ago, something somebody had to build?

## Context & background

This module is about the moment naturalization put on a lab coat. We have watched the order get *built* — the commons fenced, the worker made, land and labour and money turned into things-for-sale, the free market itself enforced into being by the state (Polanyi's point in module 06: *laissez-faire was planned* — the market was made and maintained, not left alone to grow). Now we watch a young discipline take that recent, made order and re-describe it as a system of natural laws. Same naturalization the course has tracked from the start — making a recent arrangement seem like an unchangeable fact of nature (module 01) — but performed this time in the most respectable register there is: the language of science.

Two names first, with their one-clause introductions, because this course never assumes you know who anyone is.

**Political economy** is the older name for what we now call economics, and the shift in the name is itself a clue. From the late eighteenth century into the nineteenth, the people studying markets, wages, and rent called their field *political* economy — and the word was honest. It announced that they studied wealth *and power together*: how a society's product gets divided among landlords, workers, and owners; whose interests a tax served; which class gained from a tariff. It studied a society of conflicting groups, out loud.

**Adam Smith** — an eighteenth-century Scottish moral philosopher, author of *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) — is the figure planted at the head of that tradition. We will be careful with him, because he has been turned into a cartoon, and the cartoon does work the real man would not have endorsed.

Keep one line from module 02 in your back pocket: *markets are ancient; a market society is not.* Buying and selling are old as cities. What was new — and what the new science took as its eternal subject — was a whole society organized around the market, where most people must sell their labour to live.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the cartoon, then meet the man, because the gap between them is the lesson.

The cartoon Smith is the prophet of selfishness: leave everyone to chase profit, an invisible hand turns private greed into public good, so government should get out of the way. That picture is carved over the door of a certain kind of economics. It is also a thin reading of a careful thinker. Smith wrote an entire earlier book, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), about *sympathy* — our capacity to feel with others — as the ground of moral life. He worried that merchants conspired against the public; he thought the division of labour could stupefy the worker stuck at one tiny task; he defended public education. The phrase he is hanged with, the **invisible hand**, appears exactly once in *The Wealth of Nations*, inside a limited argument, not as a cosmic law of everything. To make him the patron saint of "greed is good," you must crop the man down to a slogan — the same move module 05 caught in the polite origin story: keep the flattering fragment, lose the rest.

And the cropping *is* the naturalizing act. Take a moral philosopher's qualified, anxious, historically specific argument about a particular new commercial society, strip the qualifications and the *particular*, and what remains is a clean general law: markets, left alone, produce good outcomes by nature. That work was not done by Smith. It was done *to* him, by readers who needed a founder and a law.

Now the running image for this module. Think of a **map**. A good map is a marvel — it lets you see a city you could never see all at once, find a route, plan. But a map is always a *choice*: it leaves things out, flattens hills, picks what counts as a road. The danger comes when a map is so good, so widely used, that people forget it is a map and treat it as the territory — when they stop saying "the map shows no path here" and start saying "there is no path." A theory of the economy is a map. The naturalizing move, every time, is **the map that forgets it is a map**: the model mistaken for nature, the drawing passed off as the land.

Watch it with a second figure. **David Ricardo** — an early-nineteenth-century stockbroker turned political economist (*Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, 1817) — built tighter, more abstract models than Smith, and from them came what later got the chilling name the **iron law of wages**: the idea that wages are pushed, as if by a law of nature, down toward bare subsistence — just enough to keep workers alive and replaced, no more. Notice the word *iron*, and notice *law*. A pattern that held under particular nineteenth-century conditions — a growing propertyless population, no organized labour, no welfare floor — is restated as fixed as the freezing point of water. What was true of *this* arrangement, the one enclosure and the rest had made (module 04), gets reported as true of wages *as such*. The political effect is exact: if low wages are a law of nature, then raising them is like legislating against gravity — futile, even unnatural. The science has removed a human choice from the table by reclassifying it as weather. That is the first way the discipline naturalized the order: by promoting patterns specific to a built arrangement into natural laws. Smith's hand, Ricardo's iron — the vocabulary itself does the work.

There is a second way, later and subtler, and here the course owes you an honesty marker.

Around the 1870s, three thinkers working separately — William Stanley Jevons in England, Carl Menger in Austria, Léon Walras in Switzerland — remade the field's foundations. This is the **marginalist turn**: the late-1800s shift that recentered economics on individual preference and equilibrium. The older political economy had asked where value *comes from* — from labour, from land, divided among classes; by its name, it was about groups in conflict. The marginalists reframed value as something arising in the mind of an individual chooser at the margin: how much is *one more* cup of coffee worth to *you*, given what you already have? Value became subjective preference, and the economy a system of such choosers settling into balance — an *equilibrium* — much as a physical system settles. They reached, on purpose, for the prestige and mathematics of physics.

Now the marker. There is a widely held critical reading — the one this course works within — that the marginalist turn *depoliticized* economics: that by making the field about timeless individual preference, it dissolved the older vocabulary of class, power, and history, so capitalism's *specific* institutions vanished into "the economy as such," a neutral machine of rational agents studied like a natural system. That reading is powerful and fits everything else this course shows. But mark it as **interpretation, not settled fact.** The historical *event* is solid: the turn happened, roughly when and how described. The claim about its *meaning* — that it naturalized and depoliticized — is a critical reading, contested by economists who see ordinary scientific progress toward better models. Keep the seam visible, as module 01 insisted: *the turn happened* is evidence; *the turn naturalized the order* is a reading you may weigh.

What is not in dispute is the direction of travel. A field that began as the open study of *wealth and power together* — *political* economy — became, in name and self-image, just *economics*: a science of allocation by rational individuals, modeled on physics, with the *political* shaved off the front of the word. The map grew more elegant and forgot harder that it was a map.

And now back to the word we started with, the most teachable proof of the whole module — the one a beginner can hold in one hand.

Before roughly 1800, *economy* meant household or estate management — the Greek *oikonomia*, the running of the *oikos*, the house — or, by extension, thrift. There was no *the* economy: no single national object, distinct from government and society, that a country has and that rises and falls. That object had to be assembled, and we can roughly date the assembly. It was consolidated in the twentieth century, especially the 1930s and 1940s, when economists and governments built the **national accounts** — the statistical machinery that produces a single number for a nation's output, the ancestor of what we now call GDP. (The historian **Timothy Mitchell**, in *Rule of Experts*, 2002, tells this story: "the economy," as a thing experts measure and manage, is a twentieth-century arrival.) Only once you can *measure* the economy as one object can you speak of it the way the business page does — as a single entity with a health, one thing to be grown or saved.

Sit with that. The most ordinary noun in public life — the thing that gets governments elected and unelected, whose "confidence" must be kept up — is younger than the telephone. It did not exist, as an object of thought, for most of the people whose descendants now treat it as the permanent backdrop of life. That is naturalization caught in a single word: a recently built, deliberately measured thing, experienced as a fact of nature you live inside and never chose. Not because anyone lied — because the map got so good we forgot we drew it.

## Primary source

Here is the most famous fragment in the founding text of the tradition — the one usually quoted to prove markets work by nature. Read it as module 01 taught: as a specimen, not a slogan.

> "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker, that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."
>
> — Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776)

This wording is genuine and very well known; I am confident of it. What I have *not* done is pin it to a page number, because I will not invent one — and you should distrust anyone who supplies a precise citation they cannot check.

Notice first what is *true* and shrewd here: Smith observes that strangers who feel no special love for each other can still, through exchange, feed one another. That is a real insight about commercial life, and no fool's. Now notice what happens when the sentence is lifted out and made to stand as a *law of human nature* — when the butcher and baker become Economic Man, the eternal self-interested chooser, and self-interest becomes the master motive behind all economic life everywhere. That is the leap module 01 warned about and module 08 will take apart: from "people in *this* arrangement coordinate through self-interested exchange" to "self-interest is what humans economically *are*." The first is an observation about a shop in 1776. The second is the map forgetting it is a map. The sentence does not commit that error; the use it is put to does.

## Analysis & interpretation

Hold the seam between evidence and interpretation up to the light, because this is a delicate case: a *true* science doing *naturalizing* work, with no liar in the room.

The **evidence** is firm and checkable. Smith wrote that line in 1776, and also wrote *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* in 1759. Ricardo built abstract wage models in 1817, later labelled the "iron law of wages." Jevons, Menger, and Walras independently recentered economics on individual preference around the 1870s. The field's name shifted from "political economy" to "economics." "*The* economy," as a single measured object, was consolidated in the twentieth century with national accounting. None of that is in serious dispute.

The **interpretation** is the reading this course argues: that across this history a recent, contingent order was progressively re-described as a system of natural laws — that the vocabulary of *law*, *nature*, *equilibrium*, and a measurable "*the* economy" performed naturalization in the prestigious language of theory. That reading is strong, and the word-"economy" evidence supports it about as cleanly as history ever supports a reading. But it *is* a reading, and honesty means naming its rival: a defender of mainstream economics would say this was a science maturing — finding more general models, cleaner abstractions, exactly as physics did, with nothing sinister in it.

What would help you weigh the two? Largely the history this course keeps putting on the table — whether the order the science described was in fact built, recently, and could have been built differently (modules 04 through 06), or whether it is the natural state a science was right to take as given. That is why the lens came first: so "the economy is a natural system the science merely describes" reads not as a closing fact but as a claim with a history you can interrogate. Smith was a better thinker than his cartoon; the marginalists did real work; the discipline is no conspiracy. And a recent, made order still got dressed, in the most respectable clothes available, as a law of nature. Keeping both true — refusing both the sneer and the worship — is the discipline of this course.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below describes what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

1. **Read the specimen.** Take Smith's butcher-and-baker line and separate the genuine *observation about commercial life* it contains from the *law of human nature* it gets enlisted to prove. Where exactly is the seam, and what work does crossing it do?
2. **Hunt a forgotten map.** Find one sentence from today's news or politics that treats *the economy* as a single natural thing with needs or moods ("the economy can't afford it," "what the markets want," "you can't buck the trend"). Make it strange, the way module 01's anthropologist would, and say what the phrasing hides about the choices and people underneath the word.
3. **Steelman the science.** State the strongest honest version of the rival reading — that the abstraction of modern economics is just a science maturing, like physics, and carries no special naturalizing freight. Then say what historical evidence would have to be true for *your* eventual judgment, for or against, to hold.
4. **Don't strawman the founder.** Knowing that Smith also wrote a whole book on sympathy and worried about merchants and dull labour, argue whether it is fair to call him the father of "the market is natural." Who did the naturalizing — the man, or his readers — and how would you tell?

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Stakes a clear, arguable claim — not a summary of this module, and not a verdict on whether capitalism is good or bad.
- Works from the **specific** words (Smith's line, a real news sentence), reading the actual language rather than gesturing at "economics" in the abstract.
- Keeps **evidence visibly separate from interpretation** — distinguishes what demonstrably happened (the dates, the texts, the shift in the word) from what the writer claims it *means*.
- Steelmans the rival reading honestly — gives the "science just matured" case its strongest form before answering it — and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.

## Recap

We watched naturalization put on a lab coat. A young discipline took an order that had just been *built* — the dispossession, the fictitious commodities, the planned "free" market of the modules before this — and re-described it as a system of natural laws: Smith's invisible hand cropped from a careful philosopher into a slogan, Ricardo's wage pattern hardened into an *iron law*, and, after the 1870s, the whole field recentered on timeless individual preference and renamed from *political* economy to plain economics (that the turn *depoliticized* the science is a strong reading, but a reading — keep the seam visible). The cleanest proof needed no theory at all: *the economy* itself, the most ordinary noun on the business page, is a twentieth-century invention — before 1800 the word meant running a household, and "*the* economy" as one measurable national thing was assembled only with national accounts in the 1930s and 40s. The map grew so good we forgot we had drawn it. Next the course turns from the order on paper to the order in the body — how clocks, bells, and a particular picture of the self remade the worker, and the would-be reader, from the inside.
