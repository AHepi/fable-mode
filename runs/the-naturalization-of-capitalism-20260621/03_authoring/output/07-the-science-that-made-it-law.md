## The hook

Open a newspaper to the business page and you will find the word doing the strangest work in modern English. "The economy slowed last quarter." "The economy needs confidence." "What's good for the economy." There it sits, a single thing with a temperature and a mood, something experts measure and politicians tend like a sick relative. Of course there is *the* economy — what else would you call it?

But hand that sentence to a literate person from the year 1700 and they would not know what you meant. They knew the word *economy*; they used it daily. It meant running a household well — managing your estate, your servants, your stores, without waste. "An economical man" was a careful one. What they did not have, could not have had, was *the* economy: a single national object, separate from politics and family and morality, that goes up and down and that a country can be said to have. That thing is not much older than the photograph. And once you notice that the most ordinary word on the business page is a recent invention, a question opens under the whole page: what *else* that now reads as a permanent fact about the world was, not so long ago, a thing somebody had to build?

## Context & background

This module is about the moment naturalization put on a lab coat. We have seen the order get *built* — the commons fenced, the worker made, land and labour and money turned into things-for-sale, the free market itself enforced into being by the state (that was Polanyi's point in module 06: *laissez-faire was planned* — the market was made and maintained, not left alone to grow). Now we watch a young discipline take that recently built order and re-describe it as a system of natural laws. Same naturalization the course has tracked from the start — making a made, recent arrangement seem like an unchangeable fact of nature (module 01) — but performed this time in the most respectable register there is: the language of science.

Two names first, with their one-clause introductions, because this course never assumes you know who anyone is.

**Political economy** is simply the older name for what we now call economics. The shift in the name is itself a clue, so hold onto it. From roughly the late eighteenth into the nineteenth century, the people studying markets, trade, wages, and rent called their field *political economy* — and the word *political* was honest. It announced that they were studying wealth *and power together*: how a society's product gets divided between landlords, workers, and owners; whose interests a tax served; which class gained from a tariff. It studied a society of conflicting groups, out loud.

**Adam Smith** — an eighteenth-century Scottish moral philosopher, author of *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) — is the figure usually planted at the head of the whole tradition. We will be careful with him, because he has been turned into a cartoon, and the cartoon does work that the real man would not have endorsed.

Keep one distinction from module 02 in your back pocket, because the whole argument leans on it: *markets are ancient; a market society is not.* Buying and selling are old as cities. What was new — and what the new science took as its eternal subject — was a whole society organized around the market, where most people must sell their labour to live.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the cartoon, then meet the man, because the gap between them is itself a lesson in how naturalization works.

The cartoon Smith is the prophet of selfishness: leave everyone alone to chase profit, and an invisible hand turns private greed into public good, so government should get out of the way and let the market do what markets naturally do. That picture is carved over the door of a certain kind of economics. It is also a thin reading of a careful thinker. Smith wrote an entire earlier book, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), about *sympathy* — our capacity to feel with others — as the ground of moral life. He worried about merchants conspiring against the public; he thought the division of labour could stupefy the worker who spent his life at one tiny task; he defended public education and was no enemy of all government. The phrase he is hanged with, the **invisible hand**, appears exactly once in *The Wealth of Nations*, in a limited argument, not as a cosmic law of everything. To make him the patron saint of "greed is good" you have to crop the man down to a slogan — and you will notice that this is the same move module 05 caught in the polite origin story: keep the flattering fragment, lose the rest.

So why does the cartoon matter for *us*? Because the cropping is the naturalizing act. Take a moral philosopher's qualified, anxious, historically specific argument about a particular new commercial society, strip the qualifications and the anxiety and the *particular*, and what remains is a clean general law: markets, left alone, produce good outcomes by nature. The work of naturalization here was not done by Smith. It was done *to* him, by readers who needed a founder and a law.

Now the running image for this module, the one to carry through. Think of a **map**. A good map is a marvel — it lets you see a city you could never see all at once, find a route, plan. But a map is always a *choice*: it leaves things out, flattens hills, picks what counts as a road. The danger comes when a map is so good, so widely used, that people forget it is a map and start treating it as the territory itself — when they stop saying "the map shows no path here" and start saying "there is no path here." A theory of the economy is a map. The naturalizing move, every time, is **the map that forgets it is a map**: the model mistaken for nature, the drawing passed off as the land.

Watch it happen with a second figure. **David Ricardo** — an early-nineteenth-century stockbroker turned political economist (*Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, 1817) — built tighter, more abstract models than Smith, and from them drew what later got the chilling name the **iron law of wages**: the idea that wages are pushed, as if by a law of nature, down toward bare subsistence — just enough to keep workers alive and replaced, no more. Notice the word *iron*, and notice *law*. A pattern that held under particular nineteenth-century conditions — a growing propertyless population, no organized labour, no welfare floor — is restated as a law of nature, as fixed as the freezing point of water. The map has forgotten it is a map. What was true of *this* arrangement, made by enclosure and the rest (module 04), is reported as true of wages *as such*. And the political effect is exact: if low wages are a law of nature, then raising them is like legislating against gravity — futile, even unnatural. The science has quietly removed a human choice from the table by reclassifying it as weather.

That is the first way the discipline naturalized the order: by taking patterns specific to a built arrangement and promoting them to natural laws. Smith's hand, Ricardo's iron — *law*, *nature*, *invisible*: the vocabulary itself does the work, lifting a recent, made, contestable order up among the permanent things.

There is a second way, later and subtler, and here the course owes you an honesty marker before we start.

Around the 1870s, three thinkers working separately — William Stanley Jevons in England, Carl Menger in Austria, Léon Walras in Switzerland — remade the foundations of the field. This is called the **marginalist turn**: the late-1800s shift that recentered economics on individual preference and equilibrium. The older political economy had asked where value *comes from* — from labour, from land, divided among classes; it was, by its own name, about groups in conflict. The marginalists reframed value as something that arises in the mind of an individual chooser at the margin: how much is *one more* cup of coffee worth to *you*, right now, given what you already have? Value became subjective preference, and the economy became a system of such choosers settling into balance — an *equilibrium* — much as a physical system settles. They reached, deliberately, for the prestige and the mathematics of physics.

Here is the honesty marker. There is a widely held critical reading — the reading this course works within — that says the marginalist turn *depoliticized* economics: that by making the field about timeless individual preference and equilibrium, it quietly dissolved the older vocabulary of class, power, and history, so that capitalism's *specific* institutions vanished into "the economy as such," a neutral machine of rational agents that could be studied like a natural system. That reading is powerful, and it fits everything else this course shows. But mark it as **interpretation, not settled fact.** The historical *event* is solid: the turn happened, roughly when and how described. The claim about its *meaning and effect* — that it served to naturalize and depoliticize — is a critical interpretation, contested by economists who see the turn as ordinary scientific progress toward better models. Keep the seam visible, the way module 01 insisted: *the turn happened* is evidence; *the turn naturalized the order* is a reading you are allowed to weigh.

What is not in dispute is the direction of travel. Across a century, a field that began as the open study of *wealth and power together* — *political* economy — became, in name and in self-image, just *economics*: a science of allocation by rational individuals, modeled on physics, with the *political* sanded off the front of the word. The map grew more elegant and forgot harder that it was a map.

And now back to the word we started with, because it is the most teachable proof of the whole module, the one a beginner can hold in one hand.

Before roughly 1800, *economy* meant, as we saw, household or estate management — the Greek *oikonomia*, the running of the *oikos*, the house — or, by extension, thrift. There was no *the* economy: no single national object, distinct from government and society, that a country has and that rises and falls. That object had to be assembled, and we can roughly date the assembly. It was consolidated in the twentieth century, especially the 1930s and 1940s, when economists and governments built the **national accounts** — the statistical machinery that produces a single number for a nation's output, the ancestor of what we now call GDP. (The historian **Timothy Mitchell** tells this story in *Rule of Experts*, 2002: "the economy," as a thing experts measure and manage, is a twentieth-century arrival.) Only once you can *measure* the economy as one object can you speak of it the way the business page does — as a single entity with a health, a single thing to be grown or saved.

Sit with that. The most ordinary noun in public life, the thing that gets governments elected and unelected, the entity whose "confidence" must be kept up — *the* economy — is younger than the telephone. It did not exist, as an object of thought, for most of the people whose descendants now treat it as the permanent backdrop of life. That is naturalization caught in a single word: a recently built, deliberately measured thing, experienced as a fact of nature you live inside and never chose. Not because anyone lied. Because the map got so good we forgot we drew it.

## Primary source

Here is the line itself — the most famous fragment in the founding text of the tradition, the one usually quoted to prove that markets work by nature. Read it the way module 01 taught: as a specimen, not a slogan.

> "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker, that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."
>
> — Adam Smith, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776)

This is genuine and very well known; I am confident of the wording. What I have *not* done is pin it to a page number, because I will not invent one — and you should be suspicious of anyone who supplies a precise citation they cannot actually check.

Now read it closely, because the closeness is the point. Notice first what is *true* and shrewd in it: Smith is making a real observation about a particular kind of society, one where strangers who feel no special love for each other can still, through exchange, feed one another. That is a genuine insight about commercial life, and it is not foolish. But notice, second, what happens when this sentence is lifted out and made to stand as a *law of human nature* — when the butcher and the baker become Economic Man, the eternal self-interested chooser, and self-interest becomes the master motive behind all economic life everywhere. That is the leap module 01 warned about and module 08 will take apart: from "people in *this* arrangement coordinate through self-interested exchange" to "self-interest is simply what humans economically *are*." The first is an observation about the butcher's shop in 1776. The second is the map, forgetting it is a map — a true thing about one society's grocers promoted to a fixed fact about the species. The sentence does not commit that error. The use it is put to does.

## Analysis & interpretation

Hold the seam between evidence and interpretation right up to the light, because this module asks you to handle a delicate case: a *true* science doing *naturalizing* work, with no liar in the room.

The **evidence** is firm and checkable. Smith wrote that line in 1776. He also wrote *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, about sympathy, in 1759. Ricardo built abstract wage models in 1817 and they were later labelled the "iron law of wages." Jevons, Menger, and Walras independently recentered economics on individual preference around the 1870s. The field's name shifted from "political economy" to "economics." "*The* economy," as a single measured object, was consolidated in the twentieth century with national accounting. None of that is in serious dispute.

The **interpretation** is the reading this course argues: that across this history a recently built, contingent order was progressively re-described as a system of natural laws — that the vocabulary of *law*, *nature*, *equilibrium*, and a measurable "*the* economy" performed naturalization in the prestigious language of theory, removing human arrangements from the table by reclassifying them as nature. That is a strong reading, and the word-"economy" evidence supports it about as cleanly as historical evidence ever supports a reading. But it *is* a reading, and intellectual honesty means naming its rival. A defender of mainstream economics would say: this was simply a science maturing — finding more general models, better mathematics, cleaner abstractions — exactly as physics did, and there is nothing sinister or specially ideological in a field discovering that individual choice and equilibrium are powerful tools. On this rival reading, the abstraction is not a forgetting that the map is a map; it is what every successful science does, and the marginalist turn deserves applause, not suspicion.

What would help you weigh the two? Largely the history this course keeps putting on the table — whether the order the science described was in fact *built*, recently, and could have been built differently (modules 04 through 06), or whether it is the natural state a science was right to take as given. That is precisely why the lens came first: so that "the economy is a natural system the science merely describes" reads not as a closing fact but as a claim with a history you can interrogate. Smith was a better thinker than his cartoon; the marginalists were doing real work; the discipline is not a conspiracy. And a recently made order still got dressed, in the most respectable clothes available, as a law of nature. Both things are true, and keeping them both true — refusing both the sneer and the worship — is the whole discipline of this course.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below describes what a strong response does, whichever way you land.

1. **Read the specimen.** Take Smith's butcher-and-baker line and do what the analysis section started: in your own words, separate the genuine *observation about commercial life* it contains from the *law of human nature* it gets enlisted to prove. Where exactly is the seam between the two, and what work does crossing it do?
2. **Hunt a forgotten map.** Find one sentence from today's news or politics that treats *the economy* as a single natural thing with needs or moods ("the economy can't afford it," "what the markets want," "you can't buck the trend"). Make it strange, the way module 01's anthropologist would, and say what the phrasing hides about the choices and people underneath the word.
3. **Steelman the science.** State the strongest honest version of the rival reading: that the abstraction of modern economics is just a science maturing, like physics, and carries no special naturalizing freight. Then say what historical evidence would have to be true for *your* eventual judgment — for or against — to hold.
4. **Don't strawman the founder.** Knowing that Smith also wrote a whole book on sympathy and worried about merchants and dull labour, argue whether it is fair to call him the father of "the market is natural." Who did the naturalizing — the man, or his readers — and how would you tell?

## Recap

We watched naturalization put on a lab coat. A young discipline took an order that had just been *built* — the dispossession, the fictitious commodities, the planned "free" market of the modules before this — and re-described it as a system of natural laws: Smith's invisible hand cropped from a careful philosopher into a slogan, Ricardo's wage pattern hardened into an *iron law*, and, after the 1870s, the whole field recentered on timeless individual preference and renamed from *political* economy to plain economics (that the turn *depoliticized* the science is a strong reading, but a reading — keep the seam visible). The cleanest proof needed no theory at all: *the economy* itself, the most ordinary noun on the business page, is a twentieth-century invention — before 1800 the word meant running a household, and "*the* economy" as one measurable national thing was assembled only with national accounts in the 1930s and 40s. The map grew so good we forgot we had drawn it. Next the course turns from the order on paper to the order in the body — how clocks, bells, and a particular picture of the self remade the worker, and the would-be reader, from the inside.
