## The hook

A society can be governed from inside a room so well built that the people in it never notice it has walls. The light is even, the temperature comfortable, the floor steady. Nothing presses on you. And because nothing presses, it does not occur to you that you are anywhere in particular at all — that this is a room, with an architect and a date of construction and a door — rather than the world itself, the open air, the only place there is. The most complete form of enclosure is the kind you cannot see, because to see a wall you have to imagine the space on its other side, and that is precisely the imagining the room is built to prevent.

That room has a name now, and a slogan painted over the (invisible) door: *there is no alternative.* This module is about how the room got built — recently, deliberately, by people whose names and meeting dates we know — and then about how to find the walls. We are at the end of the course. The whole point of the lens was to bring us here.

## Context & background

Back at the start we noticed a way of talking — "the market demands it," "you can't fight economics," "it's only common sense" — in which a made arrangement is spoken of as if it were weather. We called that move **naturalization**: making a recent, contingent arrangement look like an unchangeable fact of nature (a *contingent* arrangement being one that happened this way but could have gone otherwise — module 01). Every module since has been a worked example: the worker was *made* by enclosure (module 04), the "free market" was *built and enforced* by the state rather than left alone — Polanyi's "laissez-faire was planned" (module 06) — the science of economics *recast* a recent order as natural law (module 07), and the whole thing comes to *feel* natural through the labour vanishing behind a price, consent won as common sense, and a self trained to police itself (module 10).

All of that explains how capitalism came to seem normal. This module is about the strongest version of the claim, the one that goes past *normal* to *necessary*: not "this is how we live" but "there is no other way to live, and there never could be." That feeling has a name, given by **Mark Fisher**, a British writer and critic, in a short, sharp book called *Capitalist Realism* (2009): **capitalist realism** — the felt sense that no alternative to capitalism is even imaginable. Not that the alternatives are bad. That there are none to picture. Fisher popularized a line that captures it exactly (he credited it to others, the critics Fredric Jameson and Slavoj Žižek): it has become easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism. Asteroids, plagues, the collapse of the climate — the imagination supplies these freely. A different way of organizing work and ownership it cannot supply at all.

You might take that as the final proof that the thing really is natural. If nobody can even *picture* an outside, surely there isn't one. Hold that thought, in its strongest form, because it is the deepest version of the intuition this whole course has been answering — and turn to where the room came from.

## Narrative / exposition

Start with the honest steelman, because a lens that only works on opponents is a toy. The case for capitalist realism is not stupid. Every alternative actually tried at scale in the twentieth century — the centrally planned economies above all — produced shortages, repression, or both, and many produced corpses. The reader who says "I've seen the alternatives and they were worse" is not being naive; they are citing real history. Grant it fully. The course does not pretend that record away, and it owes you no rosy substitute for it.

But notice what the steelman establishes and what it does not. It establishes that *particular* twentieth-century alternatives failed. It does not establish that the present arrangement is the natural condition of the species, the thing humans default to when left alone. And here the history bites, because the part of our common sense that feels *most* like untouched nature — the sheer obviousness of markets, the unthinkability of anything else — turns out to be the part with the clearest construction record. We can name the architects. We have the minutes of the meetings.

In April 1947, a group of economists and intellectuals gathered at a hotel on Mont Pèlerin, above Lake Geneva, at the invitation of the economist Friedrich Hayek. They founded a society — the **Mont Pèlerin Society** — with a deliberate, long-range purpose: to rebuild the intellectual case for the market at a moment when it looked finished. This was the high tide of the planned economy and the welfare state; the market order they cared about seemed to most observers like yesterday's idea. They did not regard their view as the natural common sense of mankind. They regarded it, correctly, as a *minority* view that had lost, and that would have to be *rebuilt* — through scholarship, funding, think tanks, journalists, and decades of patient work — until it became, again, the water people swam in. This is the project later historians named **neoliberalism**: the twentieth-century organized effort to remake institutions and common sense around markets.

Sit with the strangeness of that, the way our anthropologist from module 01 would. The conviction that markets are just natural, that any alternative is unserious — the conviction that feels like *no one taught it to me, I just know it* — was, only decades ago, a thing a small group set out to manufacture *because it did not yet exist.* You cannot organize a decades-long campaign to make people believe what they already spontaneously believe. The campaign is the confession: the "natural" had to be built.

Two scholars give this its sharpest form, and it is worth keeping their framing visibly *as* framing — a reading, not a fact dropped from the sky. **Philip Mirowski** and **Quinn Slobodian**, present-day historians of neoliberalism, both insist the project was not a return to leaving-things-alone. Mirowski documents the organized "thought collective" — the network of institutions built to move ideas from seminar rooms into ministries (he is polemical in tone; take the documented history, weigh the framing). Slobodian, in *Globalists* (2018), supplies the precise image. Neoliberalism, he argues, was never about *freeing* markets from rules. It was about **encasing** them — wrapping the market in law and in supranational institutions placed deliberately *beyond the reach of democratic majorities*, so that whatever a population might later vote for, the core of the market order could not be touched. Not the market left alone. The market walled off from the people, on purpose, by design.

That is the room. The walls are real, made of treaty and statute and institution; and they are built specifically so that the space outside them — the space where a population decides differently — drops out of the picture. Notice this is the exact same denaturalizing move we met two centuries earlier and a world away. In module 06 the lesson was that the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century "free market" did not grow wild; it was constructed and enforced by deliberate state action — "laissez-faire was planned." Neoliberalism is that same sentence spoken again in the twentieth century, with better lawyers. *The most "natural" thing was the most carefully made.* When you hear that an arrangement is just the natural order reasserting itself after meddling, that is exactly the claim the historical record tends to invert.

And then the slogan, which is where naturalization finally arrives at its destination. In Britain in the late 1970s and 80s, the Prime Minister **Margaret Thatcher** carried a phrase she used so often it acquired an acronym: *there is no alternative* — TINA. The phrase is the endpoint of everything this course has traced. It does not argue. It does not say *this is the best alternative, here is why.* It says there is *no* alternative — that the question of how to live is already closed, that to debate it is to misunderstand that it is not a debate. A claim about how a society ought to be organized arrives dressed, one last time, as a claim about what unalterably *is* — the costume of naturalization we learned to recognize in module 01, now worn by a head of government and treated as plain sense.

That is the achievement to see clearly. "There is no alternative" is not the discovery that no alternative exists. It is the **achievement** of a state in which no alternative can be *thought* — and an achievement is, by definition, something that was accomplished, by someone, against resistance, and could have failed. The unthinkability is not the bedrock under the room. It is the room.

## Primary source

The cleanest specimen is the slogan itself, in the form Thatcher made famous, defending her economic programme against the idea that some other course was available:

> "There is no alternative."
>
> — Margaret Thatcher, a phrase used repeatedly through her premiership (UK, 1979–1990), so insistently that it became known by the acronym TINA.

Four words. Read them as our anthropologist would — not as a position to cheer or boo, but as a specimen of a move. The sentence performs the whole thesis of the course in miniature. It takes the most contestable question a society can ask — how shall we arrange work, ownership, and care for one another? — and answers it not with an argument but with a closure: there is nothing here to decide. A genuinely open question is presented as a settled fact, the way you might correct someone about the boiling point of water.

And mark the seam between evidence and interpretation, the discipline this course has held the whole way. That Thatcher used the phrase, and that it stuck, is **evidence** — checkable, uncontested. That the phrase *naturalizes* — that it converts a political choice into a fact of nature and so forecloses the very debate it pretends to have already won — is **interpretation**. It is this course's reading, and a strong one, but it is a reading, and you are entitled to weigh it. Someone could hear "there is no alternative" as sober realism rather than ideology. The honest test between the two readings is the one the last ten modules have been laying on the table: was this order built, recently, by identifiable hands, such that it could have been built otherwise? If it was — and the construction record from enclosure to Mont Pèlerin says it was — then "no alternative" is a wall, not a horizon.

## Check yourself

Before reading on, in your own words: what is the difference between saying *no alternative exists* and saying *no alternative can be imagined* — and which one is "there is no alternative" actually reporting?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

*No alternative exists* is a claim about the world — that the present order is the only one that could function. *No alternative can be imagined* is a claim about us — about the limits of what people are able to picture. "There is no alternative," and the wider condition Fisher called capitalist realism, is really reporting the second: a state in which the imagination comes up empty. The course's point is that an empty imagination is an *achievement* of naturalization — something produced, recently and partly on purpose — not proof that the world contains only one possibility. The walls being unseeable is not evidence there's nothing outside them.

## Analysis & interpretation

Here the reader has every right to push back, and the push-back deserves a straight answer rather than a dodge. *Fine — you've shown the room was built. So what's the alternative? Knock it down and put what in its place?*

The honest answer is that this course does not have one, and that this is not an evasion but a position. Denaturalizing is not the same as prescribing. The lens gives you **eyes, not a blueprint**. Everything in these eleven modules works to recover the sentence *it didn't have to be this way* — to show, scene by scene, that a thing which feels like the floor under your feet was laid by human hands and could have been laid differently. That is a claim about contingency. It is not, and was never, a claim that some particular other arrangement is ready to be installed, or that it would be better. A worked-out replacement economy is a different and enormous project, out of this course's scope, and you should be suspicious of anyone who hands you one in a closing chapter.

But notice — this is the sharp part — that *the demand for a ready-made alternative is itself part of how the foreclosure works.* "You have nothing to put in its place, so be quiet" is the room defending itself. It sets a test no honest critic of any deep arrangement could pass on the spot: nobody who first doubted the divine right of kings, or the naturalness of a world where women could not own property, had a finished replacement constitution in their pocket. They had a prior, smaller, indispensable thing — the recovered ability to see the arrangement *as* an arrangement, and therefore as a fit subject for human decision rather than a fact to be endured. Requiring the blueprint before you will permit the doubt is requiring the cure before you will allow the diagnosis. Capitalist realism's deepest trick is to make the absence of a blueprint feel like proof that the walls are load-bearing.

So keep the two things apart, the way this course has kept evidence apart from interpretation throughout. The **evidence** is that this order was constructed, recently, by nameable people and institutions. The **interpretation** — strong, arguable, yours to weigh — is that its air of necessity is therefore manufactured rather than found. Neither of those is a prediction. Hold the line one last time against the trap this course has refused from the first page: just as capitalism was not *fated* to arrive, nothing here says it is fated to end. Contingency runs in both directions. The room was built; that it was built does not tell you it will fall, or stand. It tells you only that it is a building, and that buildings are the kind of thing people make decisions about. What you do with that is not this course's to dictate.

Which leaves the one thing the course *can* hand you, and has been handing you for ten modules without ever naming it: the method.

You already own it. Gather the moves you have watched work, again and again, and they make a small, portable toolkit — four questions to put to anything that presents itself as obviously, naturally, beyond-argument the case:

- **Historicize.** *When did this begin, and what came before it?* Almost nothing that claims to be eternal survives the question honestly asked. The factory clock had a first day (module 08); "*the* economy" as a single measurable thing is barely a century old (module 07); the unthinkability of alternatives was being manufactured in a Swiss hotel in 1947.
- **Compare.** *Do all human societies do this, or only ours?* If a single society can be found that arranged things otherwise — exchange that built bonds rather than clearing a price (module 02), debt that long preceded coined money (module 03) — then what looked like human nature is revealed as one local form of it.
- **Make-strange.** *What would this look like to someone who had never seen it?* The anthropologist's move from module 01: watch a job interview, a mortgage, a price tag as an alien would, and the habit that sanded off the strangeness lifts.
- **Ask who benefits.** The habit we started in module 01 and can now name properly — ***cui bono***, "to whose good?" Not as an accusation; as a probe. An arrangement that took one shape when it might have taken several usually took the shape it did because that shape suited someone. Ask, against any "natural" order, *who profits from its seeming inevitable* — and the answer often shows you where the walls are.

None of these is new. That is the point. The method is just the lens of module 01, wielded — and you have been wielding it the whole way down.

## Check yourself

Apply the toolkit yourself before reading on. Take one claim you have heard treated as plain common sense about how the economy *must* work — "wages just reflect what you're worth," say, or "you have to compete or you'll be left behind." Run two of the four questions on it (historicize, compare, make-strange, who benefits). What does each reveal that the claim's air of obviousness was hiding?

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

There is no single right answer; a strong one *runs the questions on the specific claim* rather than restating the toolkit. For "wages reflect what you're worth," **historicize** asks when pay came to be seen as a measure of personal worth at all (rather than of custom, status, or what one could be compelled to accept — module 08), and notices that the link is recent and arranged. **Who benefits** asks whom it serves to have low pay read as a verdict on the low-paid rather than on the arrangement that sets it — and finds the answer points away from the worker. **Compare** asks whether every society has tied sustenance to the sale of one's hours (module 02 says no); **make-strange** describes the claim as an alien would — *a person's value to others is announced by a number set by the person buying their time* — and the obviousness curdles into something contestable. The mark of a strong answer is that the air of "that's just how it is" thins once the questions are actually asked.

## Discussion & writing prompts

No single right answer. These are for thinking and writing; the rubric below is what a strong response does, whichever way you come down.

1. **Run the full method (the course in one exercise).** Pick one "natural" claim about the economy that you yourself have believed, or still believe. Run *all four* tools on it — historicize, compare, make-strange, ask who benefits — in turn. Then write the honest verdict: did denaturalizing it show it to be contingent, or did the claim survive the test? (A claim that survives is a fair result; the method is not rigged to debunk.)
2. **Steelman capitalist realism, then locate the leap.** State the strongest honest version of "there is no alternative — we've tried the others and they were worse." Then identify the exact point where it slides from *these particular alternatives failed* to *the present order is the natural and only possible one* — and say what evidence would be needed to defend that second step.
3. **The blueprint demand.** This module claims that demanding a ready-made replacement before permitting any doubt is itself part of how foreclosure works. Argue for or against that claim. Is there a version of "what's your alternative?" that is a fair challenge rather than a wall — and how would you tell the two apart?
4. **Argue against your own tool.** Where might the denaturalizing method be *overused* — where might "but it was constructed!" become a lazy way to dismiss an arrangement that was built, yes, and is also well-founded and worth keeping? Make the case that showing a thing is contingent is the beginning of judging it, not the end.

<details><summary>What a strong response does</summary>

- Stakes a clear, arguable claim — not a summary of this module, and not a flat verdict that capitalism is good or bad.
- Works from a **specific** claim or example and reads its actual details, rather than gesturing at "the system" in the abstract.
- Keeps **evidence separate from interpretation** — distinguishes what demonstrably happened (the order was built, recently, by these hands) from the reading placed on it (that its necessity is therefore manufactured).
- Steelmans the other side honestly before answering it, and never substitutes a sneer for an argument.
- Resists the twin traps the course has warned against throughout: treating "constructed" as if it automatically meant "false" or "worthless," and predicting capitalism's collapse (or its permanence) — contingency is a claim about the past's openness, not a forecast of the future.

</details>

## Recap

We have reached the place the lens was always pointing. The most "natural"-seeming common sense of all — that no alternative is even thinkable, the condition **Mark Fisher** named **capitalist realism** and **Margaret Thatcher** compressed into *there is no alternative* — turns out to be the part of our world with the clearest construction record: built recently, in living memory, by an organized project the historians call **neoliberalism**, whose architects (Mont Pèlerin, 1947) set out to manufacture precisely the obviousness that now feels like untouched nature. The same move Polanyi caught two centuries earlier — "laissez-faire was planned" — spoken again with treaties for bricks: the market *encased* from democracy on purpose (Slobodian), a room built so its walls can't be seen. So the unthinkability of alternatives is an *achievement*, not a fact of nature — and the demand for a finished replacement before one is allowed even to doubt is the room defending itself, not a fair test. This course has handed you no blueprint, and it shouldn't: it has handed you eyes. The method is the lens of module 01, now wielded — historicize, compare, make-strange, ask who benefits. Turn it on whatever next tells you, in the voice of the weather, that it is just how things are. Whether the room stands or falls is not for any course to predict. That you can now see it is a room — that was the whole journey.
