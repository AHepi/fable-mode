# Research — Brewing a better cup of coffee (audit run)

A compact dossier (the orchestrator wrote this directly for the audit run rather than fanning out a
research agent; the facts below are well-established home-brewing knowledge).

## Scope
- **In:** extraction (what dissolves, and when); the taste signature of under- vs over-extraction;
  the four home-controllable levers (grind size, brew ratio, water temperature/quality, contact time);
  single-lever diagnosis of a bad cup.
- **Out:** roasting, espresso, milk technique, origin/varietal chemistry, water mineral recipes.

## Core concepts (dependency sketch)
1. **Extraction** — brewing is dissolving soluble compounds out of coffee grounds into water. Acids
   and fruity notes dissolve *first/fast*; sugars and balancing compounds *next*; bitter, astringent
   compounds *last*. So a cup is a point on a timeline. → everything else depends on this.
2. **The taste compass** — too little extraction tastes **sour/sharp/empty** (under-extracted); too
   much tastes **harsh/bitter/drying** (over-extracted); the sweet spot sits between. Depends on (1).
3. **The four levers** (each pushes extraction up or down):
   - **Grind size** — finer = more surface area = faster/more extraction; coarser = less.
   - **Brew ratio** — coffee mass : water mass (a useful starting point ≈ 1:16). More water relative
     to coffee = a weaker, and typically higher-extraction, cup; less = stronger/lower.
   - **Water** — hotter water (≈ 90–96 °C / 195–205 °F) extracts faster; clean, non-distilled water
     tastes best.
   - **Time** — longer contact = more extraction.
   Depends on (1) and (2).
4. **Dialing in** — change **one lever at a time**, taste, and move toward the compass center. Depends
   on all above.

## Prerequisite analysis (for an absolute beginner)
- Assumed known: nothing about coffee mechanics; only that they can make a cup and taste it.
- Must teach from scratch: extraction, the sour/bitter vocabulary, every lever, the discipline of
  one-change-at-a-time.

## Hooks & examples
- The two cups problem: same beans, same machine, two wildly different cups — why? (extraction).
- The "sour vs bitter" mix-up: beginners call a thin sour cup "too strong" and add *less* water, making
  it worse — a misconception worth targeting.

## Common pitfalls / misconceptions
- Confusing **strength** (how much coffee per water) with **extraction** (how much you pulled out).
- Reading sourness as "too strong" and over-correcting the wrong way.
- Changing several levers at once, so nothing is learned.
