Every bad cup you've ever made had a reason. You just didn't know what to look for.

## The hook

Think about the last time a cup of coffee tasted off. Maybe it was sharp and puckering, like very weak lemonade. Maybe it was harsh and ashy, the kind of bitter that lingers on the back of your tongue long after you've swallowed. You probably shrugged and blamed the coffee, or the machine, or a bad morning.

But there's a single idea that explains both of those cups — and every cup in between. Once you have it, you'll never be stuck guessing again.

## The scenario

You're standing in your kitchen. You've made a pot, poured yourself a mug, and taken a sip. It tastes thin and acidic — almost unpleasant, like the coffee couldn't decide what it wanted to be.

What happened? And more importantly, what would you change tomorrow morning?

To answer that, you need to understand what you were actually doing when you made that coffee.

## Framework: what brewing really is

Brewing is **extraction**: the process of dissolving flavour compounds out of the grounds and into the water.

Think of it like rinsing and steeping. When hot water meets the grounds, it starts washing flavour off them — loosening and carrying away the soluble compounds locked inside. The grounds don't empty all at once. They give up different things at different points in time.

Here's what matters: **the compounds don't all dissolve at the same rate.** The lighter, brighter flavours — the ones that taste sharp or acidic — dissolve first, early in the brew. Then come the sweeter, rounder ones. The heavy, bitter compounds come last, near the end. Every cup you make is a snapshot of wherever you stopped on that timeline.

Stop too early, and you've only collected the sharp, acidic front end. Stop too late, and the bitter back end dominates. Stop in the right place — somewhere in the sweet middle — and you've got something worth drinking.

This gives you what I'll call the **taste compass**: two poles, one needle.

- **Sour or sharply acidic?** Your cup is **under-extracted** — you didn't extract enough. The water didn't dissolve enough of the good stuff.
- **Harsh or bitter?** Your cup is **over-extracted** — you extracted too much. The water dissolved the bitter compounds along with everything else.
- **Neither?** You've found the sweet spot.

The compass has one axis. Under-extracted sits at one end; over-extracted at the other. Every diagnostic question in this course is just asking: *which way is the needle pointing?*

<details><summary>Check yourself — what does under-extracted taste like, and what caused it?</summary>

An under-extracted cup tastes sharp, sour, or thinly acidic — sometimes like weak lemonade. The cause: the water stopped dissolving compounds too early, collecting only the bright acidic notes that come off the grounds first, before the sweeter and fuller flavours had a chance to follow.

</details>

## Strength vs. extraction — they are not the same thing

This is where most people's mental model has a gap, and it's worth filling now.

**Strength** is how much dissolved coffee is in your cup relative to the water — how concentrated the brew is. **Extraction** is how far along the flavour timeline you went.

You can have a strong cup that's still under-extracted (very concentrated, but sharp and sour — lots of flavour compounds, but they're all from the early part of the timeline). You can have a weak cup that's over-extracted (thin in body, but bitter — you extracted far into the timeline but used very little grounds, so there wasn't much to dissolve and what you got was mostly the harsh stuff from the end).

The two are controlled by different things — and we'll meet those controls in the next module. For now, the point is: don't let the words fool you. A bitter cup is not "too strong." It's over-extracted. A sour cup is not "too weak." It's under-extracted. The compass tells you which direction you need to move, and that's a different question from how much coffee you're using.

<details><summary>Check yourself — a friend says their coffee is "too weak." What do you need to know before you can help them?</summary>

You need to know *how* it tasted weak. "Weak" by itself doesn't tell you where the needle is pointing.

If it tasted thin and sour, the cup is under-extracted — the water didn't dissolve far enough along the timeline.

If it tasted thin and bitter, it may be over-extracted with too little grounds — the brew went far enough to collect the harsh compounds, but there wasn't enough coffee to give the cup any body.

The fix is different in each case, which is why the taste compass comes before any other question.

</details>

## Self-reflection prompt

Think about a cup of coffee you've had recently — one that was fine but slightly off. Now that you have the taste compass: was it closer to sour or closer to bitter? Don't worry about being precise. Just notice that you have a name for it now, and a direction to move.

## Recap

Brewing is extraction: water rinsing and steeping flavour out of the grounds, lighter compounds first, bitter ones last. The taste compass is your diagnostic — sour means under-extracted, bitter means over-extracted, and the perfect cup lives in the middle. Strength and extraction are separate things, controlled by separate inputs. The next module names those inputs: the four levers a home brewer can actually move.
