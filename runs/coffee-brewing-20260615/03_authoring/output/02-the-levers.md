Your last cup tasted off and you did what most people do: shrugged and poured a new one. But something caused that bitterness or that sourness, and something could fix it. There are exactly four things you can change between now and the next brew, and each one moves extraction in a predictable direction.

This module names all four.

## Scenario

Picture two cups sitting side by side. Same beans. Same kitchen. One tastes flat and sharp; the other is smooth and full. The person who made the good one didn't have better equipment — they'd just changed one thing. The question is: which thing, and in which direction?

## The four levers

Remember the core idea from module 1: extraction is the dissolving-over-time process that pulls flavour out of the grounds and into your water. The taste compass follows: sour means under-extracted, bitter means over-extracted.

Every variable that changes how much dissolves is a **lever**. And for a home brewer, there are exactly four of them.

---

### Lever 1: Grind size

When you grind coffee finer, you shatter the beans into smaller pieces. More surface area is now in contact with water — and that means water can dissolve flavour faster.

**Finer grind → more extraction. Coarser grind → less extraction.**

Think of it like dissolving sugar. A sugar cube dissolves slowly in cold tea. Crush it to powder and it disappears in seconds. Same mass; more surface area; faster dissolving.

<details><summary>Check yourself — which way does a coarser grind push the taste compass needle?</summary>

A coarser grind means less surface area, so water dissolves less flavour before the brew is done. That moves the needle toward *under-extracted* — the cup will likely taste more sour or thin. To compensate, you'd need to adjust another lever in the "more extraction" direction.

</details>

---

### Lever 2: Brew ratio

**Brew ratio** is the mass of coffee relative to the mass of water — usually written as something like 1:16 (1 gram of coffee per 16 grams of water). That's a solid starting point for most home brewing methods.

Change the ratio and you change how concentrated the brew is, which shifts how extraction lands on your palate.

- More coffee, same water (say, 1:14): the water has more grounds to extract from. The cup tastes stronger and can tip toward over-extracted bitterness.
- Less coffee, same water (say, 1:18): the water has less to work with. The cup tastes weaker and can tip under-extracted.

**Lower ratio number (more coffee) → richer, potentially more extracted taste. Higher ratio number (less coffee) → lighter, potentially under-extracted taste.**

Note: brew ratio is the one lever that also affects *strength* (how concentrated the cup is), not just extraction. A stronger cup isn't always a better-extracted cup — but they're tangled, which is why it helps to adjust the other three levers first before touching the ratio.

<details><summary>Check yourself — if you want a stronger cup without changing extraction, which lever should you NOT reach for first?</summary>

Brew ratio affects both strength *and* extraction at once, so it's a blunt instrument for fine-tuning. If your cup is balanced but just weak, adjust the ratio. But if it tastes sour or bitter — an extraction problem — start with grind, water temperature, or time instead. Changing ratio when you have an extraction problem is like turning up the volume when the guitar is out of tune.

</details>

---

### Lever 3: Water temperature

Water temperature controls how vigorously extraction happens. Hot water is more energetic — it breaks the bonds that hold soluble compounds in the grounds more aggressively.

**Hotter water (90–96 °C) → faster, more extraction. Cooler water → slower, less extraction.**

The 90–96 °C range is where most filter brewing methods live. Below 85 °C you're under-extracting and the cup tastes sour and grassy; above 96 °C you're forcing extraction of harsh compounds and the cup turns bitter.

One more thing about water: its *quality* matters too. Heavily chlorinated tap water or water with strong mineral flavours will show up in the cup. Filtered water doesn't taste like anything on its own — which means only the coffee's flavour comes through.

---

### Lever 4: Time

Time is the simplest lever. Water and grounds need to be in contact long enough for extraction to happen.

**Longer brew time → more extraction. Shorter brew time → less extraction.**

Brew time is partly set by your method — a French press brews for four minutes; an espresso machine runs for about 25–30 seconds — and partly influenced by grind size (a finer grind slows water flow, increasing contact time in pour-over methods). This is why levers interact: changing grind size changes both surface area and effective brew time in a drip or pour-over setup.

---

## All four together

Here they are in one place:

| Lever | More extraction | Less extraction |
|---|---|---|
| Grind size | Finer | Coarser |
| Brew ratio | More coffee (lower ratio number) | Less coffee (higher ratio number) |
| Water temperature | Hotter (up to ~96 °C) | Cooler |
| Time | Longer | Shorter |

The levers work as a set. If you change two at once, you won't know which one fixed the problem — or made it worse. That one-lever-at-a-time discipline is what module 3 is built on.

## Practice scenario

> Your morning cup is sour and a bit thin. You've been brewing with roughly a 1:17 ratio, water off a kettle (around 80 °C, which you've just measured for the first time), and a medium-coarse grind with a four-minute steep. You can change exactly one thing before tomorrow's brew.

Write down which lever you'd adjust and in which direction. Then check your reasoning against the rubric.

<details><summary>Rubric — score yourself on each</summary>

- **Did you correctly identify at least one lever pointing toward under-extraction?** The sour, thin taste is the taste compass's signal for under-extraction. Any lever moved toward "more extraction" is a defensible fix.
- **Did you pick the lever with the biggest gap?** The water temperature (80 °C) is significantly below the 90–96 °C range — this is almost certainly the dominant cause. A good answer names temperature as the first move.
- **Did you name the direction?** "Hotter water" or "raise temperature toward 90–96 °C" is the correct direction. "More time" or "finer grind" are also defensible secondary choices.
- **Did you stick to one lever?** Changing two variables at once means you can't learn which one worked. A strong answer picks one and says why.
- **Did you avoid changing the ratio first?** The ratio (1:17) is close to the standard starting point; it's not the obvious culprit here. Jumping to ratio when a more likely cause exists is a common mistake.

A strong attempt names temperature, points it toward the 90–96 °C range, and says why changing one lever at a time is the point.

</details>

## Self-reflection

Think of the last cup you made that tasted wrong. You probably didn't know then which lever to blame. With these four named and their directions clear — which one do you now suspect? And which direction would you move it?

## Recap

Four levers control extraction: grind size (finer means more), brew ratio (more coffee means more), water temperature (hotter means more, and clean water tastes cleaner), and time (longer means more). Each one is predictable. The taste compass from module 1 tells you which direction to move; these levers tell you which knob to reach for. Module 3 puts it into practice — you'll diagnose a bad cup and fix it by moving one lever at a time.
