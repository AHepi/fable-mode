# Editorial Report — Brewing a Better Cup of Coffee

Reviewable changelog of all prose changes made in the Stage 04 editorial pass. Frontmatter is untouched except where noted. All `<details>` blocks, rubrics, and pedagogical structure are preserved. This is a `skill` course; no formal/math blocks exist.

## 1. Voice levelling (Module 2 — primary target)

**Problem:** Module 2 had drifted clinical/encyclopedic. Bold arrow stamps ("Finer grind → more extraction. Coarser grind → less extraction."), bare temperature-range recitation (a list of °C thresholds with no surrounding warmth), and a section that read like a reference card rather than a friend talking.

**Changes:**

*Bold arrow stamps removed; information rewritten as integrated warm prose.*

Before (M2, Lever 1):
> "When you grind coffee finer, you shatter the beans into smaller pieces. More surface area is now in contact with water — and that means water can dissolve flavour faster.
> **Finer grind → more extraction. Coarser grind → less extraction.**"

After:
> "When you grind coffee finer, you shatter the beans into smaller pieces. More surface area is now in contact with water, and more surface area means water can dissolve flavour faster and more thoroughly.
> Grind finer and extraction goes up. Grind coarser and extraction goes down."

The direction information is identical; the stamp format is replaced with a plain sentence.

*Temperature section warmed:*

Before (M2, Lever 3):
> "**Hotter water (90–96 °C) → faster, more extraction. Cooler water → slower, less extraction.**
> The 90–96 °C range is where most filter brewing methods live. Below 85 °C you're under-extracting and the cup tastes sour and grassy; above 96 °C you're forcing extraction of harsh compounds and the cup turns bitter."

After:
> "Hotter water extracts more, and faster. Cooler water extracts less.
> For most filter brewing methods, the right range is 90–96 °C. Below about 85 °C the cup tends to taste sour and grassy — you're not extracting enough of the sweeter compounds. Above 96 °C you're forcing the harsh bitter compounds out along with everything else."

The numbers are preserved; the passive recitation is replaced with the same information in a warmer, more causal voice.

## 4. Varying the edges — openings and closings

### Openings

**Problem:** Both M2 and M3 opened on the same move — a cup tasted wrong, you did the obvious wrong thing, it got worse. (M3: "Your cup is sour. You do what seems obvious: make it stronger. You add more grounds… and now it's sour and bitter." M2: "Your last cup tasted off and you did what most people do: shrugged and poured a new one.")

**M2 opening — rewritten:**

Before:
> "Your last cup tasted off and you did what most people do: shrugged and poured a new one. But something caused that bitterness or that sourness, and something could fix it. There are exactly four things you can change between now and the next brew, and each one moves extraction in a predictable direction. This module names all four."

After:
> "Something caused that off cup you made this morning. And something could fix it. The good news is that there are exactly four things a home brewer can change between one brew and the next — and each one moves extraction in a completely predictable direction. Learn the four, learn the direction each pushes, and a bad cup stops being a mystery."

M2 now opens on the positive (there are exactly four things, and each is predictable), while M3 keeps its negative-to-positive arc — but M3's opening was rewritten to *lead* with the radio image rather than the wrong-move vignette:

**M3 opening — rewritten:**

Before:
> "## The hook
> Your cup is sour. You do what seems obvious: make it stronger. You add more grounds, brew again — and now it's sour *and* bitter and something else has gone wrong, and you're not sure what you changed or how to change it back.
> That is not bad luck. That is what happens when you move two things at once and can't read the result. This module is about not doing that.
> ## Scenario
> Imagine an old analogue radio…"

After (heading removed, radio image leads):
> "Think of an old analogue radio — the kind with a physical tuning dial, not a digital preset. Somewhere on the dial is a clear station. Everywhere else is static, or bleed from a neighbouring signal. You don't jump the dial three positions at once. You turn it a fraction, listen, turn it another fraction, listen again. Small moves, one at a time, toward the sound you're trying to find.
> That is dialing in. And it works on coffee for exactly the same reason it works on a radio: when you change one thing at a time, every result tells you something. Change two things at once and the result tells you nothing useful.
> ## Scenario
> A cup of coffee has a clear station too…"

The wrong-move vignette's information is preserved — it is the content of "The classic beginner mistake" section later in the module.

## 5. Callbacks not re-derivations

**Problem:** M3's "The classic beginner mistake" section re-derived what under-extraction tastes like and why (the lighter/brighter flavours dissolve first, etc.) — a full re-teaching of content owned by M1.

**Change (M3):**

Before:
> "You pull a cup. It tastes thin and sharp — sour, undeniably sour. Your instinct says: *it's too strong, I used too much coffee.* So you reduce the brew ratio: fewer grounds, same water. You brew again. It tastes even more sour and now also weaker.
> What happened? You diagnosed **strength** when the compass was pointing at **extraction**. A thin, sharp cup feels like 'too much coffee' — but thinness is what under-extraction tastes like. The flavour compounds that build body and sweetness haven't been dissolved yet; what you're tasting is the first wave of acids, which extract fast, with almost nothing behind them.
> Reducing the grounds didn't fix that…"

After:
> "You pull a cup that's thin and sour — under-extracted, as the taste compass from module 1 tells you. But your instinct says *it's too strong, I used too much coffee.* So you reduce the grounds. You brew again. It tastes even more sour and now also weaker.
> What happened? You diagnosed **strength** when the compass was pointing at **extraction**. Reducing the grounds didn't push extraction further — it left you with less coffee to dissolve from, and the same compounds you were already failing to reach stayed out of reach. You moved the lever in the wrong direction because you misread the compass."

The re-derivation ("The flavour compounds that build body and sweetness haven't been dissolved yet; what you're tasting is the first wave of acids, which extract fast, with almost nothing behind them") is replaced by a callback to the compass. The diagnostic use — why reducing grounds made things *worse* — is retained in full.

## 6. One running image per module (Module 2)

**Problem:** Module 2 used two distinct images: (a) a sugar cube dissolving to illustrate grind size / surface area; (b) a guitar out of tune to illustrate why adjusting brew ratio to fix an extraction problem is the wrong move.

Canon §3 assigns no running image to M2 (only M1 and M3 have assigned images). The editorial instruction: keep the single strongest, cut or downplay the other.

**Decision:** The guitar/out-of-tune image is the stronger of the two — it's vivid, memorable, and makes a more subtle point (brew ratio as a blunt instrument when the real problem is extraction). The sugar-cube image is illustrative but the same information can be conveyed in direct prose without the analogy.

**Sugar-cube removed (M2, Lever 1 section):**

Before:
> "Think of it like dissolving sugar. A sugar cube dissolves slowly in cold tea. Crush it to powder and it disappears in seconds. Same mass; more surface area; faster dissolving."

After (replaced with direct prose):
> "You can feel why: a coarser grind keeps more of the bean's interior locked away from the water, so fewer compounds dissolve before the brew is done. A finer grind opens the bean up, and the water gets at more of it."

**Guitar image kept (M2, Lever 2 section):** Preserved verbatim.
> "Adjusting ratio when you have an extraction problem is like turning up the volume when the guitar is out of tune."
