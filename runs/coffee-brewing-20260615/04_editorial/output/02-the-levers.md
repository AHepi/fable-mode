Something caused that off cup you made this morning. And something could fix it. The good news is that there are exactly four things a home brewer can change between one brew and the next — and each one moves extraction in a completely predictable direction. Learn the four, learn the direction each pushes, and a bad cup stops being a mystery.

## Scenario

Picture two cups sitting side by side. Same beans. Same kitchen. One tastes flat and sharp; the other is smooth and full. The person who made the good one didn't have better equipment — they'd just changed one thing. The question is: which thing, and in which direction?

## The four levers

Extraction — the dissolving-over-time process from module 1, where water draws soluble compounds out of the grounds — responds to four inputs you can actually control. These are the **levers**. Every change you make as a home brewer is a move on one of them.

---

### Lever 1: Grind size

When you grind coffee finer, you shatter the beans into smaller pieces. More surface area is now in contact with water, and more surface area means water can dissolve flavour faster and more thoroughly.

Grind finer and extraction goes up. Grind coarser and extraction goes down.

You can feel why: a coarser grind keeps more of the bean's interior locked away from the water, so fewer compounds dissolve before the brew is done. A finer grind opens the bean up, and the water gets at more of it.

<details><summary>Check yourself — which way does a coarser grind push the taste compass needle?</summary>

A coarser grind means less surface area, so water dissolves fewer compounds before the brew is done. That moves the needle toward *under-extracted* — the cup will likely taste more sour or thin. To compensate, you'd need to adjust another lever in the "more extraction" direction.

</details>

---

### Lever 2: Brew ratio

**Brew ratio** is the mass of coffee relative to the mass of water — usually written as something like 1:16 (1 gram of coffee per 16 grams of water). That ratio is a solid starting point for most home brewing methods.

Change the ratio and you change how concentrated the brew is, which shifts how extraction lands on your palate.

- More coffee, same water (say, 1:14): the water has more grounds to extract from. The cup tastes stronger and can tip toward over-extracted bitterness.
- Less coffee, same water (say, 1:18): the water has less to work with. The cup tastes weaker and can tip under-extracted.

Brew ratio is the one lever that also affects *strength* — how concentrated the cup is — not just extraction. That makes it a blunt instrument when you have an extraction problem. If your cup is sour or bitter, start with grind, water temperature, or time. Adjusting ratio when the real problem is extraction is like turning up the volume when the guitar is out of tune: the sound gets louder, but it's still wrong.

<details><summary>Check yourself — if you want a stronger cup without changing extraction, which lever should you NOT reach for first?</summary>

Brew ratio affects both strength *and* extraction at once, so it's a blunt instrument for fine-tuning. If your cup is balanced but just weak, adjust the ratio. But if it tastes sour or bitter — an extraction problem — start with grind, water temperature, or time instead.

</details>

---

### Lever 3: Water temperature

Water temperature controls how vigorously extraction happens. Hot water is more energetic — it breaks the bonds holding soluble compounds in the grounds more aggressively, dissolving them faster.

Hotter water extracts more, and faster. Cooler water extracts less.

For most filter brewing methods, the right range is 90–96 °C. Below about 85 °C the cup tends to taste sour and grassy — you're not extracting enough of the sweeter compounds. Above 96 °C you're forcing the harsh bitter compounds out along with everything else.

One more thing about water: its *quality* matters too. Heavily chlorinated tap water or water with strong mineral flavours will show up in the cup. Filtered water doesn't taste like anything on its own — which means only the coffee's flavour comes through.

---

### Lever 4: Time

Time is the simplest lever. Water and grounds need to be in contact long enough for extraction to happen.

More time means more extraction. Less time means less.

Brew time is partly set by your method — a French press brews for around four minutes; an espresso machine runs for about 25–30 seconds — and partly influenced by grind size. A finer grind slows water flow through the grounds, which increases contact time in pour-over or drip methods. This is why the levers interact: changing grind size changes both surface area and effective brew time in the same move.

---

## All four together

Here they are in one place:

| Lever | More extraction | Less extraction |
|---|---|---|
| Grind size | Finer | Coarser |
| Brew ratio | More coffee (lower ratio number) | Less coffee (higher ratio number) |
| Water temperature | Hotter (up to ~96 °C) | Cooler |
| Time | Longer | Shorter |

The levers work as a set. If you change two at once, you won't know which one fixed the problem — or made it worse. That one-lever-at-a-time discipline is what module 3 is built on.

## Practice scenario

> Your morning cup is sour and a bit thin. You've been brewing with roughly a 1:17 ratio, water off a kettle (around 80 °C, which you've just measured for the first time), and a medium-coarse grind with a four-minute steep. You can change exactly one thing before tomorrow's brew.

Write down which lever you'd adjust and in which direction. Then check your reasoning against the rubric.

<details><summary>Rubric — score yourself on each</summary>

- **Did you correctly identify at least one lever pointing toward under-extraction?** The sour, thin taste is the taste compass's signal for under-extraction. Any lever moved toward "more extraction" is a defensible fix.
- **Did you pick the lever with the biggest gap?** The water temperature (80 °C) is significantly below the 90–96 °C range — this is almost certainly the dominant cause. A good answer names temperature as the first move.
- **Did you name the direction?** "Hotter water" or "raise temperature toward 90–96 °C" is the correct direction. "More time" or "finer grind" are also defensible secondary choices.
- **Did you stick to one lever?** Changing two variables at once means you can't learn which one worked. A strong answer picks one and says why.
- **Did you avoid changing the ratio first?** The ratio (1:17) is close to the standard starting point; it's not the obvious culprit here. Jumping to ratio when a more likely cause exists is a common mistake.

A strong attempt names temperature, points it toward the 90–96 °C range, and says why changing one lever at a time is the point.

</details>

## Self-reflection

Think of the last cup you made that tasted wrong. You probably didn't know then which lever to blame. With these four named and their directions clear — which one do you now suspect? And which direction would you move it?

## Recap

Four levers control extraction: grind size (finer means more), brew ratio (more coffee means more, but it also shifts strength, so use it carefully), water temperature (hotter means more, and clean water tastes cleaner), and time (longer means more). Each one is predictable. The taste compass from module 1 tells you which direction to move; these levers tell you which one to reach for — and module 3 shows you how to use them one at a time.
