Think of an old analogue radio — the kind with a physical tuning dial, not a digital preset. Somewhere on the dial is a clear station. Everywhere else is static, or bleed from a neighbouring signal. You don't jump the dial three positions at once. You turn it a fraction, listen, turn it another fraction, listen again. Small moves, one at a time, toward the sound you're trying to find.

That is dialing in. And it works on coffee for exactly the same reason it works on a radio: when you change one thing at a time, every result tells you something. Change two things at once and the result tells you nothing useful.

## Scenario

A cup of coffee has a clear station too: the range where extraction — the dissolving-over-time idea from module 1 — sits at the right depth. Short of that range, the cup tastes sour. Past it, the cup tastes bitter. The compass from module 1 maps this exactly: sour means under-extracted, bitter means over-extracted, and the goal is the centre.

The four levers from module 2 — grind, brew ratio, water, and time — are the dial. Each one nudges extraction in a known direction. Your job when a cup goes wrong is to listen to the taste, read where the needle is sitting, and turn *one* lever toward the centre.

## How to dial in

The dialing-in method has three steps, repeated until the cup sits where you want it:

1. **Taste and read the compass.** Is the cup sour (under-extracted) or bitter (over-extracted)? Name it before touching anything.
2. **Pick one lever and move it one step in the right direction.** If the cup is under-extracted, choose a lever that pushes extraction *further*: grind finer, increase the brew ratio, use hotter water, or let it brew longer. If the cup is over-extracted, do the opposite. One lever. One step.
3. **Brew again and taste.** Did the cup move toward the centre? If it still reads off-compass, repeat from step 1.

That is the whole method. The discipline lives entirely in the word *one*.

### Why one lever?

If you move two levers at once and the cup improves, you don't know which lever fixed it — or whether one of your changes helped and the other hurt. You've learned nothing useful for the next brew. Worse, if the cup gets worse after two changes, you have no idea how to reverse course.

Moving one lever at a time transforms a failed cup from a mystery into a data point. Each single-lever brew tells you: *yes, that direction helped* or *no, that direction made it worse.* By the third or fourth brew you've found the station.

## The classic beginner mistake

There is one error that almost every new brewer makes at least once, and it is worth naming directly.

You pull a cup that's thin and sour — under-extracted, as the taste compass from module 1 tells you. But your instinct says *it's too strong, I used too much coffee.* So you reduce the grounds. You brew again. It tastes even more sour and now also weaker.

What happened? You diagnosed **strength** when the compass was pointing at **extraction**. Reducing the grounds didn't push extraction further — it left you with less coffee to dissolve from, and the same compounds you were already failing to reach stayed out of reach. You moved the lever in the wrong direction because you misread the compass.

When in doubt, trust the compass over the instinct. Sour means under-extracted. Move a lever to push extraction further, not to reduce it.

## Walkthrough

You're brewing with a basic drip machine. The cup comes out tasting noticeably sour with a watery finish. Here is the method applied, step by step.

**Step 1 — Taste and read.** Sour, thin finish. Compass says under-extracted. Not enough flavour has dissolved out of the grounds.

**Step 2 — Pick one lever.** You have four options: grind finer (more surface area, faster extraction), increase the brew ratio (more grounds in the same water), raise the water temperature (extracts more aggressively), or let it brew longer (more time in contact). Any of these would help — pick the one that's easiest to control on your setup. Say you choose grind size: you'll grind a notch finer.

**Step 3 — Brew and taste.** The next cup is less sour, rounder. Still a touch sharp at the front but nothing like before. The needle is moving toward centre.

**Step 4 — One more single-step turn.** Still slightly under. You grind one more notch finer. The cup comes back balanced — a bit of brightness at the front, some sweetness in the middle, clean at the end. You've found the station.

Total adjustments: one lever, two small turns. You know exactly what changed and why.

## Practice scenario

> You brew a cup and it comes back bitter and harsh with a dry, astringent finish — the back of your throat feels coated. You're brewing with a pour-over. You've been using the same grind setting for a week and it's been fine until today. The only thing you changed was the water: you used water that had been sitting in a kettle on the stove and may have been hotter than usual.
>
> Using the dialing-in method, work through what you'd do. What does the compass say? What likely happened? What single lever would you move first, and in which direction?

<details><summary>Rubric — score yourself 1–5 on each</summary>

- **Did you read the compass correctly?** Bitter and astringent is over-extraction. A strong attempt names this clearly, not just "too strong" or "too much coffee."
- **Did you identify the likely cause?** Hotter water extracts more aggressively. If the water was unusually hot, that's the most likely culprit — and it happened right when the problem appeared.
- **Did you choose one lever?** A good response names a single adjustment: lower the water temperature back to the usual range. It doesn't also change the grind, the brew ratio, and the time simultaneously.
- **Did you state the direction correctly?** Cooler water = less extraction = moving toward centre from the over-extracted side. If you said "use hotter water" to fix bitterness, that goes the wrong way on the compass.
- **Did you resist the temptation to multi-adjust?** The hardest part of dialing in is doing only one thing. A strong attempt explains *why* one lever, not just which one.

A strong attempt scores 4–5 across the board and can trace the logic from taste → compass → lever → direction without any steps missing.

</details>

## Self-reflection

Think back to the last cup of coffee you made that didn't taste right. Without knowing the exact numbers, do you have any sense of where the compass was pointing — sour or bitter? And what did you actually do about it, if anything? If you made a change, was it the right lever, the right direction, or were you going by instinct and guessing?

You don't need to answer in writing. Just sit with it. The next time a cup goes wrong, you'll know what to do.

## Recap

Dialing in is a loop: taste, read the compass, move one lever one step toward the centre, taste again. The method's power is entirely in the *one* — single-lever changes keep each result legible, so you're building real knowledge about your setup instead of stumbling toward a cup by luck. The biggest trap for beginners is misreading a sour cup as a strength problem and reducing the grounds, which moves extraction in the wrong direction and makes things worse. Trust the compass: sour means under-extracted, and the fix is always to push extraction further.

That's the complete toolkit: you know what extraction is, you know the four levers, and you know how to adjust them one at a time until the cup sits where it should. The rest is practice.
