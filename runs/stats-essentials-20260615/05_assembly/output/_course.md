Open a news app and the numbers come at you all day: a poll puts a candidate at 52%, a study says
coffee drinkers live longer, a chart shows two lines climbing together. Most of us learned to either
nod along or tune out — because nobody ever handed us the few plain ideas that make these numbers
readable. This short course hands them over.

It is the **Essentials primer** of a three-part series, and it builds everything from the ground up
for someone with no statistics background — no calculus, no formulas to grind, just clear ideas with
everyday examples. We start from the one fact the whole subject is built on: real measurements
*vary*. From there we give a pile of numbers a shape (a distribution), find its center and spread,
learn why a sample is only a handful scooped from a much bigger jar, and meet probability as the
long-run behavior of randomness. We finish on the idea the rest of the series turns on — telling a
real **signal** from random **noise**.

You won't compute much here. The payoff isn't arithmetic; it's the quiet confidence to look at a
number in the wild and ask the right question. The two follow-on courses — *Making Sense of Studies*
and *Lines Through Data* — lean on the vocabulary and the handful of pictures you'll build here, so
this is the place to start.
