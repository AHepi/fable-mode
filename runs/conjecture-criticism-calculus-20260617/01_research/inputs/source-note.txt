The Necessity of Creativity:
A Substrate-Independent Calculus of Conjecture and Criticism
June 2026 — working draft
Abstract
A companion note built a computable formalism for Deutsch’s epistemology and fixed it
to ten fidelity constraints. This note answers a sharper demand: build the epistemology
and creativity together, from primitives, in a form general enough to run on any physical
substrate — brains, computers, or alien matter — and specific enough to violate none of the
constraints. The central claim is that creativity is not a faculty added to error-correction
but a property forced by it: a fallibilist system that aspires to solve every soluble problem
in its domain must have an open-ended, computationally universal conjecture operator, and
(by the physical Church–Turing–Deutsch principle) that operator is substrate-independent.
We work in two strata. A constructor-theoretic Level 0 supplies the substrate-independence
of information and knowledge, and — through the requirement of digital error-correctability
for resilient knowledge — justifies a discrete content layer rather than stipulating one. An
invented process calculus at Level 1 supplies the dynamics: epistemic states rewritten by
explicit conjecture, criticism, adjudication, and problem-generation rules, with hard-to-vary
and reach as measured quantities and fallibilism as two structural axioms. We then prove
the necessity-of-creativity theorem, derive its substrate-independence corollary, and exhibit
biological evolution and human explanation as the uncoupled and coupled instances of one
knowledge-growth schema. The fidelity constraints are then derived where derivable; where
one is not — fallibilism itself — we explain why it must be an axiom, and show it is the
very premise the creativity theorem runs on. We close with an explicit verdict on what the
constructor theories of information, knowledge, computation, and natural selection do and
do not contribute.

1 What must be captured, and in what language
1.1 The constraints
The target is Deutsch’s epistemology in The Fabric of Reality and The Beginning of Infinity , to-
gether with its corollary account of creativity. A faithful formalism must satisfy ten constraints,
stated and defended in the companion note and used here as a fixed criticism battery.
D1 (Problems first). Problems — conflicts between ideas, or between ideas and observation
— drive inquiry; Popper’s P1 → T T→ EE → P2 is the canonical cycle.
D2 (Conjecture is unconstrained). There is no inductive road from data to theory; the
formalism is generator-agnostic and must not equate stochastic variation with explanatory
novelty.
D3 (Criticism is universal and itself criticizable). Empirical testing is one species of crit-
icism; criticisms are conjectures, and a refuted criticism reinstates its target.
D4 (T entative acceptance). Prefer what has so far survived criticism where rivals have not;
acceptance is time-indexed, never a certificate.
D5 (Hard to vary). A good explanation is hard to vary while still accounting for what it
accounts for ; graded, criticism-relative.
D6 (Reach). Good explanations solve problems beyond those they were built for; reach is
discovered, not designed.
D7 (F allibilism; no final state). No terminal status, no foundation, no final theory; even the
system’s own rules stay open to criticism.
D8 (Refutation is demotion, not annihilation). Refuted theories persist as bounded ap-
proximations and as candidates for reinstatement.
D9 (Domain universality). Mathematics, philosophy, and the arts share the dynamic; aes-
thetic problems are objective; proof is physical computation.

D10 (Rivalry is problem-indexed). Theories clash only as rivals for one problem; a persis-
tent clash is itself a new problem.
To these the present task adds two requirements that the constraints leave implicit, and that
the construction must now make explicit.
M1 (Creativity is constitutive). Creativity — the generation of new explanatory conjec-
tures — is not an optional module bolted onto the loop. It is implicit in the epistemology,
and the formalism should make the dependence derivable: the error-correcting structure
should entail the open-endedness of the creative faculty, not merely leave room for it.
M2 (Substrate independence / multiple realizability). The same epistemic process, and
the same creative faculty, must be instantiable in any sufficiently capable physical medium:
human brains, digital computers, and any alien substrate obeying the same physics. Sub-
strate is contingent; the knowledge and the creativity are not.
1.2 Choice of language
The instruction was to choose a language — algebra, set theory, category theory — or invent
one. None of the three off-the-shelf options suffices alone, and the reasons are instructive enough
to fix the architecture.
Category theory was the companion note’s diagnosed failure: the natural categorical home
for the dialectic is a 2-category whose criticism-of-criticism tower climbs to ω, and the tractable
content is recovered only by quotienting that tower down to argumentation dynamics. It pays
no rent here that the quotient does not already pay.
Set theory and algebra are static. They can describe a knowledge state but not its lawful suc-
cession; the phenomena to be captured — conjecture, refutation, reinstatement, the trajectory
of acceptance — are irreducibly processual, statements about how a state becomes its successor
in time.
Constructor theory is the right language for M2 and for the definition of knowledge, but —
by its own design — it is a theory of which transformations are possible, counterfactual and
atemporal. It cannot host a dynamics either ( § 6).
The construction is therefore pluralist, with a clear division of labour:
 a constructor-theoretic Level 0 (§ 2) for substrate independence, the definition of
knowledge, and the digital-content justification;
 an invented process calculus at Level 1(§ 3) — a labelled rewriting system on epistemic
states, presented as primitives, formation rules, and inference (transition) rules — as the
load-bearing spine;
 two imported and clearly-marked tools used inside Level 1: Dung argumentation semantics
for adjudication and algorithmic information theory for the hard-to-vary measure, neither
claimed as original.
The process calculus is the invention; everything else is borrowed and attributed.

2 Level 0: the substrate-independent foundation
2.1 Primitives and the universality axiom
We adopt the constructor-theoretic stance (Deutsch 2013; Deutsch & Marletto 2015) as the
meta-language for “the same process on any substrate.”
Definition 2.1 (Attributes, tasks, possibility) . An attribute is a specification of a class of
physical states. A task T = {x1 → y1, . . . , xn → yn} is a finite set of ordered pairs of attributes:
a specification of a transformation. A constructor for T is a physical system that can cause T to
occur on a substrate and retain the ability to do so again. The task is possible, T ✓, if the laws
of physics permit a constructor performing T to arbitrarily high accuracy; otherwise impossible,
T ×. Possibility is a property of the laws, not of any particular object or moment.
The methodological point of this stance is that epistemic operations will be specified as
tasks, so that their possibility — and hence their realizability on a given substrate — is a
substrate-independent, law-level fact rather than a contingent feature of one machine.
Axiom 1 (Physical Church–Turing–Deutsch principle; Deutsch 1985). There exists a universal
computer: a constructor U whose programs can perform, to arbitrary accuracy, every task that
any physical constructor can perform; equivalently, every finitely realizable physical system can
be simulated by U operating by finite means. Any two universal computers intersimulate via a
possible task whose overhead is additive and independent of the simulated input.
2.2 Information, knowledge, and why the substrate must be digital
Definition 2.2 (Information medium). An information medium is a substrate carrying a set of
attributes on which the copy task and the relevant permutation tasks are possible. (This is the
constructor-theory-of-information characterization of an information variable: definable purely
by which copying and distinguishing tasks are possible.)
Definition 2.3 (Knowledge as resilient information; Deutsch–Marletto) . Information instanti-
ated in a medium is knowledge if it is resilient: the task of keeping it instantiated is possible, and
in the strong case is performed by a constructor that the information itself (partly) specifies —
i.e. the information has the causal power to bring about the very tasks it is “about,” and thereby
tends to remain instantiated. Knowledge is thus information with constructor-like efficacy.
Two consequences are load-bearing below.
Theorem 2.1 (Digitality of resilient knowledge; after Marletto 2015). A medium that can carry
knowledge resiliently under perturbation must be digital: its information-bearing attributes must
form a discrete set with disjoint stability basins, so that an error-correction task on them is
possible. Continuous (analog) information cannot be resiliently maintained, because no error-
correction task on a continuum of attributes is possible to arbitrary accuracy under generic
noise.
Remark 2.1. Theorem 2.1 is the constructor-theoretic core of Marletto’s account of why accu-
rate self-reproduction with the appearance of design requires digital (discrete, error-correctable)
genetic information. We use it for a different purpose: it derives, rather than stipulates, that
the content layer of the dynamics (§ 3) must represent knowledge in a discrete alphabet Σ ∗. The

choice of strings is not a modelling convenience; resilient knowledge has no analog option. The
same theorem explains why genes are digital and why any alien knowledge-bearer’s hereditary
medium must be too.
Theorem 2.2 (Substrate independence of knowledge) . Let K be knowledge (resilient informa-
tion) instantiable in a medium M , and let M ′ be any universal medium (Axiom 1). Then the
task “instantiate K in M ′” is possible, carrying the same K. Knowledge is multiply realizable;
which physical medium bears it is contingent.
Proof sketch. Knowledge is characterized at Definition 2.3 by the tasks it performs and the
persistence task it survives — all attribute-level, hence law-level and medium-independent. By
Axiom 1, M ′ can perform any task M can, to arbitrary accuracy, including instantiation and
maintenance of K. The translation cost is the bounded intersimulation overhead. Hence K
transfers without remainder.
Theorem 2.2 discharges one half of M2 at the foundation: the knowledge is substrate-
independent. The other half — the substrate-independence of the creative faculty — is Corol-
lary 4.2, and depends on the Level 1 dynamics and the main theorem.
3 Level 1: the conjecture–criticism calculus
We now build the dynamics as a process calculus. Contents live in Σ∗ (justified by Theorem 2.1)
and are interpreted on the universal machineU of Axiom 1. The development refines the content
and dialectical layers of the companion note; the novelty here is the explicit rule presentation
and the first-class status of the generator, which the creativity theorem requires.
3.1 Syntactic categories (primitives)
Definition 3.1 (Commitment, verdict). A commitment is a pair κ = (τκ, βκ) of a test program
and a resource budget. Its verdict on content c is
V (κ, c) = U ≤βκ(τκ, c) ∈ {pass, fail, overrun}.
Verdicts are extensional, bounded, and decidable by construction; budgeting is what keeps Rice’s
theorem at bay.
Definition 3.2 (Artifact, problem, kernel). An artifact is a = (c(a), I(a)): content plus a finite
commitment interface . A problem is π = ( dπ, Critπ, provπ): a description, a growing finite
set of criteria (commitment schemas instantiable against candidates), and a provenance record.
The variation kernel µ(· |a) is a samplable distribution over bounded edits of c(a). Artifacts are
untyped beyond this: conjectures, criticisms, aesthetic standards, problem criteria, experimental
reports, and the calculus’s own rules are all artifacts — the uniformity that makes “whatever
can attack can be attacked” enforceable.
Definition 3.3 (Epistemic state; system). An epistemic state is S = (A, Π, addr, att, W): finite
sets of artifacts and problems, a declared addressing relation addr ⊆ A × Π, a warranted attack
relation att ⊆ A × A, and a warrant map W . A system is Σsys = (S, γ,{µa}, adj), where γ is
the conjecture operator (§ 4), {µa} the kernels, and adj the adjudicator. The generator γ is a
distinguished, first-class component precisely so that the formalism can state requirements on it.

Definition 3.4 (Warrants). An attack edge (k, a) ∈ att must carry a warrant of one of two types.
Demonstrative: k packages a failing verdict — a commitment κ, its trace, and V (κ, a) = fail —
together with a dependence on a validity node ν(κ), an artifact asserting the test is sound and
relevant; by a closure rule, any attacker of ν(κ) attacks k. Argumentative: k is an explanatory
artifact whose commitments include a claim about a’s deficiency. Both kinds are themselves
artifacts and hence attackable, so criticism is always contentful and never a bare verdict.
3.2 Formation rules
A state is well-formed iff every attack edge carries a registered warrant (Definition 3.4); ev-
ery problem’s criteria are commitment schemas; every addr pair is declared by the addressing
artifact; and the validity-node closure holds. All rules below preserve well-formedness.

3.5 Fallibilism as two axioms
Axiom 2 (N1: no absorbing status) . Statuses are functions of S alone, and every status admits
an exit: accepted → refuted by a new warranted attack; refuted → accepted by reinstatement
(Lemma 3.1); a demonstrative refutation reopens by an attack on its validity node. No state is
marked final, including the rule-artifacts of (Refl).
Axiom 3 (N2: perpetual proposability) . The generator’s support is unbounded: for every ac-
cepted a there exist proposable successors with strictly extended batteries. “Knowledge at t”
denotes the trajectory (Gt, HV-profile, R-profile) — a position in an open process, not a fixed
point of it.
These encode D7 directly. We will see in § 4 that they are not idle bookkeeping: N1 is the
premise the creativity theorem runs on.

4 Creativity, and why the epistemology forces it
4.1 The creative faculty, characterized functionally
Definition 4.1 (Conjecture operator; reachable set) . The creative faculty of a system is its
conjecture operator γ, the map (π, S) 7→ a supplying the premise of (Conj). Its reachable set is

Γ is the closure of γ’s range under iteration with criticism-feedback.
We characterize creativity by what its availability must satisfy, not by any internal mecha-
nism. This is deliberate, and is D2: the provenance of a conjecture is epistemically inert, and —
following the standing correction — computational nondeterminism is not logical novelty and
stochastic mutation is not a theory of creativity. The claims below are about the reach of the
operator, indifferent to whether the substrate realizing it is deterministic or stochastic.
Definition 4.2 (Openness and coupling) . γ is open (or universal) if Γ = Σ ∗: with feedback,
it can eventually emit any expressible content. γ is coupled if its output genuinely depends
on the criticism graph: there are states S, S ′ differing only in att with γ(π, S) ̸= γ(π, S ′) as
distributions. Blind variation is the negation: γ ignores what criticism has eliminated.
4.2 Two Deutschian principles
Axiom 4 (Solubility; Deutsch’s optimism) . Every problem whose solution is not forbidden by
the laws of nature is soluble: lawfully, some constructor with the right knowledge can solve it.
(“Problems are inevitable; problems are soluble.”) This is a statement about lawful possibility,
not about any particular system.
Definition 4.3 (Universal explainer; epistemic completeness) . A system is epistemically com-
plete over a domain if for every soluble problem expressible in the system there is, in principle,
a reachable solution. A universal explainer is a system epistemically complete over the domain
of all expressible explanations — one with no class of creatable explanations forever beyond it.
Deutsch’s thesis is that people are universal explainers; this is the “jump to universality.”
4.3 The main theorem
Theorem 4.1 (Necessity of open-ended creativity). Let a knowledge-growth system have content
space Σ∗ and conjecture operator γ with reachable set Γ. If the system is fallibilist (Axiom 2)
and epistemically complete over its domain (Definition 4.3), then Γ = Σ ∗: γ-with-feedback is
open. Contrapositively, if Γ is a recursively-fixed proper subset of Σ∗, the system has a blind
spot — a soluble problem it can never solve and whose open status it can never lawfully close.
Proof. Suppose Γ ⊊ Σ∗. Any actually-implemented operator has a recursively enumerable
reachable set; enumerate Γ = {g1, g2, . . .}. For epistemic purposes contents are individuated by
verdict-profiles on budgeted commitments — that is the only way the system tells two contents
apart ( § 3). Build a problem πdiag by diagonalization: choose an index-sensitive probe battery
(ρ1, ρ2, . . .) of budgeted tests and letπdiag’s single criterion be satisfied by a contentc iff, for every

Now πdiag is soluble: c∗ is a finite content, and by Axiom 4 (with Axiom 1) the task “emit

(i) It leaves πdiag permanently open. Then it is not epistemically complete — contradicting
the hypothesis.
(ii) It registers “ πdiag is unsolvable for us” as settled. That verdict is false at the level of law
(the problem is soluble), and the system can never overturn it (it can never emit c∗). It is
therefore an absorbing negative status — contradicting N1.
Both cases contradict the hypotheses, so Γ = Σ ∗.
This is M1 discharged: creativity, in the precise sense of an open-ended conjecture operator,
is entailed by fallibilism together with the aspiration to solve every soluble problem. It is not
added to the epistemology; it is a theorem of it. The theorem also draws the right boundary. A
bacterium’s mutation operator or a thermostat fails the antecedent — they are not epistemically
complete over any rich domain — and the theorem imposes nothing on them: they are knowledge-
creators of bounded reach, which is exactly the biological case below. Open-ended creativity is
forced precisely for systems that aim at universality.
Corollary 4.1 (Universality of the generator) . Γ = Σ ∗ means γ-with-feedback enumerates all
of Σ∗; an operator with this reachable set is computationally universal, since it can be steered to
emit any program and hence to simulate any other generator.
Corollary 4.2 (Substrate independence of creativity) . By Corollary 4.1 and Axiom 1, a uni-
versal generator is realizable on any universal physical medium — brain, silicon, or alien matter
— with bounded translation overhead. The creative faculty is substrate-independent: the same
creativity, multiply realized.
Corollaries 4.1–4.2 complete M2. Theorem 2.2 made the knowledge substrate-independent;
Corollary 4.2 makes the faculty that creates it substrate-independent, and ties both to the one
universality principle. Whatever an alien knowledge-creator is made of, if it is a universal
explainer then its creativity is, up to bounded translation, ours.
4.4 Blind versus explanatory creativity, and the unity with natural selection
Openness alone does not distinguish a mind from a mutation stream: enough random edits reach
any content in the limit. The distinction is coupling, and it is what produces reach.
Proposition 4.1 (Reach requires coupling). Both the coupled and the blind regimes can satisfy
Γ = Σ ∗ in the limit. They differ in expected reach. Under coupling, a conjecture is shaped
by the accumulated criticism graph, so it tends to encode constraints that criticism has already

=====PAGE BREAK=====

extracted from the problem; such a conjecture is then more likely to satisfy commitments beyond
its origin problem, giving nonzero expected reach. Under blindness, conjectures are statistically
independent of prior criticism, so expected reach beyond the origin problem is at chance. Reach
is therefore the signature of coupled (explanatory) creativity.
Remark 4.1. Proposition 4.1 is a structural claim about expected reach under the two coupling
conditions, not a proof of a numerical bound; the dependence of reach on coupling is asserted
as a modelling commitment, and is itself an attackable artifact in the system’s own ontology.
What it captures is Deutsch’s distinction between knowledge that merely adapts and knowledge
that explains.
This licenses a single schema with two canonical instances.
Definition 4.4 (Knowledge-growth schema). A knowledge-growth system is
K = ⟨ content space, problem/niche, γ, σ, ρ⟩
with a variation operator γ, a selection operator σ, and a resilience criterion ρ, iterated as a
map.
Biological evolution. Content is genomes — digital, and digital for a reason given at The-
orem 2.1, not by coincidence. The variation operator is mutation and recombination, and it
is uncoupled: variation is blind to the selection pressure, the central Darwinian commitment.
Selection σ is differential reproduction — the environment is the critic, silently culling the unfit.
Resilience ρ is the gene’s tendency to build organisms that re-instantiate the gene: resilient
information, hence knowledge in the sense of Definition 2.3. Reach is limited — adaptation to
a niche, with no explanatory content — exactly as Proposition 4.1 predicts for blind variation.
Minds and explanatory knowledge. Content is explanations. The variation operator is
conjecture — creativity — and it is coupled: the next conjecture is informed by the criticism
graph and the problem. Selection σ is criticism, empirical and rational and aesthetic. Resilience
ρ is being hard to vary while still accounting for the phenomena. Reach is potentially unbounded
— the jump to universality — because the content space is Σ ∗ and γ is open and coupled
(Theorem 4.1, Proposition 4.1).
So natural selection and human creativity are one formal skeleton, differing in the coupling
of γ and the character of σ. This is precisely Deutsch’s claim that knowledge grows by variation
and selection in both biology and minds, with the distinctive thing about people being the
reach of explanatory knowledge. The construction makes the unification exact and locates the
difference in a single structural parameter.
Remark 4.2 (The non-claim, located precisely) . The formalism guarantees that open-endedness
is forced (Theorem 4.1) and that reach tracks coupling (Proposition 4.1). It does not claim that
coupling is sufficient for good explanations, nor that any mechanism provably manufactures
high-HV, high-reach conjectures on demand. The generative achievement — producing, non-
randomly, conjectures that turn out hard to vary and far-reaching — is characterized by its
products, not reduced to an algorithm. This is the honest residue: the epistemology forces the
creative faculty to be open, universal, and (for explanation) coupled, but a constructive theory
of why a given coupled generator lands on deep explanations is exactly what remains open.
Identifying creativity with randomness, or with the nondeterministic step of a computation, is
the error the construction is built to avoid.

5 Derivation of the fidelity constraints
We now discharge D1–D10, marking each as a theorem (derived), definitional (built into a
primitive), or axiom (a constitutive choice, shown consistent and load-bearing). The honest
headline: nine of the ten are derivable from the primitives and rules; the tenth, fallibilism,
cannot be a theorem, and we explain why — and show it is the premise the creativity theorem
consumes.
D1 (Problems first) — definitional, with a transition lemma.
Problems are primitive nodes (Definition 3.2); the (Conj) rule takes a problem as premise,
so no conjecture transition is enabled in a state with Π = ∅, and (Spawn) keeps Π replen-
ished. The motor is structural, not decorative.
D2 (Conjecture unconstrained) — derived structural property .
γ enters the calculus only as the first-class parameter of Definition 3.3, constrained solely
by openness (Theorem 4.1); no rule inspects its internals and provenance is never a warrant
type (Definition 3.4), so no generator is privileged. Theorem 4.1 shows the only structural
requirement is universality, not any mechanism, and Remark in § 4 forbids identifying
novelty with randomness.
D3 (Criticism universal, criticizable) — definitional; reinstatement a theorem.

D7 (F allibilism, no final state) — axiom; not derivable, and that is the point.
N1 and N2 (§ 3.5), with (Refl) extending them to the system’s own rules. One cannot derive

is thus the single irreducible commitment of the system, and open-ended creativity is its
consequence.

6 Constructor theory: what it contributes, and what it cannot
The instruction asked whether the constructor theories of knowledge, information, computation,
and natural selection are instructive here, and to include them if so. The verdict is a qualified
yes, with a sharp boundary.
Included, and load-bearing, as Level 0.
 Substrate independence. The task/possibility framing is exactly what makes “the same
process on any substrate” rigorous rather than hand-waved. Theorem 2.2 (knowledge) and
12

=====PAGE BREAK=====

Corollary 4.2 (creativity) are constructor-theoretic in spirit: they turn “brains, computers,
aliens” from a slogan into a consequence of attribute-level possibility plus the universality
axiom.
 The definition of knowledge. Knowledge as resilient information (Definition 2.3, Deutsch–
Marletto) gives a substrate-neutral criterion for “the knowledge persists,” which the dy-
namics needs and which neither set theory nor argumentation theory supplies.
 The digitality of the content layer. Theorem 2.1 (Marletto’s constructor theory of life)
derives that resilient-knowledge media must be discrete and error-correctable. This is why
the content layer is Σ ∗ — a theorem, not a modelling convenience — and the same result
explains why genes are digital and why any alien hereditary medium must be.
 Natural selection. Marletto’s account shows that the appearance of design is constructor-
theoretically possible: no-design laws permit knowledge-creation, with the genetic sub-
strate forced digital. In § 4 this becomes the uncoupled instance of the knowledge-growth
schema, and constructor theory supplies the explanation of its digital substrate.
Not load-bearing for the dynamics, and why this is principled. Constructor theory is,
by design, a theory of which transformations are possible — counterfactual, atemporal, stated
at the level of physical law. It deliberately abstracts away from “what actually happens” and
from the unfolding of a process in time. But epistemology-as-creativity is irreducibly processual:
the conjecture–criticism loop, adjudication, reinstatement, and the trajectory ( Gt, HV, R) are
statements about dynamics over time , not about task possibility, and they cannot be phrased as
possible/impossible verdicts without losing exactly their content. So constructor theory governs
the statics — the substrate, the definition of knowledge, the digitality constraint — while the
invented calculus governs the dynamics of knowledge growth. Neither subsumes the other; the
architecture of §§ 2–3 reflects the division.
The complementary punchline. There is a clean complementarity between the two strata
on the question of creativity itself. Constructor theory establishes that creativity is possible:
a universal constructor exists (Axiom 1), knowledge can be resilient (Definition 2.3), and the
appearance of design can arise from no-design laws. What it does not and cannot establish is that
creativity is necessary or open-ended, because that is a dynamical claim about a process under
fallibilist pressure. Theorem 4.1 supplies exactly the dynamical necessity that the constructor-
theoretic possibility result leaves open. Constructor theory says creativity can happen and
that its substrate must be digital and universal; the calculus says that, given fallibilism and
the solubility of problems, creativity must happen and must be open-ended and substrate-
independent. The earlier instinct to drop constructor theory as the load-bearing language for
the dialectic is thereby vindicated and made precise: it is the right theory of the substrate and
the wrong theory of the process.
7 Synthesis
The picture is one schema over one kind of substrate. At Level 0, a digital, universal medium
can carry resilient knowledge, substrate-independently; that knowledge can be created from
no-design laws. At Level 1, knowledge grows by a fallibilist conjecture–criticism process whose

acceptance is tentative, whose refutations are demotions and generate problems, and whose
standards and rules are themselves inside the process. The knowledge-growth schema K has
biological evolution as its blind, limited-reach instance and explanatory thought as its coupled,
potentially-unbounded-reach instance; engineered systems running the calculus are a third in-
stance, and any alien universal explainer is the content of the substrate-independence corollary.
The single sentence the construction defends is this: a fallibilist error-correcting process over a
digital, universal substrate must possess an open-ended, substrate-independent creative faculty,
and that faculty together with criticism is what the growth of knowledge is — whether the
substrate is carbon, silicon, or something we have not met. Creativity is not the part of the
story that had to be added; it is the part that could not have been left out.
