# Series Research Dossier — "The Opportunity Party's 2026 Policies"

> Stage 01 planning artifact for a SERIES that explains the policy platform of **The Opportunity Party
> (TOP)** — New Zealand — heading into the 2026 general election. Audience: aspiring "policy wonks" who
> want to understand the *mechanisms* well enough to discuss them with others. **Not a history lesson** —
> explain what the policies ARE, what PROBLEM each solves, and how each FITS the NZ context. Faithful to
> TOP's rationale, but neutral: attribute claims to TOP and present the main critiques so a reader can
> actually debate. Compiled June 2026 from TOP material + NZ news/analysis (sources §9).

---

## 1. Audience & scope

**Reader:** a smart New Zealander (or interested outsider) with general civic awareness but **no
economics background**, who wants to follow and *discuss* these policies confidently — not parrot
slogans. **Assumes:** basic familiarity with everyday NZ life (rates, rent, KiwiSaver, the cost of
living). **Excludes:** party history/founding/leadership drama (only the lightest "who they are now");
formal economic math; deep tax-law detail. **Calibration:** mechanism-first (how does it actually
work?), then the problem it targets, then the NZ fit, then the honest trade-offs. Every concept lands
in plain words with a concrete NZ example before any jargon.

## 2. Who TOP is (light touch — not a history)

A small NZ party positioned as **"radical centrism"** / **"blue-green"** (free-market efficiency
arguments married to redistribution and environmentalism). Rebranded from **"The Opportunities Party"**
to **"The Opportunity Party"** (still "TOP"); led into 2026 by **Qiulae Wong**. Has never crossed the
5% MMP threshold (2.4% / 1.5% / 2.2% in 2017/2020/2023); 2026 polling ~3–6%. It pitches itself as a
potential kingmaker that would trade support for adoption of its signature policies. *Keep this to a
paragraph; the course is about the policies, not the party's story.*

## 3. The shared frame — TOP's diagnosis (for the Essentials course)

TOP's policies hang off one connected diagnosis of what's gone wrong in NZ. The whole series reuses it:

1. **The tax system rewards owning land over working or building a business.** NZ taxes wages up to
   39% and consumption (GST) at 15%, but taxes capital gains at **0%** — no comprehensive capital
   gains tax, no land tax, no inheritance/estate/stamp/wealth tax. So the rational move is to pour
   money into property, not productive enterprise.
2. **Housing has become the national investment vehicle**, pricing out the young and concentrating
   wealth in those who already own. ~48% of household assets sit in property; homeownership for
   under-40s has collapsed since 1991.
3. **Wealth is highly concentrated and lightly taxed at the top.** IRD's 2023 study of the 311
   wealthiest families found an effective tax rate of **8.9%** vs **~20%** for a median household —
   because ~80% of their income was untaxed capital gains.
4. **The burden falls on wages and consumption** (income tax + GST = ~90% of revenue; GST is
   regressive), while a **cost-of-living crisis** squeezes households and a **fiscal time-bomb**
   (aging population, rising Super costs, weak productivity, emigration to Australia) looms.
5. **Politics swings like a pendulum** — each election reverses the last, wasting money and momentum.

**The shared toolkit (concepts every course reuses), in plain words:**
- **Tax base** — *what* gets taxed (income, spending, land, gains). TOP's core move is to shift the
  base toward **land**.
- **Income vs. wealth/asset taxation** — taxing what you *earn* vs. what you *own/own's growth*.
- **Capital gain / CGT** — the profit when an asset (esp. a house) is sold for more than it cost; NZ
  largely doesn't tax it.
- **Land value tax (LVT)** — an annual tax on the *unimproved value of land* (the worth of the spot,
  not the building on it). TOP's signature idea.
- **Universal Basic Income (UBI)** — a regular, (near-)unconditional payment to everyone; TOP's
  version is the **Citizen's Income**.
- **Incentive & economic distortion ("deadweight")** — taxes change behaviour and can waste value;
  the classic argument *for* LVT is that land can't flee or shrink, so taxing it barely distorts
  behaviour (near-zero deadweight) — unlike taxing work or sales.
- **Means-testing** — restricting a payment by income/assets (TOP largely removes it for the CI;
  *adds* it to Super).
- **Tax incidence / pass-through** — who *really* bears a tax (e.g. do landlords pass an LVT to
  tenants? LVT theory says largely no, because land supply is fixed).

### 4A. The Tax Reset (flagship — an integrated package)
TOP's central policy: three interlocking parts designed to work as one system (the land tax pays for
the income).

**(i) Citizen's Income (CI) — TOP's UBI.** *What:* **$370/week (~$19,400/yr)**, paid (near-)tax-free
to almost all adults; **means-tested only above ~$350,000 income**. Replaces most welfare (Jobseeker,
Student Allowance, etc.) **and NZ Super** (with a transitional top-up for current superannuitants). A
unified **Child Support Income** (~$350/week in a child's first year) folds in Best Start/Family Tax
Credit/Paid Parental Leave. Gross cost ~**$62.8B/yr**. *Problem:* cost-of-living, welfare complexity,
means-testing stigma and "abatement traps" (losing benefit as you earn). *NZ fit:* a simple floor
under everyone in a high-cost economy. *Critiques:* the level is near poverty/Jobseeker rate and
**inadequate for disabled people** (Supported Living recipients can get ~$800/wk today); replacing
universal **Super** is a political third rail and a cut for many retirees; an independent analyst found
the costings imply ~3.2M recipients vs ~4.1M adults — **~900k may not qualify** `[verify]`; removing
progressive welfare entitlements worries the left.

**(ii) Land Value Tax (LVT).** *What:* **1.75%/yr on urban land value, 0.5% on rural** — on the
*unimproved land*, not the house/buildings. Projected **~$24B/yr** (≈$4B surplus after the CI).
**Deferral** for "land-rich, cash-poor" owners (accrue against the title, pay on sale) `[verify: interest
on deferred balances]`. *Problem:* land-banking and speculation; capital flowing into housing instead
of business; untaxed housing wealth. *NZ fit:* NZ already revalues land triennially (councils), and
historically *used* land-value rating — so it's administratively feasible; sidesteps the politically
toxic CGT. *Critiques:* the **asset-rich/cash-poor** retiree on a high-value Auckland section faces a
big annual bill (deferral eases but can force eventual downsizing); taxes only **one asset class**
(not shares/financial wealth); **land/building valuation is hard and disputed**; **Māori land** in
collective title raises fairness questions `[verify exemptions]`; revenue rides on land prices
(volatility). TOP rates may have moved under new leadership `[verify 2026 rate]`.

**(iii) Income tax + KiwiSaver 2.0.** *What:* simplify PAYE to **three brackets — 28% to $50k, 34%
$50k–$200k, 39% over $200k** `[verify thresholds]`; combined with the CI, TOP calls this "the largest
tax cut for low-income workers in history." **KiwiSaver becomes compulsory** at **6%+6% = 12%** (phased
over ~8 years), aiming at a "trillion-dollar" capital pool for infrastructure. *Problem:* a clunky
5-bracket system; thin retirement savings; under-investment in productive capital. *Critiques:* a 28%
bottom rate is *higher* than today's 10.5%/17.5% (offset by the CI, but the net depends on thresholds);
compulsory saving is a squeeze on low earners' take-home pay. *Headline claim to attribute:* TOP says
**70% of households end up better off, 20% unchanged, 10% pay more** (TOP's own modelling, not
independently verified).

### 4B. Affordable Housing
*What:* the LVT is the main lever (makes idle/under-used land expensive to hold), plus **supply-side**
reform: simpler nationally-consistent **zoning**, easier **consenting** for tiny homes/sleepouts/
extensions, support for off-site/modular builders, government **underwriting** of reputable affordable
developments to smooth the boom-bust cycle, infrastructure (water) for density, and a new **Urban
Development Act** to succeed the RMA. *Problem:* an ~80,000-home shortage and the developed world's
worst affordability (price-to-income ~7x; Auckland ~$1M). *NZ fit:* targets NZ's specific zoning/
consenting bottlenecks and the leaky-building legacy. *Critiques:* upzoning fights local opposition;
falling prices (TOP, like the Housing Minister, accepts prices should fall) hurt recent buyers;
supply reform is slow.

### 4C. Abundant Energy
*What:* a **25-year bipartisan energy strategy**; build **300% of current renewable capacity (~30 GW)
by 2050**; market reform to unlock renewable investment; a **Ratepayer/household electrification**
assistance scheme (low-interest loans). Grid idea: *overbuild* wind/solar and keep hydro lakes as a
responsive reserve. *Problem:* decades of under-investment, price spikes, and business uncertainty
("the number one thing business asks for"). *NZ fit:* NZ is already ~80–85% renewable electricity, so
deep decarbonisation and electrification are within reach. *Critiques:* huge capital cost; "300%"/30 GW
is ambitious; bipartisanship is hard to guarantee.

### 4D. Healthy Oceans
*What:* protect **30% of NZ's ocean by 2035** (from ~0.5% today); **end bottom trawling** (phased,
with worker transition support); ecosystem-based fisheries management; an **Ocean's Secretariat**;
**$500M into aquaculture** and a stated **$500M/yr** blue-economy upside. *Problem:* tiny marine
protection, destructive trawling, declining stocks, under-used "blue economy." *NZ fit:* NZ has one of
the world's largest EEZs; framed explicitly as *economic* policy (jobs in aquaculture/tourism/
biotech). *Critiques:* fishing-industry opposition and transition cost; enforcement over a vast EEZ.

### 4E. Citizens' Voice (democracy reform)
*What:* government-convened **Citizens' Assemblies** — randomly selected, paid, representative groups
who study a gridlocked issue (hearing experts and affected people), deliberate, and make
recommendations the government must formally respond to — overseen by a new **Parliamentary
Commissioner for Citizens' Voice**. *Problem:* "pendulum politics" waste (cited: ~$229M abandoned
Auckland light rail, ~$300M cancelled ferries, ~$297M polytechnic reversal) and chronic gridlock on
big issues (Super, housing, climate). *NZ fit:* aims at NZ's short 3-year cycle and lurching reversals.
*Models:* Ireland's assembly (marriage equality, abortion), France's climate convention. *Critiques:*
assemblies are advisory (Parliament still decides); risk of being ignored or captured; adds machinery.

### 4F. The Teal Card (youth) + Transport + Drug reform (people policies)
- **Teal Card** (under-30s, ~$1.5B): free GP visits, primary **dental**, eye checks/glasses, mental
  health; **free public transport**; a **$1,500** bike/e-bike credit; a **$5,000 savings boost at 18**
  unlocked by completing a **National Civic Service** (Outward-Bound-style: conservation, civil
  defence, civics, personal finance). *Problem:* young people's health, exclusion, and insecure start.
  *Critiques:* cost; tying money to mandatory service; age-cutoff fairness.
- **Transport:** **permanent free public transport** nationwide; **road-user charging** to replace
  fuel tax; "intergenerational infrastructure" (long-term, less stop-start). *Critiques:* funding the
  free-transport bill; RUC complexity.
- **Drug reform:** harm-minimisation; **legalise cannabis**; decriminalise small quantities; fund
  kaupapa Māori addiction services. *Critiques:* the usual public-safety debate; 2020 referendum
  narrowly rejected cannabis legalisation.

## 5. The NZ context backbone (facts the series leans on)

- **Tax gaps (NZ is an OECD outlier):** no comprehensive **CGT** (only NZ & Belgium lack one), no
  inheritance/estate tax, no stamp duty, no wealth tax, no national land tax since 1991. **GST 15%**,
  broad-based (even food/rent) and **regressive**. Income tax up to **39%**; **capital gains 0%**.
- **Effective-rate gap:** IRD 2023 — wealthiest 311 families **8.9%** effective vs **~20%** median
  household; ~80% of their income was capital gains.
- **Housing:** price-to-income ~**7x** (Auckland ~8.6x; median ~$1M in Auckland); homeownership ~**66%**
  (was ~75% in 1991; under-40s collapsed 20+ points); ~**48%** of household assets in property.
- **Wealth:** top **10%** hold ~**49%** of net worth; bottom 20% ~$11k median assets.
- **CGT politics:** 2019 Tax Working Group recommended a CGT; **Ardern killed it** (NZ First veto + "no
  mandate"). Labour now proposes a **narrow CGT** (28% on investment property, from 2027); National/
  ACT/NZ First rule CGT/wealth tax out; Greens propose a wealth + inheritance tax. ~65% public support
  a CGT in some form; only ~13% on the family home.
- **Why housing is tax-favoured now:** the National-led govt cut the bright-line test back to **2 years**
  (2024) and restored **100% interest deductibility** (2025) — re-tilting toward property. (A 1989 switch
  to taxing retirement-savings returns annually, while leaving owner-occupied housing untaxed, is the
  deeper structural tilt — mention as *context*, not a history lecture.)
- **NZ once led on land tax** (land-value rating was widespread for ~90 years, abandoned ~1984–91) —
  useful one-line context for "this isn't unprecedented."
- **Fiscal pressure:** aging population (worker:retiree ratio falling toward ~2:1), rising Super costs,
  bottom-quartile productivity, net emigration of NZ citizens (~45k/yr, mostly to Australia).

## 7. Candidate SHARED METAPHORS (reusable, each with its guard)

1. **A tax is a signpost (a toll that changes which road people take).** *Referent:* incentives/
   distortion — taxes steer behaviour. *Guard:* not all taxes steer equally; LVT's whole claim is that
   it *barely* moves behaviour because land can't be hidden or moved.
2. **Taxing the worth of the *spot*, not the house on it.** *Referent:* the LVT base (unimproved land
   value, created largely by community/infrastructure). *Guard:* it does NOT tax your building or
   renovations, and it's a tax on land *value*, not on "having a home."
3. **A floor you stand on vs. a safety net with holes.** *Referent:* the Citizen's Income as an
   unconditional base, vs. means-tested welfare with gaps/traps. *Guard:* a floor's *height* matters —
   TOP's floor is low; it doesn't by itself lift everyone to comfort.
4. **Scales tilted toward property over work.** *Referent:* the effective-rate gap (wage earner vs
   landlord). *Guard:* it's about the system's *structure/incentives*, not that property owners are
   villains.
5. **Land can't run away.** *Referent:* land as an immobile tax base (unlike income/capital that can
   move offshore or restructure). *Guard:* it's the *value* of the location, not the dirt, and "can't
   flee" doesn't make a big bill painless for the cash-poor.
6. **Pendulum politics.** *Referent:* the lurch-and-reverse of NZ's short electoral cycle (the case for
   Citizens' Voice). *Guard:* not all change is waste; some reversal is healthy democracy.

## 8. Accuracy & neutrality notes (the honesty backbone)

1. **Attribute.** Use "TOP proposes/argues/claims…" for the party's positions and especially its own
   modelling (e.g. the **70/20/10** split, "$24B revenue", "50,000 families out of poverty"). Mark
   independent facts (IRD effective rates, Stats NZ housing data) as such.
2. **Always pair a policy with its main critique.** The reader's goal is to *discuss*, so every policy
   module must fairly present the strongest objections (asset-rich/cash-poor; CI adequacy for disabled;
   single-asset-class; valuation difficulty; means-testing Super; cost) — without the course taking a
   side.
3. **Don't campaign.** Explain, don't advocate or attack. No "you should support/oppose."
4. **Distinguish mechanism from claim.** Be precise that an LVT taxes *unimproved land value annually*,
   that a CI is *near-unconditional*, etc. — getting the mechanism right is the whole point for a wonk.
5. **Currency caveat.** These are 2025–2026 platform figures; note they can change and flag `[verify]`
   items: exact 2026 LVT rate under new leadership; income-tax thresholds; the ~900k CI-eligibility
   gap; interest on deferred LVT; Māori-land exemptions; some long-run fiscal projections.
6. **No history lesson.** Party founding/leadership/"cat policy" etc. are out of scope; the 1989 tax
   switch and NZ's old land tax are allowed only as one-line *context* for why a policy fits NZ.
