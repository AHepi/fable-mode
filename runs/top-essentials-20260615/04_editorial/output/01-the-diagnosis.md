Meet Aroha and Glen. They are both 35. They grew up in the same town. Both work hard.

Aroha is a nurse. Every fortnight, money lands in her account — wages for her shifts. She pays rent: three hundred and seventy dollars a week for a small flat. She saves when she can, but after rent, food, and power, there is not much left over.

Glen retired early from plumbing. He owns the flat Aroha rents, plus the house next door, and a bare section two streets away. The three properties have risen steadily in value over the past ten years. Glen has not done a lot with them — he patches things when they break and collects the rent. The money he makes from those rising values is not wages. It is a different kind of money, and the rules treat it differently.

After ten years, Aroha has worked her whole career and saved what she could. Glen has grown considerably wealthier — not through harder work, but through *owning things that quietly went up in value*.

Neither of them has done anything wrong. That is exactly the point.

## Who is TOP, and why does this story matter?

**The Opportunity Party** — or **TOP** — is a small New Zealand political party. (It was previously called The Opportunities Party, but it kept the short name TOP.) Heading into the 2026 election it is led by Qiulae Wong. TOP describes itself as "radical centrism" and "blue-green," meaning it combines free-market thinking with ideas about redistribution and the environment.

TOP has never crossed the 5% threshold to enter Parliament on its own, but it is polling at a level that could make it a deciding vote in a close election. This course is about its *policies*, not the party's history — so that brief sketch is all you need.

What TOP keeps asking is: why did Glen end up so much better off than Aroha, when both lived under the same rules?

TOP's answer is that the rules were tilted in Glen's favour — not because Glen or Aroha did anything right or wrong, but because of how the system is set up.

## The big idea — before any jargon

Here is TOP's core claim, in the plainest words possible:

**In New Zealand, you tend to get ahead faster by owning things — especially land and houses — than by working for a living. And TOP argues that is not an accident. It is built into the rules.**

When Aroha earns her wages, a portion is immediately taken by the government. That payment is called a **tax** — a compulsory amount the government collects and uses to pay for things like roads, hospitals, and schools. Aroha pays this on every dollar she earns at work.

When Glen's properties rise in value, he is also better off — by tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars over the years. But under New Zealand's current rules, that growing value is largely not taxed the way Aroha's wages are.

So Aroha's income is taxed every fortnight. Glen's wealth grows, mostly untouched.

TOP argues this is a structural problem — baked into the rules, not anyone's personal failing.

## The scales

Here is a picture that will come up throughout this series. Think of an old-fashioned weighing scale — two pans.

On one side: **working and earning**. Wages, salaries, business income. These are taxed.

On the other side: **owning land and property**. The value that grows as a house or section increases in price. Largely not taxed.

TOP's argument is that the rules press down hard on the working side and barely touch the owning side. The **scales are tilted toward property over work**.

One important point about this image: it is not saying Glen is a villain. He made a completely rational choice inside the rules as they existed. The scales metaphor is about *structure* — a set of rules built up over decades. TOP's argument is that the rules need to change, not that people like Glen are bad.

## A gentle introduction to "capital gains"

There is a term you will hear a lot in this series, and it is worth planting gently here.

Imagine you buy a second-hand bike for 100 dollars. A year later, you sell it for 150 dollars. That extra 50 dollars — the profit on the sale — is called a **capital gain**. "Capital" just means an asset you own. The "gain" is the profit when you sell it for more than you paid.

Now imagine the same thing, but the asset is a house. You bought it for 400,000 dollars. A decade later, it is worth 800,000 dollars. That extra 400,000 dollars is also a capital gain.

In many countries, you pay a tax when you make a capital gain like that. New Zealand, for most of its history and for most assets, has not taxed that profit.

Glen's growing wealth is largely in the form of capital gains — profits on rising property values — that are not taxed the way Aroha's wages are. Module 2 will explain this properly. For now, just notice the pattern.

## How this connects to housing costs

If owning property is a better way to build wealth than working, then it is rational for people to put money into property — and they have. According to Stats NZ, roughly 48% of all household wealth in New Zealand sits in property.

When more and more money chases a limited supply of houses and land, prices go up. And when prices go up, buying a first home gets further out of reach. Home ownership among New Zealanders under 40 has fallen sharply since the early 1990s. A whole generation has been largely priced out.

People who cannot afford to buy end up renting — often from people like Glen. Higher house prices push up rents. The people paying the highest rents tend to be the ones who can least afford it.

TOP's view is that the housing shortage and the cost of living are not two separate problems. They are two faces of the same structural tilt.

## A word about pendulum politics

There is one more thread in TOP's diagnosis. The full picture is in Module 3, but here is the short version.

New Zealand's governments tend to reverse each other's big decisions. One government starts a long-term project. The next government cancels it. Money is spent and then wasted. Problems that need patient, long-term solutions get short, interrupted attempts.

TOP calls this **pendulum politics** — the way the system swings back and forth without ever settling.

It connects to the structural tilt because fixing something like the tax rules requires a change that holds across multiple elections. Pendulum politics makes that very hard.

More on this in Module 3.

**Exercise 3.** A friend says: "People who own property worked hard to buy it — they deserve to benefit." How does TOP's argument respond to that, without attacking property owners?

<details><summary>Example answer</summary>

TOP would agree that many people worked hard to buy property. The structural argument is not about whether someone deserved to buy — it is about what happens *after* they buy. Once you own property, the rules mean your wealth grows in a largely untaxed way, while someone earning wages keeps paying tax on every dollar. TOP says the gap between the two is what is unfair — and changing the rules would not punish past choices; it would change the incentives going forward.

## Recap

TOP's diagnosis, in plain words: in New Zealand, the rules have tended to reward owning land and property more than working for a living — not because property owners are greedy, but because of how the tax system is set up. Working is taxed; the growing value of land and property is largely not. Over time, that tilt has pushed money into housing, concentrated wealth among those who already own, and squeezed people who earn wages.

Module 2 gives you the vocabulary to understand what TOP proposes to do about it — one term at a time, each one defined before it is used. Module 3 brings in the New Zealand numbers and maps the full platform.
