Imagine two neighbours. One is a teacher — she gets up every morning and goes to work, pays income tax on every dollar she earns, and at the end of the month puts whatever is left toward rent: 370 dollars a week for a two-bedroom in a mid-sized New Zealand city. The other is a retired electrician who owns that house, and the one next door, and a section two streets over. He pays no tax on the value of the land sitting under those properties. He pays no tax when the value rises by a hundred thousand dollars in a single year. He writes off the mortgage interest against his taxable income. And when he eventually sells, he pays no tax on the gain.

Neither neighbour has done anything wrong. That is exactly the point.

## Who TOP is

The Opportunity Party — TOP — is a small New Zealand party that describes itself as "radical centrism" and "blue-green": free-market efficiency arguments married to redistribution and environmental policy. It was formerly called The Opportunities Party, still abbreviated TOP. Led into the 2026 election by Qiulae Wong, it has not yet crossed the 5% MMP threshold, though 2026 polling puts it in the range that could make it a kingmaker. The course is about the policies, not the party's story, so that is all you need to know about TOP itself. Everything else follows from one question the party keeps asking: why does the New Zealand economy work the way it does for those two neighbours?

## The diagnosis, in plain words

TOP's answer is this: **the tax system rewards owning land over working or building a business**. Wages are taxed. Spending is taxed. But capital gains — the profit you make when an asset rises in value — are largely untaxed. New Zealand has no comprehensive capital gains tax, no inheritance tax, no stamp duty, and no national land tax (the last one was wound down between the mid-1980s and 1991). So income tax rates go up to 39%, the GST (goods and services tax) sits at 15% on almost everything you buy, and the gain on a million-dollar Auckland house is 0%.

TOP argues that this arrangement is not an accident or an oversight. It is a signal — built into the structure of the economy — about where to put your money. And New Zealanders have listened.

## The scales metaphor — and its limits

Picture a set of old-fashioned scales. On one side: wages, business income, consumer spending. On the other: land, property, capital gains. For decades, TOP argues, the tax system has pressed down on the first side and left the second side nearly empty. The scales are tilted.

That image is worth holding, because every TOP policy is in some way an attempt to rebalance it. But notice what the metaphor does and does not say. It points at a **structure** — a set of rules and incentives that have existed for a generation. It is not a claim that landlords are villains or that people who own property have done something wrong. The teacher's neighbour is rational. Given the rules, he made a sensible choice. TOP's argument is that the rules themselves are the problem, and that changing them would change the choices.

## How housing became the national investment

When wages are taxed and capital gains are not, a logical consequence follows: money flows toward property rather than into businesses, innovation, or productive enterprise. According to Stats NZ data, roughly 48% of New Zealand household assets now sit in property. For comparison, the sharemarket — where businesses raise capital to hire people and build things — draws a fraction of that.

The consequences stack up. As more money chases residential property, prices rise. According to Stats NZ housing data, the price-to-income ratio has reached roughly seven times annual household income nationally, and higher in Auckland. Home ownership among New Zealanders under 40 has fallen sharply since the early 1990s — a generational shift that transfers wealth toward those who already owned before prices accelerated, and away from those trying to enter now.

IRD research from 2023 found that the 311 wealthiest New Zealand families paid an effective tax rate of roughly 8.9% — compared to around 20% for a median household. The reason, the research found, was that approximately 80% of their income came in the form of capital gains, which were untaxed. That is the effective-rate gap in a single statistic.

TOP's framing is that this is not primarily a story about individual greed. It is a story about what the system makes rational. When the tax code leaves one side of the scales nearly empty, capital pools on the light side.

## The cost-of-living squeeze — part of the same story

For people who do not own property, the tilt has a different face: the cost of living. Rents rise with house prices. The GST — which applies to food, power, and most everyday spending — hits lower-income households harder in proportion to their income (economists call this "regressive"). Meanwhile, wages have to compete with the returns available from property, and the productive businesses that might otherwise bid for labour face higher costs and less capital.

The result, TOP argues, is a cost-of-living squeeze that is not a separate problem from the housing shortage or the wealth gap. They are the same story, viewed from different angles. A young couple priced out of buying a home is paying more in rent, which transfers wealth to whoever owns the building, whose capital gain remains untaxed. The flows all run in the same direction.

Higher property prices push up rents. The GST — which applies broadly — then taxes the income people do earn when they spend it. So the same structural tilt that inflates house prices also constrains wages and squeezes everyday budgets. They are symptoms of the same underlying incentive structure, not separate policy failures that happen to occur at the same time.

## A word on pendulum politics

There is one more strand in TOP's diagnosis — though you will get the full version in Module 3, which owns it. TOP argues that New Zealand's short three-year electoral cycle makes the structural tilt worse, not better, because each incoming government tends to reverse major policies of the last. Reforms begun under one government are unwound by the next, wasting money and institutional momentum. TOP's argument is that the structural problems — the tax base, the housing incentives, the productivity gap — require changes that outlast electoral cycles, and that the political system as currently designed resists exactly those changes.

That is the "pendulum politics" thread. File it as a foreshadow; it returns in Module 3.

<details><summary>Show a strong answer</summary>

TOP's structural argument would say: rational people respond to incentives, and the incentives built into the New Zealand tax system make property investment more rewarding (after tax) than most alternatives. The landlord in the opening example isn't doing anything illegal or even unusual — he's responding logically to a set of rules that have been in place for decades.

This distinction matters practically. If the problem is individual greed, the solution is moral pressure or social disapproval — neither of which changes tax law. If the problem is structural incentives, the solution is to change the rules. TOP argues for the structural diagnosis precisely because it points toward a policy remedy (shifting the tax base) rather than a character judgment (which changes nothing).

A strong response also notes that the structural framing does not *excuse* consequences — the teacher-neighbour paying 370 dollars a week in rent still faces real hardship — but locates the cause in the system rather than the individual.

## Recap

TOP's diagnosis is the thread that runs through every policy in this series: the New Zealand tax system rewards owning land over working or building a business, and that structural tilt has channelled capital into housing, concentrated wealth, and squeezed the cost of living for those who earn wages rather than capital gains. Attributing this to a structural incentive — not to individual greed — is what makes a policy response possible. The scales metaphor captures the shape of the problem: pressed down on wages and spending, barely touched on land and capital gains. Modules 2 and 3 give you the tools to understand what TOP proposes to do about it, and the NZ facts that explain why they think these particular tools fit.
