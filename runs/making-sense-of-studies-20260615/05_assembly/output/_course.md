"New study links X to Y." "The effect was statistically significant." "The poll has a margin of
error of three points." These phrases are everywhere, and almost all of us misread at least one of
them — often the most important one. This short course is about reading them correctly.

It's the second mini-course in the *Statistics, From the Ground Up* series, and it stands on its own:
where it leans on a shared idea — that results wobble from one sample to the next, that the job is to
tell a real signal from random noise — it reminds you in a line. (If you want the full grounding, the
*Statistics Essentials* course is the warm-up.) Across three short modules we take apart the machinery
behind a study's claims: the **null hypothesis** and the **p-value** (what "could this just be chance?"
really asks), why **statistical significance** is not the same as a big or important effect, and how to
read a **confidence interval** without falling for the tempting misreading almost everyone makes.

There's no arithmetic to grind here. The whole point is the careful meaning of a few famous numbers —
because the difference between what a p-value *is* and what people *think* it is, is exactly the
difference between being informed and being fooled.
