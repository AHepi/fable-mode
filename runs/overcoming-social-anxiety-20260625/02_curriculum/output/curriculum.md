# Curriculum — Overcoming Social Anxiety: A Field Manual

## Course metadata

- **title:** Overcoming Social Anxiety: A Field Manual
- **slug:** overcoming-social-anxiety
- **description:** A practice-first set of social-confidence skills you rehearse in real-world drills with real people, built on current evidence, not a substitute for therapy.
- **level:** intro  *(general adult, no psychology background; skill kind = "no prior exposure")*
- **kind:** skill  *(framework + walkthrough + live field drill + rubric self-assessment; no math, no formal proofs)*
- **subject:** "Personal Development"
- **prerequisites (plain-language):**
  - None. If you've ever dreaded a party, blanked in a meeting, or replayed a conversation at 2 a.m., you have everything you need.
  - A willingness to do small, slightly uncomfortable things with real people between sessions.
- **tags:** [social-anxiety, social-skills, anxiety, cbt, exposure, communication, confidence, wellbeing, practice]
- **estimatedHours:** 3.5

## Design rationale (backward design)

The user asked for **less theory, more practiceable skill**, **cutting edge**, and skills that can be
**practiced live with others**. So: exactly one short module carries the model (the anxiety loop), and
it exists only to let every later module say *which link in the loop this skill breaks*. Every other
module is a skill the learner performs with real people, and **every module ends in a live field drill
scored by the same rubric** (the canon fixes it once). "Cutting edge" is honored by building on the
current mechanism science — the inhibitory-learning model of exposure (success = a *violated
prediction*, not a calm body), deliberate social-mishap and rejection practice, ACT, and
self-compassion — each graded honestly for how well-supported it is. Because the topic spans everyday
nerves to clinical social anxiety disorder, a **safety/responsibility layer** (skills not therapy, when
to seek a professional, drills stay small and ethical) is front-loaded in module 1 and reinforced in the
mishap module and the close.

## Module specs (each ends in a LIVE field drill + the shared rubric)

### M1 — Your Anxiety Loop
- **slug:** `01-your-anxiety-loop`
- **summary:** "Social anxiety is kept alive by a four-part loop you can learn to spot in yourself, and the whole course is a set of tools for breaking each link."
- **objectives:**
  - Name the four parts of the anxiety loop (inward attention, safety behaviors, catastrophic predictions, the post-event replay).
  - Spot your own self-focused attention and one of your own safety behaviors in a real interaction.
  - State plainly what this course is and is not, and when to seek a professional.
- **prereqs:** none
- **owns (canon):** the anxiety-loop model and its four named links; the **safety/responsibility framing** (skills-not-therapy, when-to-seek-help); the loop-spiral image. Front-loads the safety block.
- **field drill:** observe one real interaction this week — note which way your attention pointed and name one crutch you used. (No change attempted yet; pure noticing.)
- **est:** 20 min

### M2 — Steady the Body
- **slug:** `02-steady-the-body`
- **summary:** "A precise, exhale-led breath (the physiological sigh) takes the edge off the body's alarm in about thirty seconds, used as a doorway in, not a way to make the anxiety go away."
- **objectives:**
  - Perform the physiological sigh correctly (double inhale, long exhale).
  - Explain why an exhale-led breath lowers arousal, and why this is an adjunct, not a cure.
  - Use the breath as an on-ramp to action rather than a way to avoid feeling anxious.
- **prereqs:** M1
- **owns (canon):** the physiological sigh / extended-exhale tool; the "doorway not off-switch" framing; the brake-tap image.
- **field drill:** use two physiological sighs right before a real social entry this week, then go in anyway.
- **est:** 14 min

### M3 — Turn the Spotlight Outward
- **slug:** `03-turn-the-spotlight-outward`
- **summary:** "Anxiety runs on attention pointed at yourself; the trainable counter-skill is parking your attention on the other person and the room, and the spotlight you imagine on you is dimmer than it feels."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain how self-focused attention fuels anxiety and how external focus starves it.
  - Run the "three external details" attention-shift drill on demand.
  - State the spotlight effect and use it to recalibrate how much others notice you.
- **prereqs:** M1, M3
- **owns (canon):** the attention-shift skill (external/task focus); the **spotlight effect** hook; the "turn the spotlight outward" image.
- **field drill:** in a real conversation, each time you catch yourself self-monitoring, name three concrete external things (what they said, an object, a sound).
- **est:** 18 min

### M4 — Start a Conversation
- **slug:** `04-start-a-conversation`
- **summary:** "Openings feel high-stakes and clever-or-bust, but low-risk observations about the shared situation work fine, and strangers are friendlier and more glad to talk than we predict."
- **objectives:**
  - Open a conversation with a low-stakes observation or question plus a small piece of "free information."
  - Explain why we underestimate how well conversations with strangers go.
  - Initiate several low-stakes interactions and compare the outcome to the prediction.
- **prereqs:** M1, M3
- **owns (canon):** openers; **free information**; low-stakes initiation; the "underestimated stranger" finding.
- **field drill:** start three short conversations with weak ties or strangers this week (barista, queue, colleague); predict each first, log what happened.
- **est:** 20 min

### M5 — Keep It Going
- **slug:** `05-keep-it-going`
- **summary:** "Conversations carry themselves when you follow up on what the other person hands you: ask the next open question and offer a little of yourself back, instead of hunting for the perfect line."
- **objectives:**
  - Thread a conversation by asking a follow-up question off the other person's "free information."
  - Use reciprocal self-disclosure to deepen a conversation without dominating it.
  - Explain why question-asking and matched disclosure increase how much people like you.
- **prereqs:** M4
- **owns (canon):** **follow-up question-asking**; reciprocal self-disclosure / threading; active listening as an attention skill (callback to M3).
- **field drill:** in one real conversation, ask at least three follow-up questions and offer one piece of yourself back; notice what it does to the conversation.
- **est:** 20 min

### M6 — Warmth, and the Nerves That Don't Show
- **slug:** `06-warmth-and-the-nerves-that-dont-show`
- **summary:** "People judge warmth first and notice your nerves far less than you assume; leading with a genuine smile and open attention beats any posture trick, and the body-language 'rules' you've heard are mostly a myth."
- **objectives:**
  - Lead with controllable warmth signals (smile, open posture, comfortable eye contact, using a name).
  - State the illusion of transparency and use it to discount the feeling that your anxiety is on display.
  - Debunk the "93% of communication is body language" claim and say what actually matters.
- **prereqs:** M1, M3
- **owns (canon):** warmth-first body language; the **illusion of transparency** reframe; the Mehrabian-myth debunk; the "nerves behind glass" image.
- **field drill:** run one interaction leading with warmth signals; predict how visible your nerves were (0–100), then check it against a trusted person's read or an honest self-review.
- **est:** 18 min

### M7 — The Behavioral Experiment
- **slug:** `07-the-behavioral-experiment`
- **summary:** "The master move: turn a social fear into a specific prediction, drop the crutch propping it up, do the thing, and watch reality disagree, which updates the belief in a way that arguing with yourself never could."
- **objectives:**
  - Turn a vague social fear into a specific, falsifiable prediction with a probability and a feared cost.
  - Run the prediction → drop-the-crutch → test → observe → update loop in real life.
  - Explain why success is a violated prediction, not a calm body (the inhibitory-learning point).
- **prereqs:** M1
- **owns (canon):** **the behavioral-experiment rubric** (the four steps — this is the shared field-drill rubric every other module instantiates); **expectancy violation / inhibitory learning**; "act while anxious."
- **field drill:** design and run one full behavioral experiment on a real social fear this week.
- **est:** 22 min

### M8 — Drop the Crutches
- **slug:** `08-drop-the-crutches`
- **summary:** "The little things that make a social moment feel survivable, over-rehearsing, gripping a drink, scanning for exits, only speaking when calm, are the very things keeping the fear alive; the skill is naming them and fading them on purpose."
- **objectives:**
  - Inventory your personal safety behaviors and explain how each blocks disconfirmation.
  - Fade one or more safety behaviors during a real interaction so the test is "clean."
  - Distinguish a safety behavior from a genuine, value-driven choice.
- **prereqs:** M1, M7
- **owns (canon):** the systematic safety-behavior inventory + graded fading (deepens M1's concept and M7's "drop the crutch" step); the training-wheels image.
- **field drill:** pick one situation and run it with one safety behavior deliberately removed; log prediction vs. outcome.
- **est:** 20 min

### M9 — Court the "No"
- **slug:** `09-court-the-no`
- **summary:** "The fastest way to defang the fear of looking foolish or being rejected is to go court it on purpose in small, safe, ethical doses, and discover that the cost you dreaded is survivable and usually trivial."
- **objectives:**
  - Explain why deliberately seeking small social "failures" reduces the fear faster than avoiding them.
  - Design a small, safe, ethical mishap or rejection-seeking drill (never at others' expense).
  - Run one and record predicted versus actual cost.
- **prereqs:** M7, M8
- **owns (canon):** deliberate **mishap drills** + **rejection practice** (the cutting-edge live line); the "small safe dose / vaccine" image; reinforces the safety framing (small, ethical, never harming others).
- **field drill:** perform one small, safe, ethical mishap or rejection-seeking attempt (ask for something likely to get a "no"); the win is hearing it and being fine.
- **est:** 22 min

### M10 — Speak Up
- **slug:** `10-speak-up`
- **summary:** "Saying your view in a group and holding your ground when interrupted is a trainable skill with a simple shape, and asserting yourself both raises confidence and lowers anxiety."
- **objectives:**
  - Distinguish passive, assertive, and aggressive responses.
  - Use a simple assertiveness shape to state a view or make a request, and to reclaim an interruption.
  - Speak up once in a real group or meeting and self-assess.
- **prereqs:** M5, M7
- **owns (canon):** assertiveness (passive/assertive/aggressive distinction; the state-a-view shape); speaking up in groups; handling interruptions.
- **field drill:** state your view or make a request once in a real meeting or group this week.
- **est:** 20 min

### M11 — After the Event
- **slug:** `11-after-the-event`
- **summary:** "The 2 a.m. replay is not learning, it is the loop's last link; the skill is declining the post-event autopsy, meeting the moment with self-compassion, and acting from your values rather than waiting to feel ready."
- **objectives:**
  - Recognize the post-event autopsy and use a concrete method to decline it.
  - Run a self-compassion break after a hard social moment.
  - Apply the ACT stance: act toward what you value while anxious, instead of waiting for calm.
- **prereqs:** M1, M7
- **owns (canon):** cutting **post-event rumination** (the autopsy); the **self-compassion break**; the **ACT values/willingness** stance; the liking-gap hook; the "kind friend" image.
- **field drill:** after one real social event, decline the autopsy, run a self-compassion break, and write the one fact-based thing you'd actually adjust (if any).
- **est:** 20 min

### M12 — Build Your Ladder, and Keep Going
- **slug:** `12-build-your-ladder`
- **summary:** "Turn everything into a personal plan: a ranked ladder of feared situations you climb by violating predictions, a way to keep practicing for life, and a clear sense of when to bring in a professional."
- **objectives:**
  - Build a personal fear ladder rated by your own distress scale and choose a starting rung.
  - Assemble a sustaining weekly practice from the course's drills, and name credible next steps (improv, VR, guided programs).
  - State clearly the signs that mean it's time to see a professional.
- **prereqs:** M1–M11
- **owns (canon):** the personal **fear ladder** (SUDS, self-paced); the sustaining-practice plan; the modalities map (improv/VR/iCBT, honestly graded); the closing reinforcement of the when-to-seek-help criteria.
- **field drill:** write your own ladder (8–12 rungs), pick the starting rung, and schedule the next two weeks of drills.
- **est:** 20 min
