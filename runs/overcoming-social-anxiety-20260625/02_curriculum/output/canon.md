# Canon — Overcoming Social Anxiety: A Field Manual

The course's consistency contract. **Binding** on every authoring sub-agent and on the editorial
stage. One call per item, no TBDs. This is a `skill` course: there are no formal math blocks; the
"framework" in each module is a short named set of moves, and assessment is a **rubric**. Two shared
assets below (the field-drill rubric §6 and the safety language §7) must appear, near-verbatim, in
every module that the ledger assigns them to.

## 3. Metaphor-ownership map

One running image per module. Each must point true, pay off, and cash into the skill.

| Image | Owner | Why it points true |
|-------|-------|--------------------|
| **The loop / the spiral that feeds itself** | M1 | the four links each make the next more likely — a genuine feedback cycle |
| **Tapping the brake (not turning off the engine)** | M2 | the sigh lowers arousal a notch to let you drive into the situation; it doesn't stop the car or remove the fear |
| **A spotlight you can swing** | M3 | attention is a controllable beam; anxiety points it at you, the skill swings it outward (and the imagined audience spotlight is dimmer than it feels) |
| **Free information as conversational handholds** | M4 | each thing offered is a hold you can grab to climb the conversation |
| **Pulling the thread** | M5 | a follow-up question pulls on what they handed you and the conversation unspools |
| **Nerves behind glass** | M6 | you feel them banging on the glass; from outside they're barely visible — the transparency illusion made concrete |
| **The scientist running a test** | M7 | a prediction + a clean test + an observed result + an updated belief is literally the experimental method |
| **Taking the training wheels off** | M8 | crutches feel like what keeps you upright; you only learn you can balance once they're off |
| **A small safe dose (like a vaccine)** | M9 | a tiny, controlled exposure to social "failure" builds tolerance to the real thing — exposure, made intuitive |
| **Taking up your space** | M10 | assertiveness is occupying the room you're entitled to, neither shrinking (passive) nor shoving (aggressive) |
| **The kind friend (vs the prosecutor)** | M11 | self-compassion is talking to yourself as you would a friend; the autopsy is a hostile prosecutor |
| **The ladder** | M12 | a ranked climb, one rung at a time, at your own pace |

**Cut, don't assign:** any image that implies you must *eliminate* anxiety to succeed (it contradicts
the whole course), or that frames the breath/attention tools as control rituals. Earworm/gravity-well
and "picture the audience naked" are banned. One image per module; don't stack a second.

## 5. Voice target

One narrator across all twelve modules: a **warm, direct, experienced coach who has clearly been on the
other side of this** and respects the reader as a capable adult. **Register:** plain, concrete, a little
wry; talks *to* the reader, not *about* the condition. **Warmth without softness:** encouraging but never
coddling, never preachy, never clinical. It names difficulty plainly and asks the reader to do hard
things, because the doing is the point. **It never shames** the reader for being anxious and never
promises a cure. **The signature move** is pairing a counterintuitive, evidence-backed reframe ("your
nerves barely show," "the no is the win," "don't wait to feel calm") with an immediate, concrete thing to
*go do*. No hype, no exclamation-point energy, no therapy-speak, no "in this module we will." Vary how
each module opens (M1 a relatable scene, M2 a 30-second technique, M3 a striking study, M7 a reframe of
failure, M9 a dare, M11 the 2 a.m. replay). Drift to correct: keep the safety/honesty passages warm and
matter-of-fact, never alarming or stiff.

## 6. THE SHARED FIELD-DRILL RUBRIC (every module's practice section uses this)

Module 7 *teaches* this; every other module's field drill is an instance of it. State the drill, then
give this as the self-assessment (adapt the wording to the module, keep the four checks):

> **The drill is a behavioral experiment. Before you go:**
> 1. **Predict** — write the specific feared outcome, how likely it is (0–100%), and how bad it would
>    really be.
> 2. **Drop a crutch** — name one safety behavior you'll deliberately leave behind so the test is clean.
> **Afterward, rate yourself (the point is what you learned, not whether you felt calm):**
> - Did you actually do it, with the crutch down? (showing up is the win)
> - What *actually* happened, versus what you predicted?
> - What does that do to the belief — even a little?
> - Did you let the anxiety be there instead of waiting for it to leave first?
>
> A strong rep isn't a calm one. It's one where reality got a chance to disagree with the prediction.

"check yourself" retrieval prompts (1–2 per module, recall the framework's named moves) live in the
body, also in `<details>`. The rubric is **self-scored**, no single right answer (skill kind).

## 7. THE SAFETY / RESPONSIBILITY LANGUAGE (reuse near-verbatim where the ledger says)

**M1 (front-load), adapt for voice — keep all four ideas:**
> This is a skills course, built on solid evidence, and it works by practice. It is **not therapy, not
> diagnosis, and not a substitute** for care from a qualified professional. Everyday social nerves are
> common and very workable with the skills here. But social anxiety also runs deeper for some people —
> when the fear is **persistent**, **out of proportion**, and **gets in the way of your work,
> relationships, or daily life**, that's worth more than a self-help course. Seeing a doctor or a
> therapist for it is a smart, ordinary move, not a failure. And if you ever have thoughts of harming
> yourself, treat that as urgent and reach out to a professional or local emergency help right away.

**When-to-seek-help criteria (M1, reinforced M12):** persistent (months, not shifting); impairing
(holding back work/study/relationships/life); intensely distressing; avoidance shrinking your world;
co-occurring low mood, panic attacks, or using alcohol/drugs to cope; **any** self-harm thoughts → urgent
help. Frame warmly: "this deserves real support."

**Referral framing — generic only, NEVER invent specifics:** point to "a GP or primary-care doctor," "a
licensed therapist or psychologist (ask for CBT, which is well-supported for social anxiety)," "your
country's recognized psychology/therapy register," and "local emergency services or a recognized crisis
line in your country." Tell readers to find the version that exists where they live. **No invented phone
numbers, org names, or statistics.**

**Drills stay small, safe, ethical (M9, and any mishap/exposure drill):** court only *trivial* social
risk; never harm, deceive, frighten, or seriously inconvenience anyone; nothing illegal or dangerous;
the target is *your own* discomfort, never another person. Start small; step down a rung if a drill
overwhelms you rather than flooding yourself. If a drill provokes overwhelming shame or panic, that's a
sign to ease off and, if it persists, to talk to a professional.

**No medical/diagnostic claims; no fabricated studies or numbers.** Where a method rests on
practitioner/popular practice (rejection practice, shame-attacking) or thinner evidence (improv,
self-compassion for social anxiety specifically), say so plainly while noting the underlying mechanism
(exposure + a violated prediction) is well-supported.
