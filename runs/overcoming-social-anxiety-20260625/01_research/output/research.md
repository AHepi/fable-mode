# Research Dossier — Overcoming Anxiety in Social Situations (practice-first, intro)

Consolidated planning dossier for the curriculum stage. Synthesizes three slice notes in this folder:
`notes-cbt-mechanism.md` (the CBT maintenance model and the core skills that target it),
`notes-cuttingedge-act-practice.md` (ACT, self-compassion, deliberate-mishap/rejection practice,

micro-skills, live-drill architecture, the cognitive-bias hooks, and the safety/responsibility layer).
Those files hold full per-item detail, evidence grades, and citations; this is the map.

**Evidence grades carried through every stage:** **Strong** (meta-analyses / multiple RCTs) ·
**Moderate** (some controlled evidence) · **Emerging** (early/single/non-clinical studies) ·
**Practitioner-framework** (clinical/coaching craft, not directly outcome-tested). Honest grading is a
course value: "cutting edge" here means *current best evidence plus credible emerging practice,
labeled for what it is* — never "novel but unproven sold as fact."

## The one-paragraph through-line

Social anxiety is **maintained**, not just triggered, by a tight loop: in a feared situation attention
turns **inward** (self-monitoring), the person reads their own anxiety as evidence of how bad they look
(a distorted **observer's-eye self-image**), they deploy **safety behaviors** (over-rehearsing,
gripping a drink, scripting, avoiding eye contact) that *prevent* them from ever learning the feared
disaster won't happen, and afterward they run a **post-event "autopsy"** that cements the distortion.
Every skill in this course breaks one link in that loop, and every skill is **practiced live with real
people**. The master move, drawn from the modern **inhibitory-learning** model of exposure (Craske et
al. 2014), is the **prediction-violating drill**: name a specific feared prediction, drop the safety
behavior, do the thing, watch reality contradict the prediction, update the belief — *without waiting to
feel calm first*. ACT (act toward your values while anxious) and self-compassion (drop the shame that
fuels the autopsy) are the stance that makes the drills repeatable; a precise breathing tool (the
**physiological sigh**) is an in-the-moment adjunct, not the cure.

## Scope

**In.** A practice ladder, each rung a skill the learner *does* with real people:
- A minimal, instrumental read of your own anxiety loop (the only "model" content) — self-focused
  attention, safety behaviors, catastrophic predictions, post-event rumination (Clark & Wells, **Strong**).
- In-the-moment physiological down-regulation: extended-exhale breathing and the **physiological sigh**
  (Balban et al. 2023, **Moderate**), framed as an adjunct, explicitly *not* "just relax."
- The **attention shift**: moving attention outward onto the conversation/task (Task Concentration
  Training, **Moderate**).
- Foundational conversation micro-skills: low-stakes openers and "free information"; **follow-up
  question-asking** (Huang et al. 2017, **Strong**) and reciprocal self-disclosure (Collins & Miller
  1994, **Strong**); warmth-first body language (Fiske/Cuddy, **Moderate**) with the **Mehrabian
  "93% nonverbal" myth actively debunked**; graceful exits (Mastroianni et al. 2021, **Moderate**).
- **Dropping safety behaviors** (Wells et al. 1995, **Strong**) and running **behavioral experiments**
  (Bennett-Levy; Clark et al. 2006 CT > exposure+relaxation, **Strong**).
- The **inhibitory-learning / expectancy-violation** way to run any drill (Craske 2014, **Strong**) —
  success = prediction violated + belief updated, *not* anxiety gone.
- **Deliberate social mishaps / rejection practice** (the cutting-edge live line): shame-attacking
  (Ellis, **Practitioner**), social-mishap exposures (Fang & Hofmann 2013, **Moderate**), rejection
  practice (Comely/Jia Jiang, **Practitioner/popular**) — all gated by safety.
- **Assertiveness / speaking up** in groups (Speed et al. 2018, **Moderate→Strong**).
- Cutting **post-event rumination** (maintaining factor, **Strong**) + **self-compassion** (Neff;
  Blackie & Kocovski 2018, **Moderate**).
- **ACT stance**: values over avoidance, defusion, willingness (Craske et al. 2014 ACT≈CBT, **Strong**).
- Building a personal **exposure ladder** (SUDS, Wolpe, **Strong**) and a sustaining practice;
  pointers to credible modalities (improv **Emerging**; VR exposure & guided iCBT **Strong**).
- The **cognitive-bias reframes** as motivating hooks: spotlight effect, illusion of transparency
  (doubles as an intervention), liking gap (all **Strong/Moderate**).

**Out.** Diagnosis; pharmacology; a literature survey; deep cognitive theory beyond the minimum;
anything positioning the course as therapy. Weak/dated methods are *named and refused*: relaxation as
the primary tool, positive affirmations, distraction-as-coping, "picture the audience naked," safety
behaviors dressed as coping, "wait until you feel calm."

## Prerequisite analysis (intro / general adult, no psychology background)

**Can be assumed:** ordinary adult social experience; they have *felt* social anxiety (racing heart,
blanking, dreading a party, replaying a conversation). Nothing technical.

**Must teach from scratch (all of it, in plain words):** every term — self-focused attention, safety
behavior, behavioral experiment, exposure, expectancy violation, SUDS, defusion, self-compassion. No
jargon may go unglossed. The cognitive-bias names (spotlight effect, illusion of transparency) are
taught as plain ideas, not cited jargon.

**No silent gaps:** each rung uses only earlier rungs. The "model" (A) is deliberately minimal and
front-loaded so every later skill can point back to *which link in the loop it breaks*.

## Hooks & examples (graded; vary the opening move across modules)

- **Spotlight effect** (Gilovich et al. 2000, **Strong**) — the embarrassing-T-shirt study: people
  thought ~twice as many noticed than actually did. Opening hook for "everyone's watching me."
- **Illusion of transparency** (Savitsky & Gilovich 2003, **Strong**) — speakers thought their nerves
  showed far more than observers saw, and *being told the nerves were invisible improved performance*.
  Doubles as an intervention; anchors the warmth/visibility module.
- **The liking gap** (Boothby et al. 2018, **Moderate/Strong**) — after a chat, people underestimate
  how much the other person liked them. Anchors the post-event-autopsy module.
- **"the the the" of social anxiety** — the meeting where you rehearse one sentence, grip your pen, and
  leave sure you were weird, having watched yourself instead of reading the room. Concrete loop example.
- **"How do I kill a Python process?" → over-refusal** belongs to the *other* course; do not reuse.
- **Jia Jiang's doughnut request** (rejection practice) — asked Krispy Kreme for Olympic-ring-linked
  doughnuts expecting a laugh-off; the employee made them. The "no" you fear is rarer and cheaper than
  predicted. Anchors the mishap/rejection module (label: popular/practitioner, mechanism = exposure).
- **The physiological sigh before the door** — two double-inhale-long-exhale breaths before walking in.
- **Improv "yes-and"** — you can't pre-script, so you stop monitoring yourself and just respond.

## Common pitfalls / misconceptions (teach against these explicitly)

1. **"I have to feel calm before I socialize."** Backwards — action comes first; learning happens
   *while* anxious. Waiting for calm is avoidance. (Craske inhibitory learning.)
2. **"Safety behaviors help me cope."** They relieve anxiety for a minute but maintain it by blocking
   disconfirmation, and many worsen performance. The tell: you believe the catastrophe *would* have
   happened without the crutch.
3. **"Stay in it until the anxiety drops."** The habituation myth. Success is a *violated prediction*,
   not a calm body; fluctuating/maintained anxiety during a drill is fine.
4. **"One bad interaction proves I'm hopeless."** Catastrophizing + overgeneralization; beliefs update
   over repeated, varied tests.
5. **"Feeling anxious = performing badly."** Felt anxiety ≠ visible/poor performance (illusion of
   transparency).
6. **"Everyone notices my flaws / can tell I'm nervous."** Spotlight effect + transparency illusion.
7. **"Body language is 93% of communication."** The Mehrabian myth — **debunk it**, don't teach it;
   prioritize warmth and genuine interest over posture tricks.
8. **"I just need to learn how to socialize."** Most socially anxious adults have intact skills inhibited
   by anxiety (performance-deficit model); micro-skills are scaffolding for showing up, not the cure —
   but a minority do have genuine skill gaps, so teach the distinction.
9. **"Acceptance = giving up; self-compassion = going soft; a 'no' = failure."** The three most common
   derailers — pre-empt all three. Acceptance is willingness in service of values; self-compassion
   builds resilience; in rejection practice the "no" *is* the win.
10. **"Reviewing the event afterward helps me improve."** The post-mortem is rumination, not learning;
    it's negatively biased and makes the next event scarier.

## Open questions for the curriculum stage

1. **Module count / cut.** ~11 skill modules fits the ladder. Decide whether (a) openers and
   follow-up-questions are one module or two, and (b) post-event rumination + self-compassion + ACT
   stance are one module or split. Recommendation: keep conversation skills as 2 modules (start →
   carry/warmth), and bundle rumination+self-compassion+ACT as one "after the event / the inner stance"
   module so the *doing* modules dominate.
2. **Where the model lives.** Put the minimal anxiety-loop model in module 1 only, instrumental, and
   have every later module name *which link it breaks* (canon ledger fixes ownership) rather than
   re-explaining the loop.
3. **The live-drill rubric is the course's spine.** Every module ends in a real-world drill scored on
   the inhibitory-learning criterion (prediction stated → safety behavior dropped → reality observed →
   belief updated), **not** on "did you calm down." The canon must fix this rubric once and have each
   module instantiate it. Keep imagined scenarios only as warm-ups.
4. **Safety thread.** The "skills not therapy / when to seek help / drills stay small & ethical" content
   must appear in module 1 (up front) AND be reinforced in the mishap module (8) and the closing module
   (11). Canon should name the exact non-stigmatizing language and the referral framing (generic, no
   invented orgs/numbers).
5. **Honest grading in-text.** Where a drill rests on practitioner/popular practice (rejection therapy,
   shame-attacking) or thinner evidence (improv, self-compassion-for-SAD), say so plainly while noting
   the *mechanism* (exposure + expectancy violation) is well-evidenced. Never state a `[verify]` figure
   (DSM duration, effect sizes, eye-contact "ideal seconds") as fact — keep qualitative.
6. **Kind = skill.** No math, no formal definitions; the "framework" per module is a short named set of
   moves; assessment is a rubric, not an answer key. Vary the opening move across modules (a bias study,
   a scenario, a failure, a drill) so 11 modules don't feel template-stamped.
