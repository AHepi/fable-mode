A few years ago a man named Jia Jiang walked into a Krispy Kreme and asked the woman behind the counter to make him doughnuts linked together and colored like the Olympic rings. He had no real expectation she'd say yes. The whole point was that she'd say no, because Jiang had set himself a strange project: a hundred days of going out and getting rejected on purpose, one absurd request at a time, to wear the fear down. He'd already been turned away from borrowing a stranger's dog and refused a "burger refill." He was braced for another no.

She didn't say no. She studied the order, pulled out a piece of paper, sketched the rings, thought about which flavors would give her the colors, and about fifteen minutes later handed him a box of Olympic-ring doughnuts. For free. He filmed the whole thing, and millions of people eventually watched it, because it landed on the one nerve this module is about. The disaster he'd braced for, the contempt, the eye-roll, the *who do you think you are*, simply never showed up. Half the outrageous things he asked for over those hundred days, people just gave him.

That's the whole lesson, and you don't need a camera or a hundred days to learn it. You need to go hear a few small noes and discover you're fine.

## Why you go looking for the failure

By now you know the move that breaks the loop. In module 7 you learned to treat a feared social situation as **a behavioral experiment**: write down the specific prediction, go test it, and let reality have a vote. Success isn't a calm body. Success is a surprised prediction, a forecast that turned out wrong. This module takes that exact engine and points it somewhere that feels, at first, insane. Instead of waiting for a feared situation to come to you, you go out and *manufacture* one. On purpose. Small.

Here's why that works, and why it's faster than carefully avoiding embarrassment for the rest of your life.

Social anxiety runs on two overestimates. You overestimate how *likely* a social disaster is, and you overestimate how *bad* it would be if it came. Researchers who studied this closely found that second one, the overblown sense of the *cost*, is doing a lot of the heavy lifting. You don't just think you might fumble. You think the fumble would be catastrophic, that people would think less of you in some lasting, important way. As long as you avoid ever fumbling, that price tag never gets checked. It sits there in your head, enormous and unargued.

A deliberate small "failure" checks the price tag. You commit a tiny, harmless blunder, or you ask for something likely to get a no, and then you watch what *actually* happens. Almost always: a shrug, a polite no, a moment of nothing, and the world rolls on. The gap between the catastrophe you predicted and the shrug you got is the lesson. The bigger that gap, the more the fear has to give ground. It's the same surprised prediction from module 7, just one you arranged yourself instead of waiting for.

Think of it as a small safe dose, like a vaccine. A vaccine works by giving your body a tiny, controlled taste of the thing it's afraid of, just enough to learn the threat is survivable, so the real encounter doesn't lay you flat. A courted no does the same for the fear of rejection. A staged stumble does it for the fear of looking foolish. You take a trivial, controlled dose of the exact thing you dread, your system learns "survivable," and the dread loses its grip on the real situations that matter.

### Three flavors, graded honestly

This practice comes in three forms. They share one engine and differ in how well the research backs them, so here's the honest version of each.

**Mishap drills.** You deliberately commit a minor, harmless social blunder to test the catastrophic price tag. Mispronounce a word and don't correct it. Ask an obvious question. Walk into a café and order, then change your mind out loud. The thing you're testing is the cost: *if I look slightly foolish, what does it actually cost me?* Of the three, this one has the most evidence. It comes out of modern cognitive behavioral therapy, where clinicians found that adding deliberate small blunders to treatment, specifically to attack that overestimated social cost, tends to work better than the cognitive work alone. Call it the better-evidenced flavor.

**Shame-attacking.** This is the older cousin of the mishap drill, from a clinician named Albert Ellis who built a whole therapy style partly around it back in the mid-twentieth century. The classic versions: ask a stranger a slightly silly question, wear something mildly odd, announce the time loudly on a train. The aim is identical, to sit in your own embarrassment and prove the imagined catastrophe doesn't arrive. Be straight about the evidence here: this is a long-standing *practitioner* tradition, not something with a stack of controlled trials behind it. But the mechanism underneath it, exposure plus a checked price tag, is the same well-supported engine, so it's an honest tool as long as you know what it is.

**Rejection practice.** You make a small, genuine request that's likely to get a no, and you go *for* the no. Ask for a discount you probably won't get. Ask for a tiny favor. The aim is to hear "no" and notice that you're completely fine. This is the Jia Jiang line from the opening, which started as a game invented by a man named Jason Comely where the winning move was *getting rejected*. It's the least formally studied of the three, popular practice rather than clinical protocol, and worth labeling as such. And again, the engine underneath, exposure to the feared thing plus a prediction that gets violated, is the part the science backs.

Notice the through-line. Across all three, **the win is hearing the no, or committing the mishap, and being fine.** Not getting the yes. Not pulling off a smooth recovery. If you ask for the discount and they say yes, that's a pleasant bonus and a sign your cost estimate was even more wrong than you thought, but it isn't the goal. The goal is to collect proof that the no, the blunder, the flicker of embarrassment, is a thing you can walk through. Chasing the yes quietly puts you back in the old game, where the outcome decides your worth. The point of this drill is to retire that game.

A yes is a nice bonus and tells you your predicted cost was too high, but it isn't the scorecard. If you find yourself only feeling good when they say yes, you've slipped back into letting the outcome judge you, which is the loop this drill exists to break.

## The rules that make it safe

Before you design one of these, the boundary, because it's not optional and it's what separates a clean drill from a bad idea.

The drills stay **small, safe, and ethical.** You are courting *trivial* social risk and nothing more. You never harm, deceive, frighten, or seriously inconvenience another person. Nothing illegal, nothing dangerous, nothing that could realistically damage someone's day, a relationship, or your job. The target of every drill is **your own discomfort**, never another person. The shop assistant, the stranger, the barista are not props in your exposure, and they're certainly not the butt of it. If a drill would cost someone *else* something real, it's the wrong drill. Pick another.

Start tiny, and keep the dial in your own hands. The dose is small for a reason. If a drill overwhelms you, the move is to **step down a rung**, not to flood yourself by pushing harder. Flooding isn't bravery, it's just a worse, riskier version of the practice, and it can teach the fear rather than shrink it. A drill that leaves you shaken and avoidant taught the wrong thing. Ease off, shrink it, try the smaller version.

And one honest flag, said plainly because it matters. For most everyday social nerves, these drills are safe and genuinely useful. But if your social anxiety runs deep, or if your fear of embarrassment is tangled up with real past wounds, bullying, abuse, a history that put shame in your body for serious reasons, then jumping straight into mishap and rejection drills can backfire. They can pour salt in a wound instead of healing a habit. If that's you, build with the earlier skills first, and lean on the self-compassion you'll learn in module 11 before you come here. And as module 1 laid out, when the anxiety is severe, comes with panic, or is getting in the way of your life, a professional is the right call, not a self-help drill. A therapist who does CBT works with exactly this. None of that is the failure case. It's just matching the tool to the size of the job.

## Designing a tiny one

So how do you build a drill that's small enough to actually do this week? Walk through it the way you'd think it through yourself.

Start with the dread, not the dare. Ask: what's the specific social cost I'm most afraid of? Say it's *looking stupid in front of someone who works in a shop I go to*. Good. Now design the smallest possible thing that pokes that exact fear and nothing bigger. Not "give a speech." Something like: walk into a café and ask whether they sell something they obviously don't, a hammer, a haircut, then just accept the no and order your coffee like a normal person.

Then make it a real experiment, because that's what gives it teeth. This is **a behavioral experiment, the same as module 7**, so before you go, write the prediction down. Be specific. Not "it'll be bad" but "they'll give me a weird look, maybe a 70% chance, and I'll feel humiliated for the rest of the day." A number and a cost. That sentence is the thing reality is about to argue with.

And drop a crutch, the way you practiced in **module 8**. The classic crutch on a drill like this is the apologetic exit: the nervous laugh, the "sorry, never mind, I'm being silly," the rush to explain yourself so nobody thinks you're actually odd. That little scramble is you protecting yourself from the very discomfort you came to feel, and it robs the drill of its lesson the same way gripping the mug robbed you in module 1. Leave it behind. Ask the daft question, hear the answer, and let the small silence just sit there. That silence is the dose.

<details><summary>Check yourself: someone designs a "drill" where they cut in front of a long line at the pharmacy to practice handling disapproval. What's wrong with it, and how would you fix it?</summary>

It breaks the safety rule, because the cost lands on *other people*, not on the person doing the drill. Everyone behind them in the line is genuinely inconvenienced, and some of them might need that medicine. The target of a drill is always your own discomfort, never someone else's. The whole point is courting *trivial* risk that you carry yourself.

The fix is to find a version where you're the only one paying. Ask a clerk if you can have a small discount "just because," and accept the no. Ask a barista an obvious question. The embarrassment is yours, the inconvenience to anyone else is close to zero, and you still get to test the price tag.

</details>

## Field drill: go get one no

This week, run one. A single small, safe, ethical request that's likely to get a no, or one minor harmless mishap, performed on purpose. The cleanest starter is a polite, low-stakes ask you'd expect to be turned down on: a small discount you almost certainly won't get, a tiny favor, a free extra. Make it genuine, make it polite, and make it something a stranger could refuse without a second thought.

The win is hearing the no and noticing you're fine. Not the yes. If you get a yes, smile, take it, and mark down that your cost estimate was even more wrong than you feared.

Before you go, run a couple of physiological sighs first (module 2) if you need to take the edge off, then walk in anyway. Don't wait to feel ready. Ready isn't coming, and waiting for it is just avoidance wearing a reasonable face.

<details><summary>Score yourself (the point is what you learned, not whether you felt calm)</summary>

**Before you go:**

- **Predict.** Write the specific feared outcome, how likely it is (0 to 100%), and how bad it would *really* be. "I'll ask for the discount, they'll look annoyed, maybe 60% likely, and I'll feel like an idiot all afternoon."
- **Drop a crutch.** Name one safety behavior you'll deliberately leave behind so the test is clean. The usual one here is the apologetic escape: the nervous laugh, the "sorry, forget it." Leave it down. Make the ask, hear the answer, let the silence sit.

**Afterward, rate yourself:**

- Did you actually do it, with the crutch down? (Hearing the no and staying put is the win.)
- What *actually* happened, versus what you predicted? How big was the gap between the catastrophe and the shrug?
- What does that do to the belief that a small social failure costs you something serious, even a little?
- Did you let the anxiety be there instead of waiting for it to leave first?

A strong rep isn't a calm one. It's one where reality got a clear chance to disagree with the prediction, and the no turned out to be survivable.

</details>

## One question for you

Think of a small thing you've wanted but never asked for, because the asking felt too exposing. A seat swap on a train. A favor from a neighbor. A discount you assumed wasn't on offer. Notice that you've been quietly paying the price of the no your whole life, every time, by never asking, so as to avoid paying it once, in reality, where it usually turns out to cost almost nothing. What has the imagined no already talked you out of?

## So that's the dare

You spend an enormous amount of energy steering around small social failures, and the steering is what keeps them feeling huge. Go court a few on purpose, in tiny safe doses, and the fear learns what your reasoning never could: that the cost you dreaded is survivable, and usually trivial. The no is the win. The shrug is the lesson. Next we'll turn to holding your ground and saying what you actually think, which is its own kind of courted risk, with its own quiet payoff.
