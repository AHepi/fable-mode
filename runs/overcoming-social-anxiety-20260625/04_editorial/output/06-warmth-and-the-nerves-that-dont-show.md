## What the room actually saw

A group of people got up, one at a time, and gave a short talk with no time to prepare. Afterward, the researchers asked each speaker a simple question: how obvious was your nervousness to the people watching? The speakers were sure it had been written all over them. The flush, the shaky voice, the hands. Then the researchers asked the audience what they had actually noticed.

The gap was enormous. The watchers had seen a fraction of what the speakers were certain they'd broadcast. The nerves that felt like a siren going off were, from the outside, closer to background noise.

There's a name for this, and it's one of the most useful things in this whole course. It's called the **illusion of transparency**: the strong, automatic feeling that your inner state is leaking out for everyone to read, when in truth almost none of it shows. You feel the alarm at full volume from the inside. Other people get a faint, muffled version, if they get anything at all.

And here's the part that turns a fun fact into a tool. In that same study, the researchers tried something. Before some of the speakers went up, they simply told them this: that people watching tend to notice far less nervousness than speakers think. That was the whole intervention, a few sentences of truth. And those speakers felt better and gave better talks than the ones who weren't told. Knowing the nerves don't show, on its own, helped.

So this module asks you to do two things at once: lead with warmth, and stop trusting the feeling that your fear is on display. The second one you can start believing right now, because you just saw it measured.

## The framework: warmth leads, the nerves don't show, and the famous "rule" is a myth

Four ideas, and then a small set of things to actually do with your face and body.

**Warmth gets judged first.** When people meet you, they're reading two things: whether you're warm (friendly, safe, on their side) and whether you're competent (capable, sharp). Of those two, warmth comes first and counts for more in whether someone wants to be near you. People decide *do I like this person* before they decide *is this person impressive*, and they decide it fast. This is good news for an anxious person, because warmth is mostly about openness and attention, not polish. You do not have to be clever in the first ten seconds. You have to be approachable.

**The nerves don't show.** The illusion of transparency, from the hook. Your internal alarm is far less visible than it feels. This is not a pep talk; it's a finding, and it pairs with a friend you already met. Back in module 3 you learned that the spotlight is dimmer than it feels, that people notice you less than you assume. This is the same dimming, aimed at your nerves specifically. Not only are people paying you less attention than you fear, but the nervousness you're sure they're cataloguing is mostly invisible even when they do look.

**The famous "rule" is a myth.** You have almost certainly heard that communication is "93% body language" and only 7% the actual words, sometimes dressed up as "7 percent words, 38 percent tone, 55 percent body." It is one of the stickiest claims in the self-help world, and it is wrong. It comes from a pair of small lab studies in the 1960s that looked at one narrow thing: how people read someone's *feeling* when the words and the tone flatly contradict each other, like a sarcastic "great." Those numbers were never a law of communication. The researcher whose name is attached to them, Albert Mehrabian, has spent decades saying so himself and asking people to stop quoting it. Do not learn it. When the words and the signals genuinely clash, people lean on the signals to read your mood. That is all the studies ever showed.

**So what actually matters?** Not a percentage. Warmth, and attention pointed at the other person. The signals below are worth doing not because body language is 93% of anything, but because they're the visible side of being warm and interested, and they're things you control even when your heart is going.

Here is what to actually do, all of it within reach when you're nervous:

- **A genuine smile.** Not a held, frozen one. A small real one when you greet someone and when they say something you like. A genuine smile reaches the eyes and comes and goes; the performed kind sits there too long. You don't have to manufacture warmth, just let the real flickers of it show instead of locking your face down.
- **Open, oriented posture.** Turn your body toward the person. Uncross the arms, drop the shoulders, take up your normal space. Anxiety folds you inward and angles you toward the exit; warmth points you at the human in front of you.
- **Comfortable eye contact, not staring.** Look at the person while they talk and while you talk, and let your eyes drift away naturally now and then the way everyone's do. You are not trying to win a staring contest, and you are not trying to hit some "correct" number of seconds. Anyone selling you an exact figure is making it up. Comfortable and present, glancing away sometimes, is the whole instruction.
- **Nodding.** Small nods while they speak. It signals you're with them, and it gives your anxious body a thing to do that happens to read as warmth.
- **Their name.** Use it. "Good to meet you, Priya." A name lands as warmth and attention, and saying it once early helps you remember it, which spares you a later worry.

None of these is a trick. They're the outward face of pointing yourself at another person instead of monitoring yourself, which is the spotlight-outward move from module 3 wearing a body.

## The nerves behind glass

Hold one image for the rest of this. Picture your nervousness as something trapped behind a pane of thick glass. From your side, it's deafening. You can feel it banging and rattling against the glass, the racing heart, the heat in your face, the certainty that it's all spilling out. It feels like everyone can hear the banging.

They can't. From the other side of the glass, where everyone else is standing, almost none of that noise gets through. They see a person who turned toward them and smiled. The storm you're living through is happening behind a window they can barely see into.

This is the illusion of transparency made concrete, and it's why the two halves of this module fit together. You lead with warmth, the smile and the open posture and the eye contact, and that warmth is on the far side of the glass where people actually live. The nerves stay on your side, mostly soundproofed. You spend so much energy trying to hide a thing that is already, by and large, hidden. You can put that energy into the warmth instead.

<details><summary>Check yourself: name the warmth signals you actually control</summary>

A genuine smile; open posture turned toward the person; comfortable eye contact that drifts away naturally (no "right" number of seconds); nodding while they talk; using their name.

And the one thing *not* to do: treat the "93% body language" claim as real. It came from narrow studies about reading feelings when words and tone conflict, and the researcher behind it rejects the popular version.

## A walkthrough: the doorway and the first thirty seconds

Say you're arriving at a colleague's leaving drinks. You know maybe two people. Your old move is to find a wall, fix your eyes on your phone, fold your arms around your drink, and brace, while a voice insists that everyone can see you're a wreck.

Run it the new way instead.

You spot someone you half-know standing near the food. The nerves are loud, the glass is rattling, and that's fine; you're not waiting for them to stop. You walk over with your body actually turned toward them, not edged toward the door. You let a real, small smile happen, the kind that comes and goes, not a held grin. "Hey, Marcus, good to see you."

Now the part that matters most. The whole time, your attention wants to swing back onto yourself: *Is my smile weird? Can he tell my voice is shaking? Am I going red?* That monitoring is the inward spotlight from module 1, and it does two bad things at once. It feeds the loop, and it pulls your attention off Marcus, which makes you a worse, colder listener. So you swing the beam back onto him. You look at him while he answers, you nod, you actually hear what he says.

Here's the quiet trick underneath all of it. You can't aim your attention at warmth and at self-monitoring at the same time. Doing the warm thing, turning, smiling, listening, *is* how you stop checking the glass. The signals aren't only for him. They're what gets your attention off yourself.

And the prediction you walked in with, that your nerves were on full display? Marcus is just glad you came over. The banging you could hear so clearly never made it through the glass.

## Field drill: lead with warmth, and check who's right about the glass

This week, run one real interaction where you lead with warmth on purpose. A greeting at work, a chat with a neighbor, ordering coffee and meaning it, joining one conversation at a gathering. Going in, pick two or three warmth signals to actually do: turn toward them, let a real smile happen, use their name, hold comfortable eye contact, nod while they talk.

Then test the glass. Before you go in, predict how visible your nerves were on a scale of 0 to 100, where 100 is "obviously, painfully nervous to anyone looking." Write the number down. Afterward, find out the truth. Either ask a trusted person who was there for an honest read, "did I seem nervous, and how much?", or, if no one was, do a fair self-review the next day, after the heat has cooled, instead of in the raw minutes right after.

The win is not that you felt calm. You almost certainly won't, and that's not the test. The win is that you led with warmth and then *checked your prediction against reality* instead of just believing the feeling. This is a small experiment on the glass, and the full method for running these arrives in module 7.

<details><summary>Score yourself (the win is testing the belief, not feeling calm)</summary>

**Before you go:**

- **Predict.** Name the interaction and your specific fear about it, and put a number on how visible you think your nerves will be, 0 to 100. "Joining the kitchen conversation at the party, I'll look obviously rattled, 75 out of 100." How bad would that honestly be if it were true?
- **Drop a crutch.** Name one safety behavior you'll deliberately leave behind so the test is clean. The drink held up like a shield, the eyes on the floor, the arms folded, the early escape. Leaving one down is what lets you learn the warmth was yours.

**Afterward:**

- Did you actually do it, led with your chosen warmth signals, crutch down? (Leading with warmth is the win, not feeling calm.)
- What did the honest read or your fair self-review say about how visible your nerves were, compared to the number you predicted?
- What does that gap do to the belief that your fear is on display, even a little?
- Did you let the nerves come along behind the glass instead of waiting for them to quiet down first?

A strong rep isn't a calm one. It's one where you led with warmth and gave reality a chance to disagree with your guess about the glass.

</details>

## One honest question

Think back to a recent moment you walked away from sure you'd come across as a nervous wreck. You replayed it, you cringed. Now ask the harder question: do you actually *know* that's how you came across, or is that the view from inside the glass, where the alarm is always at full volume? You have never once heard your own nervousness the way the room hears it. The number in your head is a guess, and the research says it runs high.

## Where this leaves you

You walked in with one belief and you can walk out with a better one. Warmth is what people read first, and it's built from things you can do with a nervous body: a real smile, an open turn toward someone, steady-enough eyes, a name. The fear you're certain is broadcasting is mostly trapped behind glass, far quieter outside your own head than in it, and simply knowing that helps. The "93% body language" line you can drop in the bin where it belongs. What you've added isn't a posture to perform. It's a place to aim, outward and warm, and a reason to stop trusting the feeling that you're easy to read.
