## Interpretation notes

**What the user asked for, in plain words.** A course that *reduces social anxiety through practiced
skill*, weighted hard toward "things you actually do with other people." The user was explicit on two
points: (1) **less theory, more practiceable skill** — so every module must end in a *live* drill the
learner performs with real humans, not just a thought experiment; (2) **cutting edge** — so the content
must reflect the current evidence base and credible emerging practice, not 1990s "just relax and think
positive" advice.

**The spine I'm taking.** Modern, evidence-based treatment of social anxiety (the Clark & Wells
cognitive model and its descendants; behavioral-experiment and exposure science; ACT and
self-compassion; attention training) converges on a small set of mechanisms a learner can target
directly:
  - **Self-focused attention** is the engine: anxiety spikes when attention turns inward (monitoring
    your own heartbeat, picturing how you look). The skill is shifting attention *outward* onto the
    actual conversation. (Trainable live.)
  - **Safety behaviors** (over-rehearsing lines, gripping a drink, avoiding eye contact, mentally
    scripting) *maintain* anxiety by preventing disconfirmation. The skill is dropping them and running
    **behavioral experiments** to test feared predictions. (Trainable live.)
  - **Catastrophic predictions** ("I'll go red and everyone will judge me") are *tested*, not argued
    with, via real-world experiments and deliberate **social mishaps** (the cutting-edge
    shame-attacking / rejection-practice line: deliberately, safely courting small social "failures"
    to learn the cost is survivable).
  - **Post-event rumination** (the mental autopsy afterward) maintains anxiety; the skill is a
    structured way to cut it.
  - **Values over avoidance** (ACT): act toward what matters while anxious, instead of waiting to feel
    calm first.
  - **Foundational micro-skills** anxiety degrades and practice rebuilds: in-the-moment physiological
    down-regulation (physiological sigh / paced exhale), eye contact and warmth, small-talk openers and
    "free information," active listening, conversational threading, graceful exits.

**Scope — in.** A practice ladder: a quick read of your own anxiety cycle (1 short module, the only
"model" content, kept minimal and instrumental) → in-the-moment body regulation → the attention shift →
foundational conversation micro-skills (openers, listening/threading, eye contact + warmth, graceful
exits) → dropping safety behaviors → behavioral experiments → deliberate social mishaps / rejection
practice → assertiveness and speaking up in groups → cutting post-event rumination + self-compassion →
building a personal exposure ladder and a sustaining practice. Every module ships a **live field drill**
("do this with a real person this week") plus a self-rating rubric.

**Scope — out.** Diagnosis. Pharmacology. A literature review. Deep cognitive theory (only the minimum
model needed to make the skills make sense). This is not therapy and not a substitute for it.

**Rigor / kind.** `kind: skill` — framework + walkthrough + live practice + rubric self-assessment, no
math, no formal proofs. "Cutting edge" is honored by grounding skills in current mechanism science
(self-focused attention, safety behaviors, behavioral experiments, ACT, self-compassion, attention
training) and credible emerging practice (rejection/mishap practice, VR/app-assisted exposure, improv),
each labeled for how well-supported it is.

**Responsibility (non-negotiable, threads through the course).** Social anxiety runs from everyday
nerves to **social anxiety disorder**, a real clinical condition. The course states plainly, up front
and where relevant, that it teaches *skills*, is *not therapy or diagnosis*, and that persistent,
severe, or impairing anxiety (or any thoughts of self-harm) warrants a qualified professional; it
points to that help rather than positioning itself as a replacement. Exposure / mishap drills are
scaled to be genuinely *small and safe* (the point is learning the cost is survivable, never self-harm
or harassing others). The bias screen watches for stigmatizing framing. No fabricated statistics or
fake studies.

**Ambiguity calls.** "Cutting edge" is read as *current best evidence plus credible emerging practice,
honestly graded* — not "novel and unproven." "Practiced live with others" is read literally: the
assessment of every module is a real-world drill, with imagined scenarios only as warm-up. Running
autonomously (gates: []) per the user's clear "just build it" pattern; curriculum still self-verified.
